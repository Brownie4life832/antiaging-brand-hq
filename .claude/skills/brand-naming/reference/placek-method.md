# The Placek / Lexicon naming method — full digest

> Source material: David Placek (founder, **Lexicon Branding**, est. 1982; 4,000+ naming projects) — the *My First Million* and *Lenny's Podcast* interviews (2025), the Lenny's Newsletter writeup, Lexicon's own materials, and the academic literature on sound symbolism. Compiled 2026-06-19. This is the **reference**; the operational checklist is in [`../SKILL.md`](../SKILL.md).

Lexicon's hits: **Pentium, PowerBook, BlackBerry, Swiffer, Febreze, Dasani, Impossible Burger, Sonos, Azure, Vercel, Windsurf, Subaru Outback, Scion, OnStar, Slimfast.**

---

## 1. Why the name matters (the business case)

- **Highest-frequency, longest-lived asset.** "Nothing you do will be used more often or for longer than your name." Design, messaging, and products all change; the name persists.
- **Asymmetric advantage** — a distinctive name claims unoccupied semantic territory and hands you an edge *on launch day, before a single marketing dollar is spent.*
- **Cumulative advantage** — every time the name is seen/said/typed/searched, the bond compounds. Distinctiveness is what lets it compound; a descriptive name blurs into the category.
- **The price of getting it wrong:** Swiffer (~$5B) vs. Clorox's descriptive "ReadyMop" (~couple hundred million). Same era, same shelf. Placek attributes ~90–120%+ of the first-year difference to the name.
- The name is **the first and cheapest growth lever.**

## 2. What a *right* name does (not merely a "good" name)

Three jobs — **and almost everyone nails the first two and skips the third:**
1. **Get attention** (harder than ever).
2. **Hold it via processing fluency** — easy to pronounce *and* parse; the brain is lazy and leans toward what's easy.
3. **Surprise** — unexpected, *not* comfortable, *not* popular, *not* descriptive.

The target paradox: **"surprising, but surprisingly familiar."** Familiar enough to trust and process; novel enough to spark imagination and stick. A name should **start a story, not state a fact** — and **build trust + imagination at once** (description does neither).

> Litmus from research: you want a customer to say *"I don't know much about them, but they're **not like the other guys**."* That's a *predisposition to consider* — the job of the name.

## 3. The three-step process: **Identify → Invent → Implement**

### IDENTIFY (diagnose the strategy)
Long conversations about **behavior and experience** — "how do you behave now, how do you want to behave in the future?" — *not* "tell me your positioning/mission/values" (too logical, dated). Behavior is **bidirectional**: how the brand behaves toward the market and how the market behaves toward it (a building makes you behave differently at a temple vs. a Holiday Inn). Study the **landscape** (competitors' names + the language they all use) to know where *not* to go. Output a **creative framework** — a *window* to travel through (breadth), not a narrow objectives list.

### INVENT (generate enormously, judge nothing yet) — *Lexicon's proprietary edge*
- **Quantity → quality.** Generate **1,000–1,500+** (often 2,000–3,000) ideas/directions before evaluating anything. ~250 of 3,000 are worth polishing; shortlist ~50–100; present ~10–15. **Evaluation is banned during generation.**
- **Small teams, never big brainstorms** (peer pressure + premature evaluation kill bold ideas). Two-person teams.
- **The three-teams technique with *misleading* briefs:** Team 1 gets the real brief; Team 2 thinks they're naming a competitor or an adjacent product; Team 3 names something off-category entirely. *Most winning names come from Teams 2 & 3* (Windsurf came from a team just listing "flow" concepts, not thinking about IDEs).
- **Make the intangible tangible** (software/"code" → the physical world of *flow* → wind + surf).
- **Treasure hunt** ("Ship of Gold": map the whole sea, then dive). Mine: Latin/Greek roots, mythology, the periodic table, minerals/gems, constellations & light, botanicals, world languages, idioms, and **adjacent fields** (flip through hunting/flying magazines and steal words). **Engineer synchronicity** — collide your category with an unrelated field.
- **Morpheme combination engine** (the Vercel build): lists of evocative prefixes × suffixes, combined systematically.

### IMPLEMENT (build the case, clear, choose)
- **Global linguist gate** — Lexicon screens every finalist across ~7–15 languages via an in-house + worldwide linguist network (253+ linguists over four decades; 108 in 76 countries) for bad meanings/cultural landmines.
- **Trademark/clearability** — paralegals + a trademark attorney. Note Placek's warning: clearance is nearing a **tipping point** of difficulty; design for clearability now.
- **Present in context, never as a list** — mock ad, headline, product-on-shelf, a cap/tube. Let deciders see the **experience**, and arm your internal champion to sell it upward ("this is about the marketplace, not your boss's taste").
- **Consumer research that fires imagination** — not "does it fit the concept?" (that always rewards descriptive names) but how the name makes people *imagine/expect*.

## 3·5. The generation-strategy catalog — the 17 ways Lexicon makes a name

INVENT is not one activity. It's ~17 distinct strategies, and a real project **runs each as its own dedicated pass** — don't lean on just one or two (the classic failure mode is mining roots/foreign words and calling it done). Most famous names are *hybrids* of several (Pentium = morpheme-combo + periodic-table; Dasani = sound-first + embedded morpheme). The constructive ones sit on a **"realness-of-input" spectrum**: pure invented sound (Sonos) → embedded classical root (Dasani, Pentium, Vercel) → portmanteau (Febreze) → two whole real words (PowerBook).

**A. REFRAME moves — where to aim (do these before/while building):**

| Strategy | How to run | Example → produces |
|---|---|---|
| **Make the intangible tangible** | "If this product/feeling were physical, what world would it live in?" Convert the abstraction to a concrete scene, then mine that domain's words. | code→flow→**Windsurf** → physical-domain compounds/real words |
| **Benefit-ladder → ultimate feeling** | Climb feature→functional→emotional→worldview; stop at the most *surprising* top rung; treasure-hunt THAT feeling, not the product. (Not a publicly named Placek artifact; = the Diamond's "say to win" + the function→experience reframe.) | fiber→"lighter"→anti-gravity→**Feather** → evocative names tied to a payoff |
| **Reframe the question / name the future** | Change the question the name answers ("what is a mop?" → "what makes cleaning fun?"). Name where the company is *going*. If descriptive names still look viable, the reframe isn't done. | "ProMop"→**Swiffer** → an experience/future territory |
| **Anti-descriptor** | List every literal/ProX/EasyX descriptor, then deliberately go to the opposite — a non-descriptive word or feeling. Ban the category noun. | EasyMail→**BlackBerry** → evocative non-descriptors |
| **Win-by-contrast** | Map the category's shared naming language, then source from an unrelated, vivid domain far from that "sea of sameness." | Western SUV names→**Subaru Outback** → names in a reference world the category doesn't use |
| **Name the audacity / worldview** | Skip features; name the *stance/attitude*. Develop attitude, aim for believability over popularity, "change reality, don't describe it." | "they said it's impossible"→**Impossible** → bold, polarizing attitude names |

**B. CONSTRUCTION moves — how to build the word:**

| Strategy | How to run | Example → produces |
|---|---|---|
| **Deconstruct / reconstruct** | Strip to the physical action, ban the category noun, build a coinage from the phonestheme that *mouths* it (slice a base word, re-suffix). | swift→swiff→**Swiffer** → short sound-imitative coinages |
| **Portmanteau** | Fuse a substrate word + a benefit word, trimming at a shared sound so the seam disappears. | fabric+breeze→**Febreze** → invisibly fused coinages |
| **Morpheme engine** | Two columns — meaning-bearing roots × fluent suffixes — multiplied combinatorially over repeated waves. Lead with the consonant carrying your attribute. | **Vercel** (ver+cel; 55 prefixes × 102 suffixes, 3× over 2 weeks) → "surprisingly familiar" coinages (most trademarkable) |
| **Periodic-table / -ium coinage** | Root carrying the claim + elemental/scientific suffix (-ium/-on/-ide/-ene) → borrows chemistry's authority. (Sub-variant of the morpheme engine, science-sourced.) | pent-+-ium→**Pentium** → premium, science-credible coinages |
| **Sound-first coinage** | Decide the ONE attribute to pre-load, then bias phonemes toward it (see §5) so the *sound* connotes the benefit before meaning. | da+i+"san"→**Dasani** → phonetically-loaded coinages |
| **Structural / orthographic devices** | Engineer the word's *shape*: palindromes, clean CV-alternation, doubled consonants (judged as "more features"), symmetry. Apply as a shortlist constraint. | **Sonos**/Kayak (palindrome), Google (doubled-o) → memorable, globally robust names |
| **Compounds (1+1=3)** | Bolt a core-attribute word to a concrete ownable word (no blending) so the pair sparks a third image. Don't reject for length. | **PowerBook**, BlackBerry, Coinstar → transparent multiplied-meaning names |

**C. SOURCING moves — where to dig:**

| Strategy | How to run | Example → produces |
|---|---|---|
| **Real-word metaphor / eponym** | Refuse the descriptor; pick a dictionary word, color, place, or person whose denotation does the strategic work by association. | **Azure** (refused "…cloud"); **Scion** (=heir, Toyota) → evocative real words & eponyms |
| **World-language hunt** | Mine many languages for a word that *natively* carries the meaning/feel, then screen across markets via linguists. | **Zima** (Russian "winter") → repurposed foreign words |
| **Synchronicity / source-library raid** | Stock many unrelated wells (Greek/Latin roots, myth, periodic table, minerals, constellations, botany, aviation, nautical, idioms) and *magazines outside your reading*; harvest collisions; merge cross-category lists. | the raw-material engine behind every other pass |

**D. THE CONTAINER:**

| Strategy | How to run | Example → produces |
|---|---|---|
| **Three-team decoy** | 2–3 small pairs: Team 1 = real brief; Team 2 = a competitor/adjacent product; Team 3 = off-category (a bike). Decoy teams "are free to make mistakes" and produce most winners. Assign each a far-apart metaphor domain. Solo = several self-briefings. | **Swiffer** (toys/vacations/tools teams) → a pool that escapes category lock-in |

> **Lexicon's named assets** (for accuracy): the evaluation engine is **Predict™** (four measured dimensions), the linguistic network is the **Language Suite** (100+ PhD linguists, 78 countries, <7-day turnaround), and the sound-symbolism research is the **"Sounder" study** (Will Leben, Stanford). *(There is no Lexicon framework called "Name Lab" — that's a different, competing firm, NameLab.)* Consumer research tests **believability** — explicitly *not* "do you like it?" and *not* "does it fit the concept?" (concept-fit always rewards the descriptive name).

## 4. The Diamond exercise (run this first)

Draw a diamond (two triangles). Fill four points; spend most time on the last.

- **▲ Top — WIN:** what does winning actually look like (in 3 years)? (Ask a room of 5 and you'll get 5 answers — surface them.)
- **► Right — what we HAVE to win:** the assets/strengths already in hand (startups under-credit these).
- **▼ Bottom — what we NEED to win:** resources/truths/capabilities to acquire (people often say "a good name" — correct it to "the *right* name").
- **◄ Left — what we have to SAY to win:** *the engine of the name.* Push into metaphor, behavior, experience. This is where the name comes from.

Companion: a **Behavior & Experience brief** — finish "When someone hears this name I want them to FEEL ___ / DO ___," banning feature-adjectives.

## 5. Sound symbolism — engineer how a name *feels* before it's understood

The science is real and non-arbitrary (recurs across languages):
- **Bouba/kiki (maluma/takete):** rounded sounds → curvy shapes; sharp sounds → spiky shapes. Near-universal.
- **The size axis (front vs. back vowels):** front vowels (ee, i) feel **small, light, fast, sharp**; back/round vowels (oo, oh, ah) feel **large, heavy, rich, creamy**. (Frosh vs. Frish, Yorkston & Menon 2004: identical ice cream rated *creamier* as "Frosh" — one vowel.)
- **Processing fluency:** easy-to-say names are trusted, liked, and *bought* more (Alter & Oppenheimer 2006: easy-ticker IPOs outperformed hard ones early).
- **Consonant texture dial:** **plosives** (p,b,t,d,k,g) = hard/forceful/strong; **fricatives** (f,v,s,z) = fast/light/smooth; **sonorants — nasals (m,n) + liquids (l,r)** = comforting, flowing, luxurious.
- **Phonesthemes** (sub-morpheme clusters that leak meaning): **GL-** = light/glow (Glossier), **FL-** = flow, **SW-** = swift (Swiffer), **SM-** = smooth; **avoid SL-** (slime/sluggish) and **SN-** (sniff/sneer).

### The letter-feeling chart (Lexicon-attributed + linguistic consensus)

| Sound | Feeling | Sound | Feeling |
|---|---|---|---|
| **B** | reliable, dependable | **N** | friendly, soft |
| **C/K** (hard) | crisp, sharp | **O** (round) | rich, deep |
| **C/S** (soft) | smooth, sleek | **P** | punchy |
| **D** | solid | **Q (kw)** | quirky |
| **F** | airy, fresh | **R** | rolling, luxurious |
| **G** (hard) | strong | **S** | sleek, sonic ("noisy" signal) |
| **G/J** (soft) | gentle, lively | **T** | precise |
| **H** | breathy | **U** (round) | deep |
| **front A/E/EE/I** | open, light, bright | **V** | **most alive / vibrant** |
| **back A/AH/O/OO/U** | large, round, creamy | **W** | warm |
| **L** | flowing | **X** | fast, crisp, innovative |
| **M** | soothing | **Z** | energetic, modern, "noisy" |

> **The luxury-beauty house style:** cosmetics & hospitality cluster on the gentle sonorants **M, N, L, R, W, H** + soft vowels (L'Oréal, Revlon, Maybelline, Aramis; Marriott, Hilton, Wyndham). For a **soothing premium skincare** brand, lean on **liquids (L, R) + nasals (M, N) + round vowels (O, U)**; borrow a **fricative (F, V, S) or front vowel** for "light/fresh"; use **GL-** for glow; **avoid SL-/SN-** and harsh all-plosive stacks. A **V** can pre-load *vitality* without making a regulated claim.

## 6. Choosing — polarization, comfort, and the myths

- **"There is no power in comfort."** If the team is comfortable, you don't have the name yet.
- **Polarization is a buy signal.** A split room = energy. (Andy Grove chose **Pentium** over "ProChip" *because* it polarized: "that tells me there's energy for Pentium.") Sonos was "not entertainment enough"; Azure was "dumb"; BlackBerry's founders thought Lexicon was "crazy."
- **"You won't know it when you see it" is a myth** — across ~4,000 projects the instant-aha essentially never happens; the right bold name feels uncomfortable first, then grows on you.
- **The .com is "just an area code."** Get the right name first; buy the exact .com later (~$15–30K) or use a prefix/suffix or `.ai`/`.co`. With AI, exact-match SEO matters less.
- **AI shifts the edge to *judgment*, not generation** — anyone can spit out candidates now; discrimination/strategy is the moat.
- **Sometimes the right answer is *don't rename*** — the real problem is often messaging.

### Two field drills (evaluate without bias)
1. To colleagues: not "what do you think of this name?" → **"what could this name *do* for us?"**
2. To friends: **"A competitor just launched, they're called ____ — what do you think?"** (you learn what the name *does*, not their taste).

## 7. The case file (stories → transferable lessons)

| Name | The move | Lesson for a skin-longevity brand |
|---|---|---|
| **Pentium** | "pent-" (five) + "-ium" (sodium/titanium) — *surprisingly familiar*, unexpected for a chip | Borrow fluency from an adjacent lexicon (science/elemental) while staying unexpected for skincare. **You can brand the invisible** (a mechanism/ingredient). |
| **BlackBerry** | Reframed from EasyMail/MegaMail (function) to the *emotional* job — a device that raises BP should *calm*; dependable "B" | **Name the feeling, not the function.** Soothe the anxiety your category creates (the anti-fear opening). |
| **Swiffer** | "Avoid the word *mop* like the plague"; sound-symbolized the *action* (swipe→swiff→Swiffer); 80%+ trial intent in research | **Sound-symbolize the experience, not the object.** Make a routine feel *light & pleasurable*; ditch the tired category noun. |
| **PowerBook** | Welded two everyday words → 1+1=3; shed "notebook" baggage | **Compounds multiply meaning** — but only promise what the product keeps. |
| **Febreze** | Portmanteau: *fabric* + *breeze*; soft F + "breeze" = freshness | **Fuse substrate + benefit** into one coined word (e.g., a barrier/repair + calm fusion). |
| **Dasani** | Invented; "da" crisp + Latin "san" (health) + light final "i"; modeled cross-language health sounds | **Manufacture premium/health meaning from sound + embedded roots** in a commodity field. |
| **Azure** | Refused "…cloud"; poetic real word; attention-grabbing "z" | **Resist the literal category word**; evoke by association; expect "that's dumb." |
| **Sonos** | Coined from "sonus" (sound); **palindrome** (reads same upside down) | **Engineer elegance into the structure** (palindrome/CV-rhythm) on a meaning-rich root. |
| **Vercel** | "ver" (truth/verde) + "cel" (accelerate/cell); led with **V** (alive) | **Assemble familiar fragments**; pick the consonant that carries your core attribute (V = vitality). |
| **Windsurf** | From "flow/motion" list; athletic, natural — counter to cold "synthetic" AI names | **Win by contrast**; collide with an unrelated, vivid real-world domain. |
| **Impossible** | Someone wrote "impossible" — "a lot like them"; named the audacity | **Name the audacity / the worldview**, not the ingredient. |
| **Subaru Outback** | Field full of Western names (Explorer/Pathfinder) → went to the Australian outback | **In a clustered category, go sideways** to an adjacent-but-unexpected territory. |
| **Scion** | Real word = "heir/descendant" → borrowed parent credibility + youth | A **real dictionary word** with the right denotation can do strategic work. |
| **OnStar / Copilot** | Guiding-star imagery / non-threatening "assistant" metaphor | **Match form to the emotional promise**; a warm metaphor softens intimidating tech (= clinical science). |
| **DieHard (anti-pattern)** | Brilliant descriptor that *dated* once reliability became standard | **Avoid the descriptive/benefit trap** — don't bake "anti-aging" (a present truth) into a forever name. |

## 8. DIY founder playbook (solo / small team / few days)

1. **Diamond** (~30 min, solo) + Behavior/Experience brief (10 min).
2. **Map the landscape** — list competitors' names + shared language; decide where *not* to go.
3. **The 1,000 Sprint** (2–4 timed sessions): generate to a floor of 500, target 1,000+. **Create only — zero judgment.** Run yourself through several self-briefings (the solo version of three teams): the real brief, an adjacent product, an off-category product. Treasure-hunt roots/myth/nature/other languages; steal words from unrelated magazines.
4. **Police the language** in sessions: ban "that'll never pass legal / that's weird" → reframe to "how could we make this work?"
5. **Then** evaluate ruthlessly: surprisingly-familiar test · mouth-feel/fluency test (say it ×3 fast; can a stranger spell it?) · sound-symbolism audit · phonestheme audit (reward GL-/FL-/SW-/SM-, cut SL-/SN-) · front/back vowel swap to tune richness.
6. **Hunt polarization**; choose bold over comfortable.
7. **Pressure-test** with the two drills; mock up the top few in context.
8. **Clear** (knockout TM search + the global-meaning check); get the name first, solve the domain after.
