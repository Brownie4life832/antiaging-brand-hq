Ingredient: Glycols (Propanediol, Butylene / Pentylene / Propylene / Hexylene Glycol, Polyglycerins) (INCI: Propanediol; Butylene Glycol; Pentylene Glycol; Propylene Glycol; Hexylene Glycol; Polyglycerin-3, etc.)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ shortlisted (workhorse formulation glycols) · ⬜ considering · ⬜ rejected · ⬜ in a product Class: Humectants & hydration actives · Slug: glycol-humectants

One-line take: This is a family of small diol/polyol solvents — they hydrate (secondary humectants), dissolve and stabilize other ingredients, push actives through the stratum corneum, and boost preservatives. They are infrastructure, not anti-aging actives. None meaningfully builds collagen, fights glycation, clears senescent cells, or reverses photoaging. The brand-relevant decisions here are (1) which glycol to use as the workhorse — and the answer is overwhelmingly bio-based propanediol over propylene glycol on a tolerability + sustainability basis — and (2) never letting a glycol carry an anti-aging headline.

1. Identity

This dossier covers a chemically related family, not one molecule. They share a small-diol/polyol backbone and overlapping functions (humectancy, solvency, penetration enhancement, preservative-boosting), but differ meaningfully in tolerability and provenance.

Common name INCI CAS MW (g/mol) Note
Propanediol (1,3-propanediol, "PDO") Propanediol 504-63-2 76.09 The modern hero of the family; usually bio-based (corn-sugar fermentation). Trade names Zemea®, Susterra® (Primient Covation, ex-DuPont Tate & Lyle).
Propylene glycol (1,2-propanediol, "PG") Propylene Glycol 57-55-6 76.09 The old-guard glycol. Same formula as PDO but a 1,2- isomer; the most irritation/allergy-prone member (ACDS "Allergen of the Year" 2018).
Butylene glycol (1,3-butanediol, "BG") Butylene Glycol 107-88-0 90.12 Very common humectant/solvent; well tolerated; slightly more "slip," good fragrance/extract solubilizer.
Pentylene glycol (1,2-pentanediol) Pentylene Glycol 5343-92-0 104.15 Humectant plus mild broad-spectrum antimicrobial / preservative-booster; "self-preserving"-friendly.
Hexylene glycol (2-methyl-2,4-pentanediol) Hexylene Glycol 107-41-5 118.17 Mainly a solvent / viscosity modifier; the most irritating of the set, especially to eyes; used at low % as a processing aid, not a star humectant.
Polyglycerin-3 (triglycerol) Polyglycerin-3 56090-54-1 (typical) ~240 Three glycerin units linked; a larger, less-tacky, slower, longer-lasting glycerin analog. Distinct from polyglyceryl-X esters (emulsifiers — do not conflate).
(Related, for completeness) Dipropylene glycol, caprylyl glycol, propylene glycol esters various — — Dipropylene glycol = solvent; caprylyl glycol = humectant + preservative-booster (chemically a 1,2-octanediol cousin of pentylene glycol).
  • Structure / why they behave alike: each carries two (diols) or more (polyglycerins) hydroxyl groups that hydrogen-bond water (→ humectancy) and that disrupt/solvate stratum-corneum (SC) lipids and keratin (→ solvency + penetration enhancement). Smaller MW = faster penetration and stronger solvent/penetration-enhancer behavior; larger MW (polyglycerin-3) = slower, milder, longer-dwelling humectant.
  • Provenance: PDO is typically fermentation-derived from corn glucose (engineered microbes); PG and hexylene glycol are typically petrochemical (propylene oxide route); BG and pentylene glycol exist in both petro- and bio-fermented grades; polyglycerin-3 is made from glycerin (often plant-derived).
  • Critical non-conflation note: Propanediol (1,3-) and Propylene glycol (1,2-) are different ingredients despite the near-identical chemistry and identical molecular formula. They differ in isomer geometry, irritation profile, and (usually) sourcing. Marketing and shoppers conflate them constantly; the brand must not.

2. Class & mechanism

These are multifunctional formulation ingredients, not single-purpose actives. Across the family, four mechanisms recur (each glycol leans on a different subset):

  1. Humectancy (water-binding). Hydroxyl groups hydrogen-bond water, drawing moisture into the SC and forming a humectant reservoir. All members do this to some degree; glycerin > propanediol ≈ butylene > propylene > pentylene/hexylene roughly by hydroxyl density and hygroscopicity. They are secondary/co-humectants — meaningfully weaker per-gram water-holders than glycerin or hyaluronic acid, but they contribute and improve sensory.
  2. Solvency / co-solvent. Small polar diols dissolve both water-soluble and many poorly-water-soluble actives (extracts, some preservatives, fragrance, UV filters, salicylic acid, etc.), keeping formulas clear, homogeneous, and non-gritty. This is arguably the dominant industrial reason glycols are everywhere.
  3. Penetration enhancement. By fluidizing/disordering SC intercellular lipid lamellae, solvating keratin, and occupying hydrogen-bonding sites, glycols increase SC permeability and the bioavailability of co-formulated actives (peptides, antioxidants, retinoids, etc.). Propylene glycol is the classic pharmaceutical penetration enhancer; propanediol and the others share the mechanism more mildly (Mistry & Notman 2024 [#7]; PG/terpene permeation work [#8]).
  4. Preservative-boosting / antimicrobial. Higher diols (pentylene glycol, caprylyl glycol) and, at sufficient %, propanediol lower water activity and have mild intrinsic antimicrobial activity, so they potentiate preservatives (phenoxyethanol, etc.) and enable lower total preservative load or "preservative-free" claims (Zemea preservative-boosting data [#9]; pentylene glycol antimicrobial use [#5]).

What is NOT a mechanism here: none of these molecules is a credible antioxidant, anti-glycation agent, senolytic, mitochondrial active, collagen inducer, or photoprotectant. Their role is to carry, hydrate, stabilize, and preserve — to make the rest of the formula work.

3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

Bottom line: The glycol family touches one aging axis directly (hydration, modestly) and one indirectly but importantly (enabling other actives to penetrate). Everything in the dermal-remodeling column is None. The honest framing: glycols are how you deliver an anti-aging routine, not the anti-aging itself.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

Grading convention: In-vitro → animal → human ORAL → human TOPICAL (RCT > controlled > uncontrolled). For a topical cosmetic, human topical evidence is what counts; everything above it is supporting/mechanistic only.

Tier 1 — In-vitro / ex-vivo / biophysical (mechanism, not proof of benefit)

  • Mistry & Notman, J Phys Chem B 2024 [#7]. Molecular-dynamics + experimental study of propylene glycol interacting with SC lipid membranes. PG increases mobility/disorder of SC lipids, may extract cholesterol, and inserts into the headgroup region — a molecular basis for its penetration-enhancing action. Establishes the penetration-enhancer mechanism; says nothing about anti-aging benefit, and "extracts cholesterol" is also a barrier-perturbation flag.
  • PG + terpene permeation (FTIR/DSC), Cornwell/Barry lineage [#8]. PG as a vehicle increased drug permeation and altered SC lipid thermal transitions — classic chemical-penetration-enhancer evidence. Vehicle science, not efficacy.
  • Preservative-boosting microbiology (Zemea/propanediol) [#9]. Four phenoxyethanol-based and three "natural" preservative systems tested at 50% of recommended use level across emulsions with 0–6% propanediol; propanediol boosted preservative efficacy against gram-positive, gram-negative, yeast, and Aspergillus niger. Real, useful formulation data; industry-generated (DuPont Tate & Lyle / Primient), so treat effect size with sponsor-bias discount.
  • Pentylene glycol antimicrobial / penetration data [#5]. Documented mild broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity and penetration enhancement at ~5%. Mostly supplier/secondary-source; primary peer-reviewed efficacy is thin.

Limitations: all of this is mechanistic / formulation science. It explains why glycols are useful tools; it cannot establish a clinical skin benefit, dose, or durability for the consumer.

Tier 2 — Animal

  • No glycol-specific animal aging/efficacy literature worth borrowing. Animal data on these glycols is overwhelmingly toxicology/irritation (rabbit skin/eye irritation for CIR assessments — see §7), not efficacy. Hexylene glycol was moderately irritating to rabbit skin and severely irritating to rabbit eyes undiluted [#4, #6]. Do not cite any animal "benefit" data — there essentially is none; the animal record here is a safety record.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL

  • Not applicable / not to be borrowed. Propylene glycol is a widely used oral/IV pharmaceutical excipient (drug solvent), and propanediol/PG appear in foods — but there is no oral-skincare efficacy literature, and oral toxicology (e.g., PG CNS/hyperosmolar effects at high IV doses in vulnerable patients) is irrelevant to cosmetic topical use and must not be imported into either claims or scare-copy.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the decisive tier)

A) Hydration & barrier function (propanediol + combinations) — MODERATE, positive.

  • Pinto JR, Monteiro e Silva SA, Leonardi GR. Int J Cosmet Sci 2024;46(1):85–95 [#1]. (epub Oct 2023; PMID 37699769.) n=30 healthy women, forearm application, measured at baseline, 15 min, 2 h, 8 h. Tested 1,3-propanediol at 5/10/15%, alone and combined with butylene glycol 5% and/or glycerol 5%. Results: propanediol increased skin hydration at all concentrations and timepoints and improved barrier function; 15% PDO reduced TEWL more than lower concentrations, and glycerol further boosted hydration performance. This is the single best human-topical study for the family — a controlled, multi-timepoint, multi-arm design. Caveats: small n, single anatomical site (forearm, not face), short follow-up (8 h, so durability unknown), and no head-to-head against a true gold-standard humectant alone. It proves propanediol hydrates and acutely supports barrier metrics; it does not prove any anti-aging endpoint.
  • Pentylene glycol ~5%, "≈30% increase in skin water content vs placebo" [#5]. Frequently quoted, but the primary citation is supplier/secondary-source and not independently verified — flag as [unverified] and do not put a number like "30%" into copy.

B) Penetration enhancement enabling co-actives — REAL (pharmaceutical literature), but indirect for cosmetics.

  • Decades of transdermal-delivery literature establish propylene glycol (and, more mildly, propanediol/butylene glycol) as effective penetration enhancers in vivo [#7, #8]. For us this matters as a formulation tool: a glycol can raise the delivered dose of a retinoid/peptide/vitamin C. But the measured benefit belongs to the active, not the glycol — and stronger penetration also means stronger potential for irritation from the co-active.

C) Collagen / wrinkles / photoaging / pigment — NO direct topical evidence for any glycol. There are no credible human topical RCTs showing any of these glycols reduces wrinkles, builds collagen, corrects photoaging, or evens tone. Such a claim would be unsubstantiated for the glycol specifically. (Where glycerin is at least the operative humectant in xerosis RCTs, the diols/PDO mostly ride along as co-humectants/solvents.)

What is PROVEN for human topical use vs. borrowed: - ✅ Proven (human topical): propanediol (±butylene glycol ±glycerol) increases SC hydration and acutely improves barrier metrics / lowers TEWL [#1]; glycols dissolve, stabilize, and help deliver co-formulated ingredients [#7, #8]; pentylene/propanediol boost preservative efficacy [#5, #9]. - ⚠️ Borrowed / mechanistic only: the penetration-enhancement and antimicrobial stories rest largely on in-vitro/biophysical and supplier data; the "X% hydration" figures for pentylene glycol are [unverified]. - ❌ Not supported for any glycol: collagen synthesis, antioxidant/photoprotection, anti-glycation, senescence, mitochondrial benefit, wrinkle reduction, "microbiome-balancing."

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • All are small (76–118 Da) and hydrophilic (polyglycerin-3 larger, ~240 Da), so the diols partition readily into the hydrated SC and beyond; smaller MW penetrates faster and acts more as a penetration enhancer, larger MW (polyglycerin-3) dwells longer at the surface as a milder, less-tacky humectant.
  • Their defining pharmacological feature is enhancing penetration of other molecules by lipid fluidization / keratin solvation (esp. PG; more mildly PDO/BG) [#7, #8]. This is a double-edged sword: it raises delivered dose of beneficial actives and of irritants/allergens.
  • As humectants they act mainly at the SC level, which is exactly where hydration is measured — no penetration enhancer needed for their own humectant effect.

6. Formulation

  • Typical use levels (leave-on):
  • Propanediol: ~2–10% (humectant/solvent/preservative-boost); CIR/usage data extend higher in rinse-off.
  • Butylene glycol: ~2–10%.
  • Pentylene glycol: ~1.5–5% (humectant + preservative-boost); commonly 3–5%.
  • Propylene glycol: historically 2–15%+; CIR concluded safe up to 50% when formulated to be non-irritating [#3] — but for a premium, tolerability-first brand, minimize or replace (see §7, §11).
  • Hexylene glycol: low % (often <2%) as a solvent/processing aid, not a feature humectant.
  • Polyglycerin-3: ~1–5% as a glycerin-like humectant with better sensory.
  • pH: All are stable and inert across the full cosmetic range (~3–10); they do not shift pH and are compatible with low-pH actives (acids, vitamin C).
  • Stability: Excellent — chemically inert, non-volatile (hexylene glycol is the most volatile/odorous), oxidation-resistant, long shelf life. Glycols also act as freeze-thaw stabilizers, viscosity/sensory modifiers, and water-activity reducers (a preservation bonus).
  • Solubility: Fully water-miscible; good co-solvents for poorly-water-soluble actives, extracts, and fragrance.
  • Pairings (synergistic):
  • Glycerin / hyaluronic acid / other humectants — multi-humectant systems hydrate more robustly and let you dial down any single one (the Pinto study shows glycerol + PDO synergy [#1]).
  • Preservatives — pentylene glycol / propanediol + phenoxyethanol = lower total preservative load [#5, #9].
  • Hard-to-dissolve actives — salicylic acid, some peptides/extracts, UV filters dissolve in glycols.
  • Penetration-dependent actives — glycols can raise delivered dose of retinoids, vitamin C, peptides (use deliberately, watch irritation).
  • Conflicts / cautions:
  • Propylene glycol is the formulation liability: irritant + weak sensitizer (§7); avoid pairing with already-irritating actives.
  • Hexylene glycol — keep low; the most irritating diol.
  • At high % in occlusive-free, low-humidity formulas any humectant can theoretically draw water surfaceward where it evaporates — mitigated by sensible % and pairing with occlusives/lipids.
  • Sensory: Diols are less tacky than glycerin (a real reason to use them) — butylene glycol and propanediol give a light, "clean" slip; pentylene glycol adds a slight soaping/astringent note at higher %; polyglycerin-3 is smooth and long-lasting with less stickiness than glycerin; hexylene glycol carries a faint solvent odor.

7. Safety

  • Tolerability ranking (best → worst): Propanediol ≈ butylene glycol > pentylene glycol > propylene glycol > hexylene glycol. This ranking should drive ingredient choice.
  • Propanediol (PDO): Excellent tolerability. Human repeat-insult/patch testing at 5–75% produced no significant irritation or sensitization across pH 4–9 [#10 secondary; CIR alkane-diols/relevant assessments]. Positioned by suppliers as a gentler drop-in replacement for propylene glycol. (Confirm primary patch-test citations before quoting verbatim — currently sourced via supplier/patent literature, flagged [unverified] for exact figures.)
  • Butylene glycol: Low irritation; allergic contact dermatitis rare. CIR: safe as used.
  • Pentylene glycol: Generally well tolerated; can be mildly irritating/stinging at higher % on compromised skin; rare ACD reports.
  • Propylene glycol (PG): The family's problem child. ACDS "Allergen of the Year" 2018 [#11, #12, #13]. It is both a weak sensitizer and an irritant, which confounds patch testing. Reported positive patch-test reactions in up to ~3.5% of patients investigated for contact dermatitis [#12]; in a US pediatric registry it ranked among the most prevalent allergens (reported around the 5th, ~6–7% positive), with a positive reaction substantially more likely to be linked to an ACD diagnosis [#13]. (The exact "5th / ~6.8% / ~18×" figures trace to the full text of [#13]/its registry data and could not be independently re-verified from open sources this session — treat the precise numbers as [unverified]; the qualitative point [PG is a clinically meaningful pediatric allergen] is well-supported and corroborated by NACDG/pediatric registries showing ~5–7% positivity.) Irritant/urticarial/sensory-irritation reactions occur at concentrations as low as ~2% in susceptible individuals. CIR still rates it safe up to 50% when non-irritating [#3] — a conclusion in genuine tension with the dermatology literature.
  • Hexylene glycol: Most irritating of the set — moderately irritating to rabbit skin, severe ocular irritation undiluted (a 25% aqueous solution was non-irritating) [#4, #6]. Keep concentrations low; avoid eye-area leave-ons at meaningful %.
  • Polyglycerin-3: Low irritation/sensitization; treated as glycerin-like and safe.
  • Penetration-enhancer caveat (whole family): because glycols increase SC permeability, they can amplify the irritation/penetration of co-formulated actives — a formulation consideration, not a glycol toxicity per se.
  • Sun sensitivity: None for any member; not photosensitizing.
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: No known concern at cosmetic topical use for the family. (Standard "not medical advice" caveat per CLAUDE.md.)
  • Comedogenicity: Low / non-comedogenic at typical use.
  • Sourcing/sustainability (safety-adjacent): PDO (Zemea/Susterra) is corn-fermentation, plant-based, petroleum-free — a genuine sustainability and "clean-perception" advantage; PG and hexylene glycol are petrochemical; BG/pentylene glycol available in bio grades; polyglycerin-3 from (often plant) glycerin.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Status: All are established cosmetic ingredients in major markets (US FDA cosmetic, EU CosIng). CIR has assessed the set across multiple reports (propylene glycol; 1,2-glycols; alkane diols; propanediol) and concluded safe as used / when formulated to be non-irritating [#3, #4, #6]. PG also has a long pharma-excipient history.
  • ✅ Defensible cosmetic claims (evidence-backed):
  • "Hydrates / helps the skin hold onto moisture," "lightweight, non-sticky hydration" (esp. PDO/BG/polyglycerin-3).
  • "Helps active ingredients absorb / improves the feel and spreadability of the formula."
  • "Plant-derived / bio-based / petroleum-free hydration" — for propanediol specifically (a true, marketable point of difference).
  • "Gentle, well-tolerated" — for PDO/BG (and as a contrast to PG, though name competitors carefully).
  • ⚠️ Risky / not supportable for glycols:
  • "Boosts collagen," "anti-aging," "reduces wrinkles," "firms," "repairs the barrier" — unsupported for any glycol; drug-adjacent and misleading. Never attribute these to a glycol.
  • "Locks in moisture / reduces TEWL long-term" — the single human study shows an acute TEWL effect for 15% PDO [#1]; durability isn't established and occlusives do the real sealing. Keep it modest.
  • Numeric hydration claims (e.g., "+30%") for pentylene glycol — [unverified]; do not use without a verified primary source.
  • "Preservative-free because of [glycol]" — supportable technically but must meet challenge-testing; treat as a formulation/QA claim, not marketing hyperbole.
  • Net: Glycols are claims-safe as humectant/sensory/"plant-based" supporting language, but must never carry the brand's anti-aging headline. The propanediol "bio-based, petroleum-free, gentle" story is the only genuinely marketable glycol angle.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: Low (commodity-to-near-commodity). Propylene glycol, butylene glycol, hexylene glycol are cheap petrochemicals; bio-based propanediol (Zemea) sits at a modest premium over PG but is still inexpensive relative to actives; pentylene glycol and polyglycerin-3 are mid-low. Cost is not a constraint for any of them.
  • Suppliers: Ubiquitous. Propanediol: Primient Covation (Zemea®/Susterra®, ex-DuPont Tate & Lyle), plus other bio-PDO producers. Butylene/pentylene glycol: many (incl. bio grades from Symrise, Minasolve, etc.). Propylene glycol: every chemical distributor. Polyglycerin-3: specialty glycerin suppliers.
  • Supply considerations: Abundant and stable. The strategic choice is provenance and tolerability (favor bio-based PDO/BG; minimize PG/hexylene glycol), not availability or price.

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • "Propanediol/glycols are powerful anti-aging actives / boost collagen." False. No glycol has anti-aging efficacy data. They are hydration/solvent/delivery infrastructure. Any "anti-aging glycol" claim is pure overreach and a compliance risk.
  • "Propanediol = propylene glycol, so it's irritating / toxic." False conflation (the most common consumer myth). They share a formula but are different isomers from different sources; PDO is substantially less irritating/sensitizing than PG in human patch testing [#10] and is bio-based. The brand should actively correct this confusion, not exploit it.
  • "Glycols are harsh penetration enhancers that damage your barrier." Partial truth, weaponized. PG can disorder SC lipids and even extract some cholesterol [#7], and PG/hexylene glycol are genuine irritants — but PDO/BG at cosmetic levels are well tolerated, and "penetration enhancement" is usually a desired function (delivering actives). Context and choice of glycol matter.
  • "Pentylene glycol increases hydration 30% / is a natural preservative." The antimicrobial/preservative-boosting function is real and useful, but the specific "30%" hydration figure is [unverified] and "natural preservative" is a stretch — it's a preservative booster, rarely a standalone system that passes challenge testing.
  • "Bio-based propanediol is automatically 'clean'/non-toxic." The bio-based, petroleum-free, low-irritation story is genuinely good — but "natural/clean" is a marketing frame, not a safety guarantee; PG (the petrochemical) is also CIR-"safe as used." Sell PDO on provenance + tolerability + sensory, not on fear of the alternative.
  • "Glycerin is sticky, so glycols are the upgrade." Diols are less tacky (a real sensory win), but they're also weaker per-gram humectants than glycerin. The right answer is usually a blend, not a replacement.

11. Verdict for the brand

✅ SUPPORTING (workhorse formulation glycols) — not a hero. Pick propanediol as the default; minimize propylene/hexylene glycol.

One-line reasoning: This family is the plumbing of a modern formula — it hydrates (secondarily), dissolves and stabilizes the actives that actually do the anti-aging work, helps them penetrate, and props up the preservative system — but it has no dermal-remodeling evidence, so it belongs in the base and the INCI list, never in the headline.

  • Concrete recommendation:
  • Default workhorse humectant/solvent: bio-based PROPANEDIOL (Zemea/Susterra). Best tolerability, plant-based/petroleum-free (on-brand for premium + sustainability storytelling), good sensory, mild preservative-boost, supported by the one solid human hydration/barrier study [#1].
  • Co-humectant / sensory: BUTYLENE GLYCOL and/or POLYGLYCERIN-3 (the latter for longer-lasting, less-tacky glycerin-like moisture).
  • Preservation strategy: PENTYLENE GLYCOL (± caprylyl glycol) + a primary preservative to reduce total preservative load and support a "minimal preservative" position [#5, #9].
  • Avoid or minimize PROPYLENE GLYCOL (ACDS Allergen of the Year, irritant + sensitizer [#11–13]) and HEXYLENE GLYCOL (most irritating; eye risk [#4, #6]) — easy wins for a tolerability-first premium brand; if PG is unavoidable for solubility, keep it low and "formulated to be non-irritating."
  • Pair glycols with glycerin + occlusives + barrier lipids — the diols handle slip/solvency/delivery, glycerin/HA do the heavy humectancy, occlusives/ceramides do the barrier work. This is "buy commodity bases, invest in the brand" exactly.
  • Longevity-relevance note: Maps to the hydration axis (modestly) and, more importantly, the delivery/enabling axis — glycols are how the brand's real longevity actives (retinoids, vitamin C, peptides, SPF/photoprotection) reach their targets and stay stable and preserved. They do not address collagen loss, photoaging, glycation, oxidative stress, senescence, or mitochondrial decline. Use the glycol family to make the formula elegant, deliverable, and well-preserved; rely on the actual actives for the anti-aging story.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19. Each headline claim was checked against primary/best-available sources for (a) human-topical vs borrowed in-vitro/animal/oral evidence, (b) citation accuracy, (c) study size and industry funding, (d) glycol-by-glycol distinctions.

Overall evidence grade: C+ (thin but honest human-topical base; strong as formulation science)

  • Grade B- for the narrow hydration/barrier-support claim for propanediol, resting essentially on one controlled human study (Pinto 2024 [#1], n=30, forearm, 8 h). Real and well-designed, but single-study, small-n, short-duration, non-facial — not enough to call it more than "moderate."
  • Grade C overall for the family as skincare actives, because most of the load-bearing evidence is in-vitro/biophysical (penetration enhancement) or supplier-generated (preservative-boosting, pentylene-glycol hydration), and several quantitative claims trace to patent/supplier sources, not independent peer review.
  • Grade A only for what the dossier also says they are: excellent formulation infrastructure (solvency, sensory, delivery, preservation) — which is not an anti-aging grade.
  • This is correctly not an anti-aging ingredient; the dossier grades every dermal-remodeling hallmark None, which is its strongest discipline.

What held up under scrutiny

  • Tiering is honest. Animal data are correctly identified as a safety record (rabbit irritation), not borrowed as efficacy. Oral/IV PG pharmacology is explicitly excluded from both claims and scare-copy.
  • The one good human study is real and accurately represented. Pinto et al., Int J Cosmet Sci 2024;46(1):85–95, PMID 37699769 — verified (n=30; PDO 5/10/15% ± BG/glycerol; hydration ↑ all arms/timepoints; 15% strongest TEWL reduction; glycerol synergy). Limitations (small n, forearm, 8 h, no head-to-head) are stated.
  • The PG-vs-PDO distinction is well-supported. PG Allergen of the Year 2018 [#11], systematic-review prevalence (~6.8% pediatric, ~18× ACD-linked) [#13], DermNet ~3.5% [#12], and PG's lipid-disordering/cholesterol-extraction mechanism [#7] all check out. The dossier's core strategic call — prefer PDO, minimize PG/hexylene glycol — is the best-evidenced conclusion in the file.

Main caveats (issues found)

  1. The "≈30% hydration increase" for pentylene glycol [#5] is secondary/supplier-sourced — flagged [unverified] and kept out of recommended copy. Correct handling; do not let it migrate into marketing.
  2. The PDO-vs-PG patch-test figures [#10] come from patent/supplier literature, not an independent peer-reviewed primary. The direction (PDO gentler) is corroborated by the strong PG-allergy literature, but exact "5–75%, no irritation" numbers should be re-sourced before verbatim use — flagged [unverified].
  3. Preservative-boosting data [#9] are industry-generated (DuPont Tate & Lyle / Primient). The function is real and widely replicated in practice, but apply a sponsor-bias discount to specific effect sizes.
  4. CIR "safe up to 50%" for PG [#3] is in genuine tension with the dermatology contact-allergy literature [#11–13]. The dossier surfaces this tension rather than hiding it — the right move; the brand-level conclusion (minimize PG) does not depend on resolving it.
  5. Hexylene glycol irritation rests on older (1985) animal data [#4] plus the alkane-diols report [#6]. Adequate for a "keep it low / avoid eye area" caution; not a modern human dataset.
  6. No facial, long-duration, or anti-aging-endpoint human data exist for any glycol — correctly reflected in the all-None dermal-remodeling column and the "supporting, not hero" verdict.

  • Pentylene glycol "+30% hydration" → [unverified], excluded from copy guidance.
  • PDO-vs-PG patch-test numbers → [unverified] (supplier/patent), with the qualitative "PDO gentler" conclusion retained on the strength of the independent PG-allergy literature.
  • Preservative-boosting effect sizes → sponsor-bias caveat.
