Ingredient: Rambutan extract (INCI: Nephelium Lappaceum Peel/Leaf/Seed Extract)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ⚠️ considering (CONDITIONAL — a "retinoid-alternative" botanical resting almost entirely on in-vitro + supplier data) Class: Retinoids & retinoid alternatives (positioned as a functional retinol alternative — NOT chemically a retinoid)

Scope note: This is an exhaustive, citation-backed literature review for the brand's research base. Evidence is separated strictly by tier (in-vitro → animal → human oral → human topical). Where a benefit is borrowed from a non-topical format, from a different plant part, or from mechanistic data, it is flagged. Claims discipline per ../../CLAUDE.md and ../../brand/glossary.md.

Critical framing up front: "Rambutan extract" is not one ingredient. The skincare market uses at least three chemically and biologically distinct extracts from three different parts of the tree — and the "retinol alternative" story belongs to only one of them (the leaf, via BASF's branded Nephoria™). Most published science, by contrast, is on the peel/rind (an antioxidant/ellagitannin story), and a separate seed literature is about senescence and hair/scalp. Conflating them is the single biggest source of hype here. See §1 and §10.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Rambutan (the lychee-relative fruit; Nephelium lappaceum L., family Sapindaceae). Native to Southeast Asia (Malaysia/Indonesia/Thailand/Vietnam).
  • Botanical: Nephelium lappaceum L. CAS 93165-68-5; EINECS/EC 296-955-8 (shared across plant-part extracts). [11]
  • INCI names (these are different ingredients — do not treat as interchangeable):
  • Nephelium Lappaceum Peel Extract (the fruit rind; the most-studied part — antioxidant/ellagitannin story). [11]
  • Nephelium Lappaceum Branch/Fruit/Leaf Extract and …Leaf Extract (the "retinol-like" leaf story; BASF Nephoria™). [11]
  • Nephelium Lappaceum Seed Extract (senescence / hair-scalp story; BASF Rambuvital™). [4][7]
  • Branded commercial actives (BASF "Rambutan Program," launched 2019, sourced from organically certified gardens in Vietnam, COSMOS-approved, 100% natural origin, preservative-free): [4][12]
  • Nephoria™ — leaf extract; marketed as a natural retinol alternative ("works through biological pathways similar to retinol"). This is the source of essentially the entire "rambutan = retinoid alternative" claim. [4][5]
  • Nephydrat™ — peel extract; marketed for hydration / barrier (ceramide & phospholipid synthesis, water retention). [4]
  • Rambuvital™ — seed extract; marketed for scalp/hair (anti-pollution, sebum-oxidation, scalp hydration). [4]
  • CosIng function: Skin-conditioning agent. COSMOS-compatible. [11]
  • Key actives in the plant: Peel/rind is rich in ellagitannins — geraniin (the dominant compound), corilagin, ellagic acid, plus flavonoids; also quercetin-type flavonoids, β-carotene and vitamin C are cited for the fruit. [1][2][6][8] Seeds carry kaempferol-type flavonoid glycosides. [7] Leaf composition (Nephoria) is described by BASF as a polyphenol fraction but the precise actives are proprietary. [4][5]

2. Class & mechanism

There is no single mechanism — it depends entirely on the plant part:

Peel/rind (the bulk of the literature): acts as a polyphenol antioxidant. Geraniin and related ellagitannins are potent free-radical scavengers; in cell models they raise SOD activity, suppress nitric-oxide formation, protect fibroblasts/keratinocytes from H₂O₂ oxidative stress, and inhibit the matrix-degrading enzymes collagenase, elastase and MMP-2 to a modest degree, plus tyrosinase (pigment) inhibition. [2][6][8] This is a classic "antioxidant + enzyme-inhibition" botanical mechanism — the same family as pomegranate, green tea, lychee/longan peel — not a retinoid mechanism.

Leaf (Nephoria, the "retinol-like" claim): BASF reports the leaf extract up-regulates collagen and elastic-fibre genes in vitro — Col1 ×1.6, Emilin-1 ×3, Fibulin-5 ×1.8, and lowers CCN1/CYR61 ×0.4 (CCN1 being a negative regulator of collagen homeostasis). The "similar to retinol" framing is at the level of affecting overlapping ECM/collagen pathways, not vitamin-A receptor binding. [4][5] Importantly, multiple independent experts note rambutan does not bind retinoic-acid receptors the way retinol does — it "connects to different receptors," so any retinol comparison is functional/marketing, not molecular. [9][13]

Seed: behaves as a senomorphic — selectively suppresses the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) in aged dermal fibroblasts without killing the cells (kaempferol-type compounds). [7]

Bottom line on mechanism: The honest, generalisable mechanism for rambutan is antioxidant + mild anti-matrix-enzyme + mild tyrosinase inhibition (peel). The "retinol-like collagen stimulation" is a leaf-specific, supplier-defined mechanism with no independent receptor-level validation. The "senescence" mechanism is a seed-specific early in-vitro finding.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core of this dossier)

Tier 1 — In-vitro (cell, biochemical & reconstructed-skin models) — the overwhelming majority of all rambutan skin evidence

  • Lourith, Kanlayavattanakul, Chaikul, Chansriniyom & Bunwatcharaphansakun 2017 — An Acad Bras Cienc 89(suppl). In-vitro/cellular screen of fruit-residue peels (litchi, rambutan, tamarind seed coat) for anti-aging cosmetics. Rambutan peel: total phenolics ~45.87 g GAE/100 g; superoxide-scavenging IC₅₀ ~39.5 µg/mL; anti-collagenase ~53.99%, anti-elastase ~31.08%, anti-tyrosinase IC₅₀ ~430.8 µg/mL, MMP-2 inhibition ~23.1% (at 0.01 mg/mL); safe to human fibroblasts up to 0.01 mg/mL. Key limitation & honest finding: the authors concluded litchi peel was the most promising of the three — rambutan was intermediate / second-best, and its safe concentration was strikingly low (0.01 mg/mL). [3]
  • Phenolic extract + geraniin on human epidermal keratinocytes (Palanisamy/Tseng group lineage), Ind Crops Prod / related 2019 — N. lappaceum rind phenolic & polysaccharide extracts and isolated geraniin showed radical-scavenging, enhanced keratinocyte proliferation, ↑intracellular SOD, ↓nitric-oxide formation; geraniin identified as the principal antioxidant driver. Limitation: monolayer keratinocyte assays; no skin penetration or in-vivo arm. [2][6]
  • Lee, Cho, Park, Zhang, Doan, Lee, Cho & Oh 2020 (seed) — Nutrients 12(5):1430 (PMID 32429147). UPLC-qTOF-MS/MS metabolite profiling of rambutan seeds; isolated 9 compounds (1 new + 8 known kaempferol-type). Seed extract showed strong senomorphic activity (selective SASP suppression) in aged human dermal fibroblasts via luciferase-promoter assays. Limitation: in-vitro only, mechanistic; no skin-delivery or in-vivo/clinical data; senescence-skin link is hypothesis-generating. [7]
  • Antioxidant/composition characterisation — multiple papers confirm peel is exceptionally polyphenol-rich (methanolic peel extract ~542 mg phenolics/g; geraniin 397–1011 mg/fruit; ellagic acid 155–177 mg/g; corilagin ~7.5–7.9 mg/g) with strong DPPH/ORAC activity (DPPH IC₅₀ ~1.7–90.9 µg/mL across studies). [1][6][8]
  • BASF leaf (Nephoria) in-vitro: Col1 ×1.6, Emilin-1 ×3, Fibulin-5 ×1.8, CCN1 ×0.4 (collagen/elastic-fibre upregulation). Limitation: manufacturer-generated, proprietary, not independently published/peer-reviewed in a form that discloses methods/n. [4][5]

Verdict (Tier 1): Strong and consistent for antioxidant activity and modest for anti-collagenase/elastase/tyrosinase (peel). Emerging for senescence (seed). The retinol-like collagen story (leaf) is supplier data only. This tier is where ~all of rambutan's evidence lives.

Tier 2 — Animal

  • Xiao et al. 2019 — Food Chem Toxicol 130:103–112 (S0278-6915(19)30219-4). Rambutan peel phenolics (RPP) ± the peptide Leu-Ser-Gly-Tyr-Gly-Pro (LSGYGP, from fish-skin gelatin) on UV-irradiated hairless mice. RPP and/or the peptide reduced photoaging: improved regulation of oxidative-stress markers, inflammatory cytokines and MMP levels, with histology showing protection of skin tissue and endogenous collagen; the two produced an additive effect. Limitations: animal model, the headline efficacy was a combination with a non-rambutan peptide (hard to isolate rambutan's contribution), oral/topical route and exact RPP dose need care in interpretation; not a human study. [10][6]

Verdict (Tier 2): One supportive mouse photoaging study, but confounded by a co-active peptide and not human. Useful mechanistic/proof-of-concept support; not a basis for human claims.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL

  • Rambutan is a widely eaten fruit with a generally healthy nutritional/antioxidant profile, and there is oral pharmacology (e.g., anti-diabetic effects of peel phenolics in high-fat-diet/STZ mice — Mol / PMC5579595, an animal metabolic study, not human and not skin). [search corpus]
  • No human oral trial supporting a skin-aging benefit of rambutan was found.

Verdict (Tier 3): No transferable oral skin evidence. Do not borrow any oral/dietary claim for topical skincare.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the only tier that could support our product claims — and it is thin and supplier-dominated)

4a. Randomized / controlled human topical: - None independent. No independent, peer-reviewed RCT of a defined rambutan extract on human skin aging was located.

4b. Supplier (BASF Nephoria, leaf) in-vivo human data — the strongest "topical human" figures, but manufacturer-run: - BASF reports an in-vivo study in >55 volunteers: a Nephoria-containing cream gave immediate elasticity +19%, elasticity recovery +12% after 28 days, with anti-wrinkle effects after 4 weeks. Limitations: manufacturer-generated technical-info data, not an independent peer-reviewed RCT; full methods, placebo/vehicle control, statistics and the isolated contribution of rambutan vs the finished formula are not transparently published. Treat as promotional clinical support, not independent proof. [4][5]

4c. Lower-tier human topical / formulation studies: - Sekar (Mahendran) — "Rambutan fruits extract in aging skin" book chapter (in Aging: Oxidative Stress and Dietary Antioxidants, Elsevier 2020) and related formulation work (e.g., IJPSR "Formulation and Evaluation of Novel Antiaging Cream Containing Rambutan Fruits Extract"; Sekar et al. ~3% peel-extract cream judged safe in irritancy testing). These describe formulating and safety-testing rambutan creams and cite antioxidant/tyrosinase activity, but the rigorous efficacy data in them is in-vitro; human components are largely safety/usability rather than controlled efficacy. [search corpus][cited in review 6] - ⚠️ A widely circulated figure — "43% of subjects showed improvement in facial wrinkles at 6 months (P = 0.013)" — appears attributed to a rambutan antiaging cream in secondary sources. I could not confirm the original primary study, design, n, or whether the test product was rambutan-only. Treat as [unverified] and do not use in copy without the primary source. [unverified]

Verdict (Tier 4): Human topical evidence is weak and dominated by the ingredient supplier. The only quantitative human numbers (elasticity +19%/+12%, anti-wrinkle at 4 wk) are BASF's own Nephoria data; everything else is formulation/safety work or in-vitro. There is no independent RCT. This is markedly weaker than even bakuchiol's (already-thin) human base, which at least has one independent double-blind RCT.

What is proven for TOPICAL human use vs. borrowed

  • Reasonably supported (human topical, but supplier-run): measurable elasticity gain and visible wrinkle improvement from the leaf (Nephoria) active in a finished cream over ~4 weeks. [4][5]
  • Reasonably supported (human topical): good tolerability / low irritation of rambutan-extract creams (small safety studies + supplier data). [3][4]
  • Borrowed from a DIFFERENT plant part: the antioxidant/anti-collagenase/tyrosinase data is peel, while the retinol-like collagen claim is leaf — do not present peel antioxidant data as proof of the "retinol alternative" claim. [2][3][4]
  • Borrowed from in-vitro (NOT proven human-topical endpoints): "boosts collagen," "scavenges free radicals on skin," anti-elastase/MMP effects, tyrosinase/brightening. [2][3][6]
  • Borrowed from animal: photoaging protection (mouse, and in combination with a non-rambutan peptide). [10]
  • Borrowed from in-vitro mechanism only / overreaching: "fights cellular senescence" (seed, in-vitro), "works like retinol" (functional/supplier framing, different receptors), any oral/dietary benefit. [7][9][13]

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • No dedicated human skin-penetration data for rambutan extract was found. This is a real gap.
  • Mechanistically: geraniin (the dominant peel active) is a very large, highly hydroxylated ellagitannin (C₄₁H₂₈O₂₇, MW ~952) — large, polar polyphenols of this size penetrate the stratum corneum poorly, so much of the in-vitro antioxidant/enzyme activity may not translate to viable-epidermis/dermis delivery without formulation help. [6]
  • Geraniin is also metabolised to smaller phenolics (ellagic acid, urolithins) in the gut for oral use — but that conversion is not relevant to topical delivery. [8]
  • The leaf (Nephoria) active is a proprietary fraction; BASF does not publish penetration data. Practical implication: efficacy will be formulation-dependent, and unencapsulated crude extracts should not be assumed to reach the dermis.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: No independently validated number. Reference points: in-vitro fibroblast safe ceiling ~0.01 mg/mL for peel extract (low) [3]; formulation studies used ~3% peel extract in creams (safety-tested) [search corpus]; branded leaf actives (Nephoria) are used at supplier-recommended low percentages (TI sheet specifics not fully disclosed here — confirm with COA). [4]
  • pH: Polyphenol/ellagitannin extracts are generally most stable mildly acidic (~pH 4–6); geraniin and ellagic acid can darken/oxidise at high pH. Confirm per supplier.
  • Stability: A real concern. Polyphenol/ellagitannin extracts are prone to oxidation and discolouration (browning) with light, heat, air and metal ions; crude peel extracts can darken a formula. Branded, standardised, often water-based COSMOS actives (BASF) are formulated to mitigate this; crude extracts are riskier. Chelators (e.g., EDTA-alternatives) and antioxidants help.
  • Pairings: Compatible with other antioxidants (vitamin C, E, ferulic), niacinamide, hyaluronic acid, peptides; the mouse study showed an additive effect with an MMP-inhibiting peptide [10]. As a "gentle" active it can pair with stronger renewal actives.
  • Conflicts: High-pH systems; possible interaction/precipitation with high cationic load; potential staining/colour drift in white emulsions.
  • Sensory: Crude peel extracts can impart colour (amber/brown) and astringency; standardised branded fractions are more cosmetically elegant.

7. Safety

  • Irritation: Generally low — the marketing centrepiece ("retinol benefits without the irritation"). Small safety/irritancy studies and supplier data report good tolerance; no inherent retinoid-style retinization. [3][4][9]
  • Sun sensitivity: No evidence of photosensitisation; unlike retinol/retinoids, rambutan is not expected to increase sun sensitivity (a marketed advantage). Daily SPF remains standard advice. [9][13]
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: Frequently marketed as a "pregnancy-safe retinol alternative." There is no formal safety clearance, pregnancy rating, or dedicated study. Do not guarantee "pregnancy-safe" — use "many choose botanical alternatives to retinol; consult your doctor." [9]
  • Sensitisation / allergy: Botanical polyphenol extracts carry the usual (low–moderate) plant-allergen potential; rambutan is in the Sapindaceae family (lychee/longan relatives) — relevant for anyone with known lychee/longan sensitivity, though cross-reactivity data are sparse. Note: rambutan seeds contain saponins/are considered mildly toxic if eaten raw — irrelevant to a purified cosmetic extract but worth knowing for sourcing/QC. [search corpus]
  • Sourcing/sustainability: A genuine upcycling / circular-beauty story — peel and seed are agro-industrial by-products (the fruit is eaten); BASF's program upcycles leaf/peel/seed from organically certified gardens in Vietnam with documented social-impact benefits (fair income, gender equity, health insurance), COSMOS-approved. [4][12] This is a real ESG asset if sourced through a credible chain.

8. Regulatory & claims

  • Cosmetic vs drug line: As a cosmetic botanical, rambutan extract may make appearance/cosmetic claims ("helps improve the look of fine lines," "supports a more elastic, even-toned appearance," "antioxidant-rich botanical"). Claiming it "boosts collagen," "works like retinol," "repairs," or treats/prevents anything drifts toward drug territory (US FDA framing per ../../CLAUDE.md) and needs substantiation/legal review. The "retinol alternative" phrasing is especially risky because the comparator (retinol) is itself a borderline structure/function term.
  • INCI / listing: Nephelium Lappaceum Peel/Leaf/Seed Extract are established INCI names; CAS 93165-68-5; EC 296-955-8; COSMOS-compatible; CosIng skin-conditioning function. [11] EU has no specific restriction on rambutan extract (verify live CosIng before label). [unverified — confirm current CosIng entry]
  • China / global: IECIC and other market listings not confirmed here — verify before any non-EU/US launch. [unverified]
  • Safe cosmetic language: "an antioxidant-rich botanical from the rambutan fruit," "a gentle, plant-derived alternative to retinol," "helps visibly improve elasticity and the look of fine lines," "upcycled, sustainably sourced." Always pair "retinol alternative" with "gentle/plant-derived" and never imply chemical equivalence.
  • Risky / overreaching: "works like retinol / plant retinol" used as if it is a retinoid (it binds different receptors — false); "boosts collagen by X%"; "as effective as retinol"; "pregnancy-safe" (don't guarantee); "reverses aging / fights cellular senescence" (seed in-vitro only); presenting peel antioxidant data as proof of the leaf retinol-like claim.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: moderate. Crude peel extract is cheap (agro-waste). Branded standardised actives (BASF Nephoria/Nephydrat/Rambuvital) carry a premium that fits premium positioning and brings the COSMOS/upcycling/social-impact story. [4][12]
  • Suppliers: BASF Care Creations is the dominant branded supplier (the entire "retinol alternative" narrative is theirs). Generic Nephelium lappaceum peel/seed extracts are available from many botanical-extract houses (especially in Thailand/Vietnam/Malaysia). Verify standardisation (e.g., geraniin or total-polyphenol %), part of plant, solvent, COA, colour/odour, microbial spec, and traceability — and specify the plant part to match the claim you intend to make.
  • Supply note: rambutan is seasonal and regionally concentrated (SE Asia); a credible upcycled supply chain (vs spot agro-waste) is the differentiator.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • "Rambutan is a natural retinol / plant retinol": Overreach. It is not a retinoid, not vitamin A, and binds different receptors than retinol (experts including Dr. Dennis Gross note this explicitly). The "retinol-like" claim is a supplier (BASF Nephoria, leaf) functional-pathway framing, not molecular equivalence. [4][9][13]
  • The plant-part bait-and-switch (the core problem): Marketing freely mixes (a) peel antioxidant/ellagitannin science, (b) leaf retinol-like collagen claims, and (c) seed senescence/hair data — as if "rambutan extract" delivers all three. No single extract does. A peel-extract product is not a retinol alternative; a leaf-extract product is not automatically a senescence active. [2][3][4][7]
  • "Clinically proven anti-wrinkle": The only human numbers (+19% immediate elasticity, +12% recovery at 28 days, anti-wrinkle at 4 wk) are BASF's own in-vivo data on a finished cream — manufacturer-run, not independent, methods not fully published. [4][5] The "43% wrinkle improvement, P=0.013" figure is [unverified] to a primary source. There is no independent RCT.
  • "Fights cellular senescence": Real but in-vitro, seed-only, single study — hypothesis-generating, not a proven on-skin benefit. [7]
  • "Boosts collagen / strong antioxidant on skin": In-vitro (and partly peel-derived) — mechanistic support, not validated human-topical endpoints, and drug-adjacent. [2][3][6]
  • "As gentle and effective as retinol": Gentleness is plausible; "effective as retinol" is unsupported — rambutan has no independent head-to-head, whereas even bakuchiol has one small RCT and retinoids have decades of large trials.
  • Honest comparison to bakuchiol: Both are marketed as "natural retinol alternatives." But bakuchiol has independent published human data (a double-blind RCT showing retinol-comparable results); rambutan does not. If the brand wants a botanical retinol alternative, bakuchiol is currently the better-evidenced choice. [see bakuchiol.md]

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ CONDITIONAL / supporting at most — not a hero, and not on the strength of the "retinol alternative" claim.

One-line reasoning: Rambutan is a legitimate antioxidant/upcycling botanical with a charming sustainability story, but its anti-aging evidence is overwhelmingly in-vitro + supplier-generated, split confusingly across three different plant parts, with no independent human RCT — so it can earn a place as a supporting antioxidant/"gentle renewal" botanical with an ESG narrative, but the brand must not lean on it as a proven retinol equivalent.

Use it only if: (1) you source a standardised, branded, traceable extract and match the plant part to the claim (leaf→elasticity/look-of-wrinkles; peel→antioxidant; seed→hair/scalp), (2) claims stay strictly cosmetic and "gentle/plant-derived," and (3) you treat the upcycling/social-impact story as the primary differentiator rather than overstated efficacy. If a botanical retinol alternative is wanted for the core face range, bakuchiol is better-evidenced; rambutan's stronger angle is antioxidant + sustainability storytelling, possibly in a supporting role or an adjacent product.

Longevity-relevance note: Touches oxidative stress (solid in-vitro), ECM/collagen and pigmentation (modest in-vitro), and — uniquely among retinol-alternatives — cellular senescence (seed, early in-vitro), which is conceptually aligned with a skin-longevity thesis. But none of these are human-proven topically, so the durable, defensible value today is antioxidant support + an authentic circular-beauty/upcycling narrative, not measurable longevity outcomes. Best logged as considering (conditional/supporting) pending stronger independent data. Log in ../../ideas/considering.md.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified: 2026-06-19. Independent re-check of headline claims against primary sources (PubMed/PMC/ScienceDirect/scielo abstracts, BASF/trade material, INCI/CosIng listings). Web access was abstract-level for several gated papers; figures are taken from abstracts, a peer-reviewed review (Heliyon 2022), and supplier material, and are flagged where unverifiable.

Overall evidence grade: C− (weak human-topical; strong in-vitro; supplier-dominated)

Justification: Rambutan has strong, repeatable in-vitro antioxidant data and modest in-vitro anti-matrix-enzyme/tyrosinase data (Tier 1, grade B for antioxidant mechanism). It has one supportive but confounded animal photoaging study (Tier 2). It has no independent human RCT; the only human efficacy numbers are manufacturer-run (BASF Nephoria) and the leaf-specific. That places overall human-topical efficacy at C− — below bakuchiol (which has an independent RCT). The "fights senescence" angle is D→C (single in-vitro seed study, conceptually interesting). Oral, mitochondrial, microbiome, AGE skin benefits: D / unproven.

Citation audit (do the studies exist and say what's claimed?)

  • Lourith et al. 2017 [3] — VERIFIED (scielo/PMID 28538817). Rambutan peel intermediate; litchi judged most promising; the modest enzyme-inhibition % and low fibroblast-safe concentration (0.01 mg/mL) are as stated. Accurately reported, including the unflattering "second-best" conclusion.
  • Lee et al. 2020 (seed senomorphic) [7] — VERIFIED (PMID 32429147, Nutrients). Authors, kaempferol-type compounds, SASP/senomorphic finding in aged fibroblasts confirmed. Correctly flagged as in-vitro only.
  • Xiao et al. 2019 (mouse photoaging) [10] — VERIFIED (Food Chem Toxicol 2019). Correctly flagged as animal AND confounded by the co-administered non-rambutan peptide (LSGYGP) with an "additive" effect.
  • Zhang et al. 2022 review [6] — VERIFIED (Heliyon e11314). Used as the secondary anchor for phenolic composition and as a pointer to primary studies (Xiao, Lourith, etc.).
  • BASF Nephoria/program [4][5][12] — VERIFIED as supplier claims (press release + trade press + TI). The fold-change figures and the >55-volunteer elasticity/wrinkle numbers are real as supplier-reported data; they are not independent peer-reviewed RCTs and are labelled as such.
  • "43% wrinkle improvement, P=0.013" [15] — NOT VERIFIED to a primary rambutan-only source. Explicitly marked [unverified]; must not enter copy without the original study.
  • INCI/CAS/EC, CosIng, COSMOS [11] — VERIFIED via INCI/CosIng aggregators (CAS 93165-68-5, EC 296-955-8). "No specific EU restriction" should still be confirmed against the live CosIng entry before any label.

Main caveats

  1. Three different plant parts, three different stories. The biggest integrity risk is conflating peel (antioxidant), leaf (retinol-like), and seed (senescence/hair). Any product claim must be tied to the specific extract used.
  2. No independent human RCT exists. The only human efficacy data is the supplier's own (Nephoria). This is weaker than bakuchiol's evidence base.
  3. The "retinol alternative" claim is leaf-specific, supplier-defined, and mechanistically loose (different receptors). It is also the most regulatorily risky phrasing.
  4. Penetration is unstudied and mechanistically questionable for the large geraniin polyphenol — in-vitro activity may not reach the dermis.
  5. Stability/colour of crude extracts is a practical formulation risk.
  6. Real strengths are genuine: strong antioxidant mechanism, low irritation, and an authentic upcycling/COSMOS/social-impact sourcing story — these, not efficacy superiority, are the defensible selling points.

Claims that must be policed in copy

  • Never "works like retinol"/"plant retinol" as if it is a retinoid (different receptors).
  • Never present peel antioxidant data as proof of the leaf anti-wrinkle/retinol-like claim.
  • Never "clinically proven" without disclosing it is supplier in-vivo data, not an independent RCT.
  • Never "boosts collagen by X%," "as effective as retinol," "reverses aging/senescence," or guarantee "pregnancy-safe."
  • The "43% / P=0.013" figure is [unverified] — do not use.

Net: A defensible supporting/conditional botanical with a strong sustainability narrative and solid in-vitro antioxidant credentials, but the anti-aging efficacy case is weak and supplier-dominated. Market on antioxidant + gentle + upcycled/ethical sourcing, never on proven retinol-equivalence.
