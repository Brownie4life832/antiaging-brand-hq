Ingredient: Endogenous niche antioxidants — Bilirubin / Biliverdin & Uric Acid

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering (research) / ⬜ shortlisted / ☑ leaning reject as topical active / ⬜ in a product Class: Antioxidants (the "tetrapyrrole bile pigments" and "purine" sub-families of the body's own redox network — sits alongside vitamin C/E, ferulic, CoQ10, idebenone, glutathione)

One-line honest summary: These are real, powerful antioxidants inside the body — bilirubin is one of the most potent endogenous lipophilic chain-breaking antioxidants (it outperformed α-tocopherol at suppressing lipid peroxidation in liposomes under low-oxygen conditions; Stocker 1987), and urate carries ~50–60% of plasma antioxidant capacity. But there is essentially no human topical-cosmetic evidence for either, the bile pigments are photolabile and yellow (in cosmetics bilirubin is something brands try to remove from skin, not add), and urate has a Jekyll-and-Hyde pro-oxidant/inflammatory dark side. This dossier exists mostly to let us speak credibly about the body's own antioxidant network — not to put these in a jar.

1) Identity

Bilirubin & Biliverdin (the bile pigments / "yellow players")

  • Common names: Bilirubin (unconjugated / "indirect"), Biliverdin.
  • Chemistry: Linear tetrapyrroles ("bilins") — open-chain breakdown products of heme. Heme → (heme oxygenase, HO-1/HO-2) → biliverdin (green) + CO + free iron → (biliverdin reductase) → bilirubin (yellow-orange).
  • INCI / cosmetic status: No established cosmetic INCI in routine use for either as an added active. Bilirubin (CAS 635-65-4) and biliverdin (CAS 114-25-0 / hydrochloride) are sold as biochemical reagents, not standardized cosmetic raw materials. Crucially, where bilirubin appears in cosmetic patent literature it is as an endogenous chromophore to be degraded (a cause of sallow skin tone and under-eye darkness), not as an ingredient to add — see §8.
  • Forms: Unconjugated bilirubin (lipophilic, albumin-bound in plasma); bilirubin ditaurate and other water-soluble conjugates (used in lab work); synthetic/engineered bilirubin nanoparticles (BRNPs) — bilirubin PEGylated into self-assembling nanoparticles — the only form with any topical skin data (all preclinical).

Uric Acid / Urate

  • Common names: Uric acid (the free acid); urate / monosodium urate (MSU) is the physiological salt at blood pH.
  • Chemistry: 7,9-dihydro-1H-purine-2,6,8(3H)-trione — the terminal product of purine (adenine/guanine) catabolism in humans. Because humans and great apes lost functional uricase (urate oxidase) through evolutionary mutation, we accumulate high urate where most mammals don't.
  • INCI / cosmetic status: Not an established cosmetic active. Present endogenously in sweat, sebum, and the stratum corneum as part of skin's native antioxidant network. Some skin-relevant work uses soluble synthetic urate analogs (e.g. 6,8-dithiouric acid) precisely because native uric acid is barely soluble and poorly penetrant.
  • Forms: Uric acid (poorly soluble, ~6.8 mg/dL solubility limit — above which it crystallizes as MSU, the cause of gout); urate salts; synthetic analogs with improved solubility for research.

2) Class & Mechanism

Both are members of the body's non-enzymatic endogenous antioxidant network, the same redox web that includes vitamin C, vitamin E (tocopherols), glutathione, CoQ10/ubiquinol and the bilins. They are not "added in" by nature for skincare — they are metabolic outputs that happen to have radical-scavenging side jobs.

Bilirubin / biliverdin mechanism: - Bilirubin is a chain-breaking, lipophilic peroxyl-radical scavenger — it intercepts lipid peroxidation in membranes (where it complements water-soluble antioxidants like vitamin C and urate). - The standout feature is the biliverdin reductase amplification cycle: bilirubin scavenges an oxidant and is itself oxidized back to biliverdin, which biliverdin reductase immediately reduces back to bilirubin. This redox cycling lets nanomolar bilirubin neutralize a ~10,000-fold excess of oxidant (Barañano 2002; Stocker 1987). No topical antioxidant achieves anything like this self-regenerating economy. - Bile pigments also act through HO-1 (heme oxygenase) signaling and are anti-inflammatory and immunomodulatory (suppress NF-κB / Th17 pathways) — the basis for the BRNP work in inflammatory skin disease.

Uric acid mechanism: - A potent scavenger of singlet oxygen, hydroxyl radical, peroxyl radicals and peroxynitrite; also a metal chelator (stabilizes ascorbate by chelating iron/copper). In the extracellular aqueous compartment it behaves as an antioxidant and contributes up to ~50–60% of plasma total antioxidant capacity (Ames 1981). - The dark side — context-dependent pro-oxidant: the same molecule is pro-oxidant intracellularly and, once it crystallizes as MSU, becomes a danger signal (DAMP) that activates the NLRP3 inflammasome → IL-1β / IL-18, drives ROS, and causes gout. Soluble urate also has reported pro-inflammatory, endothelial-damaging effects at high levels. This "oxidant–antioxidant paradox" is the central caveat for any pro-aging story built on urate (Sautin & Johnson 2008).

4) Evidence review by tier (brutally honest)

Headline: For topical human cosmetic use, the evidence is effectively zero for both. Everything below is in-vitro biochemistry, animal/systemic pharmacology, or human systemic/observational (blood-level) epidemiology. None of it transfers cleanly to "put it in a cream."

4a. In-vitro / biochemical

  1. Stocker, Yamamoto, McDonagh, Glazer & Ames (1987), Science 235:1043–1046 — "Bilirubin is an antioxidant of possible physiological importance." Landmark paper: bilirubin efficiently scavenges peroxyl radicals in solution and liposomes; at physiologic (2%) oxygen tension it outperforms α-tocopherol at suppressing lipid peroxidation. Limitation: pure biochemistry, not cells/skin.
  2. Barañano, Rao, Ferris & Snyder (2002), PNAS 99(25):16093–16098 — established the biliverdin reductase redox-amplification cycle; RNAi depletion of bilirubin in cells raised ROS and caused apoptosis more than glutathione depletion. The mechanistic basis for "10 nM bilirubin beats 10,000× H₂O₂." Limitation: cell lines, not skin.
  3. Ames, Cathcart, Schwiers & Hochstein (1981), PNAS 78(11):6858–6862 — the foundational uric acid antioxidant hypothesis: urate scavenges singlet oxygen & radicals, protects erythrocytes from peroxidation, and rose during primate evolution as a plasma antioxidant. Limitation: hypothesis paper / in-vitro and ex-vivo blood, not skin.
  4. Sautin & Johnson (2008), Nucleosides Nucleotides Nucleic Acids 27(6):608–619 — "Uric acid: the oxidant–antioxidant paradox." Rigorous review showing urate is antioxidant extracellularly but pro-oxidant intracellularly; reframes urate as harmful at chronically high levels. Essential caveat source.
  5. Multiple (e.g. J. Am. Chem. Soc., Biochem Biophys Res Commun 1971; review J. Photochem. Photobiol. B 2024) — bilirubin photo-oxidation is a self-sensitized, singlet-oxygen-generating reaction; bilirubin is photolabile (the basis of neonatal blue-light phototherapy). Directly relevant to topical/UV stability and photoreactivity concerns (§6, §7).

4b. Animal / systemic pharmacology

  1. Bai, Liu, You, Li & Ma (2015), J. Photochem. Photobiol. B 144:35–41 — intraperitoneally administered biliverdin protected hairless mice against UVB photodamage: reduced histopathologic change, inhibited oxidative stress and inflammatory cytokine release. Critical limitation: systemic injection, not topical — says nothing about whether biliverdin works applied to skin.
  2. Keum H et al. (2020), J. Control. Release 325:359–369 (PubMed 32681946) — topical bilirubin nanoparticles (BRNPs) in an imiquimod psoriasis mouse model: BRNPs infiltrated the disrupted barrier, were taken up by keratinocytes, lowered intracellular ROS, suppressed pro-inflammatory signaling, and improved disease — without the systemic toxicity of clobetasol. Limitation: engineered PEG nanoparticle (not "bilirubin in a cream"), diseased/barrier-disrupted skin, mouse, drug-style indication.
  3. Self-assembled bilirubin/JPH203 nanoparticles (2022), Chem. Eng. J. 433:133761 — topical, imiquimod psoriasis mouse model; reduced oxidative stress and Th17 expansion. Same caveats as #7.
  4. Kim et al. (2025), Advanced Therapeutics 8:2400403 — synthetic bilirubin(IIIα) nanoparticle reduced atopic-dermatitis-like symptoms topically in mice (antioxidant + immunomodulatory). Caveats as above; very recent, single group lineage.
  5. Chigurupati et al. (2010), PLoS One 5(4):e10044 — topical synthetic uric acid analog 6,8-dithiouric acid (UA2), 100 µM once daily, accelerated full-thickness wound closure in C57BL/6 mice (complete closure by day 8 vs open controls), with lower protein oxidation. Crucial nuance: they used a synthetic analog precisely because native uric acid is too insoluble and non-penetrant to use — an indictment of topical urate itself, not an endorsement.

4c. Human — ORAL / SYSTEMIC / observational (blood-level)

  1. Gilbert's syndrome epidemiology (reviews e.g. J. Hepatol. 2023; World J. Hepatol. 2025) — mild lifelong unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia is associated with roughly half the all-cause mortality and reduced cardiovascular disease, type-2 diabetes and some cancers, attributed to bilirubin's antioxidant/anti-inflammatory action. This is the strongest "longevity" signal for bilirubin — but it is about endogenous blood levels, not topical or even oral dosing.
  2. Tosevska et al. (2016), Sci. Rep. 6:22300 — Gilbert's-syndrome subjects (n=60 vs 60 matched controls) had longer telomeres in peripheral blood mononuclear cells, consistent with reduced oxidative load. Nuance: the human telomere effect was statistically clearest in males, supported by a Gunn-rat model; observational, systemic, modest sample — correlation not causation, not a skin endpoint.
  3. Ames-lineage urate epidemiology — higher (but still normal) plasma urate correlates with neuroprotection in some studies (Parkinson's, MS) but with cardiovascular/metabolic harm at high levels — the paradox again. No skin-aging endpoint, no topical relevance.

4d. Human — TOPICAL (the tier that matters for us)

  • Bilirubin / biliverdin added topically as a cosmetic antioxidant: NO human studies found. None. The only human-skin context is the opposite — cosmetic R&D aimed at degrading/clearing endogenous bilirubin to reduce sallowness and dark circles (see §8).
  • Uric acid / urate applied topically as a cosmetic antioxidant: NO human efficacy studies found. Human-skin data for urate is descriptive, not interventional:
  • Weber, Thiele, Cross & Packer (1999), J. Invest. Dermatol. 113(6):1128–1132 — mapped vitamin C, uric acid and glutathione gradients in (murine) stratum corneum (lowest at the surface, rising with depth) and showed ozone depletes uric acid to ~44% of baseline. Establishes urate as a native SC antioxidant and a sacrificial target of environmental oxidants — but it's mouse SC and observational, not a topical-active trial.
  • Thiele et al. (2001), Free Radic. Biol. Med. / "antioxidant network of the stratum corneum" — positions urate within the SC's endogenous defense web alongside vitamin C, E, GSH, CoQ10. Conceptual support, not product evidence.
  • Shindo et al. (1994), J. Invest. Dermatol.; Shindo & Hashimoto — UV depletes the skin's endogenous antioxidant reservoir (enzymatic + non-enzymatic) by 26–93%; the rationale for replenishing antioxidants topically — but the replenishment evidence base is for vitamin C/E/ferulic, not for urate or bilirubin.

Bottom line by tier: in-vitro = strong (biochemistry); animal/systemic = moderate but route-mismatched (injection or engineered nanoparticles); human oral/observational = intriguing longevity epidemiology (blood-level only); human topical cosmetic = none. The honest evidence tier for our use case is none → in-vitro borrowed.

5) Topical bioavailability

  • Bilirubin/biliverdin: Unconjugated bilirubin is lipophilic, water-insoluble, light-sensitive and a chromophore (yellow-green). Naked bilirubin does not penetrate intact skin usefully and would discolor a formula. The only demonstrated skin delivery is via PEGylated nanoparticles into barrier-disrupted (diseased) skin — irrelevant to a leave-on cosmetic for healthy skin. Topical bioavailability of raw bile pigments as cosmetic actives: effectively unworkable.
  • Uric acid: Very poorly soluble (~6.8 mg/dL) and poorly tissue-penetrant — researchers consistently abandon it for synthetic soluble analogs. SC urate sits in a depth gradient (deeper = higher), and the surface pool is sacrificially consumed by UV/ozone. Delivering meaningful added urate into living epidermis topically is not realistically achievable, and pushing concentrations risks crystallization.

6) Formulation notes

  • Effective concentration (topical cosmetic): Unknown / undefined for both — no cosmetic-active precedent.
  • pH: Uric acid is a weak acid (pKa ~5.4); solubility is pH-dependent and very low across cosmetic-relevant pH — crystallization risk. Bilirubin solubility is highly pH/solvent dependent and it is unstable.
  • Stability: Poor for both. Bilirubin is photolabile (degrades and can self-sensitize singlet oxygen under light — the basis of phototherapy), oxygen-sensitive, and color-unstable. Uric acid crystallizes out of solution. Neither has the formulation robustness of a stabilized vitamin C/ferulic or idebenone system.
  • Pairings (conceptual): The idea of bile pigments + urate is to complete the endogenous network alongside the antioxidants we'd actually use — vitamin C, vitamin E, ferulic acid, CoQ10/ubiquinol, glutathione, niacinamide. If anything, our network story is better told with those proven, formulable actives.
  • Sensory/aesthetic: Bilirubin/biliverdin impart yellow/green color — an immediate consumer-acceptance and "does it stain?" problem. Urate is white/odorless but brings crystallization grit risk.

7) Safety

  • Bilirubin/biliverdin:
  • Photoreactivity: bilirubin is a photosensitizer that generates singlet oxygen on light exposure — a theoretical phototoxicity / pro-oxidant concern for a leave-on facial product worn in daylight. (At high systemic levels bilirubin is neurotoxic — kernicterus — but that is not a topical risk.)
  • Color/staining: yellow chromophore; risk of staining skin/fabric.
  • Sensitization/irritation: not characterized for cosmetic topical use (no data).
  • Pregnancy: no topical-cosmetic data; not assessable.
  • Sourcing: historically animal-derived (bovine bile); modern supply is synthetic/biotech (engineered microbial biliverdin → bilirubin). Animal origin is a vegan/clean-beauty flag.
  • Uric acid/urate:
  • Pro-oxidant / inflammatory: the major caution — intracellular and crystalline urate (MSU) are pro-oxidant and activate the NLRP3 inflammasome (IL-1β/IL-18); MSU is the literal cause of gout and a recognized DAMP. Adding urate to skin is biologically ambivalent.
  • Crystallization: low solubility → grit/precipitation in formula and potentially in tissue.
  • Irritation/sensitization/pregnancy: no topical cosmetic safety dataset.
  • Sun: unlike bilirubin, urate is not a notable photosensitizer; it is consumed by UV/ozone (acts sacrificially).
  • General: Neither has CIR (Cosmetic Ingredient Review) or SCCS opinions supporting cosmetic use as an added active, which is itself a red flag for a premium, claims-disciplined brand.

8) Regulatory & claims (cosmetic vs drug — per CLAUDE.md)

  • No recognized cosmetic-active status for bilirubin, biliverdin, or uric acid as added antioxidants in the US (FDA), EU (CosIng/SCCS) or major markets. They are not on standard cosmetic ingredient lists as actives.
  • Drug-line risk is high. The real-world evidence is in disease models (psoriasis, atopic dermatitis, wound healing) and systemic disease epidemiology (cardiovascular, mortality, telomeres). Any copy leaning on that body of work would read as a drug claim ("treats," "heals wounds," "reduces inflammation," "extends lifespan") — exactly the territory CLAUDE.md says to avoid.
  • Bilirubin is, in cosmetic regulatory practice, a target to remove, not add: Procter & Gamble patents ("Method for Degrading Bilirubin in Skin," e.g. US 10,874,600; US-2019-0380945) and niacinamide-at-low-pH compositions frame endogenous bilirubin as the cause of sallow/yellow skin tone and under-eye darkness, and claim clearing it. Marketing an "added bilirubin" antioxidant cream would directly contradict the prevailing cosmetic narrative and invite "why is there a yellow pigment in this?" scrutiny.
  • Safer factual framing (if used at all): Educational, non-product copy about the body's own antioxidant network — "your skin makes its own antioxidants (vitamin C, glutathione, urate) that UV and ozone deplete, which is why topical antioxidant support matters." This is accurate and compliant and supports our proven actives. We must not imply our product contains or supplies bilirubin/biliverdin/urate.

9) Sourcing & cost

  • Bilirubin / biliverdin: Available as reagent-grade biochemicals (historically bovine bile extraction; increasingly recombinant/biotech), not as standardized cosmetic raw materials with a supply chain, COA package, or regulatory dossier. No meaningful cosmetic-grade market → high friction, high cost, no economies of scale, animal-origin/vegan questions. Practically unsourceable as a compliant cosmetic active today.
  • Uric acid: Cheap and abundant as a lab/industrial chemical, but with no cosmetic-active grade or precedent, plus solubility/crystallization problems. Synthetic analogs (e.g. 6,8-dithiouric acid) are research compounds, not cosmetic ingredients. Not commercially viable as a cosmetic active.
  • Cost tier: Irrelevant in practice — neither is a realistic buy as a formulated cosmetic active.

10) Hype vs evidence

  • The hype (real, but mis-mapped): "Bilirubin is the most powerful endogenous antioxidant — nanomolar amounts beat 10,000× the oxidant." "Uric acid is most of your blood's antioxidant power and rose to make primates live longer." Both statements are scientifically defensible at the systemic/biochemical level and make seductive longevity copy.
  • The evidence reality for skincare: 1. Zero human topical cosmetic data for either. 2. The bilirubin skin data that exists is for engineered nanoparticles in diseased mouse skin (a drug-delivery story), and the biliverdin photoprotection data is from injection, not topical. 3. In cosmetics, bilirubin is the problem, not the solution — it's a yellow chromophore brands work to clear. 4. Urate is a double-edged sword (pro-oxidant intracellularly, inflammasome-activating as crystals) and is too insoluble/non-penetrant to deliver — researchers replace it with synthetic analogs. 5. Both are photolabile/photoreactive or crystallization-prone and unformulable to a premium standard.
  • Net: This is a classic case of impressive systemic biology that does not translate into a topical product. The longevity epidemiology is genuinely interesting and brand-relevant as education, but pretending we can bottle these antioxidants would be hype.

11) Verdict for the brand

SKIP as a topical active (both bilirubin/biliverdin and uric acid). KEEP as narrative/education only. ❌ (as ingredient) / ☑ (as story)

Reasoning: - No human topical cosmetic evidence; the supporting science is systemic, animal, or disease-model — and route-mismatched. - Bilirubin/biliverdin are photolabile, photosensitizing, yellow-staining, unsourceable as cosmetic actives, and culturally framed in cosmetics as something to remove (sallowness/dark circles) — adopting them would actively confuse the brand. - Uric acid is too insoluble/non-penetrant to deliver, crystallizes, and has a real pro-oxidant/inflammasome dark side — biologically ambivalent and reputationally risky (its public association is gout). - Neither has CIR/SCCS cosmetic backing; any efficacy story would skew drug-claim, violating our claims discipline. - We can tell a more credible, fully compliant "endogenous antioxidant network" story using the actives we'd actually formulate — vitamin C, vitamin E, ferulic acid, CoQ10/ubiquinol, glutathione, niacinamide — which are exactly the network these molecules belong to. (See vitamin-c.md, ferulic-acid.md, coq10.md/idebenone.md, glutathione.md, niacinamide.md as they land.)

Longevity note: The single most brand-relevant fact here is the Gilbert's-syndrome longevity signal — people with lifelong mildly elevated bilirubin have ~half the mortality and longer telomeres (Tosevska 2016; Gilbert reviews). It is a beautiful illustration of "antioxidant capacity ↔ healthspan," and a great educational/blog hook for a skin-longevity brand. But it argues for supporting the body's own redox network (and for sunscreen + proven topical antioxidants that spare endogenous urate/glutathione from UV/ozone depletion — Weber 1999, Shindo 1994), not for adding bilirubin or urate to a cream. Use the science to justify our antioxidant strategy, not these specific molecules.

→ Log under not-considering (as topical actives, with reason: no topical evidence, photolability/color, sourcing, urate pro-oxidant risk) and keep a note in research/science for the "skin's own antioxidant network / Gilbert's longevity" education angle.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified: 2026-06-19. Independent adversarial re-check of every headline claim and citation against external sources.

Overall grade: D (unproven topically / borrowed-evidence) for both bilirubin/biliverdin and uric acid as topical cosmetic actives. This grade agrees with the dossier's own honest self-assessment ("none → in-vitro borrowed") and its SKIP-as-active verdict — it is not a downgrade of the file's conclusion, which is already correctly cautious.

Grade rationale, by the rubric (A = strong human-topical RCT; B = moderate human-topical; C = weak/indirect; D = unproven topically/hype): - D is the correct topical grade because there is zero human topical-cosmetic interventional evidence for either molecule. The supporting science is real and often strong, but it is route- and context-mismatched: in-vitro biochemistry, systemic injection in animals, engineered PEG-nanoparticles in diseased/barrier-disrupted mouse skin, or human blood-level epidemiology. None of it supports "this molecule, applied to a cream, benefits healthy human skin." - The underlying systemic/biochemical science would grade far higher (B–A for "bilirubin is a potent endogenous antioxidant" and "urate carries most of plasma antioxidant capacity") — but that is not the claim that matters for a topical product, and the dossier is careful to separate the two.
