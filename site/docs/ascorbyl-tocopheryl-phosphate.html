Ingredient: Potassium Ascorbyl Tocopheryl Phosphate (EPC / EPC-K1) — a covalent vitamin C + vitamin E diester (INCI: Potassium Ascorbyl Tocopheryl Phosphate)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Slug: ascorbyl-tocopheryl-phosphate · Class: Antioxidants (vitamin C/E redox)

Reading note. This molecule is sold on a genuinely clever idea: covalently bolt vitamin C and vitamin E together through a phosphate bridge so a single, water-soluble, stable raw material can deliver both vitamins from one phase. The chemistry is real and the antioxidant data is real. But there is a hard, honest line running through this dossier: almost all of the actual efficacy literature on this exact molecule (under its research name "EPC-K1") is pharmacology — injected or oral, in animal models of ischemia-reperfusion injury, not topical skincare. The skin claims (collagen-activating, humectant, anti-acne, "normalizes turnover") trace to the manufacturer's own material (Senju Pharmaceutical) and patents, not to independent human topical trials. No published human topical RCT on EPC/EPC-K for wrinkles, tone or photoaging was found. This file keeps that line bright.

0. The honest punchline (read this first)

  • What it is: A single molecule — L-ascorbic acid 2-[α-tocopheryl] phosphate, potassium salt — i.e. vitamin C and vitamin E joined by a phosphate group. Research name EPC-K1; cosmetic trade name EPC (Senju Pharmaceutical, distributed via Iwase Cosfa). CAS 127061-56-7; formula C₃₅H₅₆KO₁₀P (PubChem CID 54715165) [12][13].
  • The genuine value proposition: it solves a real formulation headache. Vitamin C is water-soluble, vitamin E is oil-soluble, and L-ascorbic acid is notoriously unstable. EPC is water-soluble, far more stable than free ascorbic acid, and lets you put "both vitamins" into one water phase. That is a legitimate, defensible engineering benefit [2][6].
  • The antioxidant chemistry is real: in rigorous radical-chemistry assays (ESR spin-trapping, pulse radiolysis) EPC-K1 scavenges hydroxyl, alkyl and especially lipid radicals, inhibits lipid peroxidation, and outperformed Trolox and vitamin C overall "in vitro and in vivo" (a single radical-chemistry paper, Wei et al. 1999; the "in vivo" readout is rat-brain superoxide scavenging, not skin) [1].
  • The catch — format mismatch. EPC-K1's deep evidence base is systemic/pharmacological (IV/IP/oral) in animal models of brain, heart, lung, liver and muscle ischemia-reperfusion injury [3][4][7][8][9][10][14][15]. This tells us the molecule is a potent biological antioxidant — but says nothing directly about a cream raising skin antioxidant defense or visibly reducing wrinkles in people.
  • The skin gap. The cosmetic skin claims come from the manufacturer and patent literature [2][5][6], and the one human topical clinical paper that shows up adjacent to this molecule (Kurokawa 2019, acne) actually tested a different vitamin C/E complex, not EPC [11]. No human topical RCT on EPC itself was found.
  • Net: a well-made, stable, well-tolerated, dual-vitamin antioxidant with strong mechanistic and animal-pharmacology credentials — but borrowed-format efficacy for skin. Treat it as a supporting antioxidant / stable vitamin-C-and-E delivery system, not as a proven anti-wrinkle hero. The honest claim is "stable vitamin C + E antioxidant," not "clinically proven to reduce wrinkles."

Do not confuse with its commoner cousins (different molecules, different INCIs): - Sodium Ascorbyl Phosphate (SAP) / Magnesium Ascorbyl Phosphate (MAP) — vitamin-C-only phosphate salts; far more studied in skin. - APPS / Sodium Ascorbyl 6-Palmitate 2-Phosphate (Showa Denko's APPRÉCIER) and Sodium Tocopheryl Phosphate (TPNa) — also Japanese, also "phosphate vitamin," but different compounds; APPS is not EPC [5]. - Tocopheryl Acetate / Tocopheryl Phosphate — vitamin-E-only derivatives.

The literature frequently blurs all of these. Many "ascorbyl phosphate" collagen/melanin findings belong to MAP/SAP, not EPC — flagged explicitly below.

2. Class & mechanism

Class: Vitamin C/E antioxidant (covalent dual-vitamin diester).

Why the molecule was designed this way (the real cleverness): - Stability: free L-ascorbic acid oxidizes rapidly in water. Esterifying the C-2 hydroxyl as a phosphate blocks the main oxidation route, so the molecule is shelf-stable; the ascorbate is then "released"/regenerated in skin by phosphatases (the same logic that makes SAP/MAP work). The tocopherol is likewise carried in a water-dispersible form [2][6]. - One-phase delivery of two vitamins: because the conjugate is water-soluble, you can deliver a vitamin-E moiety from the water phase — circumventing the classic "you can't easily put vitamin C and vitamin E in the same simple system" problem [2]. - Built-in redox couple: vitamin C's job in the C+E antioxidant network is partly to regenerate oxidized vitamin E (recycle the tocopheroxyl radical back to tocopherol). Tethering them in one molecule is the structural expression of that well-established C↔E synergy [reviewed in 16].

How it scavenges radicals (demonstrated): - The active site is the enolic hydroxyl group; EPC-K1 is a moderate scavenger of hydroxyl and alkyl radicals, a potent scavenger of lipid radicals, and an effective inhibitor of lipid peroxidation (Wei et al. 1999) [1]. - Measured rate constants: reaction with ·OH ≈ 7.1 × 10⁸ M⁻¹s⁻¹; with linoleic-acid (lipid) radical ≈ 2.8 × 10⁶ M⁻¹s⁻¹ [1]. - Overall antioxidative capacity exceeded Trolox and vitamin C in the same assays, attributed to its amphiphilic (hydrophilic ascorbate + lipophilic tocopherol) structure letting it act at both aqueous and membrane interfaces [1]. - Secondary mechanisms documented in pharmacology: inhibition of phospholipase A₂ (PLA₂) [3], and in some models up-regulation of endogenous catalase [9] — i.e. it isn't purely a sacrificial scavenger; it can modulate antioxidant enzyme tone.

Skin-level mechanism (claimed, manufacturer-sourced): EPC is presented as delivering UV-radical scavenging, moisturizing/humectant action, collagen-production activation, normalization of roughened-skin turnover, and inhibition of Cutibacterium (Propionibacterium) acnes growth [2][5]. These are plausible extensions of vitamin C/E biology but are manufacturer/patent assertions, not independently RCT-verified for this molecule (see §4-D).

4. Evidence review by tier (the core of this dossier)

Tiers, weakest-format-for-skin to most-relevant: in-vitro → animal → human ORAL → human TOPICAL (RCT vs lower). For each claim I flag whether it is proven for human topical skin use or borrowed from another format/molecule. The defining feature of this ingredient is that its real evidence is animal pharmacology, and its skin evidence is manufacturer-sourced.

TIER A — In-vitro (radical chemistry & cell/biochemistry)

A1. Flagship mechanism paper — radical scavenging & lipid-peroxidation inhibition. - Wei T, Chen C, Li F, Zhao B, Hou J, Xin W, Mori A. "Antioxidant properties of EPC-K1: a study on mechanisms." Biophys Chem. 1999;77(2-3):153-160. (Institute of Biophysics, Academia Sinica.) [1] - Methods: ESR spin-trapping (·OH, alkyl, lipid radicals), TBA assay (lipid peroxidation), pulse-radiolysis kinetics; plus an in-vivo rat-brain superoxide-scavenging readout. - Findings: moderate ·OH/alkyl scavenging; potent lipid-radical scavenging; effective lipid-peroxidation inhibitor; rate constants k(·OH)=7.1×10⁸, k(lipid)=2.8×10⁶ M⁻¹s⁻¹; active site = enolic OH; better overall antioxidant capacity than Trolox and vitamin C; rat-brain superoxide-scavenging ability increased after administration. - Limitation: biochemistry/animal-brain, not skin. Proves the molecule is a strong antioxidant chemically; not a skin-efficacy result.

A2. Sebaceous/sebum model (one of the few cell studies that actually used the cosmetic EPC). - "Influence of Radical Generation and Elimination on Sebum Production of Hamster Sebaceous Gland Cells." Life (MDPI) 2025;15(2):165. EPC was supplied by Senju Pharmaceutical and used as a radical-eliminating test antioxidant in sebocyte cultures [5]. - Limitation: hamster sebocytes in culture; EPC is a tool reagent, not the study's hero; tangential to facial anti-aging.

A3. Collagen / melanin "ascorbyl phosphate" data — borrowed from cousins, NOT EPC. - Magnesium/sodium ascorbyl-2-phosphate (MAP/SAP) stimulate collagen synthesis in human dermal fibroblasts (the Mg salt ≈ ascorbic acid; the Na salt needs ~10× more) — Hata & Senoo / Geesin-type data [17]. VC-PMG (Mg-ascorbyl-2-phosphate) suppresses melanin/tyrosinase. These are different molecules; cited only to show what the vitamin-C-phosphate class can do — they are not evidence for EPC.

TIER B — Animal (systemic: IV / IP / oral — NOT topical skin)

This is where EPC-K1 is genuinely well-studied — as an injectable/oral pharmacological antioxidant in ischemia-reperfusion (I/R) injury. Powerful for proving "real biological antioxidant," irrelevant to a topical cosmetic except by analogy.

B1. Global cerebral ischemia, rats. Block F, Kunkel M, Sontag KH. "Posttreatment with EPC-K1, an inhibitor of lipid peroxidation and of PLA₂ activity, reduces functional deficits after global ischemia in rats." Brain Res Bull. 1995;36(3):257-260. IP 10 mg/kg; reduced spatial-learning deficit when given during/after reperfusion. [3]

B2. Transient focal cerebral ischemia, rats. Kato N, Yanaka K, Nagase S, Hirayama A, Nose T. "The antioxidant EPC-K1 ameliorates brain injury by inhibiting lipid peroxidation in a rat model of transient focal cerebral ischaemia." Acta Neurochir (Wien). 2003;145(6):489-493. ↓ cerebral TBARS and ↓ infarct size. [4]

B3. Cerebral energy state / consciousness recovery, gerbils. "Effects of L-ascorbic acid 2-[α-tocopheryl] phosphate potassium salt on cerebral energy state and consciousness recovery following transient forebrain ischemia in gerbils." (1994) PMID 7986254 — preserved cerebral high-energy phosphates / faster recovery. [15]

B4. 6-OHDA Parkinson's model, mice. Kabuto H, Yokoi I, Iwata-Ichikawa E, Ogawa N. "EPC-K1 … prevents 6-hydroxydopamine-induced dopamine depletion in the mouse striatum by up-regulation of catalase activity." Neurochem Res. 1999;24(12):1543-1548. Oral (0.2% diet, 2 wk) neuroprotection via catalase. [9]

B5. Lung preservation, ex-vivo rabbit. Nagahiro I, Aoe M, Yamashita M, Date H, Andou A, Shimizu N. "EPC-K1 is effective in lung preservation in an ex vivo rabbit lung perfusion model." Ann Thorac Surg. 1997;63(4):954-959. EPC-K1 (0.5 mg/L) in flush/preservation solution ↓ lipid peroxide, improved graft function. [7]

B6. Hepatic I/R, rats. "The vitamin E derivative, EPC-K1, suppresses inflammation during hepatic ischemia-reperfusion injury and exerts hepatoprotective effects in rats." J Surg Res. 2011 (PMID 22560539). Suppressed inflammatory response and apoptosis; hepatoprotective. [10]

B7. Skeletal-muscle I/R, rats. "Effects of a hydroxyl radical scavenger, EPC-K1, and neutrophil depletion on reperfusion injury in rat skeletal muscle." (2001) PMID 11580130; and timing-of-administration study PMID 10366926. Reduced reperfusion injury. [8]

B8. Cardiac transplantation, rats. "Beneficial effect of EPC-K1 on the survival of warm ischemic damaged graft in rat cardiac transplantation." (1993) PMID 8506749. [14]

Pattern (be honest about it): ~a dozen animal/pharmacology papers, route = injection or oral, target = internal organ I/R injury. They establish EPC-K1 as a potent in-vivo antioxidant. None apply EPC topically to skin, and intact internal-organ protection ≠ a cream improving facial skin.

TIER C — Human ORAL

  • No human oral trials for skin endpoints were found. (The systemic pharmacology was largely a Japanese drug-development program that did not translate into a marketed systemic drug.) Effectively no human oral skin evidence.

TIER D — Human TOPICAL (the decisive tier — and it's largely empty)

D1. No published, peer-reviewed human topical RCT on EPC / Potassium Ascorbyl Tocopheryl Phosphate (for wrinkles, firmness, tone, hydration, barrier or photoaging) was located in this search. The molecule's skin benefits rest on: - Manufacturer technical literature (Senju/Iwase via Personal Care Magazine and supplier datasheets): water-solubility, stability, UV-radical scavenging, moisturizing, collagen-production activation, turnover normalization, anti-C. acnes. [2][5] - Patents, e.g. "Tocopheryl ascorbyl phosphate-cyclodextrin clathrate and a topical dermal composition…" (US 5,879,692) — uses cyclodextrin to further stabilize and improve skin penetration of EPC-K, implicitly conceding that unaided topical penetration/stability needs help. [6]

D2. The "closest" human topical study is a DIFFERENT molecule. - Kurokawa I, Yoshioka M, Ito S. "Split-face comparative clinical trial using glyceryl-octyl-ascorbic acid / ascorbyl 2-phosphate 6-palmitate / DL-α-tocopherol phosphate complex treatment for postinflammatory hyperpigmentation, postinflammatory erythema and atrophic scar in acne vulgaris." J Dermatol. 2019;46(10):e347-e348. [11] - This tests a three-ingredient C/E complex (GO-VC + APPS + tocopheryl phosphate) for post-acne sequelae (PIH/PIE/atrophic scar) — NOT EPC / potassium ascorbyl tocopheryl phosphate, and not even an acne-treatment trial per se. It is sometimes cited near EPC because the actives are chemically adjacent, but it is not evidence for EPC and should never be presented as such. (Title and actives independently verified, 2026-06-19.)

D3. Category context (analogy only, not EPC). Topical vitamin-C-phosphate cousins (SAP, MAP) have small human topical studies for acne, melasma and wrinkle depth [e.g. SAP/AA emulgel, PMID 32383548; MAP melasma carriers], and the CE-Ferulic vitamin C+E+ferulic combination has photoprotection data in mice and people. These support the plausibility of EPC, but the specific-molecule human topical proof is absent.

Verdict on the topical tier: the efficacy story for EPC on skin is mechanism + animal pharmacology + manufacturer claims, with a genuine evidence vacuum at the human-topical-RCT level. Anything stronger than "stable vitamin C + E antioxidant that helps defend against oxidative/environmental stress (appearance-level)" is currently unsubstantiated for this molecule.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Solubility/logistics: water-soluble salt — easy to dose in aqueous serums/toners/essences; the whole point is delivering a tocopherol moiety from the water phase [2].
  • Penetration is the open question. It is a fairly large (~706 Da), charged, amphiphilic phosphate diester. Like SAP/MAP, it presumably relies on cutaneous phosphatases to cleave the phosphate and liberate active ascorbate (and the tocopherol moiety) in skin — but direct human skin-penetration/bioconversion data for EPC was not found.
  • Tellingly, the topical patent adds cyclodextrin specifically to improve EPC-K's stability and skin penetration [6] — a signal that unaided dermal delivery was considered a limitation worth engineering around.
  • Practical read: assume modest, phosphatase-dependent delivery typical of ascorbyl-phosphate salts; don't assume the deep-dermis bioavailability the injectable pharmacology achieved.

6. Formulation

  • Concentration: No RCT-derived "sweet spot" exists for EPC. Supplier/patent example dosing is low (e.g. formulation examples around 0.05%, ranging up into low single digits) [13, formulation-example context]; CIR notes cosmetic use at concentrations lower than the most-used tocopheryl ingredients [18]. Practical cosmetic inclusion is typically ~0.1–2% vendor-guided, not the 10–20% seen with L-ascorbic acid.
  • pH: as an ascorbyl-phosphate, it is expected to be most stable and best activated near-neutral to mildly alkaline (~pH 6.5–7.5), by analogy to SAP behavior — i.e. it likely does not need (and is not best at) the harsh pH < 3.5 that L-ascorbic acid requires, which would make it gentle and easy to pair. (This is a class inference: no EPC-specific pH-stability/activation curve was located in independent literature — treat as a reasoned assumption to confirm with the supplier datasheet, not an established EPC fact.)
  • Stability: a core selling point — markedly more stable in water than free ascorbic acid [2][6]; resists the rapid browning/oxidation of LAA. Still: protect from prolonged heat; opaque/airless packaging is sensible for any vitamin-C system.
  • Pairings: plays well with niacinamide, hyaluronic acid, panthenol, ceramides, peptides; conceptually self-contained as a C+E unit. Avoid forcing it into very low-pH LAA systems (defeats the purpose).
  • Conflicts: standard ascorbyl-phosphate caution — divalent/heavy-metal contamination can catalyze oxidation; keep chelators (e.g. a trace of EDTA) in the system.
  • Sensory: water-soluble powder; light, non-greasy in aqueous bases; high-purity grades effectively odorless/colorless. A practical way to add a "vitamin E" story to a water-based, non-tacky serum without an oil phase.

7. Safety

  • CIR (Cosmetic Ingredient Review): Potassium Ascorbyl Tocopheryl Phosphate was assessed within the tocopherols/tocotrienols group and judged safe as used in cosmetics; tocopherol-family ingredients were not irritants or sensitizers, with negative reproductive-tox and carcinogenicity findings supporting the group. (Final report 2002, Int J Toxicol; re-affirmed in the 2018 tocopherols/tocotrienols safety assessment.) [18][19]
  • Irritation/sensitization: low expected; both parent vitamins are well-tolerated topically, and the phosphate-salt form avoids the low-pH sting of L-ascorbic acid. (No EPC-specific human irritation dataset published; rely on the class/CIR record.)
  • Sun sensitivity: none — it is photoprotective in rationale, used in daytime antioxidant contexts; not a photosensitizer.
  • Pregnancy: no specific contraindication for topical vitamin C/E derivatives; routine "consult your clinician" framing, no disease claims.
  • Sourcing/quality: semi-synthetic specialty active; demand a CoA (purity, salt form, residual solvents, heavy metals). Niche supply (essentially a single originator, Senju) → less commoditized, see §9.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

The cosmetic/drug line. Cosmetics may improve appearance; they may not claim to treat/prevent disease or alter the body's structure/function (US FDA; analogous EU rules). Several EPC narratives drift across that line.

  • ✅ Safe cosmetic language:
  • "a stable, water-soluble form of vitamin C and vitamin E," "antioxidant that helps defend the skin's appearance against environmental/oxidative stress," "helps maintain a smooth, even-looking, hydrated complexion," "supports skin's visible radiance."
  • "delivers vitamin C and vitamin E together from one stable system" — this is a true formulation fact.
  • ⚠️ Risky / unsubstantiated for THIS molecule — flag for substantiation/legal:
  • "boosts/activates collagen" or "stimulates collagen production" — manufacturer claim, no human topical EPC data; structure/function risk. (Collagen data belongs to MAP/ascorbic acid, not EPC.)
  • "clinically proven to reduce wrinkles / firm skin" — no EPC human topical RCT exists; this would be false-claim territory.
  • "reduces acne / treats breakouts" — anti-C. acnes is a manufacturer in-vitro claim; "treats acne" is a drug claim regardless.
  • "brightens / fades dark spots" framed as EPC efficacy — borrowed from MAP/VC-PMG; not shown for EPC.
  • Implying the ischemia-reperfusion / "protects organs" pharmacology has any bearing on a face cream — scientifically and legally improper.
  • Key compliance nuance: the strong-sounding evidence ("outperforms Trolox," "protects the brain/heart/liver from injury") is injected/oral animal pharmacology. It must never be implied to substantiate a topical cosmetic benefit. Keep it, if used at all, as background education about the molecule's antioxidant potency — clearly labeled as non-topical, non-human-skin.

Supply risk Concentrated supply (essentially one originator) → higher price, potential lead-time/single-source risk; vet a second-source/equivalent before designing it into a hero SKU.

Sourcing rule (fits the brand's "buy commodity bases, invest in brand" thesis): EPC is a premium specialty input. Justified only if we want the specific "single stable molecule = vitamin C + E" story and the gentle near-neutral-pH delivery — otherwise SAP/MAP + a separate tocopherol delivers most of the category benefit more cheaply and with more skin data.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • "Vitamin C and vitamin E in one molecule — the best of both, stabilized." — True as chemistry/formulation [1][2][6]. This is the legitimate core.
  • "More powerful antioxidant than vitamin C or vitamin E alone." — True in radical-chemistry assays (beat Trolox & vitamin C) [1] — but that's in vitro / in injected animals, not "more powerful on your face."
  • "Clinically proven anti-aging / boosts collagen / reduces wrinkles." — Unsupported for EPC. No human topical RCT; collagen claims are borrowed from MAP/ascorbic acid. Overreach.
  • "Backed by dozens of studies." — Technically dozens of EPC-K1 papers exist — but they are ischemia-reperfusion pharmacology, not skincare. Classic borrowed-format/borrowed-context inflation.
  • "Treats acne / fades dark spots." — anti-C. acnes and melanin claims are in-vitro/cousin-molecule; not EPC human topical data; and "treats" is a drug claim.
  • What is NOT hype: it is a genuinely stable, water-soluble, well-tolerated, dual-vitamin antioxidant that solves a real formulation problem and has airtight mechanistic antioxidant credentials. That is a real, sellable — if modest — value proposition.

11. Verdict for the brand

  • ⚠️ SUPPORTING / CONDITIONAL — not a hero. EPC is a smart, premium, stable way to put "vitamin C + vitamin E" into a single water-phase, near-neutral-pH, low-irritation system. Its mechanistic antioxidant case is excellent and its safety is clean. But the human topical skin-efficacy proof for this exact molecule is missing, and the impressive-sounding literature is internal-organ pharmacology. It cannot honestly anchor an "anti-wrinkle/collagen" hero claim.
  • Where it fits: a supporting antioxidant in a serum/essence or in the barrier-repair cream's water phase — sold honestly as "stable vitamin C + E antioxidant defense," paired with the brand's better-evidenced actives (niacinamide, ceramides, peptides) that carry the real anti-aging claims.
  • Condition for use: (1) keep claims at appearance/antioxidant level — no collagen/wrinkle/acne efficacy claims; (2) secure CoA + ideally a second source given single-originator supply; (3) treat the "premium dual-vitamin molecule" angle as a brand-story/sensory differentiator, not a clinical one.
  • If a cheaper, better-evidenced path is acceptable: SAP or MAP (vitamin-C-phosphate, gentle, near-neutral pH, more skin data) + a tocopherol delivers ~80% of the category benefit at lower cost and with more dermatology literature. Choose EPC specifically when the single-molecule C+E stability/story is the point.

One-line reasoning: Real chemistry, real safety, real stability — but the skin efficacy is borrowed and manufacturer-asserted, so use EPC as an honest "stable C+E antioxidant" supporting player, never as a proven anti-aging hero.

Longevity-relevance note. Conceptually on-thesis: oxidative stress and lipid peroxidation are bona-fide hallmarks of skin aging, and EPC-K1 is one of the better lipid-radical scavengers in the vitamin toolkit [1]. That makes it a defensible ingredient in an "oxidative-defense / barrier-lipid-protection" longevity narrative — provided the story stays at "antioxidant defense for the look of skin" and resists the temptation to import the dramatic organ-protection pharmacology into cosmetic claims. The credibility risk (over-claiming from animal I/R data) is exactly where a science-led brand would lose trust.

Main caveats (what keeps this at D, not higher)

  1. No human topical data of any kind — not just no RCT, but no human topical study at all (no irritation, penetration, or open-label efficacy study on EPC the molecule). The entire skin case is mechanism + animal pharmacology + supplier/patent assertion.
  2. Borrowed-format inflation is the signature risk. The "dozens of studies" / "outperforms Trolox" / "protects brain, heart, liver" evidence is injected or oral animal ischemia-reperfusion pharmacology. It proves the molecule is a potent systemic antioxidant; it says nothing about a cream on a face. The dossier flags this correctly and repeatedly — keep that discipline.
  3. Single source for the flagship antioxidant claim. The headline "beats Trolox and vitamin C" rests on one 1999 radical-chemistry paper (Wei et al.). It is real and rigorous, but it is one paper, in vitro/animal — not an independently replicated, skin-relevant result.
  4. Supplier-funded / single-originator footprint. The skin narrative traces to the manufacturer (Senju/Iwase) and its patents, and the one cell study that used the actual cosmetic EPC [5] used it as a tool reagent in hamster sebocytes. The independent, disinterested skin literature on EPC is effectively empty.
  5. Penetration unproven; patent uses cyclodextrin to fix it. The topical patent [6] adds cyclodextrin specifically to improve EPC-K's skin penetration — an implicit concession that unaided dermal delivery is a limitation. No human skin penetration/bioconversion data for EPC was found.
  6. pH/activation behavior is inferred, not measured for EPC specifically (now flagged as such in §6).

Defensible claim ceiling

The strongest honest claim for EPC on skin remains: "a stable, water-soluble form of vitamin C and vitamin E that provides antioxidant defense for the look of skin against environmental/oxidative stress," plus the true formulation fact that it "delivers vitamin C and vitamin E together from one stable system." Anything implying clinical anti-aging, collagen, acne, or pigment efficacy for this molecule is unsubstantiated.
