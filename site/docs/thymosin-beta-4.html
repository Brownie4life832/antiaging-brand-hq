Ingredient: Thymosin Beta-4 (TB4 / TB-500) (INCI: sh-Oligopeptide-4)

Slug: thymosin-beta-4 · Class: Growth factors, cytokines, exosomes, conditioned media & regenerative Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product

How to read this dossier. This is an evidence-graded literature review, not marketing copy. The core is the tiered evidence review (§4): in-vitro → animal → human oral/systemic → human topical. The single most important honest finding up front: Thymosin Beta-4 (Tβ4) has a deep, decades-old regenerative-biology pedigree (actin sequestration, angiogenesis, cell migration, anti-inflammation) and real human topical RCT data — but almost entirely on the OCULAR SURFACE (eye drops) and on chronic WOUNDS, not on cosmetic facial-skin aging. There is, to date, no peer-reviewed RCT showing that a topical Tβ4 cosmetic reduces facial wrinkles in normal aging skin. On top of that, Tβ4 is a ~4.9 kDa, 43-amino-acid protein — roughly 10× the conventional ~500 Da ceiling for passive skin permeation — so a plain cream faces a serious delivery problem. And the popular "TB-500" form is a research-chemical / WADA-banned injectable, not a cosmetic ingredient. Treat regenerative pedigree, clinical wound/eye efficacy, and cosmetic anti-aging efficacy as three separate questions.

1. Identity

  • Common names: Thymosin Beta-4; Tβ4; TB4; Tβ-4. The injectable/research-chemical fragment is sold as "TB-500" (often conflated with full-length Tβ4 — see below).
  • What it is: A naturally occurring, highly conserved 43-amino-acid, ~4.9 kDa (4963 Da) polypeptide, the major G-actin–sequestering peptide in mammalian cells. First isolated from calf thymus (1981); ubiquitous in body fluids (wound fluid, tears, saliva, plasma) and cytoplasm. N-terminal residue is acetyl-blocked [1][7][13].
  • INCI (cosmetic form): sh-Oligopeptide-4 — "synthetic-human oligopeptide-4," a recombinant human Tβ4 produced by fermentation in E. coli (a synthesized copy of the human Tβ4 gene). CAS 77642-24-1. This is the form that legally appears on cosmetic ingredient lists [11][13].
  • Key forms / derivatives (do not treat as interchangeable):
  • sh-Oligopeptide-4 (recombinant full-length Tβ4) — the cosmetic/topical workhorse; e.g., supplier ingredients such as Caregen's "CG-Thymosin-β4" [12][13].
  • RGN-259 — pharmaceutical 0.1% Tβ4 ophthalmic solution (eye drops); the source of the best human topical RCT data (dry eye, neurotrophic keratopathy) [3][4][5].
  • RGN-137 — pharmaceutical topical dermal gel of Tβ4 (chronic wounds, epidermolysis bullosa) [8][9][14].
  • RGN-352 — injectable Tβ4 (cardiac/CNS indications; not topical) [14].
  • TB-500 — a synthetic actin-binding fragment, acetyl-LKKTETQ (residues ~17–23 of Tβ4), sold as a grey-market injectable "peptide." It is NOT a cosmetic, NOT full-length Tβ4, NOT FDA-approved, and is WADA-banned. Much consumer "TB-500 = anti-aging" hype borrows from full-length Tβ4 science it does not own (§4, §8, §10) [6][10].

2. Class & Mechanism

Tβ4 is a regenerative / repair signaling peptide whose "day job" is intracellular actin regulation, but which "moonlights" extracellularly as a wound-repair and angiogenic factor [1][7]. Mechanisms are well-characterized in cell and animal models (mechanism evidence is strong; whether each translates to visible topical cosmetic benefit in human facial skin is a separate, much weaker question):

  • G-actin sequestration / cytoskeletal dynamics. Tβ4 is the major monomeric (G-)actin–binding peptide; it modulates actin polymerization, enabling cell motility and migration — central to re-epithelialization [1][7].
  • Cell migration / re-epithelialization. Promotes migration of keratinocytes, endothelial cells, fibroblasts, accelerating wound closure [2][7][9].
  • Angiogenesis. A defined 7-amino-acid actin-binding motif drives endothelial migration, tube formation, and aortic-ring sprouting — Tβ4 is a genuine pro-angiogenic peptide [7][16].
  • Anti-inflammatory. Directly targets the NF-κB RelA/p65 subunit, blocking TNF-α–induced nuclear translocation and downstream IL-8 — and this anti-inflammatory action is independent of its actin binding [15]. Reduces inflammatory cytokine load in repair models.
  • Anti-apoptotic & anti-fibrotic. Inhibits apoptosis, modulates cytokines (including pruritogenic/itch-related ones), and reduces scarring/fibrosis in repair models [2][9].
  • Collagen deposition & ECM remodeling. In wound models, Tβ4 increases collagen deposition and granulation tissue [2][9][17].
  • Stem/progenitor cell activation. Mobilizes progenitor cells; in skin specifically, activates hair-follicle bulge stem cells (a stem-cell niche) [18][19].
  • "Reactivating developmental programs." A speculative, animal-model-grade hypothesis (largely cardiac) that Tβ4 can re-engage embryonic repair programs and thereby "reverse aging processes" [20]. This is a hypothesis from mouse cardiac biology, not a demonstrated human skin-aging mechanism.

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (core of this dossier)

Reading guide: Benefits proven for topical human use are confined to the human-topical tier. Critically, the human-topical evidence is on the OCULAR SURFACE (eye drops) and CHRONIC WOUNDS — not on cosmetic facial-skin aging. Everything in the in-vitro and animal tiers is mechanism/other-format evidence; the systemic/injectable tier (TB-500, RGN-352) is a different format entirely and must not be presented as proof of a topical cosmetic result.

Tier 1 — In-vitro (deep, well-replicated mechanism; least relevant to consumer cosmetic claims)

  • Qiu, Sosne et al. 2011, FASEB J 25(6):1815–1826 (PMC3101037) — Multiple epithelial/corneal cell lines: Tβ4 directly targets NF-κB RelA/p65, blocks TNF-α–induced nuclear translocation and IL-8 promoter activation; anti-inflammatory effect is independent of actin binding. Strong, mechanistically clean anti-inflammatory data — but in vitro, ocular/epithelial cells [15]. DOI 10.1096/fj.10-167940.
  • Philp/Kleinman-era cell work (reviewed in [1][2][7]) — Tβ4 promotes keratinocyte, endothelial and fibroblast migration; the 7-aa actin-binding motif is required for angiogenic activity [16].
  • Limitation across tier: cultured cells under controlled doses; migration/angiogenesis/anti-inflammation in a dish do not predict visible facial-skin anti-aging from a cream.

Tier 2 — Animal

  • Malinda, Kleinman et al. 1999, J Invest Dermatol 113(3):364–368 — Foundational dermal study. Rat full-thickness punch-wound model; Tβ4 applied topically or intraperitoneally. Increased re-epithelialization by 42% at day 4 and up to 61% at day 7 vs saline; wounds contracted ≥11% more than controls by day 7; increased collagen deposition and angiogenesis [2]. Strong, frequently-cited — but it is a wound-healing model in rat skin, not cosmetic aging in human skin.
  • Diabetic / aged / burn animal models (reviewed in Kleinman & Sosne 2016 [17]) — Tβ4 increases dermal healing rate across impaired-healing models, including aged and diabetic animals, and is active in burns.
  • Hair growth — Philp et al. 2004, FASEB J (10.1096/fj.03-0244fje) and Philp et al. 2007, Ann NY Acad Sci 1112:95 — Tβ4 increases hair growth in rats/mice (incl. a Tβ4-overexpressing transgenic mouse) via follicle bulge stem-cell activation, migration, differentiation, and protease production [18][19].
  • Limitation: rat/mouse skin ≠ human skin (penetration & follicle biology differ); wound/hair models are not cosmetic anti-aging endpoints.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL / SYSTEMIC / INJECTABLE (not a cosmetic format)

  • Oral: essentially absent and biologically implausible for an intact 4.9 kDa peptide (would be digested). No credible human oral RCT for skin.
  • Injectable "TB-500": sold widely as a grey-market peptide for recovery/anti-aging. Zero published human efficacy RCTs. Not FDA-approved; classified an FDA 503A Category 2 bulk drug substance (barred from pharmacy compounding); WADA-prohibited at all times (the cited BSCG source lists it under the S0 non-approved-substances category, not a specified class) [6][10]. RGN-352 (injectable Tβ4, cardiac/CNS) reached Phase 2 but was placed on clinical hold (manufacturing compliance), 2011 [14]. None of this transfers to a topical cosmetic claim.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters — but note WHERE it applies)

4a. Ocular-surface RCTs (RGN-259 eye drops) — the strongest human topical Tβ4 data, but mucosal, not facial skin: - Sosne, Dunn & Kim 2015, Cornea (NCT01393132) — Phase 2, multicenter, randomized, double-masked, placebo-controlled, severe dry eye. Small: 9 patients (12 treatment eyes / 6 control eyes), 0.1% RGN-259, 6×/day for 28 days. At day 56: −35.1% ocular discomfort (p=0.0141) and −59.1% total corneal fluorescein staining (p=0.0108) vs vehicle; improved tear breakup time/volume. Safe/well tolerated [4]. Limitation: tiny n; eye-surface (corneal epithelium), not skin. - Sosne et al. 2022 (SEER-1), Int J Mol Sci — Phase 3, randomized, double-masked, placebo-controlled, 13 US sites, neurotrophic keratopathy. 18 subjects (10 Tβ4 / 8 placebo), 0.1% RGN-259. Complete healing of persistent epithelial defects at day 29: 6/10 (60%) vs 1/8 (12.5%), p=0.0656 (narrowly missed); by day 43 5/10 (50%) vs 0/8, p=0.0359 (significant); significant symptom improvements; only 1 treatment-related AE [5]. Limitation: small; mucosal/corneal, not facial skin; primary timepoint missed significance. A separate Phase 2 dry-eye CAE-model study (n=72) and Nature Sci Rep model work support the program [3][16].

4b. Chronic-wound / dermal trials (RGN-137 gel) — real skin, but disease wounds and mostly SAFETY wins: - Phase 2 pressure-ulcer trial (completed ~2009) — RGN-137 gel (0.01% / 0.02–0.03% / 0.1%). Met the primary objective (safety/tolerability) but did not demonstrate a significant increase in complete wound closure vs placebo over 84 days; a trend favored the mid dose [9][14]. - Phase 2 venous-stasis-ulcer trial (completed ~2009) — Safe/well tolerated (primary objective); the mid-dose appeared most active with a trend to faster initiation of healing, but no clear efficacy win [9][14]. - European venous-ulcer study — Guarnera et al. 2010, Int Wound J (PMID 17495250) — prospective randomized safety/tolerability/healing-enhancement study; supportive of safety, modest healing signal [21]. - Phase 2 epidermolysis bullosa (EB) trial (dosed 2019, single-blinded) — case reports of complete wound healing in early enrolled EB patients; EB is an orphan disease, small/uncontrolled reporting [8][14]. - Honest read of the dermal tier: across the chronic-wound program, Tβ4 gel was consistently safe, with suggestive but not robust efficacy — the controlled pressure-ulcer and venous-stasis trials did not clearly beat placebo on their healing endpoints.

4c. Cosmetic facial-aging studies (the format the brand actually needs) — weak and largely supplier-generated: - The cosmetic anti-aging story for sh-Oligopeptide-4 rests on supplier/manufacturer materials and open-label studies, not peer-reviewed RCTs. The most concrete example surfaced is an open-label (uncontrolled) study, N=32, ages 45–65, reporting improvements in hydration, firmness, elasticity, wrinkling, pore size [12][13]. No randomized, placebo-controlled, peer-reviewed facial-wrinkle trial of a topical Tβ4 cosmetic was located.

Honest topical bottom line. Proven for topical human use: Tβ4 (as 0.1% RGN-259 eye drops) improves ocular-surface healing and dry-eye symptoms in small RCTs, and (as RGN-137 gel) is safe on chronic wounds with suggestive healing signals. Not proven: that a topical cosmetic reduces facial-skin aging/wrinkles in normal skin — that claim rests on animal wound data, mechanism, and uncontrolled supplier studies. The strongest evidence is for the wrong tissue (cornea) or wrong condition (disease wounds) relative to a face cream.

5. Topical Bioavailability / Penetration

This is Tβ4's decisive cosmetic weakness — bigger than for most peptides.

  • Tβ4 is a ~4.9 kDa (4963 Da), 43-amino-acid protein. The stratum corneum obstructs >90% of macromolecules above ~500 Da; Tβ4 exceeds that ceiling by roughly 10×. Intact, healthy skin is therefore expected to absorb very little intact Tβ4 from a conventional cream [22][23]. (This is fundamentally different from small "signal peptides" like tripeptides; Tβ4 is a protein, closer to a growth factor in size.)
  • Why the clinical wins are where they are: the ocular surface (corneal/conjunctival epithelium, no stratum corneum) and open/chronic wounds (barrier already breached) are exactly the contexts where a large peptide can reach its targets. On intact facial skin, the same molecule faces the full barrier.
  • Delivery research confirms the problem and the fix:
  • Fu et al. 2019, Int J Nanomed (PMC6885565) — explicitly frames Tβ4's 4963 Da size as "much larger than" what passive transdermal delivery suits; an ethosomal gel (phospholipid + 20–50% ethanol) raised cumulative release to 1.67× plain Tβ4 gel and halved wound-healing time in vivo [23].
  • Microneedle-loaded Tβ4 systems are in active development for the same reason (creating microchannels to bypass the barrier) [24].
  • Implication: A plain aqueous/cream Tβ4 ("sh-Oligopeptide-4") serum likely delivers little intact peptide into living facial skin. Any serious topical use should assume an encapsulation / penetration-enhancement / device-assisted strategy — and even then, cosmetic efficacy is unproven. Supplier claims of "excellent skin permeation due to low molecular weight" are misleading (4.9 kDa is not low for skin permeation) [12][13].

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: No established cosmetic efficacy concentration. Patent/cosmetic literature cites topical Tβ4 ranges around ~1 ng/mL to ~10 µg/mL in older skin-care filings — i.e., extremely low loadings [25]. Suppliers sell sh-Oligopeptide-4 as a potent low-percentage active. Because penetration is the gating issue, "more %" does not straightforwardly mean "more effect."
  • pH: Best near skin-physiological ~5.5–6.5; as a protein, Tβ4 is sensitive to extremes of pH and to denaturation — avoid low-pH (strong acid) and high-pH systems in the same phase.
  • Stability: Native Tβ4 is biologically unstable; the recombinant cosmetic form (sh-Oligopeptide-4) is positioned as more stable, but it remains a peptide/protein — vulnerable to heat, proteases/microbial contamination, oxidation, and freeze-thaw. Needs robust preservation, cool storage, and ideally encapsulation. Treat shelf-life conservatively.
  • Pairings (sensible): humectants (HA, glycerin, panthenol), barrier lipids/ceramides, niacinamide, and non-acidic soothing actives (centella, beta-glucan). A "repair/recovery" framing (post-procedure, barrier support) fits its biology better than a "wrinkle eraser" framing.
  • Conflicts: Strong exfoliating acids (AHA/BHA) and low-pH vitamin C in the same layer/product (pH stress + protease/oxidation risk to the protein); high-heat processing. No unique chemical incompatibility beyond "it's a fragile protein — don't abuse it." Sequence away from harsh actives.
  • Sensory: Water-phase, typically odorless/colorless at use levels; easy to slot into serums, eye products, post-procedure gels.

7. Safety

  • Overall: Reassuringly clean across formats. Across eye-drop RCTs (RGN-259) and dermal-wound trials (RGN-137), Tβ4 was safe and well tolerated, with few/no treatment-related adverse events [4][5][9][21]. Skin-irritation testing of an ethosomal Tβ4 gel showed no adverse reactions — but in rabbit skin (Fu et al. 2019), not human; do not present this as human irritation data [23].
  • Irritation/sensitization: Low expected irritancy; as a recombinant human-identical protein, it is generally non-irritating at cosmetic levels. As with any peptide/protein, theoretical sensitization is possible — patch test for sensitive skin.
  • Angiogenesis caution (the meaningful one): Tβ4 is pro-angiogenic and promotes cell proliferation/migration. For a topical cosmetic on intact skin this is low-risk, but it underpins legitimate caution about systemic/injectable use and about use over undiagnosed lesions or active malignancy — a reason to keep cosmetic claims modest and to avoid "wound/regeneration" positioning that invites medical use. (Conversely, some research notes Tβ4's complex role in tumor biology — another reason not to overhype "regeneration.")
  • Sun sensitivity: No evidence of photosensitization. (Pair any anti-aging routine with SPF regardless — sun is the #1 driver of photoaging.)
  • Pregnancy/nursing: No specific topical data. Given its pro-angiogenic, regenerative signaling, use conservative copy and defer to a clinician — do not make pregnancy-safety claims without review.
  • Sourcing/biological-provenance note: The cosmetic form is recombinant (E. coli fermentation) — human-identical sequence, no animal-tissue provenance, an advantage over animal-derived growth factors. Supplier QC must document purity, endotoxin (LPS), and host-cell-protein levels (recombinant proteins from E. coli carry endotoxin risk if poorly purified).
  • The "TB-500" hazard: The injectable fragment is an unapproved research chemical with grey-market sourcing, no human efficacy trials, and a WADA ban. The brand must keep cosmetic sh-Oligopeptide-4 rigorously distinct from "TB-500," in both formulation and messaging [6][10].

8. Regulatory & Claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic status: sh-Oligopeptide-4 is an established cosmetic skin-conditioning ingredient (INCI assigned; CAS 77642-24-1) used in marketed serums/creams/eye products [11][12][13]. As with all cosmetics, each finished formula still needs its own safety assessment (EU Reg. EC 1223/2009; US fairness-to-claims rules).
  • Drug status of the therapeutic forms: RGN-259 (eye drops), RGN-137 (wound gel), RGN-352 (injectable) are investigational drugs — their wound-healing/ocular data are drug data, generated for disease indications, and cannot be imported into cosmetic copy. TB-500 is an unapproved drug substance (FDA 503A Category 2) and WADA-prohibited [6][10][14].
  • The cosmetic-vs-drug line (the central tension): Tβ4's entire scientific story — "wound healing," "regeneration," "re-epithelialization," "angiogenesis," "tissue repair," "reverses aging" — is structure/function / drug language. This is precisely the ingredient where the strongest "science" is the least usable in compliant cosmetic copy.
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "supports skin's natural recovery and renewal processes," "helps comfort and condition stressed or post-procedure skin," "supports the look of resilient, healthy skin," "a recombinant peptide studied for its role in skin repair biology."
  • ⚠️ Risky / drug-like — needs substantiation or legal review: "heals," "repairs wounds," "regenerates skin/tissue," "accelerates healing," "boosts collagen," "stimulates angiogenesis," "reverses aging," "grows hair," any "X% wrinkle reduction" (no cosmetic RCT supports this), and anything referencing the eye-drop/wound-trial efficacy as if it were a cosmetic result. Do not reference "TB-500."

9. Sourcing & Cost

  • Cost tier: Mid-to-high. As a recombinant protein (vs a chemically synthesized short peptide), sh-Oligopeptide-4 is costlier and more QC-intensive than commodity peptides like GHK-Cu or matrixyl peptides — though far cheaper and more scalable than exosomes or growth-factor secretomes.
  • Supply: Available from specialty cosmetic-peptide/biotech suppliers (e.g., Caregen CG-Thymosin-β4) [12]. Fewer suppliers than commodity peptides; verify a documented cosmetic (not research-grade) supply chain.
  • Provenance & QC: Recombinant, human-identical, no animal tissue — clean ethical story. Demand endotoxin, host-cell-protein, purity, and stability data; recombinant E. coli proteins require rigorous purification for cosmetic safety. Avoid any supplier blurring cosmetic sh-Oligopeptide-4 with "TB-500" research material.

10. Hype vs. Evidence

Where marketing overreaches the data:

  1. "Clinically proven regenerative anti-aging peptide." The real RCTs are eye drops for dry eye/keratopathy and gels for chronic wounds — different tissue, different (disease) indication. No facial-aging cosmetic RCT exists. "Clinically proven" for a face cream is unsupported [4][5][9].
  2. "Heals and regenerates skin." Borrowed from rat wound models (Malinda 1999) and disease-wound drug trials — and it is drug-claim language, not a cosmetic claim [2][9].
  3. "TB-500 anti-aging / longevity." Conflates an unapproved, WADA-banned injectable fragment with full-length Tβ4 cosmetic science. The injectable has zero human efficacy trials [6][10].
  4. "Excellent skin penetration (low molecular weight)." False framing. At ~4.9 kDa, Tβ4 is ~10× over the practical skin-permeation ceiling; penetration is its biggest weakness and typically requires encapsulation/microneedles [22][23].
  5. "Reverses aging by reactivating youth programs." A mouse-cardiac hypothesis [20], not a demonstrated human skin mechanism.
  6. "Boosts collagen / grows hair." Collagen and hair data are animal/wound-model (Malinda; Philp), not human cosmetic outcomes [2][18][19].

What's genuinely good: a deep, decades-long regenerative-biology pedigree; clean mechanism (actin, migration, angiogenesis, NF-κB anti-inflammation); real human RCT data proving safety and ocular/wound efficacy; recombinant, animal-free, human-identical sourcing; and a credible "repair/recovery biology" narrative — if claims stay modest.

11. Verdict for the Brand

⚠️ Conditional (lean toward skip for a hero role; usable only as a niche supporting/"recovery" active with smart delivery and disciplined claims).

One-line reasoning: Tβ4 has an impressive regenerative pedigree and genuine human RCT safety/efficacy data — but on the cornea and on chronic wounds, not on aging facial skin, and as a ~4.9 kDa protein it barely penetrates intact skin, so a plain cosmetic likely under-delivers while the only honestly usable claims ("supports recovery/renewal") sit one word away from drug territory. The evidence-to-cost-to-claims ratio is worse than the brand's better-supported actives (retinoids, niacinamide, GHK-Cu, ceramides).

If pursued at all, conditions are strict: (a) formulate for penetration (encapsulation/ethosomes, or pair with a device/post-procedure protocol) — a plain serum is mechanistically weak; (b) position as post-procedure / barrier-recovery / soothing support (where its migration + anti-inflammatory + safety story is most defensible), not as a wrinkle hero; (c) hold claims to "supports/visibly/appearance of recovery & renewal," never import eye-drop/wound-trial efficacy, collagen %s, hair claims, or anything referencing "TB-500"; (d) demand recombinant-cosmetic-grade QC (endotoxin/purity).

Longevity-relevance note: Tβ4's longevity framing is mechanistically intriguing but topically unproven for skin — it belongs to the regenerative-aesthetics conversation (a narrative bridge), not to the brand's demonstrated-intervention shortlist. Best treated as a watch-list / optional supporting recovery active, secondary to ingredients with real cosmetic human data. Evidence verification (adversarial)

Independent adversarial re-check, 2026-06-19. Primary citations re-fetched and cross-checked against PubMed/PMC and the cited sources.

Overall grade: D for the use the brand actually needs (topical cosmetic facial anti-aging). There is no human, topical, cosmetic RCT showing a Tβ4 product reduces facial wrinkles or otherwise rejuvenates normal aging skin. The only cosmetic-facing human data is an uncontrolled, open-label, supplier-generated N=32 study — that is hype-adjacent, not evidence. The dossier already states this clearly and earns its grade by not overclaiming. - For the adjacent, non-cosmetic uses the molecule actually has data on, the grade is higher and the dossier is careful to wall them off: ocular-surface dry eye / neurotrophic keratopathy (eye drops): B− (genuine randomized, double-masked, placebo-controlled trials, but tiny n=9 and n=18, wrong tissue — cornea, not skin); chronic dermal wounds (gel): C (real skin, but disease wounds, and the controlled pressure-ulcer / venous-stasis trials hit only safety and did not clearly beat placebo on healing).

Citation audit (claims checked against the actual sources): - [4] Sosne/Dunn/Kim 2015, Cornea (dry eye): Real. Phase 2, multicenter, randomized, double-masked, placebo-controlled; n=9 patients; 0.1% RGN-259 6×/day; −35.1% discomfort, −59.1% corneal staining. Core figures verified. (The exact "12 vs 6 eyes" split and the p=0.0141 / p=0.0108 values could not be re-confirmed from the abstract-level sources fetched, but the headline magnitudes and design are correct.) Honestly framed as tiny and ocular. - [5] Sosne 2022, SEER-1, Int J Mol Sci (neurotrophic keratopathy): Real and accurately quoted — Phase 3, n=10 Tβ4 / 8 placebo; day-29 healing 60% vs 12.5% p=0.0656 (missed); day-43 50% vs 0% p=0.0359 (significant). Dossier correctly flags the primary-timepoint miss. - [2] Malinda/Kleinman 1999, J Invest Dermatol: Real and accurately quoted — rat full-thickness wound, topical or IP, +42% re-epi day 4 / +61% day 7, ≥11% more contraction, more collagen + angiogenesis. Correctly labeled animal/wound, not aging. - [15] Qiu/Sosne 2011, FASEB J: Real and accurate — Tβ4 targets NF-κB RelA/p65, suppresses IL-8, effect actin-independent; in vitro, ocular/epithelial cells. - [20] Bock-Marquette 2023, Int Immunopharmacol: Real — a review/hypothesis built on mouse cardiac models and systemic injection. Dossier's "mouse-cardiac hypothesis, not human skin" framing is exactly right. - [22][23] Penetration / MW: Verified — Tβ4 = 4963 Da, described in the literature as far above the practical passive-permeation range; Fu et al. 2019 ethosomal gel gave 1.67× release and ~half healing time in vivo. The "excellent skin penetration (low MW)" supplier claim is correctly called false. - [6] TB-500 status: Verified as FDA 503A Category 2 and WADA-prohibited at all times; the fragment Ac-LKKTETQ (residues 17–23) identification is correct.

Corrections made to the dossier in this pass: 1. §7 Safety said "Human skin-irritation testing of an ethosomal Tβ4 gel showed no adverse reactions [23]." Fu et al. 2019 tested rabbit skin, not human — corrected so it is not presented as human data. 2. §4 / §7 / ref [6] said TB-500 is "WADA-prohibited … (S2) since 2011." The cited source lists it under S0 (non-approved substances), and the "since 2011" date was not supported there — softened to "prohibited at all times (S0)" without the unsupported class/date.

Caveats / claims to keep softened (already handled well in the dossier, flagged here for emphasis): - Never let the eye-drop or wound-trial efficacy ride into cosmetic copy — different tissue, different (disease) indication, investigational-drug data. - The N=32 supplier study is the only cosmetic-facial human data and is open-label/uncontrolled — it cannot support any "clinically proven," wrinkle-%, firmness, or pore claims. - The RCTs that do exist are n=9 and n=18 — small even for their ocular indications; the dry-eye Phase 2 is physician-sponsored. - "Reverses aging," "regenerates," "boosts collagen," "grows hair" remain animal/hypothesis-grade and drug-claim language — keep to "supports the look of recovery/renewal."

Bottom line: The dossier is intellectually honest and does not overstate — its self-assessment as "conditional, lean-skip, penetration-gated, claims one word from drug territory" is correct and well-evidenced. The two factual slips (human vs rabbit; WADA S2 vs S0) are now fixed. Verdict stands: strong regenerative pedigree, real but off-target human RCT data, and D-grade evidence for the cosmetic facial-aging use the brand would actually market.
