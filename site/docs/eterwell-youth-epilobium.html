Ingredient: Eterwell Youth (alpine willowherb) — Botanical Senolytic (INCI: Epilobium Fleischeri Leaf/Stem Extract)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Senotherapeutics & senolytics (botanical / plant-derived) · Slug: eterwell-youth-epilobium

Exhaustive literature review. Brutally honest headline: the entire senolytic efficacy story for this ingredient (p16/p21 drop, +44% collagen, "years younger," senescent-cell clearance via BCL-2/apoptosis) lives exclusively in DSM-Firmenich's own patent and marketing dossier. As of 2026-06 there is no independent, peer-reviewed publication of the senolytic claims — not even a company-authored journal paper. The only peer-reviewed study on Epilobium fleischeri itself (Sfriso et al., 2022) is about sebum/5-α-reductase and the skin microbiome, not senescence, and is also DSM-authored. Treat every "clears zombie cells / reverses skin age" line as supplier marketing-grade, single-source until independently replicated. (Contrast with senolytics-os01-peptide.md, where at least the in-vitro/ex-vivo mechanism is published in npj Aging.)

1. Identity

  • Common / trade name: Eterwell Youth (also styled ETERWELL™ YOUTH), a branded cosmetic active from dsm-firmenich (the franchise originated with Mibelle Biochemistry, the Swiss alpine-botanical house DSM absorbed). Botanical common name: alpine willowherb (a.k.a. Fleischer's willowherb). [1][2][5]
  • INCI: Epilobium Fleischeri Leaf/Stem Extract (commonly listed with water and/or glycerin + a preservative system as the carrier). [1][3][5]
  • Botanical source: Epilobium fleischeri Hochst., family Onagraceae (the willowherb/evening-primrose family). Modern taxonomy reclassifies it as Chamaenerion fleischeri (syn. Chamerion fleischeri) — a rare pioneer plant endemic to the Alps (France, Switzerland, Italy, Germany, Austria) that colonises glacier moraines, alluvium and stony screes at ~600–2,700 m. DSM cultivates it organically in a Swiss "ancient glacier moraine" habitat. [1][2][6]
  • Standardisation: to flavonoids, calculated as myricitrin. [1][5]
  • Key actives / phytochemistry: Epilobium species are characterised by flavonol glycosides (myricitrin, isomyricitrin, quercitrin, isoquercitrin) and the macrocyclic ellagitannin oenothein B — the latter being the genus's signature polyphenol and a known 5-α-reductase inhibitor and antioxidant/anti-inflammatory. [4][7][8][9]
  • Key forms / derivatives: sold as a single standardised liquid extract (water/glycerin base). No ester/salt derivatives. The senolytic positioning is specific to E. fleischeri; do not conflate with the much more commonly studied fireweed / Epilobium angustifolium (a different species with its own — separate — fibroblast/photoprotection literature; see §4 Tier 1 note). [10]

Nomenclature caution: "Eterwell Youth" (brand) = Epilobium fleischeri extract (INCI) = alpine willowherb (botanical). It is not the same as E. angustifolium (fireweed), and it is not the OS-01/Decapeptide-52 peptide. Three different "senolytic/senotherapeutic" actives are now in this research base — keep them distinct.

2. Class & mechanism

Positioned as a botanical senolytic — the plant-derived flagship of the "skin longevity / war on senescence" ingredient wave. Unlike OS-01 (which its own makers call a senomorphic), DSM claims Eterwell Youth is a true senolytic: it is said to selectively kill and clear senescent cells, not merely quiet them. [2][11]

Proposed mechanism (per DSM's patent + marketing): - Selective senolysis of senescent dermal fibroblasts. In a mixed culture of normal + senescent fibroblasts, the extract preferentially reduces the senescent fraction. [3][5] - Pro-apoptotic switch: it is claimed to down-regulate the anti-apoptotic protein BCL-2 in senescent cells, tipping them toward intrinsic (mitochondrial) apoptosis and physiological clearance. [5][11] (This is the same BCL-2-family axis exploited by the reference pharmacological senolytic navitoclax/ABT-263, which DSM used as the positive control in its patent assay. [3]) - Marker reduction: ex-vivo, senescence markers p16 (CDKN2A) and p21 (CDKN1A) are reported to fall. [2][5] - SASP / inflammaging reduction: in vitro, IL-1 and other senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) factors are reportedly reduced. [5][11] - "Replacement with high-performing cells": with the senescent burden lowered, the remaining/younger fibroblast pool is said to restore collagen output. [1][5]

Net claimed cell-level story: kill the dermo-destructive "zombie" fibroblasts → drop the inflammatory SASP → let the healthy fibroblast pool rebuild collagen/ECM → visibly "younger" skin. Mechanistically coherent and aligned with mainstream senolytic theory — but, crucially, the causal final step (senescent-cell removal → better human skin) is not established for this ingredient, and is itself unproven in human skin generally (§4, §10). [12]

4. Evidence review by tier ← core of this dossier

Overarching honesty flag (read first): the senescence/senolytic efficacy package is single-source and largely grey-literature — a patent (WO2024149898A1, DSM IP Assets B.V., inventor Leithe Budel, priority 13 Jan 2023) [3] plus DSM's product page, technical brochures and trade-press write-ups. [1][2][5][11] The PDF technical deck is authored by the same inventor (Leithe.Budel@dsm.com) [internal metadata of supplier PDF, [13]]. There is, as of 2026-06, no peer-reviewed journal publication of the senolytic data, and no independent (non-DSM) replication. The numbers below are therefore supplier-reported; sample sizes for the in-vitro/ex-vivo work are mostly not disclosed.

Tier 1 — In-vitro & ex-vivo (where ALL the senolytic data live)

(a) Patent assay — selective senolysis of fibroblasts. [3] - Model: mixed culture of ~70% normal + ~30% senescent human dermal fibroblasts; treated ≥72 h; senescence read out as SA-β-gal via flow cytometry; navitoclax (ABT-263) as positive senolytic control. - Result: E. fleischeri extract significantly reduced the senescent-cell fraction, clearing the patent's preferred ≥20–30% reduction threshold, whereas four other alpine botanicals tested (Artemisia umbelliformis, Leontopodium alpinum / edelweiss, Eryngium alpinum, Asparagus officinalis) showed no senolytic effect — i.e., the activity is specific to this species, not generic "plant antioxidant." - Effective range: 0.1–3 wt% in formulation; preferred composition = 5–10% dry extract matter in a glycerin-rich carrier. - Limitation: patent-grade reporting; no peer review; replicate counts/statistics not in the public text.

(b) Marketing-reported in-vitro / ex-vivo figures (DSM product page + brochures): [1][5][11] - Senescent-cell reduction: −52% in vitro at 0.03% Eterwell Youth. - Collagen: +44% ex vivo at 1% (skin explant assay). - p16 and p21: reduced ex vivo (no magnitude given in public sources). - IL-1 / SASP: reduced in vitro. - Mechanism marker: ↓ BCL-2 in senescent cells → intrinsic apoptosis. - Limitations: explant/cell assays, no published methods, single source, sample sizes undisclosed.

Species-confusion caveat (important): the frequently-cited fibroblast paper "Epilobium angustifolium extract demonstrates multiple effects on dermal fibroblasts in vitro and skin photo-protection in vivo" (Ruszová et al., 2013, Gen. Physiol. Biophys./related) is for fireweed (E. angustifolium), a different species, and concerns MMP/collagen/photoprotection — not senolysis and not E. fleischeri. [10] Note a subtlety: that paper does engage senescence — it reports the E. angustifolium extract rescuing/reversing the senescent response of fibroblasts (a senomorphic/senescence-reversal effect, e.g. ↑viability of senescent cells, ↓CTGF/fibronectin) — which is the opposite of senolysis (killing senescent cells). So it is not only the wrong species, it points to the wrong mechanism. It is not evidence for Eterwell Youth's senolytic claim and must not be cited as such.

Tier 2 — Animal

None identified. No published rodent/in-vivo animal senolytic, efficacy or tox studies for E. fleischeri extract. (For category context, the reference topical senolytic ABT-263/navitoclax reduces senescence and improves wound healing in aged mice, and triggered a transient inflammatory/macrophage response — relevant to the senolytic concept, not to this botanical. [14])

Tier 3 — Human, ORAL

Not applicable. Eterwell Youth is a topical cosmetic extract; there is no oral format or oral evidence. (So unlike spermidine/NAD precursors, there is no "borrowed from oral supplementation" data — but equally no human ingestion safety/efficacy база to lean on.) spermidine.md

Tier 4 — Human, TOPICAL

(a) Senescence/anti-aging in-vivo claims — supplier study, NOT independently published. [1][5][11][13] - Design (per DSM): placebo-/baseline-controlled consumer in-vivo study; two groups of ~25 volunteers each (≈50 total), female, mixed ethnicities, aged 45–60 (mean ~55), 3% Eterwell Youth, assessed at 2 weeks and 3 months. - Reported results @3%: - Skin smoothness / texture +24% at 2 weeks. - "Skin physiological age" younger — DSM's current public sources (product page, SOFW, Personal Care Magazine, the in-cosmetics award write-up) consistently state "6 years younger" at 2 weeks → "9 years younger" at 3 months (verified 2026-06-19 across DSM product page, SOFW and GCI). An earlier internal-review draft of this dossier flagged a "2-years → 6-years" variant as an internal inconsistency; that variant could not be re-confirmed in any current DSM source and should be treated as a likely transcription error, not a credibility flag against DSM. The real credibility issue here is not inconsistency but that the figure rests on a proprietary "physiological age" composite (below), not that DSM quotes two different numbers. [1][5][11] - Crow's-feet wrinkles −17% volume, −11% depth at 3 months. - Limitations: small N (~25/arm); supplier-run; not peer-reviewed; "physiological skin age" is a proprietary instrumental/algorithmic composite, not a validated clinical or biological-age endpoint; ethnicity/lifestyle controls undescribed; no senescence-marker (p16/p21) measurement in the living-skin arm — i.e., the human study measures appearance, while the senolytic mechanism is only shown in cells/explants. The two are not bridged in humans.

(b) The ONE peer-reviewed human E. fleischeri study — and it is NOT about senescence. Sfriso R, Claypool J, Roche M, Imfeld D (DSM Nutritional Products). "5-α reductase inhibition by Epilobium fleischeri extract modulates facial microbiota structure." International Journal of Cosmetic Science, 2022. [7] - Design: randomised, placebo-controlled clinical study; 23 Caucasian women, 4 weeks, five facial sites. - Findings: the extract inhibits 5-α-reductase (sebum-regulating enzyme; oenothein B is the known inhibitor [8]), shifts the facial microbiome (significant ↑ Staphylococcus hominis, S. epidermidis, Micrococcus yunnanensis; significant diversity change on lateral cheek, p=0.006), ↓ porphyrin fluorescence and ↓ non-inflammatory acne lesions (p<0.01) at 28 days. - Relevance: this is real, peer-reviewed, topical human evidence — but for a sebum/microbiome/blemish benefit, not for senescence, collagen or "years younger." It is also DSM-authored (all four authors are DSM employees). It usefully proves the extract does something on human skin and is well-tolerated; it does nothing to substantiate the senolytic story.

What is actually proven for TOPICAL human use: (i) microbiome modulation + reduced non-inflammatory blemishes + reduced porphyrins over 4 weeks (peer-reviewed, N=23, DSM) [7]; (ii) supplier-reported cosmetic appearance gains (smoothness, crow's-feet, "younger-looking") in a ~50-subject in-vivo study [5][11]. The senolytic claims (clears senescent cells, ↓p16/p21, +44% collagen, ↓BCL-2) are in-vitro/ex-vivo and single-source, and are borrowed into the human narrative — they are not demonstrated in living human skin.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • The actives are small polyphenols — flavonol glycosides (myricitrin, quercitrin ≈450–480 Da) and the larger ellagitannin oenothein B (~1,569 Da, a dimeric macrocycle). The glycosides sit near the "rule-of-500" and are plausible epidermal penetrants; oenothein B is large and hydrophilic, so meaningful dermal delivery of that component is questionable. [4][8]
  • The 2022 human microbiome study confirms functional surface-level activity (5-α-reductase inhibition is at least pilosebaceous/superficial), but says nothing about dermal fibroblast exposure — which is where the senolytic action would need to occur. [7]
  • No published penetration/PK data for the standardised extract. Whether enough intact senolytic constituent reaches dermal fibroblasts in vivo at cosmetic use levels is [unverified] and is the central open question (same gap as for OS-01).
  • As with most botanical extracts, the carrier/formulation does real work and the "active" is a mixture, not a single defined molecule at a defined dose.

6. Formulation

  • Recommended use level: DSM positions the headline in-vivo data at 3%; the patent describes activity across 0.1–3%, with potent in-vitro senescence effects reported as low as 0.03%. A practical hero use level is likely 1–3%. [1][3][5]
  • Format: liquid extract in a water/glycerin base; glycerin-rich per the patent's preferred composition (acts as solvent + humectant). Preservative system + citric acid for pH/chelation typical. [3]
  • pH: not publicly specified; polyphenol/glycoside extracts are generally stable in a mildly acidic ~4.5–6 range — formulate and confirm with supplier; avoid extremes.
  • Stability: polyphenols/ellagitannins are oxidation- and light-sensitive and can discolour (browning) and interact with metals — use chelators, antioxidants, opaque/air-restrictive packaging; watch for tannin–protein/cationic interactions.
  • Pairings (rational): collagen-supporting actives (peptides, vitamin C — formulate separately for stability), niacinamide, barrier lipids/ceramides; DSM explicitly markets synergy with collagen-acting compounds. Conceptually complements other longevity actives (the senescence-narrative cluster: OS-01, spermidine, NAD). [5][11]
  • Conflicts: strong oxidisers and high metal-ion loads (polyphenol degradation); very high cationic surfactant/protein loads (tannin complexation); extreme pH. Standard "don't co-formulate with reactive partners in the same phase" caution.
  • Sensory: botanical extract at low % — minimal sensory footprint; possible faint colour contribution; fragrance-free-friendly.

7. Safety

  • Tolerability: the peer-reviewed 4-week facial study (N=23) reported a benign tolerability profile and beneficial (acne-lesion-reducing) effects — reassuring for a leave-on facial use. [7] Epilobium extracts have a long traditional/cosmetic use history (fireweed in prostate/skin folk use) with low irritation signal. [4]
  • Irritation/sensitisation: no specific HRIPT/sensitisation data located for the standardised extract [unverified]; tannins can rarely be sensitising — patch-test in finished formula.
  • Sun sensitivity: none indicated; not an exfoliant or known photosensitiser (genus polyphenols are if anything photo-protective in the E. angustifolium literature [10]).
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: no specific topical safety data — default to conservative "consult your doctor" copy; do not market as pregnancy-safe without data.
  • Mechanistic caution: a true senolytic mode of action implies inducing apoptosis — and the reference small-molecule senolytic ABT-263 caused transient inflammation/macrophage infiltration in skin. [14] No such signal is reported for the (far milder, botanical) extract, but it is an honest theoretical watch-item and another reason not to overstate "powerful zombie-cell killing."
  • Sourcing/sustainability: strong story — organically cultivated alpine willowherb (rather than wild-harvesting a rare endemic), COSMOS-approved, NATRUE organic, Fair-for-Life fair-trade, vegan, halal, China-listed; green-chemistry organic-ethanol extraction; reported carbon footprint <3 kg CO₂eq/kg; alignment with Union for Ethical BioTrade standards. [1][5][11] This is arguably the ingredient's most defensible differentiator.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

The line: under US FDA, intended use decides cosmetic vs drug. Claiming a product alters the structure/function of skin — "clears senescent cells," "kills zombie cells," "boosts collagen," "reverses skin's biological age," "reduces inflammation" — pushes it into drug territory (no OTC anti-aging monograph). Cosmetic-safe claims are limited to appearance and care. FDA has issued warning letters for unapproved drug claims on cosmetics. [15] EU/UK claims must meet the EU Cosmetics Claims Regulation (evidential support; no misleading "younger by X years" promises) — and the EU is actively scrutinising vague "anti-aging"/biological-age claims.

  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "inspired by skin-longevity science," "helps visibly smooth and refine skin texture," "helps reduce the look of crow's-feet lines," "supports a more even, balanced complexion," "from sustainably cultivated alpine willowherb." (The smoothness/crow's-feet/appearance wording maps to the in-vivo appearance data; the microbiome/blemish wording maps to the one peer-reviewed study.)
  • ⚠️ Risky / drug-like — avoid or legal-review:
  • "senolytic" / "clears / kills senescent (zombie) cells" — pharmacological structure/function claim; for this ingredient it rests on in-vitro/ex-vivo, single-source data → high exposure.
  • "reverses your skin's biological age" / "X years younger" — relies on a proprietary "physiological age" composite, not a validated clinical or biological-age endpoint.
  • "boosts collagen by 44%" — structure/function + the figure is ex-vivo, not human; do not put a number on collagen in consumer copy.
  • "reduces inflammaging / IL-1" — structure/function + in-vitro only.
  • "Senotherapeutic/senolytic" is a science/category term, not a sanctioned cosmetic claim — usable educationally but pair with appearance-only benefit language; never let it imply disease treatment.
  • Substantiation reality: because nearly all data are the supplier's, our finished-product would inherit DSM's (unpublished) dossier for ingredient support but would still need our own claim substantiation for any product-level efficacy claim we make.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Supplier: dsm-firmenich (single source / proprietary; the E. fleischeri senolytic positioning and the cultivation supply chain are DSM's). Distributed via Univar Solutions and listed on SpecialChem/UL Prospector. [1][3] This is effectively a sole-supplier, IP-protected active (patent + proprietary sourcing), not a commodity you can second-source — a strategic dependency to weigh.
  • Cost tier: premium specialty botanical active (alpine organic cultivation + fair-trade + standardisation). Exact pricing not public [unverified]; expect high per-kg but used at 1–3%. Premium positioning is consistent with the brand's "invest in brand, buy quality actives" logic.
  • Supply considerations: rare endemic alpine plant; supply rests entirely on DSM's organic cultivation program (mitigates wild-harvest pressure but concentrates supplier risk). Strong ESG/traceability story (Fair for Life, UEBT alignment, low carbon footprint). [1][5][11]
  • IP: DSM holds the composition/use patent (WO2024149898A1; note the public record shows it as "Ceased" in the fetched patent-office snapshot [3] — status [unverified]/worth a fresh FTO check). Building on the exact ingredient means buying DSM's material; you cannot generically substitute "Epilobium fleischeri extract" and inherit the senolytic data or the supply chain.

Category premise itself Even the broader science is unsettled: a 2021 review concludes evidence that senescence causes skin aging is "highly suggestive but not yet completely conclusive," and the one human senolytic skin trial (D+Q) didn't report skin-function outcomes. So "remove senescent cells → younger skin" is an unproven causal claim industry-wide, not just for this ingredient. [12]

Honest synthesis: the sourcing/sustainability story is genuinely strong and the concept is on-trend and mechanistically plausible. But the senolytic efficacy claim is the weakest-evidenced in this dossier set on a publication basis — it is patent + marketing, with the only peer-reviewed human data pointing at a different benefit (microbiome/sebum) entirely. Independent commentary on topical senolytics broadly: promising but "not miracle cures," best framed as a modest adjunct to sun protection, barrier repair and collagen support. [12][14][16]

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ Conditional — supporting "longevity-narrative + sustainability" active, NOT an evidence-backed hero on the senolytic claim. Lean on the (real) microbiome/texture data; never headline "clears senescent cells."

  • Why it's tempting: it is an award-winning (in-cosmetics Global 2024, CEW 2024), on-trend botanical senolytic with a best-in-class ESG/traceability story (organic alpine cultivation, COSMOS/NATRUE/Fair-for-Life, <3 kg CO₂eq/kg) — a natural fit for a premium skin-longevity brand and a clean complement to the senescence-narrative cluster (senolytics-os01-peptide.md, spermidine.md, nad-niacinamide-longevity.md). [2][17][18]
  • Why it's only conditional: the senolytic efficacy package is single-source (DSM patent + marketing), unpublished, unreplicated, and not shown in human skin. The only peer-reviewed human evidence is for microbiome/sebum/blemishes (N=23), and the in-vivo "younger" data are supplier-run (≈25/arm) and hang on a proprietary, non-validated "physiological age" composite rather than a real biological-age endpoint. Our brand's whole edge is claims honesty — building a hero on "clears zombie cells" would be exactly the overreach we're differentiating against.
  • Longevity relevance: high in narrative, low in proven topical effect for the senescence claim specifically. Strong as an education/story asset and a sustainability asset; weak as a substantiated efficacy hero.
  • Gating conditions before any prominent use: (1) demand DSM's full study reports (in-vitro/ex-vivo N's, methods, the in-vivo protocol) and ideally peer-reviewed publication; (2) run a fresh IP/FTO check (patent shows "Ceased" in our snapshot — verify); (3) lock a claims strategy that uses appearance + microbiome language and treats "senolytic" as educational mechanism only; (4) confirm finished-formula stability (polyphenol oxidation/colour) and tolerability.
  • Best role: a supporting active that lets us talk credibly about the skin-longevity / senescence theme via its real, sustainable botanical story and its peer-reviewed microbiome/texture benefits — paired with genuinely evidence-backed barrier/collagen heroes (ceramides, retinoids, peptides, vitamin C) that carry the actual efficacy load.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19. An independent, skeptical pass over every headline claim and citation in this dossier — checking (a) whether evidence is HUMAN + TOPICAL vs. quietly borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral data, (b) whether cited studies are real and say what's claimed, and (c) industry funding / sample size. Sources re-checked: DSM product page, SOFW, GCI, Personal Care Magazine, patent WO2024149898A1, PubMed/PMC for Sfriso 2022, Low 2021 and Ruszová 2013.

Overall evidence grade: D → C (split)

  • Senolytic / "years younger" / +44% collagen / ↓p16-p21 / ↓BCL-2 story → D (essentially unproven topically; hype-adjacent). Every senolytic-efficacy claim lives only in DSM's own patent + marketing, is in-vitro/ex-vivo or a proprietary-endpoint in-vivo study, is unpublished and unreplicated, and is not demonstrated in living human skin. Mechanistically plausible, evidentially thin. This is the grade that governs the ingredient's hero claim.
  • The one peer-reviewed human-topical benefit (Sfriso 2022: microbiome / 5-α-reductase / non-inflammatory blemishes, N=23) → C (single small DSM-authored RCT, real and on-topic — but a different benefit from the one being marketed). It would be B if it actually supported the senescence story; it does not.
  • The dossier's own headline verdict ("the entire senolytic efficacy story lives exclusively in DSM's patent and marketing") is accurate and well-supported. This is a fundamentally honest dossier; the issues below are refinements, not reversals.

Main caveats

  1. No claim in this dossier is HUMAN + TOPICAL + on-mechanism. The human-topical evidence (Sfriso) is real but is a sebum/microbiome benefit; the senescence mechanism is cell/explant only. The marketing narrative borrows the in-vitro senolytic mechanism into a human "looks younger" story without bridging the two in living skin. This is the single most important honest takeaway and the dossier already makes it.
  2. Everything efficacy-related is single-source (DSM) and unpublished. Patent + brochures + one off-target paper. Zero independent replication as of 2026-06.
  3. Sample sizes are small or undisclosed. In-vivo ≈25/arm (~50 total); in-vitro/ex-vivo N's not in the public record; the peer-reviewed study is N=23.
  4. The "physiological age" endpoint is proprietary and non-validated — not a clinical or biological-age measure. This (not any figure inconsistency) is the real weakness of the "years younger" claim.
  5. Patent is "Ceased" (WO) — verified; weakens the IP moat and warrants a fresh FTO check before building on it.
