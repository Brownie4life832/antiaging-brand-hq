Ingredient: Hydration / "aquaporin" peptides (INCI: Acetyl Hexapeptide-37 [Diffuporine], plus the bundled Acetyl Hexapeptide-20 and Acetyl Heptapeptide-9)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering (leaning ⚠️ conditional / supporting — only Acetyl Hexapeptide-37 is genuinely an "aquaporin" peptide) · Class: Peptides (signal/"gene-expression-modulating" cosmetic peptides; AQP3 promoter activator)

Scope & honesty note (read first). The slug bundles three peptides under one "hydration / aquaporin" banner, but they are mechanistically different molecules and only one is actually an aquaporin peptide: - Acetyl Hexapeptide-37 (Diffuporine, Lipotec/Lubrizol) — the real "aquaporin peptide." Claims to upregulate AQP3 (the skin's main water/glycerol channel). This dossier is centered on it. - Acetyl Hexapeptide-20 ("HYB-TIMP," hexapeptide–theophylline hybrid) — an MMP-inhibitor / TIMP-mimetic marketed for firmness + reduced TEWL. Not an aquaporin peptide; the "hydration" tie-in is only the secondary TEWL claim. - Acetyl Heptapeptide-9 — a TGF-β–mimetic firming/lifting peptide (the active in "Golden Collagenine"). Not an aquaporin peptide and not really a "hydration" peptide; included here only because the master taxonomy grouped it. Regulatory flag: its colloidal-gold (nano) complex was prohibited in the EU in 2024 (details in §8).

Lumping these three as "aquaporin/hydration peptides" is itself a marketing artifact. The honest center of gravity is Acetyl Hexapeptide-37. The other two are addressed where relevant and flagged as off-mechanism.

Plain language: Diffuporine is a lab-made six-amino-acid chain designed to switch on the gene for aquaporin-3, the tiny channel that ferries water and glycerol up through the lower epidermis toward the surface. The theory: more AQP3 → more water reaching the stratum corneum → better-hydrated, more resilient skin. The other two "bundle-mates" do unrelated things (collagen/MMP firming) and are aquaporin peptides in name only.

2. Class & mechanism

The AQP3 story (for Acetyl Hexapeptide-37). - Aquaporin-3 (AQP3) is an aquaglyceroporin — it transports water, glycerol, and (notably) hydrogen peroxide across cell membranes. It is the predominant aquaporin in skin, concentrated in the plasma membranes of basal-layer keratinocytes (Hara-Chikuma & Verkman 2008; Bollag et al. 2020). - AQP3 is genuinely important to skin physiology: AQP3-knockout mice show reduced stratum-corneum water content, reduced elasticity, lower epidermal glycerol, and impaired wound healing; glycerol replacement reverses many defects (Ma et al. 2002; Hara-Chikuma & Verkman 2008). So AQP3 is a real, validated node in skin hydration — that part is not hype. - Diffuporine's proposed action: it binds/activates the AQP3 promoter, increasing AQP3 mRNA and protein in keratinocytes, thereby (in theory) increasing the water/glycerol flux that reaches the upper epidermis and stratum corneum — improving hydration and, secondarily, keratinocyte proliferation/barrier and "anti-aging" appearance (Lipotec/Happi 2013; ci.guide).

Acetyl Hexapeptide-20 acts by a different route: it is marketed as an MMP inhibitor / TIMP mimetic — i.e., it slows matrix-metalloprotease degradation of collagen/elastin (firmness), with a secondary claim of reduced transepidermal water loss. This is a collagen-preservation mechanism, not a water-channel mechanism.

Acetyl Heptapeptide-9 is a TGF-β mimetic — it signals fibroblasts to make collagen/elastin/ECM (firming, lifting). Again, no aquaporin involvement.

The mechanistic catch (be honest). For Diffuporine, target validity ≠ proof of topical benefit: 1. The AQP3-upregulation evidence is in-vitro, on cultured keratinocytes (mRNA/protein fold-changes). Whether a topically applied hexapeptide reaches living basal keratinocytes in human skin at an effective dose, durably raises AQP3 in vivo, and translates into a measurable, controlled hydration benefit is not demonstrated in any peer-reviewed human RCT (see §4). 2. AQP3 in the stratum corneum itself is debated; the channel matters most in viable basal epidermis — exactly the layer a surface-applied peptide struggles to reach (§5). 3. There is a specific safety caveat unique to this mechanism: AQP3 also drives keratinocyte proliferation and is implicated in skin tumorigenesis — Verkman himself published a cautionary note about chronically upregulating AQP3 with cosmetics (Verkman 2008; Hara-Chikuma & Verkman 2008). This is a mechanism-level concern other humectants don't carry (§7).

Defensible framing: a peptide that activates a genuinely important hydration gene (AQP3) in vitro, but whose topical human hydration benefit is unproven and whose proposed mechanism carries a theoretical proliferation/tumorigenesis caveat.

Bottom line: the aquaporin angle maps cleanly onto the brand's hydration / barrier hallmark and is mechanistically tied (via AQP3 decline with age and UV) to photoaging. But for the peptides specifically, only target-level plausibility exists — not proven topical human delivery on any hallmark.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

Honest headline: The underlying AQP3 biology is solid (good mouse-knockout and human aging/IHC data). The peptides marketed to exploit it have essentially no independent human topical evidence — what exists is manufacturer in-vitro data plus supplier marketing claims. There are zero published topical RCTs isolating Acetyl Hexapeptide-37, Acetyl Hexapeptide-20, or Acetyl Heptapeptide-9 on hydration or AQP3 in human skin that I could verify. Treat all efficacy percentages as unsubstantiated/supplier-sourced.

Tier 0 — Foundational AQP3 biology (validates the target, not the peptides)

  • Ma T, Hara M, Sougrat R, Verbavatz JM, Verkman AS. 2002. J Biol Chem 277(19):17147–53. DOI 10.1074/jbc.M200925200 (PMID 11880378). AQP3-knockout mice: stratum-corneum water content sharply reduced (165±10 vs 269±12 conductance units, p<0.001), >4-fold lower epidermal water permeability, >2-fold lower glycerol permeability, reduced water-holding capacity and elasticity. Establishes AQP3 as functionally required for SC hydration. Limitation: mouse genetics, not a topical active.
  • Hara-Chikuma M, Verkman AS. 2008. "Roles of aquaporin-3 in the epidermis." J Invest Dermatol 128(9):2145–51. DOI 10.1038/jid.2008.70 (PMID 18548108). Review: AQP3 mediates water + glycerol transport in basal keratinocytes; deficiency → impaired hydration, elasticity, wound healing, proliferation; glycerol replacement rescues. Calls AQP3 "a potential target for drug development."
  • Bollag WB, Aitkens L, White J, Hyndman KA. 2020. "Aquaporin-3 in the epidermis: more than skin deep." Am J Physiol Cell Physiol 318(6):C1144–C1153. DOI 10.1152/ajpcell.00075.2020 (PMID 32267715). Modern review: AQP3 transports water/glycerol/H₂O₂; central to keratinocyte proliferation, migration, differentiation, survival, barrier repair; abnormal in psoriasis and non-melanoma skin cancer.
  • AQP3 declines with age (the "why bother" rationale):
  • Li J, et al. 2010. Australas J Dermatol 51(2):106–112. DOI 10.1111/j.1440-0960.2010.00629.x (PMID 20546216). AQP3 gene + protein expression in sun-protected human skin decreases with age.
  • (Intrinsic + extrinsic aging IHC/morphometric study) 2015, Histol Histopathol / J record (PMID 26509906): AQP3 immunoreactivity falls progressively 20–35 → 35–50 → >50 yr; sun-exposed < sun-protected. [verify exact journal/vol]
  • Karimi N, Ahmadi V. 2024. "Aquaporin Channels in Skin Physiology and Aging Pathophysiology." Biology (Basel) 13(11):862. DOI 10.3390/biology13110862 (PMID 39596817). Review tying AQP dysregulation to multiple aging hallmarks (mitochondrial, senescence, autophagy, inflammaging); notes AQP3 significantly declines in non-sun-exposed skin of people >60 vs <45. Lists topically/cellularly validated AQP3 boosters: all-trans retinoic acid, chrysin, glycolic acid, red-wine flavanols (with a human SC-moisture clinical study), aloe + trimethylglycine — peptides/Diffuporine are not mentioned.
  • Karimi N, et al. 2025. "Aquaporins in the Skin: Molecular Regulators of Hydration and Potential Targets for Cosmetic Applications." Cosmetics 12(6):263. DOI 10.3390/cosmetics12060263. Cosmetic-focused review of AQP-targeting actives. [full text 403 at time of writing — confirm whether Diffuporine is cited before quoting it as "reviewed."]

Tier 1 — In vitro (the only tier with peptide-specific numbers)

  • Lipotec / Diffuporine reporter-screen data (manufacturer). Human keratinocytes treated 6 h: 50 ng/mL Acetyl Hexapeptide-37 → ~1.7-fold AQP3 mRNA; 250 ng/mL → ~2.7-fold (and "up to 2×" protein) increase via AQP3-promoter activation. Source: Lipotec technical/Happi "Keeping Skin Hydrated" 2013; secondary repeats on ci.guide and supplier pages. Limitations: manufacturer-originated; cultured cells; mRNA fold-change ≠ functional skin hydration; not independently replicated in peer-reviewed literature.
  • Dumas M, Sadick NS, Noblesse E, et al. 2007. "Hydrating skin by stimulating biosynthesis of aquaporins." J Drugs Dermatol 6(6 Suppl):s20–4 (PMID 17691206). Reconstructed human epidermis; an Ajuga turkestanica extract (2.5 µg/mL) significantly raised AQP3 protein. (Plant extract, not a peptide — included to show the "raise AQP3 in vitro" bar is low and crowded.)

Tier 2 — Animal

  • None specific to these peptides. The animal evidence (Ma 2002; Hara-Chikuma & Verkman 2008) is AQP3 knockout biology — it validates the target, and one strand of it (tumorigenesis resistance in AQP3-null mice) is actually a caution against over-boosting AQP3 (§7), not an efficacy result for the peptides.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL

  • None credible. Some marketing pages assert "oral intake reduced TEWL / enhanced barrier" for Acetyl Hexapeptide-37 — this is unsupported supplier copy; an oral hexapeptide would be digested. No oral RCT exists. Treat as fabricated-tier marketing.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the only tier that matters for our product)

  • No verifiable, peer-reviewed human topical RCT exists for Acetyl Hexapeptide-37 / Diffuporine on AQP3 expression, corneometry, or TEWL.
  • No verifiable independent human topical RCT for Acetyl Hexapeptide-20 (MMP/TIMP) or Acetyl Heptapeptide-9 (TGF-β) on hydration. Supplier pages cite figures like "55% reduction in wrinkles/deep creases" for Acetyl Heptapeptide-9 and firmness/TEWL benefits for Hexapeptide-20 — these trace to in-house/marketing data, not independent peer-reviewed RCTs.
  • Branded supplier datasheets (Lipotec Diffuporine on UL Prospector/Knowde) advertise in-vivo hydration/TEWL benefits, but the primary clinical protocols and data are not published in indexed literature; they are manufacturer claims and should be labeled as such.

Tier-4 verdict: For topical human use, efficacy is unproven. The strong-looking science is all target biology (AQP3 matters; it declines with age/UV) borrowed to imply the peptide works topically — a classic mechanism-borrowing move. Any hydration a Diffuporine product shows in practice is just as plausibly the vehicle's humectants/occlusives (glycerol, HA, etc.) as the peptide.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration (a real problem)

  • Acetyl Hexapeptide-37 is a small (~6-residue) but hydrophilic, polar, charged-at-physiological-pH peptide — the profile the lipophilic stratum corneum resists. As with other cosmetic peptides (cf. the Argireline penetration literature), passive delivery of intact peptide to the viable basal epidermis is inefficient.
  • The mechanism requires the peptide to reach basal keratinocytes (where AQP3 lives) in intact, active form and durably engage the AQP3 promoter — a much deeper and more demanding target than surface humectancy. There is no published human penetration/depth-profiling study for Acetyl Hexapeptide-37 confirming it gets there.
  • In-vivo human work on AQP3 itself in stratum corneum (Boury-Jamot/Nakajima-type studies; e.g. Arch Dermatol Res 2013, DOI 10.1007/s00403-013-1365-2) underscores that the functionally relevant AQP3 is in living epidermis, not the dead SC — again the layer hardest to reach topically.
  • Conclusion: delivery is a genuine open question. The "raises AQP3 2×" figure is a cell-culture result at defined media concentrations; it does not establish that a finished topical product delivers an effective dose to basal keratinocytes.

6. Formulation

Parameter Guidance
Effective concentration Unknown for efficacy (no human dose-response). Diffuporine is sold as a dilute aqueous peptide solution; typical finished-product label inclusion of the solution is low single-digit % (≈1–5%), but true peptide content is far lower. Use supplier-recommended levels and treat as a "claims peptide," not a proven dose.
pH Formulate in mildly acidic–neutral aqueous systems (~5–6) where small acetylated/amidated peptides are reasonably stable and soluble.
Solubility / phase Water-soluble; aqueous serums, gels, O/W emulsions.
Stability Generally acceptable in water at proper pH; protect from heat; aqueous = needs robust preservation. Acetylation/amidation of the termini improves stability vs free peptide.
Pairings (sensible) Glycerol (AQP3's actual substrate — far better-evidenced humectant, and a logical mechanistic partner), hyaluronic acid, panthenol, niacinamide, ceramides/barrier lipids, ectoin/trehalose. Realistically, the co-formulated humectants do the measurable hydrating work.
Conflicts Avoid very low-pH (strong acid) co-formulation in the same step; avoid high-ionic-strength systems that may destabilize the charged peptide.
Sensory Light, water-thin; easy to drop into pleasant serums.
Reality check Best treated as a supporting "story" peptide layered onto a genuinely effective humectant/occlusive base — not as the active that carries hydration.

7. Safety

  • Irritation / sensitization: No specific published human safety dataset for Acetyl Hexapeptide-37; small acetylated/amidated cosmetic peptides are generally well-tolerated and low-sensitizing at cosmetic levels. No CIR monograph or SCCS opinion identified for the bare peptide (the SCCS opinion that exists is for the gold-nano complex of Heptapeptide-9 — see below).
  • Sun sensitivity: None known; not a photosensitizer.
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: No specific data. Systemic exposure from topical use is expected to be negligible, but with no pregnancy-specific evidence, apply standard cosmetic caution and make no pregnancy-safety claim.
  • ⚠️ Mechanism-specific caution (important and under-discussed): Verkman explicitly cautioned against cosmetics that chronically upregulate AQP3. AQP3 (via glycerol → glycerol-3-phosphate → ATP) drives keratinocyte proliferation, and AQP3-null mice are resistant to chemically induced skin tumors; human cutaneous SCC overexpresses AQP3 (Hara-Chikuma & Verkman 2008, Mol Cell Biol 28(1):326–32; Verkman 2008, Exp Dermatol 17:871–2). This is a theoretical, not demonstrated, risk for cosmetic peptide use (the in-vitro fold-changes are modest and delivery is poor), but it is a genuine mechanism-level flag that a serious skin-longevity brand should be aware of — "boost the channel that promotes proliferation/tumorigenesis" is not a costless pitch.
  • Acetyl Heptapeptide-9 regulatory/safety flag: the Acetyl Heptapeptide-9 Colloidal Gold (nano) complex was assessed by the SCCS (SCCS/1629/21), which could not conclude it safe due to missing data; the EU subsequently prohibited several gold nanomaterials including this complex via Regulation (EU) 2024/858 (in force 2024). The bare peptide (non-nano) is a different INCI and not itself the banned substance, but anyone sourcing "Golden Collagenine" must confirm it is the reformulated non-gold derivative.
  • Sourcing/sustainability: Fully synthetic (solid-phase peptide synthesis); vegan-compatible; no animal/wild sourcing concerns; ordinary synthetic-chemistry footprint.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Status: All three are cosmetic ingredients (EU Reg (EC) 1223/2009; US FDA cosmetic unless claims cross into "drug"). Exception: Acetyl Heptapeptide-9 Colloidal Gold (nano) is EU-prohibited (Reg (EU) 2024/858) — do not use that nano-gold form for any EU-facing product.
  • The drug/structure-function line. Claims that the product "increases aquaporins," "boosts AQP3," "activates water channels," or "restores the skin's water-transport system" describe altering the structure/function of the body and assert a physiological mechanism we cannot substantiate in humans — high regulatory + substantiation risk. Avoid them in consumer copy.
  • ❌ Do NOT say: "boosts/activates aquaporins," "increases AQP3 by 2×," "restores water channels," "55% fewer wrinkles" (Hep-9 supplier figure), "clinically proven hydration," any specific % hydration/TEWL improvement, "rebuilds collagen."
  • ✅ Defensible cosmetic language: "peptide that helps support skin's natural hydration," "helps skin hold onto moisture," "for visibly plumper, more hydrated-looking skin," "supports a healthy moisture barrier" — appearance/feel-based, no mechanism-of-the-body and no numbers. Even these should sit on a base whose humectants justify the hydration claim.
  • Any aquaporin/AQP3 mechanism claim or efficacy percentage → flag for legal/substantiation; we have no human data that would survive a challenge, and the AQP3-tumorigenesis literature makes mechanism claims doubly unwise.

10. Hype vs. evidence

Marketing claim Reality
"Aquaporin peptide / boosts your skin's water channels" True target (AQP3 matters; declines with age/UV) but the peptide's topical effect on AQP3 in human skin is unproven; the "2× AQP3" figure is cultured-cell mRNA, not skin.
"Up to 2× more aquaporin-3" In-vitro, manufacturer reporter-assay (1.7× at 50 ng/mL, 2.7× at 250 ng/mL). Not a finished-product, in-skin, or controlled-human result.
"Clinically proven hydration / reduces TEWL" No verifiable peer-reviewed human RCT. Supplier datasheet claims only. Any real hydration is plausibly the vehicle humectants.
"Oral intake reduces TEWL / strengthens barrier" Fabricated-tier — peptide would be digested; no oral RCT.
Acetyl Heptapeptide-9 "55% wrinkle reduction" Supplier/in-house figure; no independent RCT. Also: the gold-nano version is EU-banned.
Acetyl Hexapeptide-20 "firms + reduces water loss" TIMP/MMP marketing mechanism; no independent human RCT; not an aquaporin peptide despite being bundled as one.
(Unstated) "AQP3 boosting is purely beneficial" Ignores Verkman's caution: AQP3 promotes keratinocyte proliferation and is implicated in skin tumorigenesis.

This is a mechanism-rich, evidence-poor category: the underlying biology is real and citable, which makes the marketing sound rigorous, but the leap from "AQP3 is important" to "this peptide hydrates your skin" is unsupported in humans, and the bundling of three unrelated peptides under one banner is itself misleading.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ CONDITIONAL → SUPPORTING (at most). Not a hero. Acetyl Hexapeptide-37 only; skip the bundling.

One-line reasoning: The AQP3 target is legitimate and on-thesis (hydration/barrier, declines with age/UV), but no human topical evidence shows these peptides actually deliver it, delivery to basal keratinocytes is doubtful, and the AQP3-upregulation mechanism carries a proliferation/tumorigenesis caution — so use Acetyl Hexapeptide-37 only as a quiet supporting "story" peptide on a humectant-driven base, with strictly appearance-based claims, or skip it for better-evidenced hydration actives.

Practical guidance: - If used: Acetyl Hexapeptide-37 (Diffuporine) only, as a minor co-active beside glycerol / HA / ceramides / panthenol that do the real hydrating work; appearance-only claims; no AQP3/percentage language. - Do not treat Acetyl Hexapeptide-20 or Acetyl Heptapeptide-9 as "hydration/aquaporin" actives — they are off-mechanism; evaluate them separately on their own (also thin) firming evidence, and avoid the EU-banned gold-nano Heptapeptide-9 form entirely. - Better hydration spend overall: glycerol (AQP3's own substrate, cheap, proven), hyaluronic/polyglutamic acid, ceramides/barrier lipids, ectoin/trehalose, niacinamide — all out-evidence these peptides for the same hallmark.

Longevity-relevance note: Conceptually moderate — AQP3 decline is a real feature of intrinsic + photo-aging and links (per review-level work) to several aging hallmarks, so the target fits a skin-longevity narrative. But as a usable active, low: no human proof, delivery doubts, and a proliferation caveat that sits awkwardly with a "healthy-aging" brand. The honest longevity play is to address AQP3's decline indirectly with proven moisturization + photoprotection + retinoids (which themselves raise AQP3), not to lean on an unproven "aquaporin peptide."

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19 against PubMed/PMC primary records, journal/DOI lookups, EU/SCCS documents, and manufacturer/ingredient-encyclopedia pages.

Overall evidence grade: D (target real; peptide topical efficacy unproven in humans)

For the only tier that matters for a topical cosmetic (human, topical, objective endpoint), there is no verifiable peer-reviewed RCT for Acetyl Hexapeptide-37, Acetyl Hexapeptide-20, or Acetyl Heptapeptide-9 on hydration/AQP3. The peptide-specific data are in-vitro mRNA fold-changes from the manufacturer plus supplier marketing. The AQP3 biology itself (knockout mice, age/UV decline) is genuinely well-evidenced (that body of work would grade A–B), but it concerns the target, not these actives — using it to imply topical efficacy is mechanism-borrowing. Net usable grade for the brand: D (do not build a claim on it).

What checks out

  • Foundational AQP3 papers are real and accurately characterized: Ma 2002 (J Biol Chem, PMID 11880378); Hara-Chikuma & Verkman 2008 review (J Invest Dermatol, PMID 18548108); Hara-Chikuma & Verkman 2008 tumorigenesis (Mol Cell Biol 28(1):326–32, PMC2223314); Bollag 2020 (Am J Physiol Cell Physiol, PMID 32267715); Li 2010 aging (Australas J Dermatol, PMID 20546216); Karimi & Ahmadi 2024 (Biology 13:862, PMID 39596817); Dumas 2007 (J Drugs Dermatol, PMID 17691206); Verkman 2008 cautionary note (Exp Dermatol 17:871–2).
  • Diffuporine identity (Acetyl Hexapeptide-37, CAS 1447824-16-9, Ac-SPAGGP-NH₂, Lipotec) is consistent across ChemicalBook + ci.guide + supplier pages.
  • AQP3-tumorigenesis caution is genuine and primary (Verkman 2008; Hara-Chikuma & Verkman 2008) — arguably the dossier's most important under-marketed fact.
  • EU prohibition of Acetyl Heptapeptide-9 Colloidal Gold (nano) via SCCS/1629/21 → Reg (EU) 2024/858 is real; the bare peptide is a distinct INCI and not itself the banned substance.

Items flagged / to confirm (do not over-state)

  1. Source #7 (PMID 26509906) — finding (AQP3 falls with intrinsic + extrinsic age) is real; exact journal/volume not re-confirmed here. Marked [verify].
  2. Source #9 (Cosmetics 2025 review) — full text returned 403; I did not confirm it discusses Diffuporine. Cited as a general AQP-cosmetics review only, with a [confirm before quoting] flag.
  3. Diffuporine mRNA fold-changes (1.7×/2.7×) — manufacturer/Happi-sourced, not peer-reviewed; labeled as such throughout.
  4. "Oral intake reduces TEWL" marketing line for Acetyl Hexapeptide-37 — treated as fabricated-tier (no oral RCT; would be digested).
  5. "55% wrinkle reduction" (Hep-9) and Hex-20 firming/TEWL figures — supplier-sourced, unverified; explicitly flagged.

Main caveats to carry forward

  • The category is mechanism-rich, evidence-poor. Real AQP3 biology is being used to dress up peptides with no human topical proof.
  • Bundling is misleading: only Hex-37 is an aquaporin peptide; Hex-20 (TIMP) and Hep-9 (TGF-β) are off-mechanism.
  • Delivery is unverified — AQP3 lives in basal keratinocytes, the layer topicals reach worst.
  • Unique safety angle: boosting AQP3 chronically has a theoretical proliferation/tumorigenesis caution (Verkman 2008) absent from ordinary humectants — relevant for a longevity brand's messaging.
  • EU nano-gold prohibition applies to one specific Heptapeptide-9 form — avoid it.
