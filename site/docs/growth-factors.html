Ingredient: Recombinant Growth Factors (EGF, bFGF/FGF-2, KGF, IGF-1, TGF-β1, PDGF, VEGF, HGF, CTGF & "conditioned media")

Researched: 2026-06-19 (upgraded from 2026-06-18 stub) · Status: ⬜ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ☑ rejected (lean) / ⬜ in a product Class: Growth factors, cytokines, exosomes, conditioned media & regenerative · Slug: growth-factors

One-line bottom line: A genuinely interesting mechanism with a real but modest, heterogeneous, heavily industry-funded human topical evidence base, throttled by a hard penetration problem (these are ~6,000–30,000 Da proteins vs. a ~500 Da skin barrier ceiling) and a lingering theoretical EGFR-proliferation safety flag. Defensible as a premium supporting curiosity at best; a poor fit for a brand whose whole edge is honesty. Lean skip / conditional.

1. Identity

Common name: Growth factors (GFs); in marketing often "EGF skincare," "growth factor serum," "the new retinol."

What the category actually contains — a family of distinct signaling proteins, not one molecule:

Factor Full name Approx. MW Primary skin role
EGF Epidermal growth factor ~6.0 kDa (53 aa) Keratinocyte proliferation/migration, re-epithelialization
bFGF / FGF-2 Basic fibroblast growth factor ~17–18 kDa Fibroblast proliferation, angiogenesis, collagen/elastin
aFGF / FGF-1 Acidic FGF ~16–18 kDa Similar to bFGF; broad mitogen
KGF / FGF-7 Keratinocyte growth factor ~26–28 kDa Epidermal homeostasis, keratinocyte support
IGF-1 Insulin-like growth factor 1 ~7.6 kDa Fibroblast/keratinocyte survival & proliferation
TGF-β1 Transforming growth factor beta 1 ~25 kDa (dimer) ECM deposition, collagen synthesis (also pro-fibrotic)
PDGF Platelet-derived growth factor (e.g. -BB) ~25 kDa (dimer) Fibroblast chemotaxis, granulation, wound matrix
VEGF Vascular endothelial growth factor ~38–45 kDa (dimer) Angiogenesis
HGF Hepatocyte growth factor ~80–90 kDa Anti-fibrotic, motility
CTGF / CCN2 Connective tissue growth factor ~36–38 kDa ECM/fibrosis modulation

INCI names (cosmetic): - sh-Oligopeptide-1 = recombinant human EGF ("sh" = "synthetic human"; the sequence mirrors human EGF, made by recombinant fermentation — typically E. coli, yeast, or barley seed). - sh-Polypeptide-1 / -9 / -11 etc. = various recombinant FGFs (e.g. sh-Polypeptide-9 ≈ VEGF; numbering varies by registry — verify per supplier). - rh-Oligopeptide-1 / rh-Polypeptide-X = "rh" (recombinant human) variants, same idea. - Human Fibroblast Conditioned Media / sh-... Conditioned Media = the undefined "secretome" cocktail (e.g. SkinMedica TNS): 100s of GFs, cytokines and matrix proteins from cultured cells. This overlaps the exosome / conditioned-media category — see that dossier for the shared provenance/regulatory issues.

Key forms/derivatives: - Single recombinant proteins (most "EGF serums"): one defined factor, often E. coli- or barley-expressed. - Defined cocktails: several named recombinant GFs blended. - Conditioned media / secretome: undefined mixture from cultured human fibroblasts (neonatal/dermal), MSCs (adipose, bone marrow, umbilical cord), or plant cell culture. Composition is proprietary and lot-variable. - Plant/barley-expressed EGF (e.g. Bioeffect's barley EGF): molecularly the same protein as human-expressed rhEGF; production route differs, finished molecule and bioactivity are claimed identical. - Delivery-engineered: liposome/transfersome/elastosome-encapsulated, or designed for procedural delivery (microneedling, laser, "microinfusion").

2. Class & mechanism

Growth factors are endogenous signaling proteins that bind specific cell-surface receptor tyrosine kinases (EGFR for EGF; FGFR1–4 for FGFs; etc.), triggering autophosphorylation and downstream RAS–RAF–MEK–MAPK/ERK and PI3K–AKT cascades. The net cellular instructions: proliferate, migrate, survive, and lay down extracellular matrix.

  • EGF → EGFR: drives keratinocyte proliferation and migration (re-epithelialization), and modulates DNA synthesis (Araújo et al. 2019 [6]; sciencedirect overview).
  • FGF-2/bFGF → FGFR: stimulates dermal fibroblast proliferation and induces type I collagen synthesis; type I (80–85%) and type III (10–15%) collagen are the dermal matrix that governs firmness/elasticity, both of which decline with age (Araújo et al. 2019 [6]).
  • TGF-β1, PDGF: promote ECM deposition and fibroblast recruitment — the same machinery used in wound granulation.

The aging logic: aged/photodamaged skin has fewer, less-active fibroblasts, a fragmented collagen matrix, and blunted GF signaling. Topically supplying GFs is meant to "re-instruct" cells toward a younger, repair-oriented phenotype. This is biologically coherent in cell culture and wounds. The leap to intact, un-wounded human skin via a topical cream is where the evidence thins dramatically (see §4–5).

Honest read: GFs are an "ECM / proliferation / repair" story, not an antioxidant, anti-glycation, or microbiome story. The broad multi-hallmark claims (senescence, proteostasis, stem-cell exhaustion) come almost entirely from one manufacturer-funded preclinical+clinical paper using gene-expression and a single senescence marker — interesting, not settled.

4. Evidence review by tier — the core of this dossier

Reading guide. Almost the entire positive human topical literature is (a) on undefined conditioned-media cocktails, not single defined GFs, and (b) funded by the product's manufacturer. Cell-culture and wound-healing data are strong but are borrowed formats — they do not establish that a stable GF reaches living skin from a cosmetic cream. Treat the gap between "GFs do X in a dish / in a wound" and "this serum does X on intact aging skin" as the central honesty issue.

Tier 1 — In-vitro (cell culture) — STRONG mechanistically

  • Araújo, Lôbo, Trindade, et al. (2019), Skin Pharmacology and Physiology 32(5):275–282 — mechanistic review: FGF binds FGFR tyrosine kinases, activates MAPK/ERK, and induces type I collagen and elastin synthesis; positions FGFs as controllers of skin aging. Review of mechanism, not clinical proof. [6]
  • Conditioned media (adipose MSC) on UVB-stressed keratinocytes/fibroblasts (Kim et al., Int J Mol Sci 2020, PMC6981944) — MSC-CM prevented UVB-induced aging markers in cultured human skin cells (↑ collagen, ↓ MMPs). In-vitro only. [12]
  • Umbilical-cord-blood MSC conditioned media (Kim et al., PMC6205340) — stimulated rejuvenation-associated activity in skin cells in vitro. [13]
  • EGF improves keratinocyte migration/proliferation in culture (Spandidos / Int J Mol Med 2015). [unverified exact figures]

Limitation: Cultured cells have no stratum corneum. Direct GF access to receptors is guaranteed in a dish and not guaranteed through intact skin. In-vitro potency tells us the molecule is bioactive when it arrives — not that it arrives.

Tier 2 — Animal — supportive for wounds, thin for cosmetic aging

  • rhPDGF-BB in rabbit-ear / rodent wound models and bone-marrow MSC-CM (PMC6714083) — accelerate wound closure, reduce hypertrophic scarring. Robust for wounds, not for cosmetic photoaging of intact skin. [11]
  • General regenerative-medicine animal data underpins the medical (drug) use of PDGF/FGF; it is not evidence for an anti-wrinkle cosmetic.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL — N/A

Growth factors are not used orally for skin (proteins are digested). No relevant oral-supplement evidence. Do not borrow any "ingestible collagen-booster" framing here.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL — the decision-relevant tier

(a) Systematic reviews (highest level, but only as good as the inputs)

  • Quinlan DJ, Ghanem AM, Hassan H (2023) — "Topical growth factor preparations for facial skin rejuvenation: A systematic review," J Cosmet Dermatol 22(7):2023–2039. PMID 37222303. [1]
  • 33 studies; 9 RCTs + 24 uncontrolled case series; 1,180 participants; 23 different preparations (databases 2000–Oct 2022; ≥10 participants).
  • Investigator-assessed improvement vs. baseline was modest: skin texture median <50%, fine lines/wrinkles median <35%, overall facial appearance median <20%.
  • Participant-assessed improvement was generally higher than investigator-assessed (a classic placebo/expectation tell).
  • 3 comparative RCTs found NO statistically significant difference between treatments (i.e., differing GF products were interchangeable / didn't beat each other).
  • Low risk of adverse events.
  • Authors' own limitations: heavy heterogeneity in GF source/number, missing info on other actives in the formulas, no standardized outcome measures, and durability beyond 6 months unknown. Most data are small and/or uncontrolled.

  • Net: "appears effective" is carried mostly by uncontrolled case series and participant self-report; the controlled signal is small and benefits are modest.

  • Epidermal Growth Factor in Aesthetics and Regenerative Medicine: Systematic Review (2021, J Cutan Aesthet Surg / PMC8423211) — review of EGF across aesthetic and regenerative uses; concludes EGF shows promise but topical cosmetic evidence is limited by small studies and penetration constraints. [2] (Abstract/secondary-confirmed; full text behind reCAPTCHA — verify before quoting specifics.)

(b) Vehicle/placebo-controlled RCTs (the strongest individual data — almost all manufacturer-funded)

  • Naughton GK, Jiang LI, Makino ET, et al. (2022) — "Targeting Multiple Hallmarks of Skin Aging: Preclinical and Clinical Efficacy of a Novel Growth Factor-Based Skin Care Serum," Dermatol Ther (PMC9823186). [5]
  • Product: TNS Advanced+ (hypoxic human fibroblast conditioned media + botanical/marine extracts).
  • 24-week, randomized, double-blind, placebo (vehicle)-controlled, crossover design (Phase I baseline–wk 12; Phase II wk 12–24), with parallel active vs. placebo arms in Phase I; 66 completers of 68 enrolled (45 active / 23 placebo); biopsies from 20 active / 10 placebo subjects, with H2A.J analyzed in ~10 active. (Note: this is NOT a split-face study — earlier drafts mislabeled it as such; split-face applies to the Wu/Goldman [7] and some other SkinMedica trials, not this one.)
  • Active beat vehicle: coarse lines/wrinkles (wk 4, 8, 12), overall photodamage & fine lines (wk 8, 12), sagging & hyperpigmentation (wk 12). Subjects perceived a median ~6-year age reduction.
  • Preclinical: ↓ senescence marker H2A.J; ↑ proteasome/autophagy, connexin, collagen/elastin genes.

  • Conflict of interest: funded by Allergan Aesthetics (AbbVie); authors employed by Allergan/Histogen with stock disclosures. This is the single most-cited "it works" study and it is a manufacturer study of its own flagship.

  • Mehta/Fitzpatrick-lineage TNS trials (SkinMedica): a 60-subject, 6-month double-blind study reported significant fine-line/wrinkle improvement at 3 months with reduced surface roughness by profilometry vs. vehicle; a 24-week, 68-subject (ages 37–70, moderate–severe photodamage) split-face placebo-controlled study reported wrinkle gains starting ~2 weeks and significance vs. placebo on multiple endpoints at 8–12 weeks. [4][3] (All within the manufacturer/secretome lineage.)

  • Gold et al. (2023), J Cosmet Dermatol — postmenopausal subjects, improvements in skin-quality parameters with a topical GF serum. [3] (manufacturer-adjacent)

(c) Comparative RCTs of defined recombinant GFs

  • Wu DC, Goldman MP (2017) — "A Prospective, Randomized, Double-blind, Split-face Clinical Trial Comparing the Efficacy of Two Topical Human Growth Factors…," J Clin Aesthet Dermatol. PMID 28670356. [7]
  • n=20, 3 months, fibroblast-derived vs. adipose-MSC-derived GF cocktails, split-face.
  • Both significantly reduced wrinkling vs. baseline; no significant difference between the two products. No placebo arm (both arms were active) — so this shows change over time, not superiority to vehicle.

(d) Single-protein / barley & lower-tier topical

  • Schouest JM, Luu TK, Moy RL (2012) — "Improved Texture and Appearance… Barley Produced, Synthetic, Human-like EGF Serum," J Drugs Dermatol. PMID 22527430. [8]
  • Open-label, single-center, n=29 women (39–75), 3 months, barley rhEGF serum twice daily + sunscreen/cleanser.
  • Statistically significant improvement in fine lines/rhytids, texture, pore size within the first month.
  • No control/placebo arm; subjective grading; women only; small n; non-US-gov't research support. Weak design — hypothesis-generating only.

(e) Procedural-delivery RCTs (penetration bypassed — NOT cosmetic topical) - Microneedling + topical GFs RCT (JCAD) and laser-assisted vitamin C ± GFs (PMC8144134, n=149) show benefits — but these deliberately breach the barrier (microneedling/laser), so they evidence a procedure, not a leave-on cosmetic. Do not use these to support a cream. [9][10]

Tier-4 honesty summary

  • Proven-ish for topical human use: modest improvement in texture, fine lines, overall photoaged appearance from multi-GF/conditioned-media serums, mostly in manufacturer-funded vehicle-controlled trials (best example: Naughton 2022 [5]; SkinMedica TNS lineage [3][4]). Effect sizes are small (Quinlan medians <20–50% [1]).
  • Borrowed, NOT proven for cosmetic topical: collagen synthesis, "regeneration," senescence reversal (in-vitro / gene-expression / wound data), and all wound-healing performance (that's the rhPDGF-BB drug lane).
  • Confounded: single-defined-GF cosmetic efficacy is weakly supported; most "wins" come from undefined cocktails with other actives, so attribution to the GF itself is uncertain. The three comparative RCTs found products indistinguishable [1] — consistent with the GF not being the active driver.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration — the central weakness

  • The ~500 Da rule: passive penetration of intact stratum corneum drops sharply above ~500 Daltons. EGF is ~6,000 Da (≈12× the ceiling); FGFs/TGF-β/PDGF/VEGF are larger still (17–45 kDa); HGF ~80–90 kDa. By this rule, meaningful passive delivery of intact, bioactive protein to viable epidermis/dermis is implausible (Eskens 2021 [14]; Martínez-Carpio et al. 2023 [15]).
  • Counter-arguments / nuance (steelman):
  • Some delivery via follicular/appendageal routes, and possibly via compromised or thinner aged barrier.
  • Encapsulation (liposomes, transfersomes, elastosomes) measurably improves in-vitro permeation (Eskens 2021 [14]; MDPI elastosome 2024; transfersomal emulgel PMC7539322). A well-encapsulated 0.5% can plausibly outperform a bare 5% that degrades on the shelf.
  • GFs may also act at the surface / upper epidermis or trigger downstream signaling cascades without full-thickness penetration — but this is mechanism speculation, not demonstrated clinical pharmacology.
  • Procedural delivery (microneedling/laser) clearly gets GFs in — but that's not a leave-on cosmetic.
  • Brutal read: the molecule that works in a dish and in a wound is the molecule that struggles to get through intact skin from a cream. The clinical wins that exist (§4) may be partly real (encapsulation/follicular/surface effects + co-ingredients) and partly placebo/expectation (participant > investigator scores [1]). Penetration is the honest Achilles' heel.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: No agreed standard. Cosmetic EGF (sh-Oligopeptide-1) commonly 0.1–5%+ of a dilute stock; conditioned-media products are dosed by proprietary "activity," not %. Encapsulated low % can beat unencapsulated high %.
  • pH: EGF is inactivated at pH extremes; formulate roughly pH ~5–8 (adjust the base before adding the GF). FGFs similarly pH-sensitive.
  • Stability — the practical killer: GFs are large, fragile proteins — heat-, light-, and pH-sensitive and prone to proteolytic/oxidative degradation, with real lot-to-lot potency variability. Store <30 °C, away from light; typical claimed shelf life ~2 years, refrigeration extends it. Anhydrous, airless/encapsulated systems and validated potency assays are essential — an unprotected serum can lose activity before use.
  • Pairings: plays well with hyaluronic acid, niacinamide, peptides, ceramides (gentle, supportive). Good "barrier-friendly" companions for our barrier-repair thesis.
  • Conflicts: keep away from low-pH actives (L-ascorbic acid, AHAs/BHAs), strong oxidants, and harsh surfactants that denature protein. Don't co-formulate in the same step as a 3.5 vitamin-C serum.
  • Sensory: typically lightweight serums/essences; the proteins themselves are sensory-neutral.

7. Safety

  • Irritation/tolerability: Generally well tolerated; systematic review reports low adverse-event risk [1]. Most reactions trace to other formula components, not the GF.
  • Theoretical EGFR / proliferation concern: EGFR overexpression drives proliferation/angiogenesis and inhibits apoptosis (why EGFR inhibitors are anticancer drugs); the worry is that chronically stimulating EGFR could promote growth of pre-malignant/malignant cells (Baumann/Skin Type Solutions [16]; EGFR-inhibitor reviews). Counterweight: no reported cases of skin cancer from cosmetic EGF serums, and poor penetration limits exposure. Dr. Baumann's honest verdict: "we really do not know if EGF in skin care is safe." Reasonable conservative stance: avoid on/near active skin lesions, personal history of skin cancer, near the eyes, and during pregnancy (precaution, not evidence of harm).
  • Reassuring precedent (drug data): rhPDGF-BB (Regranex) carried a 2008 FDA boxed cancer-mortality warning based on flawed insurance-claims math; removed in 2018 after propensity-matched studies in >13,000 patients (incl. a 6,429-vs-6,429 VA cohort and a prior-cancer subgroup) showed no increased cancer risk/mortality, even at up to 140 daily wound applications (Slade, Lynch, Dickerson 2025, Wound Repair Regen; PMC12605788) [17]. This is the best real-world safety signal for a topical GF — though it's on wounds, with a different factor (PDGF, not EGF).
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: no specific data; precautionary avoidance is reasonable for a proliferative signaling protein.
  • Sensitization: low reported; conditioned-media/biologic cocktails carry a higher theoretical immunogenicity/contamination question.
  • Sourcing/sustainability/ethics: human-cell-derived conditioned media and MSC secretome raise provenance, donor-consent, and contamination/sterility questions (shared with the exosome category). Recombinant single proteins (E. coli/yeast/barley) sidestep human-tissue ethics; barley-expressed EGF is a cleaner provenance story than human-cell conditioned media.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic vs. drug line (US/FDA): In skincare, GFs are sold as cosmetic ingredients (no pre-market approval; FDA polices safety/labeling and claims). The line is the claim: improving appearance = cosmetic; affecting structure/function = drug.
  • ✅ Cosmetic-safe: "helps improve the look of firmness and texture," "visibly smooths fine lines," "supports the appearance of renewed, refreshed skin."
  • ⚠️ Drug-territory (avoid / would need substantiation + legal): "stimulates collagen production," "regenerates/repairs skin," "heals," "reverses aging," anything implying wound healing (that lane belongs to the rhPDGF-BB drug Regranex, the only FDA-approved growth factor — and it's a prescription drug, not a cosmetic).
  • The "conditioned media" trap: secretome/conditioned-media products overlap the exosome regulatory gray zone. The FDA has warned that exosome/secretome products marketed with regenerative claims can be treated as unapproved biologics/drugs. Stay strictly in cosmetic-appearance language and avoid any regenerative-medicine framing.
  • Pointed critique to respect: Martínez-Carpio et al. (2023), Cutaneous and Ocular Toxicology 42(4) — "Topical application of sh-oligopeptide-1 and clinical trials with cosmetic preparations: risk or fraud?" argues sh-Oligopeptide-1 as sold is not demonstrably a functional EGF, that its preclinical bioactivity in cosmetic form is unproven, that it can't meaningfully penetrate (~6 kDa, 12× the 500-Da limit), and that some clinical trials don't meet medical standards. A brand built on honesty must be able to answer this paper. [15]
  • See ../../brand/glossary.md claim cheat-sheet.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: HIGH. Recombinant proteins and (especially) conditioned-media/secretome are expensive, and require cold-chain handling, potency assays, and stability testing — raising true cost of goods well beyond the headline raw price.
  • Suppliers/forms: single recombinant EGF/FGF from biotech cosmetic-ingredient suppliers (E. coli/yeast/barley); branded conditioned-media (e.g. SkinMedica TNS lineage) are largely captive to their owners. Plant/barley EGF is a cleaner-provenance, marketing-friendly source with comparable molecule but thinner independent efficacy data.
  • Diligence required: demand (1) identity + potency assay (not just "contains EGF"), (2) stability/shelf-life data in the intended base, (3) source/provenance documentation (critical for conditioned media), (4) endotoxin/sterility for biologic-derived material.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • "The new retinol." No. Retinoids have decades of independent, vehicle-controlled evidence and clear structure-function effects. GFs have a smaller, more heterogeneous, largely manufacturer-funded topical record. The comparison is marketing, not science.
  • "Stimulates collagen / regenerates skin." Drug-style structure-function claim; supported mainly by in-vitro and wound data, not by intact-skin cosmetic trials. Off-limits as written.
  • "Clinically proven." Usually means a single sponsor's vehicle-controlled study of its own product, often a conditioned-media cocktail with other actives — attribution to the GF itself is unclear, and three comparative RCTs found products indistinguishable [1].
  • "Targets the hallmarks of aging." Traces to one manufacturer paper's gene-expression/biopsy data [5] — promising, not established.
  • Penetration silence. Most marketing never addresses the ~500-Da problem; the honest position is that delivery is the unsolved core issue (Martínez-Carpio "risk or fraud?" [15]).
  • Where hype meets reality: there is a real, modest cosmetic signal for texture/fine-lines from well-formulated GF/secretome serums — but it's small, possibly co-ingredient/placebo-inflated, and oversold.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️/❌ — Lean SKIP (revisit only as a substantiated SUPPORTING actor).

One-line reasoning: The "premium growth-factor" cachet doesn't earn its keep against a hard penetration ceiling (~6 kDa vs. ~500 Da), a modest and overwhelmingly manufacturer-funded human topical record, a published "risk or fraud?" critique we'd have to rebut, and conditioned-media sourcing that drags us into the exosome regulatory gray zone — a bad trade for a brand whose moat is honesty.

Longevity-relevance note: Conceptually on-thesis (ECM repair, the senescence/intercellular-communication framing in [5] is genuinely longevity-flavored), and the barrier-friendly pairing profile fits our barrier-repair core. If a "renewal/regeneration" hero is wanted, better-evidenced, cleaner-story options exist first — well-substantiated copper peptides, established peptide signalers, and (for the senolytic angle) a vetted senotherapeutic — all with fewer penetration, sourcing, and claims headaches.

If ever revisited, require: a single, well-characterized recombinant factor (prefer barley-EGF provenance) in a validated encapsulated, potency-assayed, stability-tested system, positioned strictly as supporting with cosmetic-appearance claims only — never a hero, never a regenerative/wound claim.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19 by independent re-check of the primary literature (PubMed abstracts, PMC full texts where reachable, and FDA/manufacturer records). Goal: catch claims that quietly borrow in-vitro/animal/wound/oral data to support a leave-on cosmetic, citations that don't exist or are mischaracterized, and small/industry-funded studies dressed up as proof.

Overall evidence grade: C (weak / indirect for the cosmetic claim that matters)

For the decision-relevant question — does a leave-on topical GF cosmetic measurably rejuvenate intact, aging human skin? — the honest grade is C. There is a real but modest human-topical signal (which keeps it out of D/"unproven hype"), but that signal is (a) carried mostly by undefined conditioned-media cocktails with other actives, not single defined GFs; (b) overwhelmingly manufacturer-funded; (c) inflated by participant > investigator self-report; and (d) shadowed by an unsolved ~500-Da penetration ceiling and a published "risk or fraud?" challenge to the premise. The strongest mechanistic tiers (in-vitro collagen induction, wound healing) are genuinely A-grade — but for the wrong question (a dish or a wound, not a cosmetic on intact skin). The dossier's own §4–5 framing already grades this correctly; this verification confirms it rather than overturning it.

What I verified (citations are REAL and accurately characterized)

  • [1] Quinlan, Ghanem, Hassan 2023 (J Cosmet Dermatol 22(7):2023–2039, PMID 37222303) — confirmed: 33 studies, 9 RCTs + 24 uncontrolled case series, 1,180 participants, 23 preparations; investigator medians (texture <50%, fine lines <35%, overall <20%); participant scores > investigator; 3 comparative RCTs found no significant between-treatment difference; low AE risk; durability beyond 6 months unknown. Accurately reported. Note the review's own verdict is a hedged "appears to be effective," driven heavily by uncontrolled case series — the dossier represents this honestly.
  • [5] Naughton et al. 2022 (Dermatol Ther, PMC9823186) — confirmed real; product TNS Advanced+; 24-week, randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled crossover; 66/68 completers (45 active / 23 placebo); biopsies 20 active / 10 placebo; active beat vehicle on coarse/fine lines, photodamage, hyperpigmentation, sagging at wks 4–12; perceived age median −6 yr at wk 12; preclinical ↓H2A.J + ↑proteasome/autophagy/connexin/collagen/elastin genes. Funding: Allergan Aesthetics (AbbVie); authors are AbbVie/Histogen employees with stock. COI accurately disclosed. One correction made: the dossier had called this "split-face" — it is not split-face (parallel arms + crossover); fixed in §4(b).
  • [6] Araújo et al. 2019 (Skin Pharmacol Physiol 32(5):275–282, PMID 31352445) — confirmed: a mechanistic review (not clinical), describing FGF→FGFR tyrosine-kinase→MAPK/ERK and collagen/elastin synthesis. Correctly cited as mechanism, not clinical proof.
  • [7] Wu & Goldman 2017 (J Clin Aesthet Dermatol, PMID 28670356) — confirmed: n=20, 3 mo, randomized double-blind split-face, fibroblast-derived vs adipose-MSC-derived GF cocktails; both improved wrinkling, no significant between-product difference; no vehicle arm. Accurately reported (this IS the split-face study).
  • [8] Schouest, Luu, Moy 2012 (J Drugs Dermatol, PMID 22527430) — confirmed: open-label, single-center, n=29 women, ages 39–75, 3 mo, barley rhEGF serum; significant fine-line/texture/pore gains within first month; no control, subjective grading. Weak design, accurately flagged.
  • [15] Martínez-Carpio 2023 (Cutan Ocul Toxicol 42(4):190–197) — confirmed real; argues sh-oligopeptide-1 clinical trials "have no scientific basis," preclinical bioactivity unproven, "risk or fraud?" Accurately characterized; the dossier correctly treats it as a critique to rebut.
  • [17] Slade, Lynch, Dickerson 2025 (Wound Repair Regen, DOI 10.1111/wrr.70108) — confirmed real. Independently verified the surrounding facts: Regranex approved 1997; boxed warning added ~2008 for ≥3 tubes/day based on incomplete insurance-claims math; removed Nov 2018 after VA cohort of 6,429 vs 6,429 (and additional postmarketing studies) showed no increased cancer incidence/mortality; up to 140 daily applications shown safe. Accurately reported. Caveat the dossier already states: this is wounds, PDGF (not EGF) — reassuring precedent, not direct cosmetic-EGF safety proof.
  • Penetration premise — independently confirmed: human EGF ≈ 6,045 Da / 53 aa, ~12× the ~500-Da passive-permeation ceiling; <1% of topically applied EGF reaches viable epidermis through intact skin; penetration rises 10–100× only in wounded skin. The dossier's central "Achilles' heel" thesis is well-grounded.
  • "Only FDA-approved growth factor" — confirmed: becaplermin/Regranex (rhPDGF-BB, yeast-expressed, 1997) remains the only FDA-approved GF drug; cosmetic GFs are unapproved cosmetic ingredients. Accurate.

Main caveats (the honest reader's warnings)

  1. Human-topical evidence is thin and borrowed-adjacent. The robust data (Tiers 1–2) are in-vitro and wound models — strong for mechanism, irrelevant to delivery through intact skin. The dossier resists the common trap of laundering dish/wound data into cosmetic proof; keep it that way.
  2. Industry funding dominates the positive topical record. The single most-cited "it works" RCT [5] is an AbbVie study of AbbVie's flagship, authored by AbbVie/Histogen employees. The SkinMedica TNS lineage [3][4][20] is the same owner. Treat "clinically proven" as "the maker's own vehicle-controlled study" until independent replication exists.
  3. Small n + soft endpoints + self-report. Key studies are n=20–29 (or ~45 active), several open-label/uncontrolled, with subjective grading; participant scores systematically exceed investigator scores ([1]) — a classic expectation/placebo tell.
  4. Attribution problem. "Wins" come mostly from undefined multi-GF/conditioned-media cocktails with other actives; comparative RCTs found products indistinguishable ([1],[7]) — consistent with the GF not being the active driver. Single-defined-GF cosmetic efficacy is weakly supported.
  5. Secondary/unreachable sources remain hedged, correctly. [2] (PMC8423211), [3]/[4] (TNS lineage), [20] (Mehta/Fitzpatrick 2008) are abstract-/secondary-confirmed or "verify exact citation" — do not quote specific figures from these without retrieving the primary text. [14] and [15] full texts are paywalled/403; claims rest on verified abstracts.

Claims that should be softened (and were, or are flagged)

  • CORRECTED in-file: Naughton 2022 [5] mislabeled as "split-face" → fixed to randomized double-blind vehicle-controlled crossover (parallel arms). This mattered because "split-face" implies tighter within-subject control than the study actually used.
  • Already appropriately hedged (no change needed, but reaffirmed):
  • "Proven-ish for topical human use" (§4 summary) — keep the "-ish"; it is a C-grade, manufacturer-weighted, small-effect signal, not B/A.
  • §3 multi-hallmark plausibility table — senescence/proteostasis/stem-cell rows are correctly marked Weak / gene-expression-only / one industry study. Do not upgrade.
  • Any future marketing must not promote the in-vitro "induces type I collagen/elastin" ([6]) or wound-healing ([11],[17]) data as if they were intact-skin cosmetic outcomes. The dossier's §10 hype-vs-evidence section already polices this; the verification endorses it.
  • Watch phrasing for "best-supported clinical endpoint" (photoaging, §3): defensible relative to other hallmarks, but the absolute effect is small (Quinlan medians <20–50%) and vehicle-controlled mostly within manufacturer trials — keep "moderate," never "strong."

Net

The dossier is unusually honest and its citations check out. No fabricated or materially mischaracterized sources were found; one design-label error (split-face) was corrected. The overall Lean SKIP / conditional-supporting verdict is well-supported by the evidence. Confidence in the C grade: high.
