Ingredient: Adapalene (INCI: Adapalene)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ rejected (as a brand actives candidate) — see Verdict Class: Retinoids & retinoid alternatives (third-generation, receptor-selective synthetic retinoid; a drug — OTC in the US at 0.1%, Rx at 0.3%; not a cosmetic ingredient)

Headline for the brand, up front. Adapalene is the gentle, photostable, receptor-selective member of the prescription-retinoid family — and it occupies an unusual regulatory middle ground: in the US the 0.1% gel has been an over-the-counter (OTC) drug since 2016 (Differin), the first retinoid to make that switch, while the 0.3% gel stays prescription-only; in the EU/UK it remains a prescription medicine. "OTC" is the trap word here: OTC does not mean cosmetic. Adapalene is regulated as a drug everywhere it is sold, under an FDA new-drug approval for the acne indication — we cannot put it in a cosmetic. This dossier documents it because (1) it is the retinoid most often confused with a cosmetic by consumers ("you can buy it at the drugstore, so it must be like a serum"), which is a claims-discipline landmine; (2) its evidence base for acne is genuinely deep, but its evidence base for anti-aging/photoaging is thinner and weaker than tretinoin's or even retinol's — an honest distinction the marketing world routinely blurs; and (3) its real lesson for us is the adapalene-ester precursor route (oleyl adapalenate / "adapinoid"), a 2024 cosmetic-leaning prodrug that points at where the category is heading. See siblings tretinoin.md (the gold-standard benchmark), tazarotene.md, and the cosmetic-legal actives retinoids.md and bakuchiol.md.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Adapalene
  • Brand / trade names: Differin (gel 0.1% OTC and 0.3% Rx; cream 0.1%; lotion 0.1%), Epiduo / Epiduo Forte (adapalene + benzoyl peroxide 0.1%/2.5% and 0.3%/2.5%), Tactupump, plus generics. Developed by Galderma (Switzerland/Nestlé Skin Health lineage).
  • INCI name: Adapalene
  • Chemical name (IUPAC): 6-[3-(1-adamantyl)-4-methoxyphenyl]naphthalene-2-carboxylic acid (a naphthoic-acid derivative)
  • Molecular formula / weight: C₂₈H₂₈O₃ / ~412.5 g·mol⁻¹
  • CAS: 106685-40-9
  • Physical: yellowish-white powder; lipophilic (log P ≈ 6); poorly water-soluble.
  • Class: Third-generation, receptor-selective synthetic retinoid ("retinoid-like" naphthoic-acid derivative). Unlike retinol/retinaldehyde/retinyl esters (which must be enzymatically converted up the vitamin-A ladder to retinoic acid), adapalene is not a vitamin-A molecule at all — it is a designed-from-scratch synthetic that mimics retinoic-acid receptor binding directly. It is not a prodrug in the metabolic-conversion sense; it is active as applied. (Contrast tazarotene, which is a prodrug → tazarotenic acid.)
  • Key related form / "sibling" relevant to us: Oleyl adapalenate (a.k.a. "adapinoid") — an ester of adapalene with oleyl alcohol, marketed as a cosmetic-leaning active by Actera Ingredients and positioned as a precursor/prodrug that converts to adapalene in the epidermis (see §4). This is the only adapalene-adjacent material with any plausible cosmetic narrative — and even it inherits adapalene's drug-status question.

2. Class & mechanism

Adapalene is a nuclear-receptor agonist that engages the retinoic-acid signaling pathway, but with a deliberately narrowed receptor profile:

  • Receptor selectivity. Adapalene binds the nuclear retinoic-acid receptors RAR-β and RAR-γ with high affinity and has essentially no binding to RAR-α or to the retinoid-X receptors (RXRs) and no binding to cytosolic retinoic-acid-binding proteins. RAR-γ is the dominant RAR in epidermis, and agonism of the γ subtype is largely responsible for adapalene's clinical effects (co-administration of a RAR-γ antagonist abolishes its efficacy). The narrowed profile is the design rationale for its comparatively lower irritation vs pan-RAR tretinoin (which hits α/β/γ) — fewer off-target retinoid pathways are triggered. (StatPearls; Differin label; Wikipedia compilation of label data.)
  • Genomic action. Ligand-bound RAR–RXR heterodimers act as transcription factors at retinoic-acid response elements, switching keratinocyte growth/differentiation genes on and off — normalizing the disordered follicular keratinization that drives microcomedone formation (the acne mechanism), and, in photoaged skin, promoting more orderly epidermal differentiation.
  • AP-1 antagonism (the anti-aging-relevant arm — shared retinoid mechanism, weaker direct adapalene proof). Activated RAR transrepresses the AP-1 (c-Jun/c-Fos) transcription complex. AP-1 is the UV-triggered master switch for matrix metalloproteinases (MMP-1/3/9) that fragment dermal collagen, and it also suppresses TGF-β/procollagen synthesis. Damping AP-1 → less collagen breakdown + de-repressed collagen synthesis. This is the canonical retinoid-class rationale (Fisher/Voorhees photoaging work, established for tretinoin) — but the direct, adapalene-specific molecular demonstration in human skin is far thinner than tretinoin's. Adapalene is assumed to share it by class membership and by its clinical wrinkle/lentigines signal, not because the MMP/procollagen cascade has been nailed down for adapalene the way it has for tretinoin (see §4 honesty notes).
  • Anti-inflammatory & antioxidant-adjacent actions (well-characterized, mostly acne-relevant). Adapalene inhibits chemotaxis of polymorphonuclear leukocytes, dampens the lipoxygenase (15-LOX) / arachidonic-acid inflammatory pathway, down-regulates toll-like receptor 2 (TLR-2) signaling, and reduces oxidative metabolism of arachidonic acid — anti-inflammatory effects described as comparable in some assays to betamethasone-valerate/indomethacin. These matter for acne and plausibly for "inflammaging," but the inflammaging link is mechanistic inference, not a proven topical anti-aging endpoint.
  • Photochemical stability (a genuine differentiator). Unlike tretinoin and tazarotene (both photolabile — they isomerize/degrade in light), adapalene is chemically and photochemically stable, tolerates daylight, and is compatible with benzoyl peroxide (it does not oxidatively degrade the way older tretinoin does). This is its standout formulation property.

Bottom line on hallmarks: adapalene's real hallmark wins are pigmentation/tone and (modestly) fine wrinkles, plus a credible anti-inflammatory mechanism. It is not a barrier, hydration, glycation, senescence, mitochondrial, or microbiome ingredient — and like its retinoid siblings it opposes the barrier-repair thesis short-term.

4. Evidence review by tier (core of the dossier)

The honest framing for this molecule. Adapalene has a deep, pharmaceutical-grade evidence base for ACNE (multicenter RCTs, meta-analyses, ~20 years of post-marketing data). It does not have a comparably deep base for anti-aging / photoaging — that body of work is a handful of trials, several small, one of which is a non-inferiority comparison against the lowest tretinoin strength. The single most important thing to carry forward: do not let adapalene's acne evidence be silently re-used as anti-aging evidence. They are different endpoints with very different depths of proof.

Tier 1 — In-vitro / molecular (mechanism only; not for efficacy claims)

  • Receptor-binding & selectivity characterization. Adapalene binds RAR-β/γ, not RAR-α or RXRs; γ-agonism drives efficacy (RAR-γ-antagonist co-treatment abolishes it). Establishes the mechanism and the lower-irritation rationale. Limitation: receptor pharmacology, not clinical efficacy. (Summarized in StatPearls and the Differin label.)
  • Anti-inflammatory pathway work. Adapalene inhibits PMN chemotaxis, the 15-lipoxygenase/arachidonic-acid pathway, neutrophil ROS, and TLR-2 signaling. Limitation: cell/assay-level; relevant to acne and "inflammaging" only as inference.
  • AP-1 / MMP transrepression is assumed by class (well-shown for all-trans retinoic acid; Fisher GJ et al., Nature 1996; NEJM 1997). Honesty flag: I did not find a clean, adapalene-specific human-skin dataset quantifying MMP-1 suppression + type-I-procollagen induction the way Griffiths/Voorhees did for tretinoin. Treat the collagen-synthesis mechanism for adapalene as inferred, not directly proven. [partly unverified for adapalene specifically]

Tier 2 — Animal

  • Dermal photo-co-carcinogenicity (the notable safety-relevant animal finding). In hairless-mouse studies, topical retinoids of this class (including adapalene-vehicle work cited on the FDA label) were associated with an increased tumorigenic response to UV — i.e., faster onset of UV-induced skin tumors. This is the basis of the label's "minimize sun exposure" warning. This is a SAFETY signal from animals, not an efficacy finding, and its human relevance is uncertain — but it belongs in any honest read. [label-sourced; magnitude figures not independently re-verified here — treat as directional]
  • Reproductive toxicity: oral adapalene in rats/rabbits at high systemic doses produced developmental effects (basis of the historical Pregnancy Category C). Topical systemic exposure is far below these doses (see §7). Safety, not efficacy.
  • No animal model meaningfully extends the anti-aging efficacy story beyond the human data.

Tier 3 — Human, ORAL

  • Not applicable. Adapalene is a topical-only drug; there is no oral adapalene anti-aging (or any oral adapalene) literature to borrow from. This cleanly avoids the common cosmetic trap of importing oral-supplement data into topical claims.

Tier 4a — Human, TOPICAL, RCT (the load-bearing tier — and for ANTI-AGING it is modest)

Anti-aging / photoaging trials:

  1. Kang S, Goldfarb MT, Weiss JS, et al. 2003 — Assessment of adapalene gel for the treatment of actinic keratoses and lentigines: a randomized trial. J Am Acad Dermatol 49(1):83–90. PMID 12833014. Design: prospective, 2-center, randomized, investigator-masked, vehicle-controlled, parallel-group; adapalene gel 0.1% or 0.3% vs vehicle, n=90, daily ×4 wk then twice-daily up to 9 months. Results: mean actinic-keratosis count fell 0.5±0.9 (0.1%) and 2.5±0.9 (0.3%) vs an increase of 1.5±1.3 with vehicle (p<0.05); solar lentigines and other photodamage features improved; a treatment-blinded retrospective photo evaluation found significant improvement in wrinkles and other photoaging signs vs vehicle. Well tolerated. This is the foundational adapalene-photoaging RCT — but n=90, two centers, and the wrinkle endpoint was a retrospective photo read, not the prespecified primary outcome. Industry-associated (Galderma).
  2. Bagatin E, Gonçalves HS, Sato M, et al. 2018 — Comparable efficacy of adapalene 0.3% gel and tretinoin 0.05% cream as treatment for cutaneous photoaging. Eur J Dermatol 28(3):343–350. Design: randomized, investigator-blinded, parallel-group, n≈86 photoaged women (Brazil), once daily, 24 weeks, with histopathology + digital morphometry. Results: no significant difference between adapalene 0.3% and tretinoin 0.05% on global photoaging, periorbital/forehead wrinkles, ephelides/melanosis, or actinic keratoses → non-inferiority of adapalene 0.3% to low-strength tretinoin, with similar safety. Honest caveats: (a) it's non-inferiority against the LOWEST common tretinoin strength (0.05%), not vs 0.1% or vs vehicle — "as good as a weak comparator" is weaker than "beats placebo by X"; (b) investigator-blinded only, modest n; (c) histology/morphometry showed the expected epidermal/pigment changes but the dermal collagen signal was not a clean, large win — consistent with retinoids' generally modest histologic collagen effects. Industry-associated (Galderma).
  3. Tran QT, et al. 2025 — Effectiveness and tolerability of adapalene cream 0.1% in the treatment of female skin ageing: a randomised controlled trial. Indian J Dermatol Venereol Leprol 91(5):571–576. PMID 40990960. Design: RCT, adapalene cream 0.1% vs no-treatment control, n=58 completers, 6 months. Results: total Skin Aging Score fell 38.2→32.5 (treatment) vs 38.5→37.9 (control); between-arm difference −5.40 (95% CI −7.26 to −3.54; p<0.001); pigmentation and wrinkles improved significantly. Side effects: stinging 64.3%, xerosis 25%, mostly resolving by month 2. Caveats: small, single-center, short, and crucially the control arm was NO TREATMENT (not vehicle) — so part of the "effect" is the act-of-applying-something + unblinding, not necessarily the drug. A no-vehicle control substantially weakens causal attribution.
  4. Nguyen N, … Sivamani RK. 2024 — A prospective, double-blinded, randomized head-to-head clinical trial of topical adapinoid (oleyl adapalenate) versus retinol. Skin Health Dis 4(6):e469. PMC11608876. Design: double-blind, randomized, parallel-group; oleyl adapalenate (OA, an adapalene ester / precursor) 0.5% vs retinol 0.5%, n=48 enrolled / 39 completers, 12 weeks. Results: OA improved wrinkle severity 9.45% vs retinol's 4.11% (between-group p=0.001, favoring OA); pigment improvement comparable (OA 3.88% vs retinol 3.15%, p=0.62, NS); OA reduced erythema 13.4% and lowered TEWL ~15% while retinol raised TEWL ~21% at wk 4; OA better tolerated (wk-12 burning 5% vs 21%). Caveats: single-site, 12 wk, predominantly Fitzpatrick I–III, n=39 completers, and it is industry-funded by Actera Ingredients (the OA maker) with an advisory-board author — exactly the sponsor-favorable design to read cautiously. Why it matters to us: OA is the closest thing to a cosmetic-positioned adapalene-route active, and the better-TEWL/lower-erythema profile is the interesting signal — but it is one small sponsored trial of a novel precursor, not adapalene itself, and the regulatory status of an adapalene-releasing ester is unsettled.

Acne RCTs (deep tier — but ACNE, not anti-aging; included to show where the real evidence weight sits):

  1. Adapalene vs tretinoin for acne — multiple multicenter RCTs and reviews show comparable efficacy with better tolerability for adapalene (lower discontinuation: ~1.3% adapalene vs ~2.4% tretinoin in pooled data). Onset of lesion reduction as early as week 1.
  2. Adapalene–benzoyl peroxide (Epiduo) fixed-dose combo — three multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled studies, n=3,855, demonstrated significant IGA success and reductions in total/inflammatory/non-inflammatory lesions by week 12 (decreases as early as week 1). Meta-analysis (Cao et al. 2014, PLoS One; PMC3956794) of six studies confirmed superiority of the combo over vehicle for success rate and satisfaction. This is genuine pharmaceutical-grade evidence — for ACNE.
  3. Atrophic acne scars (collagen-adjacent signal). Loss MM, et al. 2018 (PMC6002315) — open-label, single-center, n=20 (16 followed up), adapalene 0.3% gel ×24 wk: atrophic-scar grade improved (ANOVA p=0.02), scar count fell 18.7→11.8, with non-significant increases in procollagen-3 IHC and COL1A1/COL3A1 expression. Read honestly: open-label, tiny, and the collagen markers did NOT reach significance — a hint of dermal remodeling, not proof.

Tier 4b — Lower-quality human topical (open-label, retrospective)

  • Retrospective photo evaluations and open-label series report adapalene gel improving mottled hyperpigmentation and fine wrinkles over ~9 months (e.g., the Kang-extension retrospective read). Consistent with the RCTs but not independent high-tier evidence.

Synthesis: where adapalene sits in the photoaging evidence hierarchy

Lin et al. 2025 — Comparative efficacy of topical interventions for facial photoaging: a network meta-analysis. Sci Rep 15 (July 2025). 23 RCTs, 3,905 participants. For fine wrinkles, ranking ran roughly isotretinoin (OR ~116) > retinol (~14) > adapalene (OR ~9.29, 95% CI 0.95–90.92, p=0.055 — not statistically significant, very wide CI) > tretinoin (~6.9) > tazarotene (~4.0). The authors' verdict on adapalene: it "may offer a lower irritation profile, but its clinical effectiveness for photoaging appears less robust." Honest read of that OR: adapalene's point estimate looks high, but the confidence interval crosses 1 and it failed significance — i.e., the network MA could not confirm an adapalene fine-wrinkle benefit; the wide CI reflects how few and small the adapalene photoaging trials are. Tretinoin's own SR/MA (Huang & Lee 2025) rests on 8 RCTs / 1,361 patients for tretinoin alone — an order of magnitude more anti-aging evidence than adapalene has.

Bottom line by tier for ANTI-AGING: adapalene's anti-aging benefits (pigment > fine wrinkles) are directly studied in human topical use but on a thin, mostly industry-associated, small-trial base, with one non-inferiority vs weak tretinoin and a network MA that calls the effect "less robust" and statistically unconfirmed for wrinkles. This is NOT borrowed from oral/animal data (good) — but it is far weaker than tretinoin's or retinol's anti-aging evidence, and the molecule's deep evidence belongs to acne, a different endpoint.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Penetrates the epidermis effectively (lipophilic, log P ≈ 6; concentrates in follicles — part of its acne mechanism) and acts locally on RAR-β/γ. It does not require enzymatic conversion (it is active as applied).
  • Systemic absorption is very low — the reassuring, OTC-enabling fact. Plasma levels after topical use are typically below the limit of quantification (label studies report <0.35 ng/mL, with chronic-use levels around 0.25 ng/mL), and clearance from plasma within ~72 h. An FDA large-surface-area, maximal-use absorption study supported the Rx→OTC switch for the 0.1% gel in 2016. (These are drug findings, relevant to understanding the molecule — not cosmetic claims.)
  • Formulation modulates tolerability/delivery: lotion and cream vehicles, and the adapalene-ester (OA) prodrug approach, aim to soften irritation while preserving delivery — the same delivery-engineering logic the brand applies to cosmetic retinoids.

6. Formulation (reference only — not for our products)

  • Effective concentrations (drug): 0.1% (gel/cream/lotion; OTC gel in US) and 0.3% (gel; Rx). Higher % → modestly more efficacy and irritation. Combos: adapalene 0.1% or 0.3% + benzoyl peroxide 2.5%.
  • pH: retinoids do not require low pH; formulated near skin-compatible pH.
  • Stability — adapalene's stand-out strength: photostable and chemically stable (vs photolabile tretinoin/tazarotene); compatible with benzoyl peroxide (no oxidative knock-down). For a formulator this is genuinely attractive — if it were a usable cosmetic ingredient, which it is not.
  • Pairings/conflicts (drug context): pairs well with benzoyl peroxide (unusual for a retinoid). General retinoid layering caution still applies with strong AHA/BHA and high-strength vitamin C (cumulative irritation). Can be used AM or PM given photostability, though sun protection is still required.
  • Sensory: gentler than tretinoin/tazarotene but still an irritant — dryness, scaling, stinging, erythema, especially weeks 1–4 ("retinization"). The Tran 2025 RCT logged stinging 64%, xerosis 25% at 0.1% cream.

7. Safety

  • Irritation (dominant issue, but milder than other Rx retinoids): dryness, erythema, scaling, burning/stinging — label data put a brief warmth/stinging sensation in roughly 1–10% of users early on, usually resolving within ~4 weeks; real-world tolerability is better than tretinoin/tazarotene (lower discontinuation), which is adapalene's selling point. Still net-irritant short-term.
  • Photosensitivity & a photo-co-carcinogenicity flag: the drug increases UV sensitivity and animal (hairless-mouse) data showed an increased UV-tumorigenic response with retinoids of this class — hence the label's "avoid/minimize sun, use sunscreen" instruction. Use sun protection regardless. (Animal signal; human relevance uncertain.)
  • Pregnancy / teratogenicity: historically Pregnancy Category C; oral adapalene was developmentally toxic in animals at high systemic doses. Topical systemic absorption is minimal. The largest human dataset — Panchaud et al. 2012 (European Network of Teratology Information Services, J Clin Pharmacol; PMID 22174426), 235 first-trimester topical-retinoid-exposed pregnancies vs 444 controls — found no significant increase in spontaneous abortion or major/minor malformations. However: there is an isolated case report of anophthalmia after topical adapalene (causality unproven), and the standard, conservative guidance is to avoid topical retinoids in pregnancy/breastfeeding (UKTIS; FDA caution). For us: any retinoid-adjacent messaging needs a pregnancy caution + "consult your doctor."
  • Sensitization: true allergic contact sensitization is uncommon; reactions are usually irritant. Rare hypersensitivity (facial/lip/eyelid swelling) reported.
  • Sourcing/sustainability: fully synthetic; low allergen profile; unremarkable environmental footprint. (Largely moot for us — see §9.)

8. Regulatory & claims (the decisive section)

  • Adapalene is a DRUG, not a cosmetic — "OTC" does not change that.
  • US (FDA): approved as a new drug for acne vulgaris. The 0.1% gel is OTC since July 2016 (first Rx→OTC retinoid switch; Galderma/Differin); 0.3% gel and combos remain prescription. OTC status means it can be sold without a prescription as a nonprescription drug — it is not a cosmetic ingredient, and its approval/indication is acne, not anti-aging. A cosmetic skincare product cannot lawfully contain adapalene as an active (it would be an unapproved new drug / misbranded). The photoaging use is off-label even for the drug.
  • EU / UK: prescription medicine. Because it's a medicine, it falls outside the EU Cosmetics Regulation entirely (it is not on Annex II/III because medicines aren't cosmetics) — i.e., it cannot be used in an EU/UK cosmetic. (Separately, the EU now caps cosmetic retinol at 0.05% face / 0.3% body — a different, cosmetic-legal molecule; see retinoids.md.)
  • The instructive claims line for the brand: even though a consumer can buy adapalene off the shelf in a US drugstore, we may not (a) put it in our cosmetic, (b) imply our cosmetic "works like Differin / adapalene," or (c) borrow adapalene's drug outcomes ("clears acne," "reduces actinic keratoses," "treats photoaging") for a cosmetic active. The OTC-availability fact makes this more dangerous, not less — consumers conflate "drugstore retinoid" with "cosmetic," and our copy must not encourage that. Stay in cosmetic register for our actual actives: "helps visibly smooth the look of fine lines," "supports skin's natural renewal," "improves the appearance of uneven tone." See ../../brand/glossary.md and the CLAUDE.md claims guardrail.
  • The oleyl-adapalenate (adapinoid) gray zone: marketed as a cosmetic active, but it is designed to release adapalene (a drug) in skin — a structure-function argument a regulator could treat as delivering an unapproved drug. Do not adopt it without specific regulatory counsel. Logged as a watch-item, not a green light.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • As a raw material: synthetic, mature, inexpensive per gram (off-patent API; generics widespread). Irrelevant to us as a buyer — we cannot formulate a cosmetic with it without becoming a drug manufacturer (NDA/OTC-drug pathway, GMP).
  • The transferable lesson: adapalene's real gift to a formulator is its photostability + BPO compatibility — properties we'd love in a cosmetic retinoid. We pursue that durability instead through encapsulated retinol/retinaldehyde and stable alternatives (see retinoids.md, hydroxypinacolone-retinoate.md, bakuchiol.md). Stabilization + packaging remain the cost driver for any retinoid SKU.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • Honest strengths: for acne, the evidence is real and deep; it is the gentlest, most photostable, BPO-compatible prescription-class retinoid; pigment/fine-wrinkle anti-aging signal is real if modest; systemic absorption is negligibly low.
  • Where the category / consumer narrative overreaches:
  • "It's OTC, so it's basically a cosmetic/serum." False. OTC = nonprescription drug, approved for acne, not a cosmetic and not approved for aging.
  • "Adapalene is a proven anti-aging powerhouse like tretinoin." Overstated. Its anti-aging evidence is a handful of small/industry-associated trials; the 2025 network MA found its fine-wrinkle effect statistically unconfirmed and "less robust" than tretinoin/retinol, and the headline Bagatin result is non-inferiority vs the weakest tretinoin (0.05%), not superiority over placebo.
  • "Adapalene rebuilds collagen." Weak. The dermal-collagen/histology signal (Bagatin morphometry; Loss scar study) is modest and largely non-significant — far short of the tretinoin procollagen-restoration data. Treat collagen mechanism as inferred by class.
  • "Buy a drugstore adapalene to anti-age." This is off-label drug use; the brand should never route customers there or imply equivalence to our cosmetics.
  • Oleyl adapalenate ("adapinoid") as a clean cosmetic retinoid. One small sponsored trial; it's an adapalene-releasing prodrug with an unsettled drug-status question. Promising signal, not a validated, regulator-blessed cosmetic.

11. Verdict for the brand

❌ SKIP as an ingredient — adapalene is a DRUG (OTC or Rx depending on market/strength), approved for ACNE, not a cosmetic and not approved for aging — and even as a drug its anti-aging evidence is thin. One line: Adapalene is the gentle, photostable retinoid everyone can buy at the drugstore — which makes it a claims-discipline hazard (consumers think "drugstore retinoid = cosmetic"), and its actual anti-aging proof is far weaker than tretinoin's or retinol's; we keep it as a benchmark/watch-item, not an ingredient.

  • Longevity-relevance note: adapalene reinforces two brand lessons. (1) The photoaging hallmark (pigment + fine wrinkles) is partly addressable with receptor-selective retinoid signaling at lower irritation — validating the "renewal at tolerable cost" thesis we pursue with cosmetic-legal retinoids. (2) The adapalene-ester precursor route (oleyl adapalenate) is an early signal of where "gentler, more stable retinoid delivery" is heading; worth tracking, but the regulatory status of a drug-releasing ester must be cleared before anyone touches it. Actionable heroes live in retinoids.md (encapsulated retinol/retinaldehyde) and bakuchiol.md (sensitive/pregnancy-conscious). Log in ../../ideas/not-considering.md as an ingredient (reason: drug, not cosmetic-legal; acne-indicated; thin anti-aging evidence), with oleyl adapalenate noted as a regulatory watch-item.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified: 2026-06-19. Method: independent re-check of load-bearing citations against PubMed/PMC abstracts, the Differin FDA/DailyMed labels, the 2025 Scientific Reports network meta-analysis, and ENTIS/UKTIS pregnancy data. This section records what held up and what was softened.

Overall evidence grade: B− for ANTI-AGING; A for ACNE (different endpoint, not this brand's claim)

The split grade is the whole point. For acne, adapalene earns an A (large multicenter RCTs, a real meta-analysis, ~30 years of safety data, an FDA OTC switch backed by maximal-use PK). For anti-aging/photoaging — the only endpoint relevant to this brand — the grade is a B−: the human topical evidence is real and not borrowed from oral/animal tiers (good), but it is thin (Kang 2003 n=90 with a retrospective wrinkle read; Bagatin 2018 n≈86 non-inferiority vs weak tretinoin; Tran 2025 n=58 vs a no-treatment control; OA 2024 n=39 of a precursor, sponsor-funded), and the 2025 network MA could not statistically confirm an adapalene fine-wrinkle benefit (OR 9.29 but 95% CI 0.95–90.92, p=0.055) and explicitly called it "less robust." This is weaker anti-aging evidence than tretinoin or even retinol.

Citations checked and confirmed (claim matches source): - Identity — C₂₈H₂₈O₃, MW ~412.5, CAS 106685-40-9, naphthoic-acid/adamantyl retinoid, RAR-β/γ-selective, no RXR. ✔ - Kang 2003 (PMID 12833014) — n=90, 0.1%/0.3% vs vehicle, 9 mo; AK count −0.5/−2.5 vs +1.5 vehicle; retrospective photo read showed wrinkle/photoaging improvement. ✔ (the wrinkle endpoint being retrospective is correctly flagged.) - Bagatin 2018 (Eur J Dermatol) — adapalene 0.3% non-inferior to tretinoin 0.05% over 24 wk, n≈86, investigator-blinded, similar safety. ✔ (correctly framed as non-inferiority vs the lowest tretinoin strength, not vs vehicle.) - Tran 2025 (PMID 40990960) — adapalene 0.1% cream, n=58, 6 mo, SAS −5.40 (95% CI −7.26 to −3.54; p<0.001), stinging 64.3%/xerosis 25%. ✔ — and the no-treatment (not vehicle) control caveat is real and load-bearing. - Nguyen/Sivamani 2024 OA vs retinol (PMC11608876) — n=39 completers, 12 wk, wrinkle 9.45% vs 4.11% (p=0.001), pigment NS, TEWL/erythema favored OA; Actera-funded. ✔ - Lin 2025 network MA (Sci Rep) — 23 RCTs, 3,905 participants; adapalene fine-wrinkle OR 9.29 (95% CI 0.95–90.92, p=0.055, NS); "less robust." ✔ - Panchaud 2012 ENTIS (PMID 22174426) — 235 exposed vs 444 controls, no significant malformation increase. ✔ plus the isolated anophthalmia case report and conservative "avoid in pregnancy" stance. ✔ - Regulatory — US 0.1% gel OTC since July 2016 (first OTC retinoid; Galderma), 0.3% Rx; EU/UK prescription medicine; adapalene is a drug, not a cosmetic ingredient in all markets. ✔

Main caveats a skeptic should keep

  1. OTC ≠ cosmetic. The single biggest misread risk. Adapalene's US OTC status is for a nonprescription drug indicated for acne — it does not make adapalene cosmetic-legal and does not approve it for aging. The dossier leans hard on this.
  2. Anti-aging trials are few, small, and mostly industry-associated. No large independent vehicle-controlled adapalene photoaging RCT exists on the scale of tretinoin's. The "OR 9.29" looks impressive in isolation but is not statistically significant and rests on sparse data.
  3. Collagen mechanism is inferred, not proven for adapalene. The AP-1→MMP→procollagen story is solid for all-trans retinoic acid; for adapalene the human-skin collagen/histology signal (Bagatin morphometry, Loss scar IHC) is modest/non-significant. Flagged [unverified for adapalene specifically] where relevant.
  4. Oleyl adapalenate is a precursor, not adapalene, and not a settled cosmetic. One small sponsored trial; drug-status of an adapalene-releasing ester is unresolved. Treated as a watch-item, not a recommendation.

Net

The dossier's central claims are sound and honestly tiered: it separates adapalene's strong acne evidence from its weak anti-aging evidence, flags the OTC-≠-cosmetic trap, marks the collagen mechanism as inferred, and treats the OA precursor cautiously. The verdict (SKIP as ingredient — a drug, acne-indicated, thin anti-aging proof; keep as benchmark/watch-item) is well-supported. Anti-aging grade stands at B−.
