Ingredient: Idebenone (INCI: Hydroxydecyl Ubiquinone)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ⬜ considering / ✅ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Antioxidant (synthetic Coenzyme Q10 analogue) · Slug: idebenone

TL;DR for a busy reader. Idebenone is a synthetic, lower-molecular-weight cousin of CoQ10 that the manufacturer's lab work (McDaniel 2005) crowned the most powerful topical antioxidant ever ranked. An independent lab (Tournas/Pinnell 2006, J Invest Dermatol) then tested it head-to-head on pig skin and found it gives essentially no meaningful UV photoprotection versus a C+E+ferulic acid serum. The only human topical efficacy trial (n=41) was vehicle-uncontrolled and industry-linked (a randomized 0.5%-vs-1.0% comparison with blinded graders, but no placebo arm). It is a known contact allergen, is unstable/oxidation-prone, tints formulas orange-brown, and penetrates poorly without specialized carriers. The "95/100 antioxidant champion" marketing comes from one biased study; the honest read is promising mechanism, thin and conflicted human evidence. See Verdict.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Idebenone (trade/branded as the active in Elizabeth Arden PREVAGE, originally Allergan).
  • INCI: Hydroxydecyl Ubiquinone.
  • Chemistry: 2,3-dimethoxy-5-methyl-6-(10-hydroxydecyl)-1,4-benzoquinone. CAS 58186-27-9. Synonyms: CV-2619, QSA-10. MW ≈ 338 g/mol.
  • Relationship to CoQ10: A synthetic short-chain analogue of ubiquinone (CoQ10, MW ≈ 863). Idebenone replaces CoQ10's long lipophilic isoprenoid tail with a short hydroxydecyl chain — lighter, less lipophilic, theoretically more able to cross membranes (it was developed as a brain-penetrant drug). [1][4][7]
  • Key forms/derivatives: Used as the free molecule. Because it is poorly soluble and unstable, cosmetic and pharma R&D wraps it in delivery systems — solid lipid nanoparticles (SLN), nanostructured lipid carriers (NLC), nanoemulsions, liposomes, cyclodextrin complexes, polymeric/chitosan nanoparticles — and chemists have made side-chain–modified analogues to raise potency/bioavailability. None of these is a standard off-the-shelf cosmetic INCI yet. [7]
  • Regulatory identity note: Idebenone is also an approved drug (oral, Raxone®/EU) for Leber hereditary optic neuropathy — relevant to claims discipline (§8).

2. Class & mechanism

Idebenone is a redox-active benzoquinone antioxidant and mitochondrial electron carrier. Proposed skin-level mechanisms:

  1. Free-radical scavenging / lipid-peroxidation chain breaking. Like CoQ10, the quinone↔quinol couple can accept/donate electrons and quench reactive oxygen species (ROS) generated by UV and pollution. This is the headline claim. [1][4]
  2. Mitochondrial electron shuttle. It can feed electrons into the electron-transport chain (the basis of its neurology use), theoretically supporting cellular energy/ATP under oxidative stress. Skin relevance is largely extrapolated, not demonstrated. [4][7]
  3. Downstream anti-melanogenic / anti-glycation signals (in vitro). Cell studies report suppression of melanin synthesis and of glycation end-product formation (see §4). [1][5]

⚠️ Mechanistic caveat that matters for an evidence-led brand: idebenone is not an unconditionally clean antioxidant. Its protective effect depends on full two-electron reduction to idebenol; when only partially reduced it can form an unstable semiquinone that behaves as a pro-oxidant (it can inhibit Complex I and generate superoxide). The neurology literature explicitly debates whether it is "antioxidant, pro-oxidant, or electron carrier," context-dependent. As an untargeted antioxidant it also fails to concentrate at the subcellular sites where damage occurs, and skin-fibroblast work found it needed higher concentrations than CoQ10 to be cytoprotective. [6][9] This is the scientific crack under the marketing.

4. Evidence review by tier — the core of this dossier

Read this section as a ladder. The marketing lives at the bottom rungs (in-vitro, manufacturer-run). The top rung (independent human topical RCT) is essentially empty, and the one independent contradicting experiment (Tournas 2006) was run on pig skin, not human skin — and is unfavorable.

Tier 1 — In-vitro / biochemical

  • McDaniel, Neudecker, DiNardo, Lewis, Maibach (2005). "Idebenone: a new antioxidant — Part I. Relative assessment of oxidative stress protection capacity…" J Cosmet Dermatol 4(1):10–17. PMID 17134415. A multi-step assay battery (photochemiluminescence radical scavenging, UVB-irradiated human keratinocytes, pro-oxidant systems) plus an in-vivo sunburn-cell assay was combined into a single "Environmental Protection Factor (EPF)" 0–100 score. Idebenone scored 95, ahead of dl-α-tocopherol (80), kinetin (68), ubiquinone/CoQ10 (55), L-ascorbic acid (52), and α-lipoic acid (41). This single composite score is the origin of nearly all "most powerful antioxidant" marketing. ⚠️ Limitations: the EPF metric was invented for this paper (not validated/independent), authors are tied to the commercial developer, and a composite in-vitro index does not predict clinical benefit. [1]
  • Pulmonary-fibrosis fibroblast work: idebenone suppressed TGF-β–induced collagen production and fibroblast activity (lung model). Interesting biology, not a skin-rejuvenation result. [10]
  • Depigmentation screen: idebenone inhibited melanin synthesis in SK-MEL28 melanoma cells dose-dependently, reportedly more than hydroquinone with less cytotoxicity (in-vitro only). [5]
  • Mechanistic critique: semiquinone/pro-oxidant behaviour and Complex-I inhibition documented in bioenergetics literature. [6][9]

Tier 2 — Animal / ex-vivo skin

  • Ex-vivo skin permeation (pig/guinea-pig): SLN-loaded idebenone accumulated in stratum corneum + epidermis without permeating through the skin; NLCs increased skin deposition and improved chemical stability vs nanoemulsion/oil. (Montenegro/Fadda group, reviewed in [7].) Tells us where it sits, not that it works clinically.
  • Microneedle + bioadhesive nanoparticles, UV-damage model: "Microneedle-Assisted Topical Delivery of Idebenone-Loaded Bioadhesive Nanoparticles Protect against UV-Induced Skin Damage," Biomedicines 2023, 11(6):1649 (MDPI; PMID 37371744). Engineering study showing microneedles overcome the stratum-corneum barrier and reduce UV damage markers — i.e., plain topical idebenone barely gets in on its own; you need a device or advanced carrier. [2][8]

Tier 3 — Human ORAL (different format; for context only — DO NOT borrow these claims for a cream)

  • Friedreich's ataxia: dose-escalation/PK and Phase-3 trials at high oral doses (up to ~75 mg/kg; common ~900 mg/day). Cardiac-hypertrophy signals seen; neurological benefit inconsistent. PMID 17562928; JAMA Neurology Phase-3. Oral bioavailability is <1% (heavy first-pass). [3]
  • Leber hereditary optic neuropathy (LHON): idebenone (Raxone®) 900 mg/day is EMA-approved (2015); LEROS study (NCT02774005) reports vision stabilization. [3]
  • Relevance to skincare: establishes systemic safety at high doses and proves real mitochondrial bioactivity — but says nothing about topical anti-aging. Borrowing "clinically proven" from these is a classic format-transfer overreach.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL

(a) The supportive trial (uncontrolled, industry-linked): - McDaniel et al. (2005). "Clinical efficacy assessment in photodamaged skin of 0.5% and 1.0% idebenone." J Cosmet Dermatol 4(3):167–173. DOI 10.1111/j.1473-2165.2005.00305.x. n=41 women, 30–65, moderate photodamage; commercial 0.5% and 1.0% idebenone creams twice daily for 6 weeks (randomized between the two strengths, blinded expert graders). Reported improvements in roughness/dryness (~26% at 1.0%), hydration (~37%), fine lines/wrinkles (~29%) and global photodamage (~33%), with the 1.0% performing better than 0.5%. ⚠️ Serious limitations: no vehicle/placebo control (so vehicle moisturization is unaccounted for — the headline failure), small, short, only a dose-vs-dose comparison (no untreated/placebo arm), grader-rated, and tied to the commercial developer. This is the human topical efficacy citation — and it is weak. [11]

(b) The independent experiment that contradicts the hype — most important paper here (but note: animal skin, not human): - Tournas, Lin, Burch, Selim, Monteiro-Riviere, Zielinski, Pinnell (2006). "Ubiquinone, Idebenone, and Kinetin Provide Ineffective Photoprotection to Skin when Compared to a Topical Antioxidant Combination of Vitamins C and E with Ferulic Acid." J Invest Dermatol 126(5):1185–1187. PMID 16528359. (Independent academic group, Duke.) ⚠️ Substrate is pig skin, not human — solutions were applied to pig skin daily for 4 days, then UV-irradiated (1–5× MED, solar simulator), with day-5 endpoints of erythema, sunburn cells, and thymine dimers. Idebenone and ubiquinone gave only ~1–2× protection (0.5% idebenone afforded "slight" protection against thymine-dimer formation), whereas C+E+ferulic acid protected beyond 5× MED. Conclusion: idebenone is an ineffective topical photoprotectant relative to the gold-standard combo. [9] This is a peer-reviewed, independent (porcine-skin) rebuttal to the "95-score" narrative — but it is not human evidence, so it is a strong rebuttal of the photoprotection claim, not a human-efficacy verdict. There is still no independent human topical RCT showing idebenone beats a credible control.

Bottom line by tier: strong in-vitro (but manufacturer-run and methodologically self-serving) → animal/ex-vivo shows poor native penetration → robust oral drug data (irrelevant to creams) → one vehicle-uncontrolled human topical trial for, one independent pig-skin experiment against. No high-quality (RCT, vehicle-controlled, independent) human topical evidence currently substantiates idebenone's anti-aging claims — and the strongest contradicting data is itself animal, not human.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Poor on its own. High lipophilicity + very low water solubility (inherited from CoQ10 despite the shorter chain) mean low cosmetic bioavailability; ex-vivo studies show it lodges in stratum corneum/epidermis and does not permeate appreciably into dermis without help. [7]
  • Carrier-dependent. NLCs > nanoemulsions > plain oil for skin deposition and stability; microneedles markedly increase delivery. A premium brand using idebenone seriously would need an encapsulated/advanced-delivery format, not a simple oil dispersion. [2][7][8]
  • Implication: low molecular weight ≠ good skin delivery here. Penetration is a real formulation hurdle and a cost driver.

6. Formulation

  • Typical concentration: 0.5–1.0% (the trialed range; 1.0% > 0.5%). Marketed products commonly 0.5–1%. Higher is not better and worsens color/stability/cost.
  • pH: most stable ~5.0–7.0 (target ~5.5–6.5). [12]
  • Stability: oxidation- and heat-sensitive (degrades >40 °C); needs airless, opaque/light-blocking packaging, antioxidant system, ideally anhydrous or low-water base. Degraded product browns. [7][12]
  • Color/sensory: intrinsically light yellow/orange; tints formulas (does not stain skin at use levels). Lipophilic — suits oils/anhydrous serums, balms, creams. [12]
  • Pairings: logically with vitamin E and other lipophilic antioxidants; encapsulation strongly recommended.
  • Conflicts/cautions: unbuffered low-pH systems (e.g., strong L-ascorbic acid serums) sit outside its stability window — co-formulating with classic LAA is awkward. Avoid high heat in processing.

7. Safety

  • Contact allergy / sensitization — documented, not theoretical. Multiple published case reports of allergic contact dermatitis to idebenone in anti-aging creams confirmed by patch testing (e.g., Mc Aleer & Collins, Contact Dermatitis 2008;59(3):178–179, PMID 18759903; Natkunarajah & Ostlere case). Notably higher allergenicity profile than most antioxidants. [13]
  • Irritation: generally low at 0.5–1% in well-made formulas, but the sensitization signal is the standout concern.
  • CIR (US): the 2022 CIR review of ubiquinone ingredients (incl. Hydroxydecyl Ubiquinone) concluded the group "safe… in the present practices of use and concentration," while explicitly noting evidence that topical hydroxydecyl ubiquinone can cause allergic contact dermatitis. Concern partly mitigated by lack of complete impurity/use-concentration data and CoQ10's natural occurrence. [14]
  • Sun sensitivity: no evidence it increases photosensitivity; it is marketed as photoprotective (a claim independent data don't support — §4b). Not a sunscreen.
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: no topical-cosmetic safety data; standard "consult your doctor" boilerplate. (Distinct from the high-dose oral drug.)
  • Sourcing/sustainability: wholly synthetic (no animal/plant sourcing tension). Main quality risk is oxidative degradation in the supply chain, not provenance.

8. Regulatory & claims

  • Cosmetic vs. drug line — extra-sharp here. Idebenone is a licensed drug (oral, LHON). Topically as Hydroxydecyl Ubiquinone it is a cosmetic ingredient, but the dual status means antioxidant/"mitochondrial energy" language is easy to push into drug territory. Keep all copy structure/appearance-based.
  • ✅ Defensible cosmetic language: "antioxidant," "helps defend the look of skin against environmental/oxidative stress," "helps improve the appearance of fine lines and uneven tone," "for visibly smoother, more radiant-looking skin."
  • ⚠️ Risky / likely needs substantiation or legal review (and our data is thin): "most powerful antioxidant," "EPF 95 / 95% protection," "boosts collagen," "repairs sun damage / reverses photoaging," "protects against UV"/"photoprotection," "energizes skin cells / boosts mitochondria." The "EPF-95" stat is a manufacturer's composite in-vitro index, not a clinical or regulatory metric — using it in consumer copy is a substantiation liability, especially against the independent Tournas finding.
  • See ../CLAUDE.md guardrails and ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Tier: mid-to-premium raw material. Synthetic, made at scale (numerous Chinese API/cosmetic-grade suppliers; pharma-grade also available). Costs more than commodity antioxidants (niacinamide, basic vitamin C derivatives) but is not exotic. The real cost is in stabilization + advanced delivery + airless packaging needed to make it perform and not brown — i.e., it's a formulation-cost ingredient more than a raw-material-cost one.
  • Finished-product positioning is overwhelmingly premium/"clinical" (PREVAGE legacy), which is consistent with the brand's premium intent — if the evidence supported a hero claim, which it doesn't.

10. Hype vs. evidence

Marketing claim Reality
"The most powerful antioxidant — EPF 95/100, beats vitamins C & E." From one manufacturer-run study using a self-invented composite index (McDaniel 2005). An independent study (on pig skin) found it gives poor photoprotection vs C+E+ferulic acid (Tournas 2006). [1][9]
"Clinically proven anti-aging." The only topical trial is uncontrolled, n=41, 6 weeks, industry-linked. No vehicle-controlled independent RCT exists. [11]
"Penetrates better than CoQ10." Lower MW, yes — but ex-vivo data show it barely permeates without advanced carriers/microneedles. [2][7]
"Pure antioxidant power." Can act as a pro-oxidant (semiquinone) and inhibit Complex I in some conditions. [6][9]
"Mitochondrial energy for your skin." Borrows credibility from the oral LHON drug; no topical human data support it. [3]

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ CONDITIONAL — supporting at best; do NOT make it a hero. One line: A scientifically interesting CoQ10-analogue antioxidant whose strongest "proof" is a biased in-vitro composite score, undercut by the one independent (pig-skin) photoprotection test and undermined by allergenicity, instability, and poor native penetration — and with no independent human topical RCT either way; not a credibility-safe hero for an evidence-led premium brand.

  • If used: only as a supporting lipophilic antioxidant in an encapsulated/anhydrous, airless-packaged format at 0.5–1.0%, with no "most powerful / photoprotection / EPF" claims, and patch-test/sensitization messaging. Our better-substantiated antioxidant story already runs through vitamin C (THD) [see vitamin-c.md] and ergothioneine — idebenone adds marketing romance but not evidentiary strength.
  • Longevity relevance: the mechanism (mitochondrial electron carrier / ROS modulation) maps onto the mitochondrial-decline and oxidative-stress hallmarks and onto the brand's longevity framing — but mechanism-to-skin-outcome is unproven topically, so longevity copy would outrun the data.
  • Recommendation: shortlist as a watch-item / optional supporting actor, not a lead. If we want the "mitochondrial longevity antioxidant" narrative with better evidence, prioritize alternatives. Log in ../../ideas/considering.md as conditional.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified: 2026-06-19. Independent adversarial re-check of every headline efficacy claim and every key citation against primary sources (PubMed, J Invest Dermatol, J Cosmet Dermatol, CIR final report).

Overall evidence grade: D → C (essentially unproven topically; mechanism is real but human-topical efficacy is not)

  • D for the anti-aging efficacy claim specifically: there is no independent, vehicle-controlled, human topical RCT. The single supportive human topical trial (McDaniel 2005, n=41) has no placebo/vehicle arm, and the one independent contradicting experiment was on pig skin, not human.
  • Nudged to C only because the mechanism (redox-active quinone, real CoQ10-analogue bioactivity proven in the oral drug setting) and the safety/penetration picture are genuinely well-evidenced. Treat the dossier's own "promising mechanism, thin and conflicted human evidence" read as correct. This ingredient does not clear a B (moderate human-topical) bar, and is nowhere near an A.

Citation audit — all real, mostly accurate

Every cited PMID/DOI was checked and resolves to a real paper that says what the dossier claims: - McDaniel 2005 Part I (PMID 17134415, DOI 00152.x): confirmed. Scores 95 / 80 / 68 / 55 / 52 / 41 are exact. Note: the paper's own term is "oxidative stress protection capacity score"; "EPF" is best treated as a downstream marketing coinage (the dossier already flags the metric as self-invented — fair). - McDaniel 2005 clinical (DOI 00305.x, citation 11): confirmed n=41, 30–65, 0.5%/1.0%, 6 wk, "nonvehicle control." (Refinement applied: it was randomized between the two strengths with blinded graders — so "single-arm / investigator-graded" was imprecise; the load-bearing criticism, no vehicle/placebo arm, is correct and now stated precisely.) - Tournas 2006 (citation 9): confirmed real; PMID 16528359 was missing and has been added. - Jaber & Polster 2015 (PMID 25262284): confirmed verbatim — "championed as an antioxidant, idebenone can also act as a pro-oxidant by forming an unstable semiquinone at complex I." Pro-oxidant claim is solid. - Mc Aleer & Collins 2008 (PMID 18759903): confirmed ACD case report, patch-test diagnosed. - CIR 2022 (citation 14): confirmed "safe in present practices of use and concentration," with documented ACD to hydroxydecyl ubiquinone noted. - Biomedicines 2023 microneedle paper (PMID 37371744): confirmed; supports "plain topical idebenone penetrates/retains poorly without a device or carrier."

Main caveats (the things a marketer must not forget)

  1. The "champion antioxidant" claim is in-vitro and manufacturer-run. The 95 score is a composite index from a study by authors tied to the commercial developer; an in-vitro index does not predict clinical benefit.
  2. The strongest contradicting evidence is animal (pig skin), not human. This cuts both ways: it is a legitimate rebuttal of the photoprotection claim, but it is not a human-efficacy verdict. The dossier originally over-stated it as a "human-skin" study (the very borrow-from-non-human fallacy it polices elsewhere) — corrected throughout.
  3. No vehicle-controlled human RCT exists in either direction. Any "clinically proven" copy is unsupported.
  4. Pro-oxidant risk is real and well-cited (semiquinone / Complex-I), not hand-waving.
  5. Allergenicity and instability/poor native penetration are well-evidenced and are the practical, not just rhetorical, problems.
  6. Oral LHON/Friedreich's drug data prove systemic bioactivity but say nothing about topical skin outcomes — do not format-transfer.

Claims that were overstated and have been softened in this dossier

  • Tournas 2006 relabelled from "human-topical / human-skin rebuttal" to pig-skin experiment (TL;DR, §3 table, §4 intro, §4 Tier-4b, §4 bottom line, §10 table, §11 verdict, citation 9). This was the one material accuracy defect.
  • McDaniel clinical trial: "single-arm, investigator-graded" → precise "randomized 0.5%-vs-1.0%, blinded graders, no vehicle/placebo arm."
  • §11 one-liner: removed the implication that idebenone was "contradicted by the one independent human test"; now correctly notes the contradicting test is pig-skin and that no independent human topical RCT exists either way.

Claims that survived scrutiny unchanged (correctly stated)

The EPF-95 provenance critique, the pro-oxidant/semiquinone mechanism, poor native penetration / carrier-dependence, contact-allergy signal, CIR safety conclusion, oral-vs-topical format-transfer warning, and the overall "supporting actor, never hero" verdict are all accurately evidenced. The dossier is unusually honest; its self-skeptical framing held up.
