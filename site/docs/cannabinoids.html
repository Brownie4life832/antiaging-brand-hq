Ingredient: Cannabinoids — CBD & CBG (INCI: Cannabidiol; Cannabigerol)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Experimental / frontier & pharma-adjacent longevity actives (phytocannabinoids; endocannabinoid-system modulators)

One-line honest summary: CBD and CBG have a genuinely interesting skin biology — a real endocannabinoid system in the skin, strong in-vitro antioxidant/anti-inflammatory/sebostatic mechanisms, and a handful of small human studies — but the human topical anti-aging evidence is thin, mostly combination products or company-funded, and the category is dominated by hype + regulatory landmines (the FDA's Epidiolex / hemp-rule problem). Treat cannabinoids as a conditional supporting/marketing-driven ingredient: defensible as a soothing/antioxidant adjunct with hedged claims, not a proven anti-aging hero.

1. Identity

  • Common names: Cannabidiol (CBD); Cannabigerol (CBG, the "mother cannabinoid"/biosynthetic precursor). Both are non-intoxicating phytocannabinoids from Cannabis sativa / hemp (distinct from psychoactive Δ9-THC).
  • INCI: Cannabidiol (CBD); Cannabigerol (CBG). Frequently appears in cosmetics under hemp-extract INCIs: Cannabis Sativa (Hemp) Seed Oil (note: seed oil contains essentially no CBD/CBG — it is a fatty oil, a common mislabeling/marketing trap), Cannabis Sativa Leaf Extract, Cannabis Sativa Flower/Leaf/Stem Extract. "Full-spectrum," "broad-spectrum," and "isolate" describe the extract, not a single INCI.
  • CAS: CBD 13956-29-1; CBG 25654-31-3.
  • Molecular formula / weight: CBD C₂₁H₃₀O₂, ~314.5 g/mol; CBG C₂₁H₃₂O₂, ~316.5 g/mol. Both are small enough by MW to be plausible skin penetrants — but see lipophilicity caveat below.
  • Chemical class: Meroterpenoid phenols (resorcinol + terpene). Highly lipophilic (logP ≈ 6–7) with very low water solubility (~2–5 µg/mL). [10]
  • Key forms/derivatives:
  • CBD isolate (≥98% pure crystalline CBD) — cleanest for labeling and stability.
  • Broad-spectrum (CBD + minor cannabinoids/terpenes, THC removed) and full-spectrum (includes trace THC) extracts — carry the "entourage effect" marketing and the THC-contamination risk.
  • CBG isolate and CBG-rich extracts (increasingly yeast-biosynthesized for purity/sustainability). [4]
  • Cannabinoid acids (CBDA, CBGA — the raw, un-decarboxylated forms) and derivatives/delivery systems (nano-encapsulated CBD "nCBD," ethosomes, microemulsions, water-dispersible CBD). [1][10]

2. Class & mechanism (how it works at the skin/cell level)

The skin has its own endocannabinoid system (ECS) — this is the scientifically sound core of the story. [3]

Mechanistic pillars (most are well-established biology, demonstrated for skin largely in cell/animal models):

  1. A real cutaneous ECS. Keratinocytes, sebocytes, mast cells, fibroblasts, melanocytes, hair-follicle cells and cutaneous nerves express CB1 and CB2 receptors and produce endocannabinoids (anandamide/AEA, 2-AG). The ECS "constitutively controls the proper and well-balanced proliferation, differentiation and survival" of skin cells. [3]
  2. CBD is largely a non-CB1/CB2 actor. CBD has low direct affinity for CB1/CB2; in skin it acts mainly through TRP channels (TRPV1–4, TRPA1), PPARγ, adenosine (A2A) receptors, and direct antioxidant chemistry — not classic cannabinoid receptors. [2][7]
  3. Sebostatic / anti-acne (best-characterized CBD mechanism). CBD suppresses sebocyte lipogenesis and proliferation via TRPV4 → ERK1/2 ↓ → NRIP1 ↓, and is anti-inflammatory via A2A → TRIB3 ↑ → NF-κB inhibition. [2]
  4. Antioxidant (direct + Nrf2/BACH1). CBD is a direct ROS scavenger and an indirect antioxidant: it induces HMOX1 strongly by degrading the repressor BACH1 (NRF2-independent), and also raises NRF2 under UVB stress. [7][8]
  5. Anti-inflammatory / immunomodulatory. Both CBD and CBG downregulate pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNFα, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8) in keratinocytes and skin models; CBG additionally works through JAK/STAT/NF-κB and MAPK pathways. [4][12]
  6. Anti-proliferative / pro-apoptotic in keratinocytes (relevant to psoriasis, not obviously to anti-aging). AEA/CBD inhibit keratinocyte growth and can induce apoptosis. [3][6]
  7. CBG as a "more active" sister molecule (in vitro). In a 3D-skin gene-expression screen, CBG regulated more genes than CBD, including stronger upregulation of collagen I/III/IV, elastin, hyaluronan synthase (HAS2), and IL-10 — the most ECM/anti-aging-flavored cannabinoid dataset to date (company-funded). [4]

Skin-relevant translation (honest): pillars 1–6 are well-proven biology but demonstrated for skin mostly in cell/animal/ex-vivo systems. The anti-aging-specific claims (collagen, elastin, hydration genes) rest heavily on one company-funded in-vitro CBG study [4] and combination human studies [9] — not on clean, independent, topical-CBD human RCTs.

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

The single most important honesty point: Cannabinoid skin biology is real and interesting at the bench, but for human topical anti-aging there is no large, independent, monotherapy RCT. The strongest human data are (a) a tiny (n≈19) pilot RCT showing nano-CBD reduces UVA-induced DNA damage [1]; (b) a combination (CBD+EPA, n=33, no control) cosmetic study [9]; (c) a company-funded CBG SLS-irritation study (n=20) [4]; and (d) an atopic-dermatitis RCT in which CBD alone did not beat placebo [11]. Everything else is in-vitro, animal, or uncontrolled case series.

Tier 1 — In vitro (cell & 3D models) — the strongest, deepest layer

  • Oláh A. et al., 2014, J Clin Invest 124(9):3713-24 (DOI 10.1172/JCI64628). Foundational. CBD on cultured human sebocytes + human skin organ culture: suppressed lipogenesis and proliferation via TRPV4 → ERK1/2 → NRIP1, and was anti-inflammatory via A2A → TRIB3 → NF-κB. Limitation: cell/organ culture; mechanism, not a finished-formula human outcome. [2]
  • Casares L. et al., 2020, Redox Biology 28:101321 (published 2019). CBD induced HMOX1 ~58-fold in keratinocytes by degrading the repressor BACH1 (NRF2-independent); raised cytoprotective antioxidant tone. Limitation: HaCaT/NHEK in vitro (mouse confirmation of epidermal HMOX1). [8]
  • Jastrząb A. / Atalay S. et al. (Białystok group), 2019–2021 — multiple papers (e.g., Antioxidants; J Nat Prod 2021, DOI 10.1021/acs.jnatprod.1c00083): CBD protects keratinocytes from UV/H₂O₂ oxidative stress, modulates the caspase-1–IL-1β axis and lipid-peroxidation/redox proteome. Limitation: in vitro. [7]
  • Perez E. et al., 2022, Molecules 27(2):491 — in-vitro arm (CBG via yeast biosynthesis). Gene microarray on 3D skin equivalents: CBG regulated ~5,241 genes vs CBD ~3,766; CBG upregulated collagen I (3×), III (15×), IV (3×), elastin (5×), HAS2 (2.1×), IL-10 (2.9×), and was 2–5× more potent than CBD at suppressing UVB-TNFα, UVA-IL-6, TPA-IL-8; both were strong cell-based ROS scavengers. Major limitation: all authors are employees/shareholders of Signum/Willow Biosciences — clear financial interest; in-vitro fold-changes do not equal finished-product human results. [4]
  • Tóth K.F. et al., 2019, Molecules 24(5):918 — "Cannabinoid Signaling in the Skin" review consolidates the in-vitro/ECS literature (barrier, ceramide via CB1, hair follicle, pigmentation). [14]

Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

  • Li H. et al., 2022, Molecules 27(19):6740 — UVB photoprotection. HaCaT cells (CBD 1–16 µM) ↓ROS (−41% at 8 µM), ↓γH2AX (−38–53%), ↓CPDs, ↑NRF2 (2.1×), ↑autophagy (LC3B-II). A 5% CBD cream on mouse skin (UVB 120 mJ/cm²/day ×5) reduced erythema/scaling, lowered photodamage score, and ↓COX-2. Limitation: cells + mice only; no human. [13]
  • Wound-healing / anti-inflammatory rodent models (various; summarized in reviews [5][6]): topical CBD/CBG accelerate aspects of repair and dampen inflammation. Limitation: acute injury models, not chronic intrinsic aging; species translation uncertain. [5][6]

Tier 3 — Human ORAL / systemic (large evidence base — but NOT skin)

The deepest human cannabinoid data are pharmaceutical and not dermatologic: - Epidiolex (purified oral CBD), FDA-approved 2018 for Lennox-Gastaut, Dravet, TSC seizures. Large RCTs establish CBD's systemic efficacy and a detailed safety/toxicity profile (notably dose-dependent hepatotoxicity, drug interactions). Relevance to us: safety signal only; oral, high-dose, not skin. [15] - No oral cannabinoid RCT demonstrates skin-aging benefit. Importing the seizure/pain literature into a skincare claim is a category error.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters — and it's thin & impure)

  • Nano-CBD UVA pilot RCT — JAAD 2024 (S0190-9622(24)02490-3). Prospective, single-center, randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled, n≈19 (of 20 enrolled). nCBD cream vs vehicle on randomized buttock sites BID ×14 days, then UVA challenge. Result: nCBD-treated skin showed less epidermal hyperplasia, lower 8-oxoguanine (nuclear DNA damage), and reduced mitochondrial DNA damage vs vehicle; ~21% of participants had less erythema. Limitations: pilot, tiny n, sun-protected non-facial site, short term, surrogate (DNA-damage) endpoints not clinical wrinkles; concentration not reported in summary; the single best topical anti-aging-relevant RCT and it's preliminary. [1]
  • CBD + EPA anti-aging gel — Cohen et al., 2023, J Cosmet Dermatol (DOI 10.1111/jocd.15815). In-vitro/ex-vivo (HaCaT/skin organ) + clinical n=33, up to 56 days. Reported +31.2% hydration, +25.6% elasticity, reduced crow's-feet area/volume, −8.8% SLEB, fewer red spots. Critical limitations: combination (EPA + CBD — cannot attribute to CBD alone), no control/vehicle/randomization described, manufacturer-linked; classic cosmetic-efficacy study design (weak). [9]
  • CBG SLS-irritation study — Perez et al., 2022 (clinical arm). n=20, single-blind, vehicle-controlled, 0.1% CBG serum on SLS-irritated forearm ×~14 days: significantly lower TEWL and faster resolution of redness vs placebo/untreated. Limitations: irritation model (not aging), small, single-blind, author financial conflict. [4]
  • Atopic-dermatitis RCT (JW-100) — Gao Y. et al., 2022, J Cosmet Dermatol (DOI 10.1111/jocd.14263). Double-blind, placebo-controlled. After 2 weeks ISGA fell 1.28 (CBD+aspartame) vs 0.81 (CBD alone) vs 0.71 (placebo); only the combination reached significance (p=0.042). CBD alone did not significantly beat placebo. This is the most rigorous topical-CBD RCT and it is a partial-null for CBD monotherapy — a crucial counterweight. [11]
  • CBD-enriched ointment case series — Palmieri B. et al., 2019, Clin Ter 170(2):e93-99. Retrospective, uncontrolled, "anecdotal" n=20 (psoriasis/AD/scars), 3 months BID: reported improved hydration, TEWL, elasticity, PASI, no adverse reactions. Limitations: no control, no randomization, self-selected, investigator-rated — hypothesis-generating only. [16]
  • Acne / sebum — small split-face and open studies of hemp/cannabis-seed-extract creams report reduced sebum/erythema, but use seed extract or 3% "Cannabis seed extract," not standardized CBD, and are low quality. [5]

Bottom line by tier: Deep, credible evidence exists for in-vitro antioxidant/anti-inflammatory/sebostatic mechanisms (CBD) and a striking-but-funded CBG gene-expression profile. Human topical support for anti-aging is limited to one small pilot RCT on a surrogate UVA-DNA endpoint [1], uncontrolled/combination cosmetic studies [9][16], and a CBD-monotherapy partial-null in eczema [11]. No independent monotherapy RCT shows topical CBD/CBG improves human skin aging on clinical endpoints.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • The lipophilicity paradox. CBD/CBG are very lipophilic (logP ≈ 6–7, water solubility ~2–5 µg/mL). [10] Lipophilic small molecules partition into the stratum corneum well, but the aqueous viable epidermis/dermis becomes the rate-limiting step — so much of an applied dose lodges in the stratum corneum and reservoirs there rather than reaching deeper targets. In porcine-skin studies, topical CBD remained largely in the stratum corneum with low permeation into deeper layers at 24 h. [10]
  • Local vs systemic: Conventional topical CBD acts mostly locally/superficially; meaningful systemic absorption through intact skin is low without enhancers (good for safety, limiting for deep-dermal anti-aging action). [10]
  • Delivery systems matter a lot. Penetration is materially improved by nano-encapsulation (the JAAD UVA study used nCBD for exactly this reason [1]), ethosomes/liposomes, microemulsions (one study showed 11–17× permeation increase at pH ~5.2), and chemical enhancers (oleic acid, ethanol, Transcutol). [1][10]
  • Practical read: Penetration to the sebaceous gland / upper epidermis (acne, surface antioxidant defense) is plausible with ordinary formulas; reaching dermal fibroblasts for a real collagen effect likely requires a delivery system and is not yet demonstrated in humans. No published human study quantifies how much topical CBD/CBG reaches viable dermis at cosmetic use levels.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: No validated human topical anti-aging dose. Anchors from the literature: 0.1% CBG worked in the SLS model [4]; in-vitro actives were 1–16 µM [13]; cosmetic CBD products span ~0.1–5% (the 5% figure comes from a mouse cream [13]). Most reputable cosmetic use sits ~0.25–2%. Higher % drives cost and color, not proven benefit. Formulate conservatively and never imply a validated dose-response.
  • Solubility/vehicle: Highly lipophilic → needs oils/esters, solubilizers, or nano/micro-emulsion systems; poor in simple aqueous gels without help. Oil-rich anhydrous balms or emulsions are natural homes.
  • pH: No strict pH requirement for CBD/CBG themselves; formulate to skin-friendly ~5–6. (Acidic forms CBDA/CBGA differ.)
  • Stability: Light- and oxygen-sensitive — CBD oxidizes and can photo-/thermo-degrade (CBD can convert toward THC under strongly acidic/forced conditions — a real QC concern). Use opaque/airless packaging, antioxidants, avoid high heat, and run stability + cannabinoid-assay testing over shelf life. Color/odor drift is common with full/broad-spectrum extracts; isolate is more stable and cleaner-labeled.
  • Pairings (conceptual): Antioxidants (vitamin C/E, the Nrf2/HMOX1 story dovetails), niacinamide (barrier + anti-inflammatory), ceramides/the barrier thesis (CB1 links to ceramide synthesis [14]), EPA/omega lipids (the one human anti-aging combo [9]), bisabolol/centella for a "soothing" stack. CBD+CBG combinations are marketed as complementary.
  • Conflicts: Strong oxidizers/high heat (degradation); be cautious layering with high-strength acids/retinoids if the goal is "soothing" (defeats the calm-skin positioning). Fragrance load already high in many hemp extracts.
  • Sensory: Isolate is near-odorless; full-spectrum carries an earthy/hempy scent and green-brown color that can be polarizing in a premium product — a real sensory/positioning decision.

7. Safety

  • General tolerability (topical): Topical CBD/CBG are generally well tolerated in the small studies to date; the controlled trials [1][4][11] reported no significant irritation vs vehicle, and the case series reported none. [16] No specific phototoxicity signal (mechanistically CBD is photo-protective in models [13]).
  • Irritation/sensitization: Low but not zero. Allergic contact dermatitis and contact urticaria to Cannabis sativa are documented (mostly occupational/handling), and botanical full-spectrum extracts carry terpene/other-allergen load. Generate our own HRIPT/patch data on the finished formula. [18]
  • THC contamination — the under-discussed safety/compliance risk. Hemp extracts can contain trace THC; "full-spectrum" especially. This is both a regulatory problem (see §8) and a consumer-trust/drug-test concern. Demand a COA with cannabinoid profile + heavy-metal/pesticide/residual-solvent panel for every lot (cannabis is a known bioaccumulator of soil contaminants).
  • Sun: No sun-sensitivity flag; CBD may be mildly photoprotective in models, but do not position as sun protection (no SPF).
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: Caution / advise consulting a doctor. Oral/systemic CBD (Epidiolex) showed developmental toxicity in animals and is used in pregnancy "only if benefit justifies risk"; no human-milk data. [15] Topical systemic exposure is low, but absent topical pregnancy data, default to a conservative "consult your physician" stance.
  • Hepatotoxicity (context, oral): High-dose oral CBD causes dose-dependent liver-enzyme elevation and drug interactions. [15] Not a topical-cosmetic risk at sane use levels, but it shapes the regulatory mood around CBD generally.
  • Sourcing/quality: Highly variable market — mislabeling is rampant (products over/under-stating CBD, undisclosed THC). Provenance, third-party assay, and contaminant testing are non-negotiable.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails) — the biggest single liability of this ingredient

  • US federal status is genuinely unsettled and tightening. FDA's longstanding position is that CBD cannot be lawfully added to foods or marketed as a dietary supplement because CBD is the active ingredient in an approved drug (Epidiolex). Cosmetics are a relatively more permissible path (a cosmetic may contain CBD if safe and not making drug claims), but MoCRA (2022, enforced through 2025) now subjects cosmetic CBD topicals to facility registration, ingredient listing, adverse-event contact info, and safety substantiation. [17]
  • Hemp definition has now changed — this is enacted law, not a rumor. The Continuing Appropriations and Extensions Act of 2026 (P.L. 119-37), signed 2025-11-12, redefines "hemp" on a total-THC basis (delta-9 + THCA, decarboxylation-adjusted) and caps finished products at ≤0.4 mg total THC per container, applying 2025-11-12 → effective 2026-11-12. Legal analyses estimate this makes the majority of "full-spectrum" CBD products non-compliant (commonly cited as ~90%) because they carry trace THC — a material supply/legality risk to designing a product around these actives. [17] Isolate/broad-spectrum and per-market law still need verification before committing; legality varies sharply by jurisdiction (EU treats CBD as a cosmetic ingredient with conditions; some markets restrict or ban).
  • The drug/cosmetic line (the core trap). Cannabinoids attract drug-style language. Any claim to "treat acne/eczema/psoriasis," "heal," "relieve pain," "anti-inflammatory," "reduce wrinkles by X%," or "boost collagen" pushes into drug territory and draws FDA warning letters (the agency has issued many to CBD marketers). MoCRA flags therapeutic claims for "immediate warning letters." [17]
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "helps calm the look of stressed skin," "supports a comfortable, balanced complexion," "antioxidant-supported defense against environmental stress," "helps skin feel soothed," "visibly improves the appearance of redness/uneven tone," "for skin that looks fatigued." Even these need finished-formula substantiation under MoCRA.
  • ⚠️ Risky / substantiation-or-legal-review: "anti-inflammatory," "treats acne/eczema," "reduces sebum," "boosts collagen/elastin," "repairs UV damage / DNA," "anti-aging at the cellular level," "endocannabinoid balancing," "relieves pain." The mechanistic literature does not license these as topical human claims, and several are drug claims.
  • Reality: To say anything beyond gentle cosmetic/sensory language we'd want our own finished-formula human testing plus current legal sign-off per market — this ingredient has the heaviest legal overhead of anything in the considering set. See ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Forms/suppliers: CBD isolate is now a commodity (broadly available, GMP-grade from hemp processors). CBG isolate is pricier but increasingly available via fermentation/yeast biosynthesis (more consistent, sustainable, no field-contaminant load). [4] Broad/full-spectrum extracts vary widely in quality.
  • Cost tier: CBD: low-to-mid (isolate prices have fallen substantially). CBG: mid-to-high (less abundant in plant; biosynthesis premium). Delivery-enhanced versions (nano/lipo) add cost.
  • Supply/standardization risk: High. Cannabinoid content varies; contamination (heavy metals, pesticides, solvents, undisclosed THC) is real; regulatory supply risk (§8) could strand inventory. Require lot-level COA + third-party assay; prefer isolate or fermentation-derived CBG for clean labeling and contaminant control.

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • "Endocannabinoid system → it must work topically." The cutaneous ECS is real [3], but having a receptor system is not proof that a topical cosmetic dose meaningfully modulates it for anti-aging in living human skin.
  • The combination & funding problem. The flattering human numbers (+25.6% elasticity, +31.2% hydration) come from a CBD+EPA gel with no control group [9]; the best CBG data are employee/shareholder-authored [4]. Marketing presents these as clean CBD/CBG proof. They aren't.
  • The monotherapy null. In the most rigorous topical RCT, CBD alone did not beat placebo in eczema; only CBD+aspartame did [11]. That's the inconvenient datapoint the category ignores.
  • "Seed oil = CBD." Many "hemp" beauty products use Cannabis seed oil, which contains negligible CBD/CBG — a pervasive consumer deception.
  • "Anti-inflammatory/healing/pain" copy. These are drug claims the cosmetic format can't legally make and the topical human data don't support. [17]
  • DNA-repair / "cellular" claims. The one human signal is a pilot (n≈19) on a surrogate UVA-DNA endpoint with nano-encapsulated CBD [1] — promising, not proof, and not a wrinkle outcome.
  • THC-free assumptions. "Full-spectrum" can carry trace THC; assuming zero is a compliance and trust risk.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ CONDITIONAL — interesting science + real legal/quality overhead; a marketing-led "soothing/antioxidant" supporting actor at best, not a proven anti-aging hero. One-line: Cannabinoids (CBD/CBG) have legitimately compelling in-vitro skin biology and a story consumers already love, but the human topical anti-aging evidence is thin, combination-confounded, or funded, and the regulatory/sourcing burden is the heaviest of any ingredient we're considering — so they only make sense as a carefully-claimed supporting active if the brand specifically wants a calm-skin / antioxidant-defense angle and is willing to fund legal review + finished-formula testing.

Why not "skip": the mechanism is real, the consumer pull is strong, CBG's ECM/antioxidant in-vitro profile is genuinely intriguing, and the soothing/antioxidant positioning is defensible with honest, hedged copy. Why not "hero": no independent monotherapy human anti-aging RCT; the marquee human numbers are combination/uncontrolled/funded; CBD-alone underperformed placebo in the cleanest topical RCT; penetration to dermal fibroblasts is unproven; and the legal/THC/supply risk could derail a SKU.

Conditions to upgrade to supporting/hero: (1) current legal clearance in every target market (the hemp-rule change in §8 is a live threat); (2) isolate or fermentation-CBG with lot-level COA (cannabinoid + contaminant + THC panel); (3) a delivery system if any dermal/collagen ambition; (4) our own finished-formula human testing before any efficacy claim beyond gentle sensory language.

Longevity-relevance note: For a skin-longevity brand the honest fit is defense, not rebuilding — cannabinoids slot into the antioxidant / anti-inflammaging / "calm the exposome stress" narrative (Nrf2/HMOX1, ROS scavenging, ↓inflammatory cytokines), which is the most evidence-aligned framing. The "rejuvenation/collagen" framing is where the hype lives and the evidence thins. If we use them, lead with soothing + environmental-stress defense, not wrinkle reversal.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19 against primary literature. Each headline claim was checked for: (a) human + topical vs. borrowed in-vitro/animal/oral evidence; (b) whether cited studies are real and say what's claimed; (c) industry funding / sample size.

Overall grade: C (weak/indirect for the anti-aging claim). The mechanism layer is genuinely strong, but the human topical anti-aging monotherapy evidence is thin, surrogate-endpoint, combination-confounded, or industry-funded. This dossier already grades itself honestly; verification confirms its caution rather than overturning it.

Per-tier grades: - In-vitro antioxidant / anti-inflammatory / sebostatic mechanism (CBD): A for mechanism (real, replicated, independent) — but mechanism ≠ topical human outcome. - CBG ECM/anti-aging gene profile [4]: C — striking but single study, in-vitro 3D-skin, authors are Signum/Willow employees/shareholders (confirmed COI). - Human topical anti-aging (collagen/elasticity/hydration/wrinkles): C/D — rests on one uncontrolled CBD+EPA combination cosmetic study (n=33) [9] and a funded CBG irritation-model study (n=20) [4]; no independent monotherapy RCT on clinical aging endpoints. - Human topical photoprotection / DNA: C — one pilot RCT (n≈19), surrogate DNA-damage endpoints, sun-protected non-facial site [1]. - Soothing / antioxidant-defense positioning: B- — the most defensible framing, but still needs finished-formula substantiation.

Citation integrity: Every reference spot-checked ([1], [2], [3], [4], [8], [9], [10], [11], [13], [17]) is real and accurately described, including correct flagging of the [4] financial conflict and the [11] CBD-monotherapy partial-null (CBD alone −0.81 vs placebo −0.71, not significant; only CBD+aspartame reached p=0.042). No fabricated or misrepresented studies were found. This is a rare clean bill on sourcing.

Main caveats (confirmed): 1. No independent, monotherapy, topical, human anti-aging RCT on clinical endpoints exists. All flattering human numbers are combination, uncontrolled, funded, or surrogate. 2. The marquee numbers are confounded: +25.6% elasticity / +31.2% hydration come from a CBD+EPA gel with no control arm [9] — cannot be attributed to CBD. 3. The best CBG data are conflicted (employee/shareholder authors) [4]. 4. The cleanest topical RCT is a partial-null for CBD alone [11]. 5. Penetration to dermal fibroblasts is unproven in humans — likely requires a delivery system. 6. Regulatory risk is now realized, not hypothetical (see correction below).

Corrections applied to the dossier: - §8 / [17] strengthened (was understated). The hemp redefinition the dossier had marked "reported... [partially unverified — fast-moving]" is enacted law: Continuing Appropriations and Extensions Act of 2026 (P.L. 119-37, signed 2025-11-12), total-THC standard, ≤0.4 mg total THC per container, effective 2026-11-12, estimated to render ~90% of full-spectrum CBD products non-compliant. This makes the dossier's central regulatory warning more serious, not less. - §3 photoaging row softened to reflect that the human mtDNA-protection signal is a single n≈19 pilot on a surrogate endpoint, not an established human outcome.

Claims to keep softened / never make in copy: "boosts/builds collagen," "reduces wrinkles by X%," "repairs UV/DNA damage," "anti-inflammatory," "treats acne/eczema," "endocannabinoid balancing." These are drug claims and/or unsupported by topical human monotherapy data. Defensible framing remains soothing + antioxidant/environmental-stress defense with finished-formula substantiation only — consistent with the existing §11 verdict.

Net: The dossier was already calibrated correctly (verdict: conditional supporting actor, not hero). Verification upholds that verdict, confirms citation integrity, and tightens the one place the file was too soft — the now-enacted federal hemp law.
