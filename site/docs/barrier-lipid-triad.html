Ingredient: Ceramide–Cholesterol–Free-Fatty-Acid Physiological Triad (3:1:1)

Slug: barrier-lipid-triad · Class: Barrier lipids & skin-identical actives Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ✅ shortlisted (HERO-system candidate — the delivery logic behind the barrier-repair cream)

Scope note. This dossier is about the ratio/system, not the molecules in isolation. The individual building blocks have their own files: ceramides.md, cholesterol.md, free-fatty-acids.md. The thesis here is narrow and well-defined: applying ceramides + cholesterol + free fatty acids together in a physiological molar ratio (canonically 3:1:1, ceramide-dominant) repairs the barrier faster and more completely than any single lipid — and applying lipids in the wrong ratio can actively delay repair. As a mechanism, this is among the most rigorously-established "barrier repair" principles in dermatology (decades of primary mechanistic + animal work, plus human barrier-recovery and eczema RCTs), and it is the scientific backbone of the brand's barrier-cream thesis. Caveat up front: the strongest human evidence is for barrier-recovery/TEWL and eczema-prone skin — not visible anti-aging endpoints in healthy aged skin, and the best aged-skin ratio data are animal-led (see §4).

1. Identity

  • Common name: Physiological lipid mixture (PLM); "triple lipid"; "barrier-repair lipid complex"; "skin-identical lipids."
  • The three components (INCI examples):
  • Ceramides — Ceramide NP (Cer3), Ceramide AP (Cer6-II), Ceramide EOP (Cer1), Ceramide NS/NG, often co-listed with Phytosphingosine (a ceramide precursor / sphingoid base).
  • Cholesterol — Cholesterol (INCI), sometimes with Cholesteryl Esters.
  • Free fatty acids (FFA) — typically Palmitic Acid, Stearic Acid, Linoleic Acid, Behenic Acid, Lignoceric Acid. Linoleic acid (an essential FFA) is functionally important beyond barrier filling (PPAR signaling — see §2).
  • The ratio. The canonical, evidence-backed target is a 3:1:1 molar ratio of ceramide : cholesterol : FFA (ceramide-dominant). Critically, the literature shows the dominant lipid can be any of the three (up to ~3-fold over the other two) and still work — and which one should dominate depends on the skin state (see §4). This is why commercial products vary: SkinCeuticals Triple Lipid Restore uses 2:4:2 (cholesterol-dominant) for aged skin; many barrier creams use ceramide-dominant blends.
  • Off-the-shelf forms / pre-made blends (relevant to "buy the base, invest in the brand"):
  • Evonik SK-INFLUX (ceramides NP/AP/EOP + phytosphingosine + cholesterol + fatty acids, lamellar/MVE-style delivery).
  • Evonik / Croda (SphingoCare) ceramide + sphingolipid ranges; Croda now owns Solus BioTech (fermentation-derived Ceramide NP/AP).
  • CeraVe MVE technology (ceramides NP/AP/EOP + cholesterol; note: no added free fatty acid in the classic MVE — so it is a partial triad, see honesty flag in §4).
  • Lotioncrafter / Formulator Sample Shop "Ceramide Complex" (NP/AP/EOP + phytosphingosine) for prototyping.

2. Class & mechanism — how it works

The brick-and-mortar model. The stratum corneum (SC) is corneocytes ("bricks") embedded in a lipid "mortar." That mortar is ~50% ceramides, ~25% cholesterol, ~10–15% free fatty acids by mass, arranged into stacked lamellar bilayers that are the actual permeability barrier (Mao-Qiang/Elias/Feingold foundational work; ratio reviewed in Elias/Man 2022). All three lipid classes are required; the bilayers don't assemble correctly if one is missing or grossly out of proportion.

Why a single lipid isn't enough — and can backfire. The defining experiments (Man, Feingold, Thornfeldt & Elias, J Invest Dermatol 1996; Mao-Qiang et al., earlier 1990s) showed: - Applying one or two of the three lipids to acutely barrier-disrupted skin delays recovery — the incomplete mixture is incorporated into nascent lamellar bilayers and disorganizes them. - Applying all three in a complete (≥ equimolar 1:1:1) mixture normalizes recovery. - Increasing any one of the three up to ~3-fold over the others (i.e., reaching a 3:1:1-type ratio) further accelerates repair beyond equimolar.

The delivery insight (why this is "skin-identical" not just occlusive). Topically-applied physiological lipids don't just sit on top like petrolatum. They penetrate to the nucleated (living) epidermis, are taken up by keratinocytes, co-packaged into the cell's own lamellar bodies, and then re-secreted at the SC interface as part of the skin's normal lipid-export machinery (Mao-Qiang/Elias). So the skin "uses" them as raw material — which is why the ratio matters (it must match what the lamellar-body assembly line expects) and why occlusion-only moisturizers behave differently.

Linoleic acid does double duty. Beyond filling the FFA slot, linoleic acid is a PPAR-α/ligand that upregulates the skin's own lipid synthesis, lamellar-body secretion and barrier maturation (Elias/Man 2022). So a well-chosen FFA component both supplies barrier lipid and signals the skin to make more of its own.

pH matters. Barrier lipid processing enzymes (β-glucocerebrosidase, acid sphingomyelinase) are acid-pH-optimal. An acidic SC (~pH 4.5–5.5) speeds lipid processing and barrier maturation; neutral pH impedes it (Elias/Man 2022; Hachem et al. on pH and barrier recovery). Formulating the product at low pH (~5) is therefore mechanistically supportive, not just tolerability theater.

3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

Hallmark Relevance Strength
Barrier decline The core, direct target. Aged skin has globally reduced SC lipids — and notably reduced cholesterol synthesis specifically (Ghadially et al. 1995). Replenishing the full triad rebuilds lamellar bilayers. Strong / direct
Hydration / transepidermal water loss (TEWL) Restored bilayers reduce water loss; this is the measured endpoint in nearly every study. Strong / direct
Inflammaging A leaky barrier drives the "outside-in" cytokine cascade ("cytokine atmosphere"); restoring it lowers baseline low-grade inflammation. Mechanistically strong; aged-skin clinical data thinner. Moderate (mechanistic)
Photoaging Indirect: a resilient barrier tolerates actives (retinoids/acids/vitamin C) and recovers from environmental insult; the triad is supportive, not a photoprotectant. Indirect / supporting
Collagen/ECM loss, glycation/AGEs, senescence, mitochondrial decline, pigmentation, microbiome Not a meaningful direct target. (Acidic pH may indirectly favor a healthier microbiome and AMP production.) Weak / not a target

Honest framing for an anti-aging brand: the triad is a barrier/hydration/comfort play with a strong enabling role for the rest of the routine. It is not a wrinkle eraser or collagen booster on its own — that is the retinoid/peptide/vitamin-C layer's job. Its longevity relevance is "keep the barrier young," which is real but lateral to ECM rejuvenation.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

Read this section as: the ratio principle is rock-solid in mechanism and animal models; the strongest human topical data is in compromised/eczema-prone skin; dedicated anti-aging RCTs in healthy aged skin for a full marketed triad are thin and partly industry-sourced. Be honest about that gap in copy.

Tier 1 — In-vitro / biophysical / ex-vivo

  • SC lipid bilayer modeling consistently shows the three lipid classes in physiological proportion are needed to form the dense, ordered lamellar/orthorhombic packing that constitutes a low-permeability barrier (multiple model-membrane studies; e.g. de Jager / Bouwstra group work on synthetic SC lipid mixtures). Limitation: model membranes, not skin.
  • Topically applied ceramides interact with the SC lipid matrix in compromised ex-vivo skin — Schmitt et al., PMC5801391. Shows uptake/interaction of applied ceramides with the matrix. Limitation: ex-vivo, ceramide-focused.

Tier 2 — Animal (hairless / aged mice) — this is where the ratio rules were established

  • Man MQ, Feingold KR, Thornfeldt CR, Elias PM. "Optimization of physiological lipid mixtures for barrier repair." J Invest Dermatol 1996;106(5):1096–1101. PMID 8618046. Primarily hairless mice, with preliminary confirmatory results in acutely damaged human skin (not a standalone human trial). Equimolar 3-lipid mixtures normalize recovery; increasing any one lipid up to 3-fold further accelerates repair; one- or two-lipid mixtures delay it. Glucosylceramides and triglycerides can substitute within their class; cholesterol esters and phospholipids cannot. This is the foundational "3:1:1" paper. Limitation: animal model; acute tape-strip/solvent perturbation, not chronic aging; the human data are preliminary.
  • Mao-Qiang M, Elias PM, Feingold KR. "Fatty acids are required for epidermal permeability barrier function." J Clin Invest 1993;92:791–798. Establishes FFA as non-negotiable in the triad. Limitation: murine, synthesis-inhibition design.
  • Zettersten EM, Ghadially R, Feingold KR, Crumrine D, Elias PM. "Optimal ratios of topical stratum corneum lipids improve barrier recovery in chronologically aged skin." J Am Acad Dermatol 1997;37(3 Pt 1):403–408. The most brand-relevant ratio paper. In aged mice and aged humans, a cholesterol-dominant mixture (cholesterol : ceramide : palmitate : linoleate ≈ 3:1:1:1, i.e. cholesterol as the lead lipid) accelerated barrier recovery, whereas a ceramide- or FFA-dominant mixture did not (FFA-dominant even delayed recovery in aged skin while helping young skin). Rationale: aged skin is specifically cholesterol-synthesis-deficient, so cholesterol is the rate-limiting lipid to replace. Limitation: small human arm; acute-perturbation endpoint, not long-term cosmetic outcomes.
  • Ghadially R, Brown BE, Sequeira-Martin SM, Feingold KR, Elias PM. "The aged epidermal permeability barrier…" J Clin Invest 1995;95:2281–2290. DOI 10.1172/JCI117919. Foundational aging paper: aged human/murine SC has globally reduced lipids and a barrier that recovers slowly and is easily perturbed; underpins why lipid replacement is rational in aging. Limitation: descriptive/mechanistic.

Key takeaway for the brand: for an anti-aging product on mature skin, the textbook 3:1:1 ceramide-dominant ratio may be suboptimal; the Zettersten data argue for a cholesterol-leaning blend (the logic SkinCeuticals' 2:4:2 follows). This is a genuine, citable formulation differentiator.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL

  • Not applicable. This is a topically-delivered structural-lipid system. (Oral ceramides/phytoceramides are a different ingredient story — see how the brand treats borrowed-format claims. Do not borrow oral ceramide data for a topical triad claim.)

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL

4a. RCT / controlled (strongest human evidence — but mostly in compromised/eczema-prone skin): - Andrew PV, et al. "Topical supplementation with physiological lipids rebalances the stratum corneum ceramide profile and strengthens skin barrier function in adults predisposed to atopic dermatitis." Br J Dermatol 2025;193(4):729–740. DOI 10.1093/bjd/ljaf200. Randomized, double-blind, intra-participant-controlled, n=58 adults (mean age 46; included a ≥60 subgroup), 28 days twice-daily. The lipid (MVE) cream raised SC Ceramide NP/AP, produced >2-fold shifts in 6 lipid species, and cut post-tape-strip TEWL by 22% (p<0.001) vs. no significant change for the glycerin-only emollient. Honesty flags: the test product was ceramide+cholesterol but NOT a full FFA-containing triad (it's CeraVe MVE chemistry); funded by L'Oréal Dermatological Beauty; eczema-predisposed (not healthy-aging) population; lipidomics were exploratory. - Sugarman JL, Parish LC. "Efficacy of a lipid-based barrier repair formulation in moderate-to-severe pediatric atopic dermatitis." J Drugs Dermatol 2009;8(12):1106–1111. PMID 20027938. 5-center, investigator-blinded RCT, n=121 children. A ceramide-dominant triple-lipid cream (EpiCeram) vs. fluticasone propionate. Fluticasone won at day 14, but by day 28 SCORAD, pruritus and sleep scores were statistically indistinguishable between the lipid cream and the mid-potency steroid. Limitation: disease (eczema) population, not cosmetic anti-aging; non-inferiority-style read at 28 d only. - Coderch L, López O, de la Maza A, Parra JL. Body of human SC-lipid-supplementation work (review: "Ceramides and skin function," Am J Clin Dermatol 2003;4(2):107–129) summarizing that topical SC lipids accelerate lamellar-bilayer formation and barrier recovery in humans. Limitation: largely barrier-recovery endpoints in normal/compromised skin; review, not a single RCT.

4b. Lower-tier human (open-label, manufacturer studies, marketing-grade): - SkinCeuticals Triple Lipid Restore 2:4:2 — the flagship cholesterol-dominant anti-aging triad. The supporting study is published and peer-reviewed (Kelloway-type pilot: "Pilot Evaluation of a Novel Topical Formulation Containing High Level, Cholesterol-Dominant, Physiological Lipids for Specific Targeting of Skin Barrier Deficits in Aging Skin," J Drugs Dermatol 2016;15(12):1513) — but it is an 8-week, single-center, OPEN-LABEL, UNCONTROLLED pilot in women aged 55–75 (no placebo/comparator arm), conducted by L'Oréal/SkinCeuticals-affiliated investigators. It reported barrier-function gains and lipidomic increases (epidermal ceramides/cholesterol/triglycerides) plus the marketing cosmetic-endpoint numbers (≈66% smoothness, ≈35% laxity, radiance/pore improvements). Treat as low-tier / manufacturer-sourced: the cosmetic percentages come from an open-label, uncontrolled, industry-run study — not an RCT and not independent, so they cannot carry an efficacy claim. The ratio rationale (2:4:2 cholesterol-dominant for cholesterol-deficient aged skin) is, however, directly supported by Zettersten 1997. - Elias PM, Man MQ, Darmstadt GL. "Optimised emollient mixture for skin barrier repair: applications to global child health." J Glob Health 2022;12:03019. Review + small cohort (n=7 moderate-severe AD) where the optimized 3:1:1-type mixture improved clinical signs within ~5 days. Useful synthesis of the ratio/pH science; tiny clinical n.

Bottom line by use-case: | Claim | Proven for human TOPICAL use? | |---|---| | Complete-ratio lipid mix restores barrier / lowers TEWL in compromised skin | Yes — RCT-level (Andrew 2025; Sugarman 2009; Coderch corpus) | | Wrong/incomplete ratio can delay repair | Yes — but animal-established (Man 1996; Zettersten 1997); strong mechanism | | Cholesterol-dominant ratio better for aged skin | Yes — small human arm (Zettersten 1997) | | Visible anti-aging (smoothness/firmness) from a marketed triad | Weak — manufacturer/open-label only [unverified RCT] | | Comparable to mid-potency steroid for eczema | Yes — pediatric RCT (Sugarman 2009) — but a disease claim; do not use in cosmetic copy |

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Physiological lipids in an appropriate vehicle penetrate beyond the SC to nucleated keratinocytes, are incorporated into endogenous lamellar bodies, and re-secreted into the extracellular space — i.e., they enter the skin's native lipid-processing pathway rather than acting purely as a surface occlusive (Mao-Qiang/Elias mechanism work).
  • Delivery system is decisive. Lamellar/multivesicular emulsions (MVE), liposomal, and well-structured oil-in-water creams improve penetration and deposition vs. crude oil blends. Off-the-shelf delivery-optimized blends (e.g., Evonik SK-INFLUX, CeraVe MVE) exist precisely to solve this.
  • Ratio in the formula ≠ ratio delivered. Differential penetration/partitioning means the applied ratio and the delivered ratio can diverge; this is a known formulation challenge and a reason to lean on validated commercial blends.

6. Formulation

  • Concentration / ratio: Total physiological lipids are typically a few percent of the formula; ratio matters more than absolute dose. Targets: 3:1:1 ceramide-dominant (general/young/dry-from-ceramide-loss) vs. cholesterol-dominant ~2:4:2 / "3:1:1:1 cholesterol-led" (mature/aged skin). For a barrier-and-anti-aging cream, a cholesterol-leaning physiological blend is the more defensible choice.
  • pH: Formulate acidic (~5, range 4.5–5.5) — supports lipid-processing enzymes, barrier maturation, AMP production, and microbiome (Elias/Man 2022; Hachem pH work).
  • Stability: Lipids; need proper emulsification, an antioxidant system (e.g., tocopherol) to prevent FFA/ceramide oxidation, and protection from rancidity. Generally robust in well-made creams; poor in watery serums.
  • Pairs well with:
  • Niacinamide — upregulates the skin's own ceramide synthesis (synergy: supply + stimulate).
  • Phytosphingosine (ceramide precursor), hyaluronic acid / glycerin (humectant layer), fatty alcohols / squalane / occlusives (vehicle).
  • Retinoids, AHAs/BHAs, vitamin C — the triad's best supporting role is as a buffer/recovery base that makes irritating actives tolerable.
  • Conflicts: No true chemical incompatibilities. Practical: high-water/low-viscosity systems and surfactant-heavy cleansers undercut lipid deposition.
  • Sensory: Rich, cushioning, "balm-cream" feel — premium and on-thesis for a barrier hero. Cholesterol/FFA can feel waxy if overdosed; needs sensory tuning.

7. Safety

  • Irritation/sensitization: Very low. These are skin-identical lipids; suitable for sensitive, compromised, post-procedure skin (a real positioning angle). FFA and oxidized lipids are the main theoretical irritant risk → antioxidant + good packaging mitigate.
  • Sun sensitivity: None.
  • Pregnancy/lactation: No known concern; considered safe topically. (Not medical advice.)
  • Sourcing/sustainability: Modern ceramides are synthetic or fermentation-derived (Croda/Solus yeast fermentation; Evonik) — can be vegan and plant/biotech-sourced. Cholesterol is historically lanolin (sheep-wool)-derived; plant/synthetic cholesterol exists but costs more and must be specified for a vegan claim. FFAs are typically plant-derived (palm/coconut/sunflower) — specify RSPO/sustainable palm or palm-free to avoid a sustainability flag. Action item: if "vegan" is a brand claim, lock in plant/synthetic cholesterol and fermentation ceramides at sourcing.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic vs. drug line: "Barrier repair," "restores/replenishes lipids," "strengthens the skin barrier," "reduces moisture loss" are widely used cosmetic claims — keep them tied to appearance, feel, support and the skin's own structure, not to disease.
  • ✅ Safer language: "replenishes the skin's essential barrier lipids," "helps restore and strengthen the look and feel of a healthy barrier," "in the skin's own 3:1:1-type ratio," "skin-identical lipids," "reduces the appearance of moisture loss," "comforts dry, compromised-looking skin," "supports a resilient barrier so the rest of your routine works better."
  • ⚠️ Risky / needs substantiation or legal review: anything invoking eczema / atopic dermatitis / dermatitis ("treats eczema," "as effective as a steroid" — true in the pediatric RCT but a drug claim; keep it internal), "repairs/rebuilds your skin barrier" stated as a cure, "restores youthful collagen," and specific % numbers (e.g., "57% more ceramides," "66% smoother") unless the brand holds its own substantiating study — borrowed manufacturer percentages are not the brand's to claim.
  • Ratio claims: Stating a specific ratio (e.g., "3:1:1" or "cholesterol-dominant for mature skin") is fine as a description of the formula and is scientifically grounded; avoid implying clinical superiority the brand hasn't measured.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: Moderate–high. True ceramides are expensive; cholesterol and FFAs are cheap, but a delivery-optimized physiological blend (MVE/liposomal) plus antioxidant system raises cost vs. a basic emollient. Justified — it is the hero mechanism.
  • Suppliers: Evonik (SK-INFLUX, ceramide range), Croda Beauty / Solus BioTech (SphingoCare, fermentation Ceramide NP/AP), plus prototyping blends (Lotioncrafter, Formulator Sample Shop). Low supply risk; multiple sources.
  • "Buy the base, invest in the brand" fit: Excellent. A validated commercial physiological-lipid base (correct ratio + delivery already solved) lets the brand buy proven barrier science and spend its differentiation budget on brand, sensory, and the cholesterol-dominant aged-skin angle — rather than re-inventing lamellar delivery.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • Real: The ratio principle (complete > incomplete; wrong ratio can delay repair) and the cholesterol-deficiency-in-aged-skin insight are genuine, decades-deep, primary-literature findings — unusually strong for skincare. The triad is the legitimate scientific core of "barrier repair."
  • Overreach to avoid: 1. Precise ratio mysticism. "3:1:1" is presented as a magic constant; in reality the data say any lipid can lead up to ~3-fold and the best lead depends on skin state. Don't fetishize one number. 2. Borrowed percentages. "66% smoother / 57% more ceramides" style stats are manufacturer/open-label, not independent RCTs — [unverified as peer-reviewed]. Don't repeat as fact. 3. Anti-aging conflation. Most rigorous human data are barrier/eczema endpoints, not wrinkle/firmness in healthy aged skin. The triad's anti-aging value is enabling and barrier-preserving, not ECM-rejuvenating. 4. "Just add ceramides." A ceramide-only product is the exact mistake the foundational studies warn against — single-lipid loading can slow repair. The system, not the buzzword, is the point. (Good differentiator vs. "ceramide" hype products.)

11. Verdict for the brand

✅ HERO-system (the engine of the barrier-repair cream), formulated cholesterol-leaning for mature skin. One line: the most evidence-backed "barrier repair" mechanism in dermatology — deliver the full triad in the right ratio (cholesterol-dominant for aged skin), at ~pH 5, with niacinamide synergy, and let it be the credible spine of the hero cream and the tolerability base for the actives layer. Longevity-relevance note: keeps the barrier young (counters the documented age-related lipid/cholesterol decline and barrier slowdown) and lowers inflammaging-driving water loss — a real but lateral longevity lever; it preserves and enables rather than rejuvenates the dermal matrix. Evidence verification (adversarial)

Adversarial fact-check performed 2026-06-19. Every headline citation was independently checked against PubMed / the primary literature for (a) human-vs-animal/in-vitro provenance, (b) whether the study is real and says what is claimed, and (c) funding and sample-size red flags.

Overall evidence grade: B− (split grade)

The grade is deliberately split because the dossier bundles two very different evidentiary stories under one ratio thesis:

  • The ratio mechanism (complete > incomplete; wrong ratio can delay repair; cholesterol is rate-limiting in aged skin): grade B+ / borderline A — but the foundation is animal + acute-perturbation, not human-aging RCT. The principle is genuinely decades-deep and reproduced, but the pivotal ratio experiments (Man 1996, Zettersten 1997, Mao-Qiang 1993) are hairless-mouse / acute tape-strip or solvent / synthesis-inhibition designs. Human confirmation exists but is small (Zettersten's aged-human arm; Man's "preliminary" human data) and uses barrier-recovery/TEWL endpoints, not cosmetic anti-aging.
  • Human topical efficacy in real skin: grade B for barrier/TEWL & eczema-prone skin; grade C–D for visible anti-aging of a marketed triad in healthy aged skin. The strongest human RCTs are in compromised/eczema populations, and one of them (Andrew 2025) tests a non-FFA product. There is no independent RCT showing wrinkle/firmness benefit from a full marketed triad — only an open-label manufacturer pilot.

So: as a mechanism-of-action / barrier-repair claim, the dossier is on solid (B-to-A) ground. As an anti-aging efficacy claim, it is C–D and must not be overstated. The dossier's own framing largely already respects this; the edits below tighten the few places it drifted.

Main caveats (read before writing any copy)

  1. Borrowed evidentiary tiers. The rock-solid part (ratio mechanism) is animal + acute-perturbation; the human part (TEWL/barrier) is B-level and mostly in eczema-prone skin; the anti-aging part is open-label manufacturer data only. Do not let the strength of the mechanism halo onto the anti-aging claim.
  2. Two of the load-bearing human studies are industry-funded (Andrew 2025 = L'Oréal; SkinCeuticals pilot = L'Oréal/SkinCeuticals). Not disqualifying, but not independent.
  3. The single best human RCT (Andrew 2025) does not test the full triad — it tests a ceramide+cholesterol MVE with no added FFA. So the strongest modern human data validate a partial triad, not the 3:1:1/2:4:2 complete system the dossier champions. This is a real gap between the thesis and its best human proof.
  4. No independent RCT supports visible anti-aging (smoothness/firmness/wrinkle) for any marketed triad. The only data are open-label and manufacturer-run.
  5. Endpoint mismatch. Almost all positive data use TEWL / barrier-recovery / SCORAD — not the cosmetic endpoints a consumer infers from "anti-aging."
  6. Eczema/steroid data are drug-territory and must stay internal (already flagged in §8).

  • ✅ Done — Man 1996 "(+ human validation)": changed to "preliminary confirmatory results in damaged human skin (not a standalone human trial)."
  • ✅ Done — SkinCeuticals 2:4:2 "[unverified as peer-reviewed RCT]": corrected to reflect that the study is published & peer-reviewed but open-label, uncontrolled, single-center, industry-run — still low-tier, but no longer implying it may not exist.
  • ⚠️ Still worth watching in future copy/derivative docs:
  • The §11 verdict line "the most evidence-backed 'barrier repair' mechanism in dermatology" is defensible only for the mechanism, not for anti-aging efficacy — keep the word "mechanism" attached if reused.
  • The "cholesterol-dominant is better for aged skin" formulation differentiator rests on one small aged-human arm (Zettersten 1997) with an acute endpoint plus one open-label pilot — present it as a rational, literature-grounded design choice, not as a clinically proven superiority claim.
