Ingredient: Melatonin (INCI: Melatonin)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Antioxidants (indoleamine hormone; chronobiotic / free-radical scavenger / "night-repair" active)

One-line honest summary: Melatonin is one of the better-evidenced topical antioxidants in the longevity space — unusually, its strongest human data are topical, RCT-grade, and skin-specific (UV-erythema photoprotection), not borrowed from oral/animal work. The catch: that strong RCT evidence is for pre-UV photoprotection of redness, not for the wrinkle/firmness "anti-aging" claims most night creams imply. Topical anti-aging evidence exists but is weaker (small, often open-label, combination-product, or very-low-concentration studies). It's an excellent supporting antioxidant with a built-in "night repair / circadian" story — but it is not a substitute for SPF and the anti-aging copy must stay hedged.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Melatonin
  • INCI: Melatonin
  • Chemical name: N-acetyl-5-methoxytryptamine (N-[2-(5-methoxy-1H-indol-3-yl)ethyl]acetamide)
  • CAS: 73-31-4
  • EINECS/EC: 200-797-7
  • Molecular formula / weight: C₁₃H₁₆N₂O₂ · 232.28 g/mol (small molecule — relevant to penetration; see §5) [9][13]
  • Chemical class: Indoleamine — a tryptophan-derived indole hormone. Biosynthesized in the pineal gland (and, importantly, in the skin itself) from tryptophan → serotonin → N-acetylserotonin → melatonin. [7][8]
  • Physical/chemistry notes: Pale-yellow to off-white crystalline solid. Amphiphilic but net lipophilic (logP ~1.32), non-ionizable at physiological/skin pH (no charged groups), modestly water-soluble and freely soluble in alcohols/glycols/oils — a near-ideal profile for crossing the stratum corneum. [4][9]
  • Forms / derivatives used in cosmetics:
  • Plain synthetic melatonin (the dominant form; pharmaceutical/cosmetic grade, vegan/synthetic).
  • Delivery-enhanced forms — nanostructured lipid carriers (NLC), lipospheres, aspasomes (ascorbyl-palmitate nanovesicles), liposomes — explored to boost penetration/loading (mainly in scalp/alopecia work). [1]
  • Active metabolites (formed in situ in skin, not usually added directly): AFMK (N1-acetyl-N2-formyl-5-methoxykynuramine) and AMK (N1-acetyl-5-methoxykynuramine) — these are themselves antioxidants (the "antioxidant cascade," §2). [7][8]
  • Often paired in finished products with the "melatonin + bakuchiol + vitamin C" or "melatonin + niacinamide + HA" night-serum archetypes (e.g., ISDIN Melatonik). [9][13]

2. Class & mechanism (how it works at the skin/cell level)

Melatonin is far more than a "sleep hormone." Skin is both a target and a source: keratinocytes, melanocytes, fibroblasts and hair follicles synthesize, metabolize, and respond to melatonin — a self-contained cutaneous melatoninergic system. [7][8] Mechanistically it acts on several fronts:

  1. Direct free-radical scavenging (receptor-independent). Melatonin directly neutralizes ROS/RNS (hydroxyl radical, peroxyl, singlet oxygen, peroxynitrite). Crucially, when it scavenges a radical it is converted to AFMK and then AMK, which are also potent scavengers — so one melatonin molecule can detoxify multiple radicals in an "antioxidant cascade." This is a distinctive feature vs. most antioxidants that are spent after one hit. [7][8]
  2. Indirect antioxidant — upregulation of endogenous defense. Via Nrf2 activation it upregulates superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase, and glutathione peroxidase, raising the cell's own antioxidant capacity. [2][6][8]
  3. Receptor-mediated signaling. Acts on membrane GPCRs MT1/MT2 (expressed in fibroblasts, keratinocytes, follicles, vasculature) and on nuclear receptors AhR and PPARγ, modulating PKA/MAPK/cAMP-cGMP cascades and gene expression. [1][7]
  4. Mitochondrial protection ("melatonin–mitochondria axis"). Mitochondria are a hub of melatonin metabolism in skin; melatonin preserves mitochondrial membrane potential, supports the electron transport chain, and blunts mitochondria-driven apoptosis after UV. This is the mechanistic link to mitochondrial decline as an aging hallmark. [5][8][10]
  5. DNA protection & repair. Reduces UV-induced DNA photolesions, supports p53/Ser-15 signaling and DNA repair, and inactivates oxidative DNA damage — partly chemically, partly by stimulating repair. [3][6][12]
  6. Anti-inflammatory. Suppresses COX-2/iNOS (in part via p300 HAT inhibition), interferes with NF-κB, modulates the NLRP3 inflammasome, and inhibits MMP-9 — relevant to inflammaging and to MMP-driven collagen breakdown. [1][7]
  7. Chronobiotic / circadian. Melatonin is the body's "night" signal; skin DNA-repair (NER), barrier function and proliferation are circadian-gated. The "night-repair" positioning rests on this — though direct proof that topical melatonin re-entrains skin clocks is thin. [11][12]

Skin-relevant honesty: items 1–6 are well-established cell/molecular biology, much of it demonstrated specifically in skin cells/models. The leap from "melatonin does X in a keratinocyte dish" to "a melatonin night cream reverses wrinkles in your face" is exactly where the evidence thins (see §4).

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

The single most important honesty point: Melatonin is one of the rare longevity-adjacent actives whose best human evidence is genuinely topical and skin-specific — multiple randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trials show topical melatonin (applied before UV) reduces UV-induced erythema. But "reduces sunburn redness when applied pre-UV" ≠ "reverses wrinkles." The anti-aging/anti-wrinkle topical evidence is markedly weaker: small samples, frequent open-label designs, combination products, or very low melatonin concentrations. Be precise about which claim each study actually supports.

Tier 1 — In vitro (cell & molecular)

  • Izykowska et al., 2009, In Vivo 23(5):739. Human keratinocytes (NHEK) and dermal fibroblasts (NHDF), melatonin 10⁻³/10⁻⁶/10⁻⁹ M applied 30 min before UVA (15 J/cm²) or UVB (30–60 mJ/cm²). Melatonin 10⁻³ M protected keratinocytes against both UVB doses and UVA; 10⁻⁶ M protected keratinocytes vs lower UVB; fibroblasts got modest protection at 10⁻⁶ M. Notable caveat: at 10⁻³ M melatonin was toxic to fibroblasts over 24 h — a reminder that "more is better" is false. Limitation: monolayer cells, single time point, dose-cell-type interactions. [19]
  • Fischer / Slominski group in-vitro body of work. Melatonin and its metabolites (AFMK, AMK) protect keratinocytes, melanocytes and fibroblasts from UVB/UVA via anti-apoptotic, antioxidant, DNA-protective/repair and mitochondrial-protective mechanisms; maintain mitochondrial membrane potential and attenuate caspase activation in UVR-exposed HaCaT cells. [5][7][8][10]
  • Janjetovic et al., 2017, J Pineal Res / related — melanocytes. Melatonin and metabolites protect human melanocytes against UVB-induced damage and enhance DNA repair via NRF2-mediated pathways; ↑p53(Ser-15). Limitation: cell model. [6]
  • Antioxidant cascade chemistry. Melatonin's oxidation product AFMK is itself an antioxidant — documented biochemically (the feature that lets one molecule quench several radicals). [7][8]

Read: In-vitro evidence for melatonin as a bona fide skin antioxidant / UV-cytoprotectant is broad, consistent, and mechanistically deep — among the better in-vitro pictures of any antioxidant in this collection. The dose-dependence (and fibroblast toxicity at high dose) matters for formulation.

Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

  • Numerous rodent/animal models support melatonin's photoprotection, anti-inflammatory action and mitochondrial protection in skin (covered in the Scheuer 2014 systematic review, which included 16 experimental studies). [3][8] Limitation: animal skin differs from human; most informative for mechanism, not dosing.
  • Melatonin's systemic geroprotective/antioxidant pedigree (lifespan/oxidative-stress work in rodents) is real but oral/systemic — do not import it into topical claims.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL (context, not the topical case)

  • Sagan et al., 2017 (open-label). 90 women; oral melatonin 2.5 mg (plus a 0.012% topical arm) improved sebum, moisture and elasticity (oral arm); the topical-only arm improved sebum only — but topical concentration was extremely low (0.012%). Open-label, tiny subgroups (8–14), 4 weeks. [1][20]
  • "In & Out" strategy trial (2025), PMC11767497. Adding oral melatonin to a topical regimen improved skin lipid profile and moisturization vs topical alone — supports a combined oral+topical narrative but can't isolate topical melatonin's anti-aging effect. [20]
  • Oral melatonin is widely used for sleep/circadian indications with a strong safety record — useful safety context, not skin-efficacy evidence.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters most for a skincare brand)

4a. Photoprotection / UV-erythema (the STRONG sub-tier — RCT-grade):

  • Bangha et al., 1996, Dermatology / 1997, Arch Dermatol Res (dose-response & timing). Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled; ~20 volunteers each. Topical melatonin applied before UV (up to ~0.6 mg/cm²; 15 min pre-irradiation) almost completely suppressed UV-induced erythema in the dose-response work; benefit was strongest in "high responders." Critical mechanistic finding: protective only when applied BEFORE UV — no effect applied after. [3][21]
  • Dreher et al., 1998, British Journal of Dermatology 139(2):332 (Dreher, Gabard, Schwindt, Maibach). Randomized, double-blind, in-vivo; topical melatonin (up to 2.5%) ± vitamins C & E, applied 30 min pre-UV. Dose-dependent photoprotection from melatonin; vitamins alone modest; melatonin + both vitamins best (combination markedly enhanced erythema reduction; reported SPF-equivalent ~2.1 at 2.5%). Limitation: small n, acute erythema endpoint only. [4]
  • Scheuer et al., 2016, Journal of Dermatological Science 84(2):178–185 — "Dose-dependent sun protective effect of topical melatonin," natural sunlight. Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled; 23 volunteers (8 M/15 F); melatonin 0.5% / 2.5% / 12.5% vs placebo against natural-sunlight erythema. 12.5% was significantly protective; significant dose-dependent erythema reduction — but the effect was driven by erythema-responders and treated areas were small. Limitation: small n, small treated zones, responder-dependent. [14]
  • Scheuer 2017 (PhD thesis / Dan Med J B5358; safety crossover). 10 volunteers, 12.5% melatonin on ~80% body surface (crossover, double-blind): plasma melatonin rose to ~5 ng/mL with no cognitive dysfunction and no systemic toxicity — important high-dose safety datapoint. [14][22]
  • Scheuer et al., 2014, Photodermatol Photoimmunol Photomed — systematic review (4 human + 16 experimental studies): concludes melatonin protects against UV when applied before exposure (not after), acting as a direct antioxidant plus gene-regulation/DNA-stabilizing effects; flags that most human work used artificial UV and called for natural-sunlight and longer-term safety studies (later partly addressed by Scheuer 2016). [3]

Photoprotection verdict: This is genuinely good, convergent, RCT-grade topical human evidence — rare in this collection. But it supports "reduces UV-induced redness when applied pre-exposure," NOT "replaces SPF" (the erythema reduction is modest, responder-dependent, and not a measured broad-spectrum SPF). Marketing must not let "photoprotective antioxidant" drift into "sunscreen."

4b. Anti-aging / wrinkles / elasticity (the WEAK sub-tier):

  • Morganti et al., 2012. Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, 70 women — significant improvements in hydration, elasticity, wrinkles, photoaging signs. BUT melatonin concentration was 0.0002% plus vitamin E + β-glucan. The melatonin dose is so low, and the product so combined, that benefit cannot be attributed to melatonin. [1]
  • Milani & Sparavigna, 2018. Randomized, split-face, open-label, 22 women; 0.1% melatonin in lipospheres → improvements in wrinkles (p≈0.05), microrelief, tonicity, dryness. No placebo; open-label; small. [1]
  • Goldberg et al., 2019. Open-label, non-comparative, 39 women; melatonin + bakuchiol + vitamin C serum-in-oil → improved wrinkles, pigmentation, firmness at 4/8/12 wks. No placebo; combination; concentrations undisclosed. [1]
  • Granger et al., 2020. Open-label night cream (melatonin + niacinamide + HA + others) → hydration/wrinkle/elasticity/brown-spot improvements. No placebo; combination; small/unspecified n. [1]
  • "Antiaging efficacy of melatonin-based day & night creams" RCT (Clinical, Cosmetic & Investigational Dermatology / Dove). Randomized trial of melatonin day/night creams reporting wrinkle/elasticity/hydration/tone benefits — but, as with the others, a finished multi-ingredient cosmetic, so not a clean melatonin readout. [23]
  • Proya-funded "novel blend" study, 2026 (vendor-funded, ex vivo / model). A lavender-extract + dipeptide-4 (LA-DP4) blend designed to boost the skin's own melatonin, targeting oxidative stress, glycation, autophagy and circadian disruption; the cited press piece reports melatonin production +10.69% in ex vivo human skin. Note: this blend does not contain added melatonin — it is a "melatonin-booster," not a topical-melatonin study. The "Type I collagen +280.95%" figure circulating in some summaries is not stated in the cited CosmeticsDesign-Asia article and could not be verified from the primary press source — do not repeat it. Vendor-funded, ex vivo/model, combination — marketing-adjacent, not proof.** [16] [collagen +280.95% UNVERIFIED — absent from cited source]

Anti-aging verdict: Suggestive but not rigorously established for melatonin monotherapy topically. Nearly every positive anti-aging trial is confounded by combination formulas, open-label design, tiny/very-low melatonin doses, or industry funding. The honest statement: topical melatonin plausibly contributes to anti-aging via its proven antioxidant/photoprotective biology, but clean RCT proof that melatonin itself reduces wrinkles is lacking.

4c. Adjacent topical human evidence (supportive of safety/utility):

  • Radiotherapy dermatitis RCTs — Ben-David 2016 (n=47) & Zetner 2023 (n=48 analyzed, 2.5% + DMSO). Topical melatonin reduced acute radiation dermatitis severity in breast-cancer patients (Ben-David: 59% vs 90% grade 1–2; Zetner: lower symptom score but primary endpoint not statistically significant). Supports anti-inflammatory/cytoprotective topical action in irradiated skin. [1][15]
  • Alopecia — Fischer et al., 2004 (RCT, n=40 women, 0.1% solution, 6 months). The one sound-design single-ingredient scalp study: modest anagen-hair increase (occipital hair in androgenetic, frontal in diffuse) + demonstrated percutaneous absorption — plasma melatonin rose under treatment, though the paper states it did not exceed the physiological night peak (i.e., a measurable but small systemic signal, not the specific ~35–50 pg/mL figure cited in some secondary summaries). Several other alopecia studies exist but are open-label/combination. Scalp/hair, not facial anti-aging. [verify exact plasma values against primary BJD 150(2):341 paper before quoting numbers] [1]

Bottom line by tier: Proven topically in humans → pre-UV erythema reduction (multiple RCTs) and reduced radiation dermatitis. Strong in vitro/mechanism → antioxidant cascade, mitochondrial/DNA protection, Nrf2, MMP-9/NF-κB. Weak/confounded topically → wrinkle/firmness "anti-aging." Not demonstrated → replacing sunscreen; clean monotherapy wrinkle reversal; microbiome effects.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Strongly in favor: Small (232 Da), non-ionized at all skin pH (no charge to repel it), and amphiphilic/lipophilic (logP ~1.32) — a textbook profile for stratum-corneum penetration. Reviews state melatonin "easily penetrates the skin." [4][9]
  • Demonstrated percutaneous absorption (the key real-world datapoint): Scalp-applied 0.1% melatonin measurably raised plasma melatonin (Fischer 2004) — the primary paper notes the rise did not exceed the physiological night peak, so this is a small systemic signal; the precise ~35–50 pg/mL figure used in some secondary write-ups is not supported by the source and has been removed. The more striking datapoint is the high-dose safety study: 12.5% over ~80% body surface reached ~5 ng/mL plasma (Scheuer) — proving it crosses skin into circulation at scale. [1][14][22] Penetration evidence here is still better than most "longevity" actives (peptides, spermidine, growth factors) can show, but note that crossing into plasma proves stratum-corneum transit, not that an anti-aging-relevant concentration parks in the dermis.
  • Delivery systems still add value: NLC, aspasomes, lipospheres improve loading, follicular targeting and residence time (mainly shown in scalp work), and may give more controlled/sustained delivery to viable layers. [1]
  • Practical read: Penetration is proven, not just plausible — a genuine differentiator. The open question is not "does it get in" but "what intra-skin concentration and duration are needed for an anti-aging (vs erythema) outcome," which clinical data don't pin down.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration:
  • Photoprotection RCTs: meaningful effects at 2.5% (Dreher) and strongest at 12.5% (Scheuer, natural sunlight); ~0.5% weaker. So robust effects skew high (2.5–12.5%).
  • Anti-aging products: span an absurdly wide range — 0.0002% to ~0.1–0.5% — with no validated dose-response for wrinkles. Many marketed "melatonin" creams use fractional-percent levels (likely below where the RCT erythema data apply).
  • Practical target: for a credible antioxidant/night-repair active, ~0.1–1% is a defensible cosmetic range; going to 2.5%+ aligns with the photoprotection literature but raises cost/color considerations and shifts toward "drug-like" expectations. Avoid implying a validated anti-aging dose-response. Note the in-vitro fibroblast toxicity at very high (10⁻³ M) concentrations — high-and-higher is not automatically better. [4][14][19]
  • pH: Non-ionizable; stable across the typical cosmetic range (~4–7). No acid-mantle disruption. Compatible with a normal skin-friendly pH (~5–5.5).
  • Stability: Light-sensitive — the main formulation risk. Melatonin photodegrades on UV exposure; protect with opaque/airless packaging and consider antioxidant co-stabilizers. (Ironically, melatonin can itself stabilize certain hydrophilic gelling polymers against light-induced viscosity loss — a patented effect.) Reasonably heat/oxidation-stable in the dark; run stability + challenge testing. Solubility: use glycols/alcohol or solubilizers; it is only modestly water-soluble. [24][25]
  • Pairings (logical and partly evidenced):
  • Vitamins C + E — evidenced synergy for photoprotection (Dreher 1998); also a "network antioxidant" rationale. [4]
  • Niacinamide / hyaluronic acid / panthenol — common in night/barrier formulas (combination-product evidence). [9]
  • Bakuchiol — common "gentle night retinol-alternative" pairing in melatonin serums. [9]
  • Fits a "PM antioxidant + barrier" product cleanly (aligns with the brand's barrier-repair thesis and a night-repair story).
  • Conflicts: No notable hard incompatibilities at cosmetic pH. Keep away from strong oxidizers and prolonged light exposure. If paired with L-ascorbic acid, manage the low-pH/oxidation environment (packaging, antioxidants).
  • Sensory: Plain melatonin at low % is largely odorless/colorless in finished product; pale-yellow raw material can tint high-% formulas. No tackiness/known sensory penalty.

7. Safety

  • Irritation/sensitization: Very favorable. Endogenous to human skin; reviews describe it as "substantially safe and practically devoid of allergenic potential." No meaningful irritation/sensitization signal across the topical literature. Caveat: still run our own HRIPT/patch testing on the finished formula — endogenous ≠ automatically non-irritating in every vehicle. [1][9]
  • Systemic absorption / sedation: It does penetrate to circulation (§5). High-dose whole-body application (12.5% over ~80% BSA → ~5 ng/mL plasma) caused no cognitive impairment or sedation in the crossover safety study — and normal facial-serum doses are vastly smaller. Sedation risk from a face-sized topical dose is negligible, plausibly aided by the convention of PM application. Still, conservative copy is wise. [14][22]
  • Sun: Not photosensitizing — opposite, it's photoprotective when applied pre-UV. BUT the critical safety/efficacy nuance is the timing + the "not a sunscreen" point: melatonin reduces erythema applied before UV and has no benefit applied after; it is not a measured broad-spectrum SPF and must not be positioned as sun protection. Daytime use should sit under real SPF; the natural home is PM. [3][14]
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: No specific topical-cosmetic contraindication data. Because it is a hormone with systemic absorption, default to conservative copy ("consult your doctor if pregnant or breastfeeding"). Oral melatonin in pregnancy is generally advised only under medical guidance — mirror that caution. [unverified — no topical-pregnancy study found]
  • Hormonal note: It is a hormone; while topical facial doses yield trivial systemic levels, avoid any claim implying systemic hormonal action, and flag for anyone on melatonin-interacting medication out of caution.
  • Pigmentation safety: Despite the amphibian-derived name, melatonin does not darken human skin; if anything it mildly inhibits melanogenesis (tyrosinase ↓ in vitro; lightening reported in melasma). No hyperpigmentation risk. [17][18]
  • Sourcing/allergen: Synthetic melatonin is vegan, animal-free, and free of common allergens. No gluten/wheat/animal-origin concerns (unlike some "natural" actives).

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic ingredient status: Listed as INCI Melatonin (CAS 73-31-4; EC 200-797-7), cosmetic function antioxidant (also skin-conditioning). Appears in marketed cosmetics globally. However — important regulatory flag: melatonin is a hormone / regulated supplement or medicine in several jurisdictions (e.g., prescription-only oral status in parts of the EU/UK; OTC supplement in the US). Its use in cosmetics is generally treated as a topical cosmetic ingredient, but confirm legality per target market before formulating — the hormone status makes this less of a free pass than a typical antioxidant. [9][13] [partially unverified — verify current per-market cosmetic legality with regulatory counsel]
  • The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame): This is where melatonin is especially tricky because the strong evidence is for a physiological (drug-like) endpoint — sunburn/erythema reduction and radiation-dermatitis — which sits close to OTC-sunscreen / drug territory.
  • ⚠️ High-risk (drug/structure-function or sunscreen): "protects from the sun," "photoprotective / SPF," "prevents sunburn," "repairs UV/DNA damage," "treats radiation dermatitis," "boosts collagen by 280%" (also factually unverified — see §10), "reverses aging," "anti-inflammatory." The erythema/DNA/radiation language is the trap — it's the best-evidenced part scientifically but the most dangerous to claim commercially.
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "antioxidant that helps defend skin against free radicals / environmental stress," "supports skin's overnight recovery," "a molecule skin naturally makes, here to support its nighttime renewal," "helps improve the look of tired, stressed skin," "supports a healthy-looking, even tone." Even "supports the barrier / hydration" should stay hedged given combination-product evidence.
  • Substantiation reality: The photoprotection RCTs would tempt bold sun claims — don't; those are regulated drug/sunscreen claims and the studies measure erythema in tiny responder subsets, not labeled SPF. For honest anti-aging performance claims we'd want our own finished-formula human testing; the published anti-aging data are too confounded to substantiate monotherapy wrinkle claims. See ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Forms/suppliers: Synthetic melatonin is a mature, inexpensive, widely available fine chemical (pharma + cosmetic grade) from numerous suppliers (e.g., COSROMA and other personal-care raw-material vendors; abundant pharma-grade sources). Delivery-enhanced grades (NLC/liposomal) cost more. [26]
  • Cost tier: Low as a raw material — one of the cheaper credible antioxidants per gram (high-volume supplement-driven supply). Cost rises only with encapsulation/delivery tech or very high use levels (2.5%+).
  • Supply/standardization: Synthetic = consistent purity, no botanical batch variability, vegan/clean-label friendly. Specify pharma/cosmetic grade with COA (purity, residual solvents). Low supply risk.

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • "Melatonin repairs your skin while you sleep / night-repair miracle." The circadian/night-repair rationale is real (DNA repair and barrier are clock-gated), but there is no clean human trial showing a topical melatonin serum measurably re-times skin clocks or out-repairs a daytime application. It's a strong story, lightly supported as a proven outcome. [11][12]
  • "Clinically proven photoprotection." True for erythema, applied pre-UV, in small RCTs — but routinely stretched into implied sun protection / SPF, which it is not. The post-UV-no-effect finding alone kills the "apply after sun to undo damage" angle. [3][14]
  • "Boosts collagen 280%." Worse than confounded — the +280.95% Type I collagen figure could not be verified in the cited press source (which actually headlines a +10.69% rise in skin's own melatonin from a lavender/dipeptide "booster" blend that contains no added melatonin). It is vendor-funded, ex-vivo/model, combination, and the headline number itself is unsubstantiated from the source on file. Classic single-number marketing bait — do not use. [16]
  • "Anti-aging proven in clinical studies." The positive anti-aging trials are mostly open-label, combination, or use melatonin at 0.0002% — i.e., they don't isolate melatonin and several can't support causal wrinkle claims. [1]
  • "Natural sleep hormone for your skin → it'll relax/de-stress skin." Pleasant framing; the anti-inflammatory biology is real in models, but "de-stresses skin" as a measured human outcome is not established.
  • The genuine, under-hyped strengths (worth leaning on honestly): the antioxidant cascade (one molecule, multiple radicals), proven percutaneous penetration, mitochondrial/DNA protection mechanism, excellent tolerability, and the fact that — uniquely — it has real topical human RCTs, even if for the "wrong" (erythema) endpoint.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ SUPPORTING — a genuinely well-evidenced topical antioxidant with a built-in "night-repair / circadian" narrative; strong enough to be a near-hero in a PM product, but the anti-aging claims must stay hedged and it must never masquerade as SPF.

One-line: Melatonin is one of the few longevity-adjacent actives with real topical human RCT data (UV-erythema photoprotection) plus proven skin penetration, a distinctive self-regenerating antioxidant cascade, deep mitochondrial/DNA-protection mechanism, and an excellent safety/clean-label profile — making it an ideal science-forward supporting antioxidant, especially for a night/overnight product — but its anti-wrinkle evidence is weak/confounded and its best evidence (erythema) is a regulated drug/sunscreen-adjacent endpoint we cannot claim.

Why supporting and not hero: The proven topical outcome (less UV redness, pre-exposure) isn't a marketable cosmetic anti-aging claim, and the anti-aging trials don't isolate melatonin. As a supporting antioxidant in a barrier-repair PM cream — paired with vitamins C/E (evidenced synergy), niacinamide, ceramides — it's compelling, honest, and on-thesis.

Longevity-relevance note: Melatonin punches above its weight for a skin-longevity brand: it targets multiple aging hallmarks at once (oxidative stress, mitochondrial decline, DNA damage, inflammaging) through a mechanistically rich, well-published cutaneous melatoninergic system — and it ties the brand to circadian biology, a credible and differentiated longevity theme. The honest gap is the endpoint translation: strong on antioxidant/photoprotection biology, thin on proven topical wrinkle/firmness outcomes. The honest path to bold claims is our own finished-formula human testing; until then, frame it as the antioxidant skin makes itself, supporting overnight recovery — and keep SPF as the real sun-protection step.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Independent fact-check performed 2026-06-19. Each headline claim tested for: (a) is the evidence HUMAN + TOPICAL, or borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral? (b) are cited studies real and accurately summarized? (c) industry-funded or under-powered?

Overall grade: B (moderate human-topical evidence — with one A-grade pocket and several C/D claims to keep penned in)

The grade is a deliberate average. Melatonin earns an A on a narrow endpoint (pre-UV erythema photoprotection) and slides to C/D on the anti-aging endpoint the category actually sells. A single letter that captures the dossier honestly is B: real, convergent, RCT-grade topical human data exists, but not for the wrinkle/firmness claims, and a couple of the dossier's supporting numbers were overstated (now corrected).

Main caveats / claims softened in this pass

  1. Penetration overstated (§4c, §5). The "~35–50 pg/mL" Fischer plasma figure is not in the primary paper (which says the rise stayed within the physiological night peak). Softened to "measurable but small systemic signal"; flagged that plasma transit ≠ proof of an anti-aging-relevant dermal concentration.
  2. "+280.95% collagen" (§3, §4b, §8, §10, [16]) is unverified and quarantined. Not present in the cited CosmeticsDesign-Asia article; the actual reported number is +10.69% melatonin from a lavender/dipeptide booster blend (no added melatonin). Do not repeat the 280% figure anywhere in copy.
  3. Citation [1] authorship corrected (Greco et al., not Bocheva/Slominski).
  4. Anti-aging endpoint stays C-grade. No clean monotherapy human RCT shows topical melatonin reduces wrinkles; the dossier's existing hedging is correct and should not be loosened.

Net

The dossier was already unusually honest — its tier structure and "erythema ≠ wrinkles" framing are sound and survive scrutiny. The only genuine integrity problems were two overstated numbers (a penetration figure and a vendor collagen figure) and one author misattribution, all now corrected. With those fixes, the document is defensible. The marketing-relevant verdict is unchanged: supporting antioxidant with a credible night-repair story; keep all anti-aging and all sun claims hedged.
