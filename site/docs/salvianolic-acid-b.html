Ingredient: Salvianolic acid B / Danshen (INCI: Salvia Miltiorrhiza Root Extract; isolate INCI not standardized)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Anti-glycation & related (polyphenolic antioxidant / NRF2 activator / MMP-inhibitor; the "anti-glycation" labeling is largely aspirational — see §3 and §10)

One-line honest summary: Salvianolic acid B (Sal B) is the most abundant polyphenol in Danshen (Salvia miltiorrhiza) and has a genuinely deep, top-journal evidence base — but almost all of it is systemic (IV/oral) cardiovascular/anti-fibrotic pharmacology in animals and humans. For skin, the data are real but in-vitro and nude-mouse photoaging (NRF2/antioxidant, MMP-1 suppression, procollagen). There is no human topical RCT of purified Sal B, the molecule is large, charged, and penetrates skin poorly (its own delivery papers say so), it is chemically unstable at cosmetic-relevant pH/temperature, and the "anti-glycation" framing for the molecule specifically is weakly evidenced. Treat it as a supporting antioxidant / soothing botanical with a strong science narrative, not a proven topical hero.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Salvianolic acid B (Sal B / SalB / SAB). Botanical source: Danshen = Salvia miltiorrhiza Bunge (red sage / Chinese sage), Lamiaceae.
  • Synonyms: Lithospermic acid B; Dan Shen Suan B; Danfensuan B. (Historically confused with "lithospermic acid B.")
  • INCI:
  • No standardized monograph INCI for the purified isolate "Salvianolic acid B" in common cosmetic use. In practice it appears on labels via the botanical: Salvia Miltiorrhiza (Sage) Root Extract (and …Leaf/Flower Extract), or in branded actives. Confirm the exact INCI a supplier will let you declare before relying on isolate-level claims. [12][13]
  • CAS: 121521-90-2 is the most-cited CAS (Sigma/SCBT EP reference standard). Note a second CAS, 115939-25-8, is widely used by suppliers/Good Scents — likely a salt/stereochemistry or registry-duplication issue. Verify CAS + form on every COA. [4][14]
  • Molecular formula / weight: C₃₆H₃₀O₁₆ · ~718.6 g/mol — a large molecule (≈5× the size of spermidine, ≈4× vitamin C). Size alone predicts poor passive skin penetration (see §5). [4]
  • Chemical class: Polyphenol; a caffeic-acid-derived oligomer — formally a member of 1-benzofurans, an enoate ester, a dicarboxylic acid, and a catechol. Built from danshensu (salvianic acid A) and caffeic acid units; multiple catechol (ortho-dihydroxyphenyl) groups drive both its strong antioxidant activity and its oxidative instability. [4]
  • Physical/chemistry: Water-soluble (it is a "water-soluble" Danshen constituent, vs. the lipophilic tanshinones); acidic (dicarboxylic acid). Highly oxidizable.
  • Key forms/derivatives:
  • Purified Sal B (reference-standard / "98% HPLC" research and cosmetic-active grades).
  • Magnesium salt of Sal B (the form used in the Chinese drug salvianolate/丹参多酚酸盐 injections).
  • Standardized Danshen root extract (the realistic cosmetic route; Sal B is the marker but the extract carries danshensu, rosmarinic acid, tanshinones, etc.).
  • Sibling actives worth noting because marketing blurs them: danshensu / salvianic acid A (the small monomer) and salvianolic acid A (Sal A) (separately studied for UV/cGAS-STING). [11]

2. Class & mechanism (how it works at the skin/cell level)

Sal B is fundamentally a polyphenolic redox-active antioxidant with several converging, well-mapped molecular actions. The skin-relevant pillars (with honesty about where each was shown):

  1. Direct ROS/free-radical scavenging. Its multiple catechol groups make it a potent radical scavenger; in the lead characterization study it outperformed vitamin C across DPPH, ABTS, hydroxyl, and superoxide assays (VCEAC ≈18.6 vs 10.0 for vitamin C by ABTS), but was weaker than vitamin C at iron chelation and H₂O₂ scavenging. So antioxidant potency is assay-dependent — strong on radical quenching, not universally "stronger than vitamin C." (Chemical-assay data only, not skin.) [9]
  2. NRF2/ARE activation (the headline skin mechanism). Sal B activates the NRF2 transcription factor → upregulates endogenous antioxidant enzymes (HO-1, SOD, catalase, NQO1), reducing UVB-driven oxidative damage and protecting mitochondria. This is the mechanism in the 2024 Phytomedicine and 2025 senescence papers, and also how it protects against γ-radiation (Nrf2/Bach1). [6][8][16]
  3. MMP-1 / AP-1 suppression + procollagen support (photoaging axis). In UVB-irradiated human dermal fibroblasts, Sal B inhibited ERK/JNK/p38 MAPK phosphorylation → reduced AP-1 (c-Fos/c-Jun) → down MMP-1 and IL-6, up type I procollagen and TGF-β1. This is the classic "anti-photoaging" cascade. [1]
  4. Anti-inflammatory. Suppresses NF-κB and pro-inflammatory cytokines; Danshen root extract suppresses COX/PGE2 in stressed skin (basis of a topical soothing patent). [6][12]
  5. Anti-fibrotic / TGF-β-modulating (context-dependent — and a caution). In internal-organ and scleroderma skin fibrosis models, Sal B does the opposite of a "collagen booster": it reduces fibroblast proliferation, ECM gene transcription, and collagen deposition via TGF-β/Smad inhibition. This is real, repeated pharmacology (heart, lung, scleroderma skin). [3][17][18]

The central mechanistic tension (be honest in copy): Sal B raises procollagen in UVB-damaged fibroblasts [1] yet lowers collagen in fibrotic/activated fibroblasts [3][10][17]. It is best read as a TGF-β/MMP normalizer and antioxidant, not a simple "collagen builder." A wound-migration study even found it slowed fibroblast migration and reduced surface coverage at 75 µg/mL [10]. So "boosts collagen" is an over-simplification the evidence does not cleanly support.

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

The single most important honesty point: Sal B's deepest and highest-quality evidence — including a human Phase 1 trial — is for intravenous / systemic cardiovascular and anti-fibrotic use, not skin. The skin evidence is genuine but tops out at in-vitro + nude-mouse topical. There is no published human topical RCT of purified salvianolic acid B, and the one product-level human cosmetic signal is a Danshen-extract soothing patent, not isolate efficacy. Any topical anti-aging claim today is borrowed (systemic format) or preclinical (cell/mouse).

Tier 1 — In vitro (cells & co-cultures)

  • Sun et al., 2015, Photochemistry & Photobiology (DOI 10.1111/php.12427). Normal human dermal fibroblasts; Sal B 1–100 µg/mL; UVB. Sal B ↓MMP-1, ↓IL-6, ↑type I procollagen, ↑TGF-β1, and inhibited ERK/JNK/p38 phosphorylation → ↓AP-1 (c-Fos/c-Jun). The foundational topical-photoaging mechanism. Limitation: monolayer fibroblasts, no in-vivo/human; high µg/mL doses. [1]
  • Chen et al., 2014, Molecules (DOI 10.3390/molecules19022029; PMC6271020). Detroit 551 human fibroblasts + B16 melanoma; Sal B tested to very high mM ranges. Effects were modest: proliferation ~120% of control, collagen only ~110%, melanin ↓~20% at 0.5 mM, dose-dependent tyrosinase/dopachrome suppression. Limitation: 24 h only, isolated cells, mushroom tyrosinase, effect sizes small and at non-physiological concentrations; the parent extract showed opposite melanin effects — confounders present. [15]
  • Meng et al., 2022, J. Cosmetic Dermatology (DOI 10.1111/jocd.14516). Indirect mechanism: Sal B acted on human dermal microvascular endothelial cells (HDMEC), ↑NO and growth-factor output; HDMEC-conditioned medium then ↑collagen in human dermal fibroblasts — i.e., a microvascular/paracrine route to collagen, not a direct fibroblast effect. Limitation: in-vitro conditioned-medium model; clever but indirect; small. [2]
  • Sun et al. (Haifeng Sun, Shen, Zhang, Zhou), 2024, Natural Product Communications (DOI 10.1177/1934578X231219604). Co-cultured A375 melanoma + HaCaT; Sal B (and salvianic acid A) inhibited tyrosinase activity and tyrosinase-related-protein mRNA → ↓melanin. Frames both as candidate cosmetic whiteners. Limitation: in-vitro co-culture only; smaller journal; no human or formulation data. [5]
  • Szwedowicz et al., 2021, Int. J. Molecular Sciences (DOI 10.3390/ijms22147728; PMC8307677). Human gingival fibroblasts; "wound-healing" gap assay; Sal B 25–150 µg/mL. At 75 µg/mL Sal B reduced migration (~42% vs ~99% coverage at day 3) while increasing collagen III and viability — i.e., it slowed migration. Limitation: gingival (not facial/dermal) fibroblasts, in-vitro; complicates a simple "wound-healing/collagen booster" story. [10]
  • Antioxidant characterization — Huang/Zhao et al., 2007 (radical-scavenging characterization of danshensu and Sal B). Sal B > vitamin C on DPPH, but < vitamin C on iron chelation / H₂O₂. Limitation: chemical assays, not skin. [9]

Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

  • Sun J.M. et al., 2024, Phytomedicine 130:155676 (DOI 10.1016/j.phymed.2024.155676). Nude-mouse UVB photoaging + HaCaT + ex-vivo human skin. Topical Sal B improved wrinkles and epidermal thickness — reported as more effective than tretinoin in this model — via NRF2 activation, ROS scavenging, mitochondrial protection, and NF-κB/MAPK inhibition. The strongest "skin anti-aging" study to date. Limitations: nude mice (no immune/melanin equivalence to humans), single study, the "better than tretinoin" line is a model-specific headline that should not be repeated as a human claim; ex-vivo human skin ≠ living human RCT. [6]
  • Wu, Tao, Xu et al., 2025, Naunyn-Schmiedeberg's Arch. Pharmacol. (DOI 10.1007/s00210-025-04411-5). Transdermal-delivery study: because "therapeutic activity is severely hindered by poor skin permeability," the authors built a thermosensitive hydrogel with chitosan-coated gold nanoparticles + hyaluronic acid + Pluronic F-127 to push Sal B through skin; this improved penetration/retention and photoaging outcomes (↑collagen, ↓ROS). Two readings: (a) confirms efficacy when delivery is engineered; (b) is an explicit admission that plain Sal B does not penetrate skin well — central to §5. [7]
  • Liang et al., 2018, Biomedicine & Pharmacotherapy (DOI 10.1016/j.biopha.2018.11.108; PMID 30530290). Bleomycin-induced systemic-sclerosis skin fibrosis in mice: Sal B reduced skin thickness and collagen deposition, and in vitro reduced SSc fibroblast proliferation + ECM/collagen via TGF-β. Anti-fibrotic — the collagen-lowering face of Sal B; relevant as a caution against "collagen booster" framing. [3]
  • Anti-fibrosis corroboration: pulmonary fibrosis (TGF-β inhibition) [17]; diabetic-cardiomyopathy myocardial fibrosis (↓collagen via Smad7) [18]; epidural fibrosis reduction in rats. All reinforce a net anti-fibrotic / ECM-restraining systemic phenotype. [3][17][18]
  • γ-radiation protection — Wang et al. (PMC6323199): Sal B protected mice from radiation damage via Nrf2/Bach1 — supports the antioxidant/NRF2 mechanism in vivo (non-skin endpoint). [16]

Tier 3 — Human ORAL / SYSTEMIC (deepest human data — but not skin)

  • Cheng et al., 2023, Frontiers in Pharmacology 14:1146309 (DOI 10.3389/fphar.2023.1146309; PMC10133543). Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled Phase 1, n=63 healthy volunteers, IV Sal B (SAD 25–300 mg; MAD 150/250 mg ×5 d). Excellent safety/tolerability, no serious AEs; treatment-related AEs ~19–25% (transient ↑liver enzymes 4%, ↑BNP, dizziness, chest discomfort). PK: dose-proportional Cmax, short half-life 0.67–2.13 h, no accumulation. Hugely valuable for systemic safety; but IV, healthy adults, not a skin endpoint — and the short half-life underlines that this is a rapidly-cleared molecule. [8]
  • Salvianolate / Sal-B-magnesium injections (China): Approved cardiovascular drug; large clinical and meta-analytic literature for angina/atherosclerosis/stroke. Establishes a long human systemic safety track record — but again not skin and not topical. [21][22]
  • Oral Danshen (whole-herb) — cardiovascular RCT (Qian et al., 2012, PMC4508048): Danshen water extract showed no benefit on CV risk factors in a double-blind crossover — a useful reminder that whole-herb ≠ isolated-active efficacy. [23]
  • Oral Danshen-combination for melasma — "Lutai Danshen Baishao granules," 2024 RCT (placebo-controlled): Reported efficacy/safety for female melasma. Oral, multi-herb combination — cannot attribute to topical Sal B; pigment relevance only by analogy. [24]

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters for a skincare brand — essentially absent)

  • No published human topical RCT of purified salvianolic acid B was found. This is the decisive gap.
  • Danshen-extract topical soothing — SILAB patents (e.g., EP3508195A1; US 2019/0209457). Salvia miltiorrhiza root extract claimed to soothe/reduce redness and sensitivity and suppress COX/PGE2 in stressed skin. Patent/commercial, extract-level (not isolate), self-substantiated; the closest thing to a "human topical" signal and it's for soothing, not anti-aging. [12][13]
  • Ex-vivo human skin (within Sun 2024): supportive of penetration-independent activity in excised skin, but not a living-human trial. [6]

Bottom line by tier: Proven for Sal B specifically → systemic antioxidant/anti-inflammatory/anti-fibrotic pharmacology and human IV safety [8]. Strong preclinical skin antioxidant/anti-photoaging signal (in-vitro + nude mouse) [1][6]. Not proven for purified topical Sal B in living human skin — zero RCTs. Anti-glycation, "collagen-boosting," and brightening claims are in-vitro or borrowed.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Against (the dominant reality): Sal B is large (~719 Da), highly polar/water-soluble, multiply ionizable (dicarboxylic + many phenolic OH) — exactly the profile that penetrates the lipophilic stratum corneum poorly. Researchers building Sal B skincare explicitly state its "therapeutic activity is severely hindered by poor skin permeability" and therefore engineer nanoparticle/hydrogel delivery systems. [7]
  • Implication: A plain aqueous Sal B serum likely delivers little active to viable epidermis/dermis. The mouse efficacy that exists used either excised/ex-vivo skin (bypassing intact barrier kinetics) or delivery-enhanced formulations. [6][7]
  • Short residence even systemically: IV half-life 0.67–2.13 h [8] — a rapidly-metabolized molecule (degrades to danshensu, caffeic acid, etc.; see §6), so even delivered active is short-lived.
  • Practical read: Penetration is the #1 formulation problem. Realistic options: (a) accept it as a surface antioxidant/soothing actor, (b) invest in a delivery system (liposome/nanoparticle/penetration enhancer) and substantiate it, or (c) use a standardized Danshen extract and claim at the extract/sensory level. Do not imply deep dermal collagen remodeling from a basic serum.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: No validated human topical use level. In-vitro actives spanned 1–100 µg/mL (fibroblast) up to mM (Chen 2014) — these are culture doses, not formulation guidance. Cosmetic use is typically low single-digit % of a standardized extract (Sal B a fraction of that). Formulate conservatively; do not imply a proven dose-response. [1][15]
  • pH: Sal B is most stable at LOW pH — degradation accelerates as pH (and temperature) rise. [25][26] So it behaves like an acidic-pH active: target a low pH (~3.0–4.0) environment (similar logic to L-ascorbic acid). High-pH formulas will degrade it.
  • Stability (a real weakness): Polyphenolic and readily oxidized; aqueous solutions degrade into 17+ products (danshensu, lithospermic acid, salvianolic acids A/D/E, caffeic acid, protocatechuic aldehyde). Heat, high pH, light, and oxygen all accelerate this. [25][26] Mitigations: low pH, low water-activity or anhydrous base, chelators, co-antioxidants, opaque air-tight packaging, cool storage; run real-time + accelerated stability and color/odor checks. Expect browning risk.
  • Pairings (conceptual): Co-antioxidants that share its low-pH home and reinforce the NRF2/ROS story — vitamin C / ferulic acid / vitamin E (classic antioxidant network), niacinamide (barrier + NRF2-adjacent; watch pH compatibility), and humectants. Mechanistically it overlaps with other NRF2 botanicals (sulforaphane, etc.). A delivery system (liposome/nanoparticle) is arguably its most important "pairing." [7][9]
  • Conflicts: High-pH systems; strong oxidizers; unbuffered metal ions (drive oxidation) — chelate. As a polyphenol it may discolor / interact with proteins and amines over time.
  • Sensory: Danshen extracts can carry a reddish-brown color and herbal odor (the root is literally "red sage") — a real formulation/aesthetic consideration; purified Sal B is less colored but oxidation can brown it.

7. Safety

  • Systemic safety (strong, but not topical): Human Phase 1 IV trial (n=63) → no serious AEs, well tolerated to 300 mg single / 250 mg ×5 d; minor transient liver-enzyme/BNP changes. Decades of cardiovascular use of salvianolate injections. [8][21]
  • Topical irritation/sensitization: No published human topical patch-test / HRIPT data for purified Sal B found. As a catechol polyphenol there is a theoretical contact-sensitization / oxidation-product irritancy consideration; the Danshen extract soothing patents actually claim anti-irritant behavior. Generate our own HRIPT/patch data before claiming "gentle." [12]
  • Sun: No photosensitivity signal; mechanism is photo-protective (antioxidant/NRF2). No human topical photo-safety data. Not a sunscreen — pair with SPF messaging.
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: Caution by default. Danshen is traditionally contraindicated in pregnancy (TCM "blood-moving" herb) and Sal B has antiplatelet/anticoagulant and vasodilatory activity. No topical pregnancy data. Use conservative copy ("consult your doctor") and flag for review. [21][22]
  • Bleeding/drug-interaction note (systemic context): Danshen↔warfarin interactions are documented for oral/systemic use. Topical exposure is presumably negligible, but worth noting for transparency. [21]
  • Sourcing/allergen: Botanical extract → batch-variable composition; standardize to Sal B and screen for solvent residues, heavy metals (root crop), and pesticides.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic ingredient status: Realistically declared as Salvia Miltiorrhiza Root/Leaf Extract (a recognized cosmetic botanical); the purified "salvianolic acid B" lacks a clean standalone cosmetic INCI in common use — confirm with the supplier and per target market before label/claims. The magnesium-salt drug form (salvianolate) is a pharmaceutical in China — keep cosmetic and drug identities cleanly separated. [12][13][21]
  • The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame): "Repairs," "boosts collagen," "anti-glycation," "reverses photoaging," "more effective than retinoid/tretinoin" all read as structure/function (drug) claims and/or unsubstantiated comparatives. The "better than tretinoin" line from the nude-mouse study [6] is especially dangerous to repeat — it's a model-specific preclinical finding and would invite both substantiation and comparative-claim challenges.
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "antioxidant extract from Danshen (red sage)," "helps defend against environmental/oxidative stress," "supports skin's own antioxidant defenses" (mechanism-framed, hedged), "helps soothe the look of redness," "helps improve the appearance of uneven tone." (Even tone/soothing wording should stay appearance-based and hedged given in-vitro-only pigment data.)
  • ⚠️ Risky / substantiation-or-legal-review: "boosts collagen," "anti-glycation / breaks down AGEs," "reverses wrinkles," "outperforms retinol/tretinoin," "repairs the barrier," "anti-aging at the cellular level." None are supported for purified topical human use.
  • Substantiation reality: To make even modest topical performance claims we'd need our own finished-formula human testing (and probably a delivery system); the literature will not substantiate a basic Sal B serum. See ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Forms/suppliers: Reference standards and ~98% HPLC isolate from fine-chemical/phytochemical houses (Sigma-Aldrich SML0048, Santa Cruz, ChemFaces, LGC/TRC, Selleck); bulk cosmetic-grade isolate and standardized Danshen extracts from Chinese botanical suppliers (e.g., AIHerba, GAOYUAN); branded Danshen actives exist for personal care (e.g., Gerbex Salvia; SILAB Salvia extracts). [4][12][14][27]
  • Cost tier: Mid-to-high for purified isolate (multi-step purification of a fragile polyphenol; research-grade quantities priced like specialty phytochemicals). Standardized extract is cheaper and the more realistic cosmetic route. Any delivery-enhanced version (liposome/nanoparticle) adds cost.
  • Supply/standardization risk: Botanical → Sal B content varies with cultivar, harvest, and processing, and Sal B degrades during extraction/storage (higher temp/pH/time → loss) [25][26]. Require a COA quantifying Sal B, plus heavy-metal/pesticide/solvent screens, and specify storage/handling to protect the active.

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • The format switch. The deepest, top-journal Sal B evidence (incl. the human trial) is IV/systemic cardiovascular & anti-fibrotic — then a "Danshen anti-aging serum" implies that transfers to topical skin. It doesn't automatically. [8][21]
  • "Boosts collagen." Oversimplified. Sal B raises procollagen in UV-damaged fibroblasts [1] but lowers collagen in fibrotic/activated fibroblasts [3][10] — it's a TGF-β/MMP normalizer, not a one-way collagen builder. Marketing picks the flattering half.
  • "Anti-glycation." This is the class it's filed under, but direct evidence that Sal B inhibits AGE formation in skin collagen is weak; the AGE link is mostly diabetic-organ AGE-RAGE signaling, not skin anti-glycation. Don't lead with "anti-glycation." [19][20]
  • "More effective than retinol/tretinoin." A nude-mouse, single-study result [6] — scientifically interesting, legally and scientifically reckless to put on a label.
  • "Penetrates and rebuilds the dermis." Its own delivery papers say it penetrates poorly; efficacy needed engineered delivery or excised skin. [7]
  • "Stronger antioxidant than vitamin C." Partly true but assay-dependent and tube-only. In the source study Sal B beat vitamin C on radical-quenching assays (DPPH, ABTS, hydroxyl, superoxide) yet was weaker on iron chelation and H₂O₂. These are chemical-tube assays, not skin — they say nothing about in-use topical antioxidant performance, so don't market it as a vitamin C-beating antioxidant on skin. [9]
  • "Proven brightening." In-vitro tyrosinase/melanin effects were modest and at high concentrations, parent extract showed opposite effects, no human data. [5][15]

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ SUPPORTING / CONDITIONAL — credible antioxidant-soothing botanical with a strong science story, but no human topical proof and a hard penetration/stability problem. One-line: Salvianolic acid B is a genuinely well-researched molecule (NRF2 antioxidant, MMP-1 suppression, real human systemic safety) that fits a longevity narrative beautifully — but for topical skin it's preclinical only, penetrates poorly, is unstable, and its headline cosmetic claims (collagen-boosting, anti-glycation, "beats tretinoin") are not supportable. Use it as a supporting NRF2/antioxidant + soothing botanical, ideally as a standardized Danshen extract in a low-pH, well-protected, delivery-considered formula, with strictly mechanism-framed, hedged copy — not as a proof-backed hero, and not under an "anti-glycation" headline.

Conditional path to "hero-adjacent": it could earn a bigger role only if we (1) solve delivery (liposome/nanoparticle), (2) stabilize it (low pH, anhydrous/airtight), and (3) run our own finished-formula human testing. Absent those, keep it supporting.

Longevity-relevance note: Mechanistically it's on-thesis for skin longevity — NRF2 is a bona-fide longevity/hormesis pathway, and Sal B's antioxidant + senescence-protective + anti-inflammaging signals map onto multiple hallmarks. Its TGF-β/anti-fibrotic biology even gives it a "matrix-normalizing" angle distinct from collagen-stimulant peptides. The gap, as with several of our longevity actives, is purely the topical-human-evidence + delivery translation. The misfiling under "anti-glycation" should be corrected in the master index — it is far more an NRF2 antioxidant / MMP-modulator than an anti-glycation agent.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified: 2026-06-19 · independent check of headline claims against the cited primary literature (web-retrieved).

Overall grade: C → D for topical anti-aging (with isolated A-grade evidence that is for the wrong route/endpoint)

The honest split is the whole story here: - Systemic Sal B safety/PK in humans: A — a real, well-conducted RCT exists. - Topical anti-aging in living human skin: D — zero RCTs; everything is in-vitro, nude-mouse, ex-vivo, or borrowed from the systemic format. - Topical antioxidant/soothing plausibility (mechanism-framed): C — strong preclinical mechanism, no human topical efficacy proof, and a hard penetration barrier.

A single grade overstates either the strength or the weakness, so the dossier's "supporting/conditional, not a hero" verdict is the defensible call. Grade for the cosmetic use being contemplated: C, trending D if claims drift toward efficacy.
