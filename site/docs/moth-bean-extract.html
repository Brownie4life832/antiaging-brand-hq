Ingredient: Moth Bean Extract (INCI: Vigna Aconitifolia Seed Extract)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Retinoids & retinoid alternatives (botanical "retinol alternative") Slug: moth-bean-extract

One-line honest summary: A protein-rich legume seed extract marketed as a "plant-based retinol." It has a plausible, manufacturer-backed in vitro anti-apoptotic / cell-vitality story and a single industry-funded human clinical study — but that human study tested it only inside a 5-ingredient retinal complex, never alone. There is no published RCT of moth bean extract by itself, and despite the marketing it contains no actual retinoids or vitamin A. Treat as a gentle supporting "story" active, not a proven hero retinoid.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Moth bean (also "mat bean," "matki," "dew bean," "Turkish gram"). The plant is a drought-hardy Indian/South-Asian legume.
  • Botanical / species: Vigna aconitifolia (Jacq.) Maréchal (Fabaceae).
  • INCI: Vigna Aconitifolia Seed Extract (cosmetic supplier blends often appear as Water & Glycerin & Vigna Aconitifolia Seed Extract & Sodium Citrate). [10]
  • Key forms / derivatives:
  • Vitoptine® — the original branded active, a protein extract of the seed, developed by Laboratoires Sérobiologiques (the lineage that became Cognis → BASF Beauty Care Solutions). This is the source of essentially all the foundational efficacy data. [3][4]
  • Generic "Vigna aconitifolia seed extract" — water/glycerin extracts sold by indie/cosmetic suppliers (e.g. Formulator Sample Shop) at lower characterization. [10]
  • It is one of three botanicals (with bakuchiol and melatonin) used in ISDIN's "3RC / tri-retinoid complex" alongside encapsulated retinal. [1][2]
  • What it is NOT: It is not retinol, not a retinoid, and not a vitamin A derivative. No primary phytochemistry of the seed reports retinol, retinyl esters, or meaningful carotenoid/β-carotene content. [8][9] "Bio-retinoid" and "rich source of retinol" are marketing terms, not chemistry.

2. Class & mechanism

Moth bean extract is grouped commercially with retinoid alternatives (alongside bakuchiol) because brands position it as delivering "retinol-like" smoothing without retinoid irritation. Mechanistically it does not act through retinoic-acid-receptor (RAR/RXR) signaling the way true retinoids do. The proposed mechanisms, drawn from the manufacturer's patent/technical data, are:

  1. Cell-vitality / anti-apoptotic support. The branded protein extract (Vitoptine) increased fibroblast metabolic markers in vitro — MTT viability, total protein, ATP synthesis, and glutathione (GSH) — and reduced apoptosis (DNA-fragment readouts) in both fibroblasts and keratinocytes deprived of growth factors. The pitch: it "rescues" stressed/aging skin cells and keeps them productive. [3][4]
  2. Epidermal renewal. In the manufacturer's in-vivo example, emulsions with 3–5% extract raised an epidermal cell-renewal/turnover readout — framed as a "retinol-like" renewal effect, though via a protein/peptide-amino-acid mechanism rather than RAR activation. [4]
  3. Barrier / differentiation modulation (combination only). In the ISDIN reconstructed-skin work, adding Vigna aconitifolia extract to retinal nudged epidermal-barrier and keratinocyte-differentiation genes (e.g. claudin-1/-7, desmoglein-1, filaggrin) — i.e. it potentiated retinal, it was not shown to do this alone. [1][2]
  4. Antioxidant (generic legume-phenolic claim). Suppliers attribute caffeic/ferulic/cinnamic acid + kaempferol antioxidant activity to the seed. Caveat: primary phytochemistry of moth bean confirms tannins, phytic acid, trypsin inhibitors and measurable DPPH antioxidant activity, but did not isolate/quantify those specific named phenolics — that compound list is a generic "legumes contain phenolics" extrapolation, not verified for this seed. [8][9]

Bottom line on mechanism: plausible "feed/protect the cell" amino-acid/protein story + modest antioxidant capacity. It is not a receptor-level retinoid mimetic, and the strongest mechanistic data are manufacturer-generated.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core of this dossier)

Read this first: Almost all favorable data come from (a) the manufacturer's own patent/technical dossier for Vitoptine, and (b) one industry-funded human study where moth bean was never isolated. Independent, peer-reviewed, monotherapy human data on Vigna aconitifolia topically: none found.

Tier 1 — In vitro (cell/tissue)

  • BASF / Laboratoires Sérobiologiques patent data (Contet-Audonneau, Danoux, Gillon, Pauly, Moser; priority 2001; US 8,697,151 B2 / US 2005/0019301 A1). Branded protein extract "Vitoptine." Reported in human fibroblasts/keratinocytes: at 0.1% — protein ~107%, ATP ~113% of control; at 1% — MTT ~111%, protein ~118%, GSH/protein ~122%; anti-apoptosis — cytoplasmic DNA fragments fell to ~74% (fibroblasts) and ~46% (keratinocytes) of control. Preparation: aqueous seed extraction, low molecular-weight protein/peptide fraction (≈80% <5 kDa). Limitations: patent (not peer-reviewed), manufacturer-generated, no blinding/replication standards of a journal, designed to support IP. [3][4]
  • ISDIN reconstructed-skin / gene-expression (Brown et al., 2023). Vigna aconitifolia extract (VAE) studied only in combination with 0.1% retinal (± bakuchiol/melatonin/niacinamide). The combination shifted barrier/differentiation genes (claudin-1/-7, desmoglein-1, filaggrin) and "potentiated" retinal. VAE was explicitly NOT tested in isolation. [1][2]
  • Seed phytochemistry (Bhardwaj et al. / Phytochemical Evaluation of Moth Bean Seeds, 2016, 3 Biotech PMC4867057): tannins 1.4–3.2 mg/g, phytic acid 0.26–1.22 mg/g, trypsin inhibitors, DPPH 5.6–38.3% inhibition. No carotenoids/vitamin A; specific phenolics not isolated. Establishes antioxidant capacity but undercuts the "retinol/vitamin-A" marketing. [9]

Tier 2 — Animal / in-vivo (non-human, manufacturer)

  • The Vitoptine patent describes an in-vivo "epidermal cell renewal" readout: emulsions with 3% or 5% extract produced higher renewal/turnover luminescence (~54% at 5%) vs a vitamin A palmitate comparator (~41%). This is the basis of the "renewal comparable to retinol" claim. Limitations: manufacturer/patent data, surrogate endpoint, not an independent clinical wrinkle outcome. [4]
  • No independent peer-reviewed rodent/animal photoaging studies of moth bean extract were found. (Note: a separate species, Vigna angularis / adzuki, has anti-inflammatory dermatitis data — that is a different plant and must not be cited for moth bean. [11])

Tier 3 — Human ORAL

  • Moth bean is a nutritious food legume (high protein, minerals; reviewed in Discover Food 2022 and Frontiers/PMC agronomy reviews). [8] No oral-to-skin (nutricosmetic) human trials exist. Dietary data are not transferable to topical anti-aging claims.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL

RCT (placebo/vehicle-controlled): NONE for moth bean alone or in any controlled head-to-head.

Lower-tier human (uncontrolled, combination, industry-funded): - Brown A, Furmanczyk M, Ramos D, Ribes A, Pons L, Bustos J, Fernandez de Henestrosa AR, Jourdan E. "Natural Retinol Analogs Potentiate the Effects of Retinal on Aged and Photodamaged Skin." Dermatology and Therapy (Heidelb). 2023; 13(10):2349–2365. doi:10.1007/s13555-023-01004-z (PMC10539272). [1][2] - Design: open-label, single-arm proof-of-concept. No control/placebo group (authors' own stated limitation). - Product: a finished serum ("3RC"): 0.1% liposomal retinal + bakuchiol + melatonin + Vigna aconitifolia extract + niacinamide. Moth bean is one minor component; its individual contribution cannot be isolated. - N: 34 enrolled, 32 completed; women aged 41–70 (mean 53.5); Fitzpatrick I–IV; ~66% self-described sensitive skin; Glogau ≥ II. Nightly use, 28 days. - Results (day 0→28): elasticity +13.9% (p<0.001); wrinkle count −43.2% (p<0.001); firmness change −5.6% (p<0.05); tone uniformity −7.0% (p<0.05); crow's-feet reduced in 100% of subjects. Well tolerated, no significant erythema. - Limitations: no placebo (regression-to-the-mean and the known efficacy of retinal itself plus niacinamide almost certainly drive these results); 28 days only; multi-ingredient; all authors are ISDIN employees (the manufacturer). This is evidence for the retinal complex, not for moth bean.

Explicit "proven topical for moth bean alone" verdict: Essentially none. Every favorable human number belongs to a retinal-containing combination. The closest thing to a stand-alone benefit (renewal, anti-apoptosis) is in-vitro/manufacturer data.

Comparator context — why bakuchiol is the stronger "retin-alt"

Moth bean is constantly bracketed with bakuchiol, but bakuchiol has independent RCT evidence moth bean lacks: Dhaliwal et al., Br J Dermatol 2019;180(2):289–296 (PMID 29947134) — randomized, double-blind, n=44, bakuchiol 0.5% vs retinol 0.5%, 12 weeks: comparable wrinkle-area and pigmentation improvement, better tolerated. [7] If we want a "gentle retinol alternative" with real human data, bakuchiol currently outranks moth bean.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • The active is a low-molecular-weight protein/peptide + amino-acid fraction (~80% <5 kDa per the patent). Small peptides/amino acids and phenolics are more skin-permeable than intact proteins, but no published human penetration/pharmacokinetic data exist for this extract. [4]
  • "Penetrates to jump-start collagen" is a marketing assertion, not a measured penetration finding. [unverified]
  • Because the effect is hypothesized to be metabolic/anti-apoptotic on viable epidermal/dermal cells, meaningful delivery past the stratum corneum would be required — and that is unproven for this ingredient.

6. Formulation

  • Use level: branded/in-vivo data used 1–5% of the active fraction; generic suppliers recommend 1–5% of the finished formula. [4][10]
  • pH: supplier guidance ~5.5–7.5; the protein extract is prepared/stabilized around pH 5.5–6.5 — avoid very low pH (denatures proteins) and high pH. [4][10]
  • Phase: water-soluble; add to the aqueous phase, ideally post-emulsification / cool-down to protect heat-sensitive proteins. [10]
  • Stability: protein/peptide actives are sensitive to heat and microbial load; needs robust preservation. Typical shelf life ~12 months protected from light/heat. [10]
  • Pairings (sensible): niacinamide, peptides, ceramides/barrier lipids, hyaluronic acid, panthenol, mild antioxidants — all align with a "support/repair" formula and with how it was actually deployed (with niacinamide + retinal in 3RC). [1]
  • Conflicts / cautions: strong acids (low-pH vitamin C, high-% AHA/BHA) and high heat may degrade the protein fraction; the marketing "use with everything daily" framing is plausible (it's gentle) but unproven for any active synergy beyond the retinal combo.
  • Sensory: glycerin-based, water-thin, generally pleasant/neutral; no inherent color/odor issues reported.

7. Safety

  • Irritation: Appears low. In ISDIN's in-vitro irritation testing, retinal+VAE (±bakuchiol) showed irritation potential similar to retinal alone (ET50 > 24 h), and the finished serum was clinically well tolerated in sensitive-skin subjects. [1] As a gentle protein/glycerin extract, stand-alone irritation risk is expected to be minimal, but isolated patch-test data are sparse. [unverified]
  • Sun sensitivity: No known phototoxicity; unlike true retinoids it is not expected to increase photosensitivity. (Still pair any anti-aging routine with daily SPF on general principle.)
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: A genuine selling point vs retinoids. Because it is not a retinoid, it does not carry the teratogenicity concern that makes retinoids contraindicated in pregnancy. No specific pregnancy safety studies exist, but there is no mechanistic red flag. Frame as "non-retinoid; commonly chosen as a pregnancy-friendly alternative," not as a medical clearance.
  • Sensitization / allergy: It is a legume protein extract. Legume proteins can be allergens; theoretical sensitization/cross-reactivity risk for legume-allergic individuals. Low-MW processing reduces but does not eliminate this. Worth a caution line. [unverified for documented cosmetic cases]
  • Sourcing / sustainability: Strong story — moth bean is an extremely drought-tolerant, low-input legume grown in arid India (Rajasthan), nitrogen-fixing and climate-resilient; agronomy reviews frame it as a sustainable "minor legume with major potential." [8] Good fit for a sustainability narrative if sourcing is verified.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic vs drug: In the US, "retinol/retinoid" is allowed in cosmetics, but structure/function or treatment claims ("boosts collagen," "treats wrinkles," "renews skin like retinol") drift toward drug territory and need substantiation. Moth bean's evidence does not support hard efficacy claims on its own.
  • The biggest claims trap: calling it "natural retinol," "plant retinol," "bio-retinoid," or "vitamin A from plants." It is not a retinoid and contains no vitamin A — these claims are factually unsupported and could be challenged as misleading. Avoid them.
  • "Comparable to 2.5% / 0.5% retinol" / "comparable to retinol": This phrasing circulates widely but traces to manufacturer/patent surrogate data (epidermal-renewal readout vs vitamin A palmitate) and to the combination clinical study — not an independent head-to-head on moth bean. Do not repeat the comparison as fact. [unverified as independent claim]
  • ✅ Safer language we could use: "a gentle, non-retinoid botanical that helps support skin-cell vitality and the appearance of smoother, more even skin"; "a plant-based alternative for those who can't use retinoids (incl. during pregnancy)"; "antioxidant-supporting legume extract." Hedge with "helps," "supports," "visibly," "appearance of." (See ../../brand/glossary.md.)
  • ⚠️ Flag for legal/substantiation: any retinol-equivalence %, any "boosts collagen," any "vitamin A / bio-retinoid" wording.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: Low–moderate. It is a glycerin/water botanical extract (cheap base) but the branded/characterized actives (Vitoptine and similar) carry a premium over commodity moth-bean extract. Net: cheaper than encapsulated retinal/retinol systems, comparable to other branded botanical extracts.
  • Suppliers: BASF Beauty Care Solutions (Vitoptine lineage); generic Vigna aconitifolia seed extract via indie/cosmetic ingredient houses (e.g. Formulator Sample Shop). [3][10]
  • Supply considerations: crop is regional (South Asia) but hardy and not scarce; verify the grade — a characterized protein active is very different from a token "label-claim" botanical at <0.5%.

10. Hype vs. evidence

Marketing claim Reality
"Plant-based / natural retinol," "bio-retinoid," "rich in vitamin A" False on chemistry. No retinoids, no meaningful vitamin A/carotenoids in the seed. [8][9]
"As effective as retinol / 2.5% retinol, without irritation" Unproven independently. Comes from manufacturer surrogate data + a combination study; no head-to-head RCT. [1][4]
"Clinically proven to reduce wrinkles 43% / crow's feet in 100%" Those numbers are from a 5-ingredient retinal serum with no placebo arm; cannot be attributed to moth bean. [1]
"Penetrates to boost collagen" No human penetration or stand-alone collagen data. [unverified]
"Gentle, pregnancy-friendly, antioxidant" Most defensible claims. Non-retinoid, low irritation, real (if modest) antioxidant capacity. [1][9]
Caffeic/ferulic/cinnamic/kaempferol content Generic legume-phenolic extrapolation; not isolated for this seed in primary work. [9]

Net: the hype massively outruns the data. The honest, defensible story is "gentle non-retinoid botanical that supports cell vitality and is suitable for retinoid-avoiders," not "natural retinol that matches the real thing."

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ Conditional / supporting — NOT a hero, and never as a retinoid claim.

One-liner: A pleasant, low-irritation, pregnancy-friendly botanical with a tidy in-vitro "cell vitality" story and a good sustainability narrative — but with no independent stand-alone human evidence, it can only earn a supporting-cast role, and the "natural retinol" framing must be avoided.

  • Use it if: we want a gentle, marketable, non-retinoid botanical for a sensitive-skin or pregnancy-friendly SKU, paired with workhorses (niacinamide, peptides, ceramides) that carry the real evidence.
  • Don't use it if: we need a hero "retinoid" claim — for that, choose encapsulated retinal/retinol (proven) or, for a gentler hero, bakuchiol (has independent RCT data moth bean lacks [7]).
  • Longevity relevance: Its anti-apoptotic / ATP-GSH "keep stressed skin cells alive and productive" angle is conceptually on-theme for a skin-longevity brand (senescence/cell-vitality hallmark), but that link rests on manufacturer in-vitro data only — interesting story, thin proof. Promising narrative fit, unproven longevity claim.
