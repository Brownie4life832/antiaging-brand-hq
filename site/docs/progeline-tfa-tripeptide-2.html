Ingredient: Trifluoroacetyl Tripeptide-2 (Progeline™)

Slug: progeline-tfa-tripeptide-2 · Class: Peptides Researched: 2026-06-19 (exhaustive) · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product

How to read this dossier. This is an evidence-graded literature review, not marketing copy. The core is the tiered evidence review (§4). The single most important honest finding up front: Trifluoroacetyl Tripeptide-2 has an elegant, biologically plausible mechanism (it inhibits production of progerin, a real and well-validated biomarker of cellular aging in human skin) — but the efficacy evidence is almost entirely manufacturer-generated and unpublished. As of mid-2026 there is no independent, peer-reviewed, placebo-controlled RCT on this specific peptide. The two human "studies" most often cited are (a) the supplier's own in-vivo marketing data (never published in a journal) and (b) a 2025 preprint (not peer-reviewed) that is open-label, uncontrolled, n=21, 4 weeks — and which almost certainly tests a different molecule (a small-molecule "progerin inhibitor," most likely Progerinin, from the authors' own company) rather than this peptide. Treat the progerin mechanism and the cosmetic efficacy claims as two separate questions — the first is solid science, the second is largely faith.

1. Identity

  • Common / trade name: Progeline™ (the original branded trade ingredient from Sederma / Lucas Meyer Cosmetics, now part of IFF). Sold by other suppliers under names like Pep®-Geline and generic "Trifluoroacetyl Tripeptide-2."
  • INCI (active): Trifluoroacetyl Tripeptide-2 (also written F3-Acetyl Tripeptide-2 on some trade labels).
  • INCI of the trade product (Progeline™): typically Glycerin (and) Water/Aqua (and) Dextran (and) Trifluoroacetyl Tripeptide-2 — i.e. the peptide is delivered diluted in a glycerin/water carrier with dextran. (This matters enormously for dosing — see §6.)
  • Chemical name: N-(2,2,2-trifluoroacetyl)-L-valyl-L-tyrosyl-L-valine.
  • Sequence: TFA–Val–Tyr–Val (often written F3Ac-Val-Tyr-Val or TFA-VYV). A three-amino-acid peptide capped at the N-terminus with a trifluoroacetyl group.
  • CAS: 64577-63-5 (free peptide). Acetate salt form is also sold (CAS for the acetate differs; PubChem CID 170915527).
  • Molecular formula / weight: C₂₁H₂₈F₃N₃O₆, MW ≈ 475–487 (≈475.5 reported for the acetate salt). PubChem CID 40785040 (free form) [10][11].
  • Biomimetic of: elafin (a.k.a. SKALP / peptidase inhibitor-3), a 57-aa endogenous elastase-specific inhibitor found in human skin [3][4]. Progeline is designed as a small-molecule mimic of elafin's elastase-inhibiting function.
  • Key forms / derivatives: No meaningful derivatives in cosmetic use. Available as a research-grade peptide (Cayman, MedChemExpress, etc., as an enzyme-inhibitor reagent) and as the cosmetic trade dilution (Progeline™ and copies).

2. Class & Mechanism

Progeline is marketed as a "senescence-targeting" / "anti-progerin" signal peptide. Its proposed mechanism is genuinely distinctive among cosmetic peptides — it is one of the only marketed actives whose stated target is a senescence biomarker rather than collagen synthesis directly. Mechanisms claimed by the supplier and supported (to varying degrees) by literature:

  • Progerin down-modulation (the headline mechanism). Progerin is a truncated, farnesylated, toxic splice variant of lamin A (the LMNA gene product). It is the protein responsible for Hutchinson-Gilford Progeria Syndrome (HGPS), but — crucially — it also accumulates at low levels in normal aging skin (see §3, [1][2][6]). Sederma reports that TFA-Val-Tyr-Val reduces progerin synthesis in mature human fibroblasts in a dose-dependent way (≈18% at 0.005 ppm, ≈22% at 0.05 ppm by ELISA after 96 h) [7][8]. Reducing progerin is proposed to slow the senescence/structural decline of dermal cells.
  • Elastase inhibition (the elafin-mimic mechanism). As a confirmed biochemical fact, the peptide is a genuine inhibitor of pancreatic and neutrophil elastases (reported Kᵢ values on the order of 5.6×10⁻⁴ mM for pancreatic and ~0.5 mM for neutrophil elastase) [10]. By inhibiting elastase it is meant to protect elastin fibers from enzymatic breakdown — the same job elafin does endogenously.
  • MMP modulation. Supplier data report down-regulation of matrix metalloproteinases (notably MMP-1, with claims also of MMP-3/MMP-9) [7][8], which would reduce collagen/ECM degradation.
  • ECM protection ex vivo. In human skin explants challenged with collagenase or elastase, the supplier reports the peptide protected collagen fibers (~43%) and elastic fibers (~100%) from degradation [7][8]. (These are protection-from-enzymatic-challenge assays on excised skin, not living-skin outcomes.)
  • Claimed "rejuvenation" framing. The marketing line that it "brings cell activity back to a 20-year-old level" is a promotional extrapolation from the progerin-reduction data, not a measured endpoint.

Honest mechanistic read: The progerin-targeting rationale is scientifically real and clever — progerin's role in skin aging is well-documented in primary literature ([1][2][6]). The elastase-inhibition is a confirmed enzymatic property. What is not established is whether topically applied TFA-Val-Tyr-Val (a) penetrates to living dermal fibroblasts at active concentrations and (b) produces visible, durable clinical benefit. The mechanism is a hypothesis with good biochemical scaffolding, not a proven topical pathway.

3. Hallmarks of Skin Aging Targeted

Hallmark Progeline relevance Strength (for topical human use)
Cellular senescence Core thesis: lowers progerin, a validated senescence/aging biomarker in skin fibroblasts & keratinocytes Mechanistically plausible; clinically unproven
Collagen / ECM loss Down-modulates MMP-1; protects collagen from collagenase ex vivo Weak (supplier in-vitro only)
Loss of elasticity / laxity (elastin) Elastase inhibition → elastin protection; the lead marketed benefit ("firmness, jawline, neck") Weak–moderate (supplier in-vivo only)
Photoaging Progerin & elastase/MMP activity are downstream of UV; indirectly relevant Inferred, not directly tested
Inflammaging Senescent cells drive SASP; lowering progerin could reduce senescent burden Speculative for topical
Oxidative stress / glycation (AGEs) / mitochondrial decline / barrier / microbiome / pigmentation / hydration No meaningful direct evidence None established

The cleanest framing: this is positioned as a "firmness / laxity / sagging" active (neck, jawline, lower face) operating via a senescence + elastin-protection story. That is the only lane where even supplier data attempts to substantiate a benefit.

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (core of this dossier)

Reading guide: Benefits proven for topical human use would have to live in the human-topical tier. As you'll see, that tier is effectively empty of independent, controlled, peer-reviewed evidence. Everything else is mechanism (in-vitro / biochemical) or unpublished/uncontrolled.

Tier 1 — Foundational biology of the target (progerin) — STRONG, independent, peer-reviewed

(This tier validates the target, not the peptide.)

  • McClintock D, Ratner D, Lokuge M, et al. (2007). "The Mutant Form of Lamin A that Causes Hutchinson-Gilford Progeria Is a Biomarker of Cellular Aging in Human Skin." PLOS ONE 2(12):e1269. DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0001269 [1]. The keystone paper. Examined 150 skin biopsies (newborn → 97 y) plus 60 additional biopsies and multiple fibroblast cultures. Found progerin mRNA stays low at all ages but progerin protein accumulates progressively with age, spreading from near the basement membrane through the dermis; concentrates in terminally differentiated keratinocytes and growth-arrested (senescent-phenotype) fibroblasts. Notably, sun-exposed vs non-exposed sites showed similar patterns (UV not the determining factor). → Establishes progerin as a real, age-correlated skin biomarker.
  • Scaffidi P, Misteli T (2006). Science 312(5776):1059-63. "Lamin A-dependent nuclear defects in human aging." Showed sporadic progerin production via cryptic LMNA splicing in normal aged cells; blocking the splice site reversed nuclear defects. (Foundational; widely cited as the origin of the "progerin in normal aging" concept.) [unverified — citation details from memory; verify DOI 10.1126/science.1127168 before quoting].
  • Cao K, Blair CD, Faddah DA, et al. (2011). "Progerin and telomere dysfunction collaborate to trigger cellular senescence in normal human fibroblasts." J Clin Invest 121(7):2833-44. DOI: 10.1172/JCI43578 [2]. Showed telomere shortening upstream-activates progerin splicing; immortalized/telomerase-expressing cells made essentially no progerin. → Ties progerin mechanistically to replicative senescence.

Takeaway: The premise — "progerin is a legitimate aging target in skin" — is well supported by independent science. This is the strongest part of Progeline's story, and it is about the target, not the ingredient.

Tier 1b — Elafin / elastase biochemistry — STRONG, independent

  • Wiedow O, Schröder JM, Gregory H, et al. (1990). "Elafin: an elastase-specific inhibitor of human skin. Purification, characterization, and complete amino acid sequence." J Biol Chem 265(25):14791-5. PMID: 2394696 [3]. Characterizes the 57-aa endogenous elastase inhibitor that Progeline mimics.
  • Muto J, Kuroda K, Wachi H, et al. (2007). "Accumulation of elafin in actinic elastosis of sun-damaged skin: elafin binds to elastin and prevents elastolytic degradation." J Invest Dermatol 127(6):1358-66. PMID: 17139263 [4]. Confirms elafin binds elastin and protects it from elastase — the biological role Progeline is designed to imitate.

Takeaway: Elastase inhibition as an elastin-preservation strategy is real biology; and TFA-VYV is a confirmed elastase inhibitor [10]. Again, this validates the rationale, not the topical clinical outcome.

Tier 2 — In-vitro / ex-vivo data on the peptide itself — MODERATE quantity, ALL supplier-origin, unpublished

All numbers below trace to Sederma/Lucas Meyer technical literature and trade-press coverage, not to peer-reviewed journals: - Dose-dependent progerin reduction in mature fibroblasts: ≈18.0% at 0.005 ppm and ≈21.9% at 0.05 ppm (ELISA, 96 h incubation) [7][8]. - MMP-1 (and claimed MMP-3/-9) down-regulation, dose-dependent [7][8]. - Ex-vivo skin-explant protection: ~43% protection of collagen (vs collagenase) and ~100% protection of elastic fibers (vs elastase) [7][8]. - Original trade launch (2012) framed it as targeting "toxic progerin" with elafin-mimetic ECM protection [5].

Limitations: No independent replication. No journal peer review. Concentrations are vanishingly small (parts-per-billion to parts-per-million in the dish) — impressive for potency arguments but says nothing about delivered dose in living skin. "100% elastic-fiber protection" is an enzyme-challenge artifact, not an in-vivo elasticity gain.

Tier 3 — Animal data

None located. No rodent or other animal efficacy studies on TFA-Val-Tyr-Val for skin were found in the literature search. (Progerin-inhibitor drugs — distinct compounds, e.g. farnesyltransferase inhibitors, lonafarnib — have HGPS mouse data, but that is not this peptide and must not be borrowed.)

Tier 4 — Human ORAL data

Not applicable / none. Progeline is a topical cosmetic active; there is no oral-supplement evidence and none should be implied.

Tier 5 — Human TOPICAL data (the tier that should carry consumer claims) — VERY WEAK

5a. Supplier in-vivo "clinical" data (unpublished marketing data): - A 2% Progeline™ cream reportedly lifted the sagging jawline by up to ~10% in 56 days and improved elasticity/firmness by ~20% in 28 days [5][7][9]. - Critical caveats: These figures appear only on the manufacturer's promotional materials and trade pages. Sample size, controls, blinding, and statistics are not publicly available; no peer-reviewed publication exists. Dermatologist Leslie Baumann explicitly notes: "Studies have been done in vitro so we do not have a lot of data on if Progeline works when used topically… I have seen this [2%/20%/10%] claim on the manufacturer's website but cannot find any proof or publication to support it" [9]. "2% Progeline cream" also does not mean 2% peptide (see §6) — the actual delivered peptide dose is in the ppm range.

5b. The 2025 preprint (closest thing to independent human topical data — still weak): - "Topical application of a skin serum with Progerin inhibitor helps alleviate human skin aging." medRxiv preprint, posted 2025-09-12. DOI: 10.1101/2025.09.10.25335131 [12]. - Design: open-label, uncontrolled (no placebo/vehicle arm), n=21 women aged 30–50, 4 weeks, once-daily evening serum containing a "1% progerin inhibitor." - Reported results (all p<0.05 vs baseline): crow's-feet wrinkles −5.6%, facial lifting +3.2%, hydration +18.4%, elasticity +8.6%, skin density +23.6%, tone/brightness +3.4%. - Severe limitations: (1) Not peer-reviewed (preprint). (2) No control/vehicle arm — cannot separate active effect from the base formula, hydration, or measurement drift; in skincare, a good moisturizer base alone moves hydration/elasticity/wrinkle metrics over 4 weeks. (3) The active is almost certainly NOT this peptide. The serum is described only as a "1% progerin inhibitor." The senior author is Bum-Joon Park, the developer of Progerinin (SLC-D011) — an orally active small-molecule progerin–lamin A binding inhibitor — and the study is run/funded by Cymbiotics Biopharma (Bengaluru / Novato), the company commercializing that molecule. So the most defensible reading is that the "progerin inhibitor" is Progerinin (or a related Park-group small molecule), a completely different compound from the Sederma peptide Progeline (Trifluoroacetyl Tripeptide-2). Neither the preprint nor the company page ever names TFA-Val-Tyr-Val. This study therefore should not be cited as evidence for THIS peptide at all — at most it is weak, COI-laden evidence that some progerin-inhibitor concept can move cosmetic metrics. (4) Vendor affiliation / COI confirmed — authored and funded by the biopharma selling the serum. (5) n=21, 4 weeks is small and short; effect sizes are tiny (lifting +3.2%, tone +3.4%) and the headline "+23.6% density" has no control to anchor it. Hypothesis-generating at best; for Progeline specifically, essentially irrelevant.

5c. Multi-ingredient products containing Progeline: Many marketed serums/creams (Garden of Wisdom, Reviva Labs multi-peptide complexes, etc.) include Progeline alongside other peptides (Matrixyl/Palmitoyl Tripeptide-5, Syn-Coll, etc.). Any positive user/efficacy data on those is confounded — it cannot be attributed to Progeline specifically.

Bottom line for Tier 5: There is no independent, controlled, peer-reviewed RCT demonstrating that topical Trifluoroacetyl Tripeptide-2 firms skin, lifts jowls, or reduces sagging in humans. The benefit is plausible and manufacturer-asserted but not proven.

5. Topical Bioavailability / Penetration

  • No published skin-penetration (Franz cell / tape-strip / in-vivo permeation) data specific to TFA-Val-Tyr-Val were located. This is a major evidence gap given that the mechanism requires reaching living dermal fibroblasts (where progerin/lamin A biology happens), several hundred microns below the surface.
  • Physicochemistry is moderately favorable for a peptide: it is a very small tripeptide (MW ≈ 475), and the trifluoroacetyl cap increases lipophilicity relative to a bare tripeptide, which plausibly aids stratum-corneum partitioning more than larger peptides (e.g. GHK-Cu or pentapeptides). This is the strongest theoretical argument in its favor.
  • But small + somewhat lipophilic ≠ proven dermal delivery at active concentrations. The supplier's potency (effective at ppb–ppm in the dish) is often used to imply "tiny amounts are enough," but that conflates intrinsic potency with delivered dose. Penetration to the dermis remains assumed, not demonstrated.
  • No nano-carrier/encapsulated delivery systems are standard for this ingredient (unlike GHK-Cu), suggesting formulators rely on the free peptide's intrinsic permeation — which is unquantified here.

6. Formulation Notes

  • The dosing trap (read this first). The Progeline™ trade ingredient is a dilute solution (peptide carried in glycerin/water/dextran). Industry/DIY guidance to use the trade product at 1–3% (commonly "2%") therefore delivers only parts-per-million of actual peptide — consistent with the supplier's ppb–ppm in-vitro active range. So "2% Progeline" ≈ ~0.002% peptide. Always specify whether a % refers to the trade dilution or the peptide. Copy that says "2% peptide" would be inaccurate.
  • Typical use level (trade product): ~1–3% of the Progeline™ solution in the finished formula (supplier-recommended range; ~2% is the common reference point).
  • pH: Peptides of this type are generally formulated in a mildly acidic to near-neutral range (~pH 5–7). No published optimal-pH/stability curve specific to TFA-VYV was located — treat exact pH tolerance as unverified and follow the supplier datasheet.
  • Solubility: The trade ingredient is water/glycerin-based (water-dispersible). Some "Pep-Geline" copies are sold as oil-soluble — confirm the specific grade before formulating.
  • Stability: No independent stability data found. The trifluoroacetyl cap and short sequence suggest reasonable robustness vs. some larger peptides, but assume standard peptide precautions: avoid extreme pH, high heat in processing, and add in the cool-down phase. Unverified — rely on supplier specs.
  • Pairings (rational, not proven): Pairs logically with other ECM/firmness peptides (Matrixyl/palmitoyl peptides, Syn-Coll) and humectants (HA, glycerin) for a "firming" serum. Often co-formulated with antioxidants (resveratrol, SOD) in marketed products.
  • Conflicts: No documented hard incompatibilities, but as with most peptides, co-formulating in a high-acid (low-pH) vehicle (strong AHA/BHA, pure L-ascorbic acid) is generally avoided to protect peptide integrity. Layering after strong acids/retinoids is fine in a routine (different products).
  • Sensory: As supplied (glycerin/water carrier) it is light and water-thin; contributes humectant slip at use level; no color or odor issues typical.

7. Safety

  • Irritation / sensitization: Supplier and ingredient-database documentation report no skin/eye irritation and no known sensitization at cosmetic use levels [7]. Used at ppm peptide concentrations, systemic/irritation risk is low. Marketed products report only rare, mild redness in sensitive skin — patch testing is sensible but the safety signal is benign.
  • Sun sensitivity: No evidence it causes photosensitivity. (Daily SPF still applies for any anti-aging routine — but that's general, not Progeline-specific.)
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: No specific safety data. Default guidance is "insufficient data; consult a clinician / avoid if in doubt." There is no published evidence of harm from topical use, but also no studies establishing safety — so we should not market it as pregnancy-safe.
  • The PFAS flag (important for a premium brand). The trifluoroacetyl group means EWG's Skin Deep database classifies Trifluoroacetyl Tripeptide-2 within the per-/polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) category [13]. The specific concerns EWG lists are enhanced skin absorption, use restrictions (high), endocrine disruption (moderate), persistence & bioaccumulation (high), and non-reproductive organ-system toxicity (moderate) — and notably EWG VERIFIED products cannot contain this ingredient [13]. (Verification note 2026-06-19: an earlier draft of this dossier listed the flags as "cancer / allergies / developmental-reproductive toxicity" — that was inaccurate; the actual EWG concern set is the one above.) Critical nuance: this is a class-based, precautionary auto-classification by EWG (any fluorinated structure gets the PFAS tag), not a finding of demonstrated harm for this specific molecule, which has no toxicity data showing it behaves like legacy long-chain PFAS (PFOA/PFOS). Scientifically the equivalence is weak. However, reputationally it is real: "contains PFAS" is a live consumer-safety and (increasingly) regulatory narrative, EWG/clean-beauty retailers may down-rate or screen the ingredient, and the EWG VERIFIED ban is a hard exclusion from that seal. For a science-forward premium brand this is a genuine downside to weigh (see §10/§11).
  • Sourcing/ethics: Fully synthetic; vegan/cruelty-free compatible, no animal-derived inputs.

8. Regulatory & Claims

  • Cosmetic vs. drug line: Progeline is a cosmetic ingredient. Its mechanism (modulating progerin / a senescence biomarker, inhibiting an enzyme, "slowing cellular aging") flirts with structure/function ("drug") territory under US FDA logic. Claims must stay in appearance language.
  • Safe (cosmetic) phrasings: "helps improve the appearance of firmness," "visibly supports a more defined-looking jawline/neck contour," "helps the skin look more elastic/lifted," "peptide that supports skin's resilience." Per ../../brand/glossary.md: use "supports / visibly / helps improve the appearance of," not "boosts X%."
  • Risky / unsupported — DO NOT USE without substantiation/legal review:
  • Any "reduces progerin," "anti-senescence," "rejuvenates cells," "turns back cellular age," "20-year-old cell" claim → these are mechanistic/structure-function claims AND are not substantiated by independent human data. Doubly problematic.
  • Quantified efficacy ("lifts jowls 10%," "+20% firmness in 28 days") → these are manufacturer in-house numbers without published methodology; using them on-pack/in-ads invites a substantiation challenge. If ever used, they must be flagged as supplier-claim and ideally backed by our own consumer test.
  • "Treats / prevents sagging," "rebuilds elastin" → drug-like; avoid.
  • EU/other markets: INCI "Trifluoroacetyl Tripeptide-2" is an established cosmetic ingredient (listed in supplier/CosIng-type databases). The PFAS regulatory wave (EU REACH universal PFAS restriction proposals; US state-level PFAS-in-cosmetics laws) is worth monitoring — depending on final definitions, fluorinated cosmetic ingredients could face future restriction or disclosure requirements. Track in research/science.

9. Sourcing & Cost

  • Suppliers: Original = Sederma / Lucas Meyer Cosmetics (IFF) as Progeline™. Numerous Chinese/Korean peptide houses sell generic Trifluoroacetyl Tripeptide-2 (BOC Sciences, Y&R/YR Chemspec, Omizzur, etc.); research-reagent grade from Cayman Chemical, MedChemExpress, GlpBio.
  • Cost tier: Mid-to-premium for the branded trade material; the peptide itself is potent at ppm so cost-in-use of the dilute trade product at 1–3% is moderate rather than extreme. Generic copies are cheaper but carry identity/purity and "is it really the same dilution?" risk — for a premium brand, branded (Progeline™) or fully spec'd material is safer.
  • Supply: Synthetic, scalable, no botanical/seasonal constraints.

10. Hype vs. Evidence

  • Biggest overreach: The "anti-progerin = reverses cellular aging / restores 20-year-old cell activity" narrative. The target is legit science; the topical clinical payoff is unproven. Marketing routinely cites the McClintock/Scaffidi progerin biology (independent, strong) and then silently borrows its credibility for the peptide's efficacy (manufacturer-only, weak). That is a classic mechanism-to-outcome leap.
  • Quantified claims masquerade as clinical proof. "10% jowl lift / 20% firmness" are repeated everywhere as if from a published trial; they are unpublished in-house figures with no available methodology [9].
  • The 2025 preprint is being used as "now there's a human study." It is uncontrolled, unblinded, n=21, 4 weeks, not peer-reviewed, vendor-funded — and on inspection its active is most likely Progerinin (a small molecule from the authors' own company, Cymbiotics/Park group), NOT Trifluoroacetyl Tripeptide-2 [12]. Borrowing it as proof for Progeline is a category error on top of a weak study.
  • "Potent at ppm" ≠ "works topically." High intrinsic potency in a dish is used to wave away delivery questions; dermal penetration is unmeasured (§5).
  • Under-disclosed downside: the PFAS classification [13] is rarely mentioned in the glowing write-ups, yet it's the single most likely thing to bite a clean/premium brand.
  • Fair steelman: the mechanism is more interesting and more target-validated than most "firming peptides," the molecule is small/lipophilic enough that penetration is at least plausible, the safety/irritation profile is benign, and it occupies a differentiated "senescence" story angle. It is not snake oil — it's an under-evidenced but mechanistically credible active.

11. Verdict for the Brand

⚠️ CONDITIONAL — supporting actor at most, not a hero; default lean is to skip for a flagship.

One-line reasoning: A mechanistically elegant, well-targeted (progerin/elastase) and benignly safe peptide whose human topical efficacy is essentially unproven (no independent RCT; claims are manufacturer-generated) and which carries a PFAS classification that is reputationally awkward for a premium, science-credible brand.

  • Why not hero: The brand's credibility rests on evidence over hype (CLAUDE.md). Building a flagship around an active whose efficacy data is unpublished and supplier-owned — and that can be tagged "PFAS" — runs directly against that. We could not honestly make strong, substantiated claims for it.
  • Where it could fit: as a supporting "firmness/contour" peptide in a multi-peptide neck/jawline product where the hero claim rests on better-evidenced actives, and copy stays strictly appearance-based ("helps skin look firmer/more lifted"). Even then, the PFAS optics should be a conscious decision.
  • Longevity-relevance note: This is one of the few cosmetic actives that explicitly engages a bona fide longevity/senescence axis — progerin is a real cellular-aging biomarker tied to telomere-driven senescence [1][2]. That makes it narratively on-thesis for a "skin-longevity" brand. But narrative fit must not override the evidence gap: the target is longevity-relevant; the proof that this peptide moves it in human skin is not there yet. If the brand wants a senescence-axis active, weigh it against better-evidenced senotherapeutic candidates (see senolytics-os01-peptide.md) before committing.

Recommendation: Keep in ../../ideas/considering.md as a conditional supporting peptide; do not shortlist as a hero. Revisit if (a) an independent, controlled, peer-reviewed RCT appears, and/or (b) the PFAS regulatory/optics picture clarifies.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Independent adversarial fact-check performed 2026-06-19. Method: re-checked each headline claim against primary sources for (a) human-vs-topical provenance, (b) whether cited studies are real and say what is claimed, and (c) funding/sample-size red flags. Sources re-verified live: PLOS ONE 10.1371/journal.pone.0001269; JCI 10.1172/JCI43578; Cayman reagent data; EWG Skin Deep + EWG Cleaners entries; medRxiv 2025.09.10.25335131 + the sponsoring company's own page.

Overall evidence grade for topical human efficacy of Trifluoroacetyl Tripeptide-2: D (essentially unproven topically / mechanism-led hype). There is zero independent, controlled, peer-reviewed human-topical RCT on this peptide. Every efficacy number in circulation is either supplier-generated/unpublished or comes from a study that most likely tests a different compound. (For context, the underlying progerin-target biology grades A and the elastase biochemistry grades A — but those validate the rationale, not the product. The dossier's own §4 tiering already keeps these separate and is correct to do so.)

What held up under scrutiny (no change needed): - [1] McClintock 2007 (PLOS ONE) — REAL and accurately described. 150 biopsies, newborn–97 y; progerin mRNA low at all ages while protein accumulates with age; sun-exposed ≈ non-exposed. Human tissue, but it is about the target, not the peptide. - [2] Cao 2011 (JCI 121:2833-44) — REAL and accurate. Telomere damage upstream-activates progerin splicing; immortalized/telomerase cells make essentially no progerin. Again validates the target only. - Elastase Kᵢ values — REAL. Cayman reagent data confirm Kᵢ ≈ 5.6×10⁻⁴ mM (pancreatic) and ~0.5 mM (neutrophil) elastase; MW 475.5; formula C₂₁H₂₈F₃N₃O₆. This is biochemical (test-tube), not human or topical — the dossier labels it correctly. - The mechanism→outcome separation — the dossier's central honesty (target biology is strong/independent; topical efficacy is weak/supplier-owned) is sound and survives verification.

Corrections made to the dossier during this pass: 1. EWG concern set was factually wrong. The prior draft said EWG flags "cancer / allergies / developmental-reproductive toxicity." EWG's actual listed concerns are enhanced skin absorption, use restrictions (high), endocrine disruption (moderate), persistence & bioaccumulation (high), non-reproductive organ-system toxicity (moderate), plus a hard EWG VERIFIED exclusion. §7 and source [13] corrected. (The PFAS-is-precautionary nuance was fair and is retained.) 2. The 2025 medRxiv preprint almost certainly is NOT this peptide. Senior author Bum-Joon Park is the inventor of Progerinin (SLC-D011), an oral small-molecule progerin–lamin A binding inhibitor, and the study is run/funded by Cymbiotics Biopharma, which commercializes that molecule. The serum's "1% progerin inhibitor" is never named as Trifluoroacetyl Tripeptide-2. The dossier previously hedged ("plausibly Progeline, but unverified"); that has been hardened to the defensible read — it is a different compound, so the preprint is not evidence for Progeline at all. §5b, §10, the lead box, and the summary table updated.

Main caveats (the things most likely to bite): - Provenance laundering. The strongest citations ([1][2][6], elastase biology) are human/independent but are all about progerin-as-a-target or elastase enzymology — none test the peptide on human skin. Marketing (and casual readers) borrow that A-grade credibility for a D-grade efficacy claim. The dossier resists this; copy built from it must too. - All peptide-specific efficacy data are single-source (Sederma/Lucas Meyer) and unpublished. No independent replication of progerin reduction, MMP-1 down-regulation, or the "43% collagen / 100% elastic-fiber protection" explant numbers. The "100%" figure is an enzyme-challenge artifact, not an elasticity gain. - Quantified consumer claims ("+20% firmness / 28 d", "10% jowl lift / 56 d") have no published methodology and trace only to promotional materials; Baumann [9] independently flags the same gap. - Delivery is assumed, not shown. No penetration data; "potent at ppm in a dish" ≠ "reaches dermal fibroblasts." - PFAS / EWG VERIFIED exclusion is a real reputational/regulatory liability for a clean-leaning premium brand, even though the toxicological equivalence to legacy PFAS is weak. - Unverified-by-design items the dossier already flags honestly: Scaffidi 2006 [6] DOI (from memory), Cosmetics & Toiletries [8] and ULProspector [14] (403/access-blocked on direct fetch). These remain unverified-direct; treat as such.

Claims that should be softened / never stated as fact (most already handled in-file): - ❌ "There's a 2025 human study showing Progeline works." → It is a preprint, uncontrolled, and most likely on a different molecule (Progerinin). Do not attribute it to this peptide. - ❌ "Reduces progerin / anti-senescence / restores 20-year-old cell activity." → mechanistic structure/function claim, unproven in human skin; promotional extrapolation. - ❌ Quantified efficacy ("+20% firmness", "10% lift", "43%/100% protection", "+23.6% density"). → unpublished supplier numbers or uncontrolled/likely-wrong-molecule preprint; never use on-pack/in-ad without our own substantiated test. - ⚠️ "Penetrates to fibroblasts." → soften to "plausibly penetrates (small, lipophilic) but unproven." - ⚠️ EWG/PFAS — describe with the correct concern list above; do not overstate as proven toxicity, do not understate the EWG VERIFIED ban.
