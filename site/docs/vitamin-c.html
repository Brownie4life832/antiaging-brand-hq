Ingredient: Vitamin C — L-Ascorbic Acid + derivatives family (INCI: Ascorbic Acid; Tetrahexyldecyl Ascorbate; Sodium Ascorbyl Phosphate; Magnesium Ascorbyl Phosphate; 3-O-Ethyl Ascorbic Acid; Ascorbyl Glucoside; Ascorbyl Palmitate)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ✅ shortlisted (HERO candidate) · Class: Antioxidants

Exhaustive, citation-backed literature review. Evidence is graded by tier (in-vitro → animal → human oral → human topical) and the dossier is deliberately blunt about where the evidence is thin or borrowed across formats. Cosmetic-claims guardrails per ../../CLAUDE.md and ../../brand/glossary.md.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Vitamin C (ascorbate / ascorbic acid).
  • Parent molecule — INCI: Ascorbic Acid (L-ascorbic acid, "LAA"): the bioactive, naturally occurring form. Best-studied, most potent, least stable, water-soluble. This is the molecule all derivatives must convert into in skin to "count."
  • Key forms / derivatives (each is a distinct INCI and a distinct evidence story — do not treat as interchangeable):

2. Class & mechanism

Vitamin C is a small, water-soluble antioxidant and an enzyme cofactor. Topically it acts at the skin/cell level through several mechanisms:

  1. Free-radical scavenging. Ascorbate donates electrons to neutralize reactive oxygen species (ROS) generated by UV and pollution, regenerating itself and recycling oxidized vitamin E (tocopherol) — the basis of the C+E synergy.
  2. Collagen cofactor. LAA is an essential cofactor for prolyl-4-hydroxylase and lysyl hydroxylase, the enzymes that hydroxylate proline/lysine residues so collagen triple helices are stable and properly cross-linked. Without ascorbate these enzymes stall (the biochemistry of scurvy).
  3. Collagen gene upregulation. Beyond the cofactor role, ascorbate increases type I and III procollagen mRNA in fibroblasts via increased transcription and transcript stabilization — a separate effect from hydroxylation (Geesin/Pinnell, in-vitro).
  4. Pigment modulation. Interacts with copper at the tyrosinase active site and reduces dopaquinone, interrupting melanogenesis → less new melanin (brightening/tone evening).
  5. Photoprotective adjunct. Reduces UV-induced erythema, sunburn-cell formation, thymine-dimer formation and p53 induction in human skin when applied before UV (esp. as C+E+ferulic). It is not a sunscreen (no meaningful UV absorption / SPF) — it works downstream of the photons.

3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

Pigmentation / uneven tone ✅ Yes Moderate human (incl. melasma signal)
Inflammaging ➖ Partial Indirect (antioxidant); weak direct data
Barrier / hydration / TEWL ➖ Marginal Weak — moisture improved no better than vehicle in RCT pooling

Microbiome ❌ Not established No data

Bottom line: vitamin C's defensible territory is oxidative/photoaging defense, collagen support, and tone/brightness. Claims tying it to senescence, mitochondria, glycation or the microbiome are not supported for topical use.

4. Evidence review by tier

Read this as a hierarchy. In-vitro and animal work explains why it should work; only human topical RCTs tell us it does in real skin. Where a benefit rests on oral or in-vitro data, that is flagged so it is never sold as a topical claim.

Tier 1 — In-vitro (cell/biochemistry)

Strong and consistent — but mechanism, not proof of cosmetic benefit:

  • Murad, Tajima, Pinnell (1981) — ascorbate regulates prolyl/lysyl hydroxylase activity in cultured human skin fibroblasts. Biochem Biophys Res Commun. https://doi.org/10.1016/0006-291X(81)91830-1
  • Geesin, Darr, Kaufman, Murad, Pinnell (1988) — ascorbic acid specifically increases type I and III procollagen mRNA in human skin fibroblasts (transcription + stabilization), independent of the hydroxylase cofactor role. J Invest Dermatol 90(4):420-4. https://doi.org/10.1111/1523-1747.ep12460849 (PMID 3351329)
  • Tajima & Pinnell (1982) — ascorbic acid increases type I procollagen mRNA in human skin fibroblasts. Biochem Biophys Res Commun. https://doi.org/10.1016/0006-291X(82)91157-3 (Note: the often-quoted "~3-4× collagen synthesis tracking ~2-3× mRNA rise" figure is from Geesin 1988, above, not this paper.)
  • Kameyama et al. (1996) — magnesium ascorbyl phosphate (VC-PMG) suppresses tyrosinase/melanin formation in melanoma cells (in-vitro arm). J Am Acad Dermatol 34(1):29-33. (PMID 8543691)
  • Swindell et al. (2021) — THDC degrades rapidly under oxidative/singlet-oxygen stress, is a poor standalone antioxidant, and alone triggered type-I interferon (pro-inflammatory) signaling; stabilized by acetyl zingerone, after which it repressed MMP1/MMP7 and raised fibroblast collagen. Important caveat to THD marketing. Int J Mol Sci 22(16):8756. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8395926/
  • Limitations: monolayer fibroblasts in ascorbate-free media wildly over-respond vs. real dermis; saturating concentrations; no barrier; no UV-aged matrix. In-vitro collagen "boosts" do not license a topical collagen claim.

Tier 2 — Animal

  • Hairless-mouse and porcine-skin models show topical LAA reduces UV-induced erythema, sunburn cells and photodamage, and that the C+E+ferulic combination roughly doubles photoprotection — but the landmark photoprotection work (Lin/Pinnell) used pig skin and human skin (below). Animal data here mainly de-risked the human work; sparse standalone rodent efficacy literature. Treat as supportive, not decisive.

Tier 3 — Human, ORAL (diet / supplement)

Relevant to longevity framing, not a topical claim:

  • Cosgrove et al. (2007), NHANES I — in 4,025 US women (40-74 y), higher dietary vitamin C intake was associated with lower likelihood of a wrinkled appearance (OR 0.89; 95% CI 0.82-0.96) and senile dryness (OR 0.93; 0.87-0.99), independent of age, sun exposure, BMI, etc. Am J Clin Nutr 86(4):1225-31. https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/86.4.1225 (PMID 17921406)
  • Limitation: observational, single 24-h dietary recall, residual confounding (healthy-diet halo). Association, not causation. Says nothing about topical serums.
  • Ascorbate is the rate-limiting cofactor for collagen synthesis system-wide (scurvy biology), so adequate dietary intake plausibly supports skin collagen — a whole-body argument, not evidence that more oral C in a replete person improves skin, and not transferable to topical claims.

Tier 4 — Human, TOPICAL

4a. RCTs / controlled clinical trials (the load-bearing evidence)

  • Traikovich (1999) — vehicle-controlled, double-blind, randomized; n=19 mild-moderate photodamage; 10% LAA vs vehicle, 3 months. Computer image-analysis of surface topography showed statistically significant improvement in the vitamin-C side vs vehicle. Arch Otolaryngol Head Neck Surg 125(10):1091-8. https://doi.org/10.1001/archotol.125.10.1091 (PMID 10522500). Limitation: tiny n, single site, surface-topography endpoints.
  • Fitzpatrick & Rostan (2002) — double-blind, split-face, n=10; complex containing 10% LAA + 7% THD ascorbate in anhydrous silicone gel vs vehicle, 12 weeks. Statistically significant improvement in photoaging scores (cheeks P=0.006; peri-oral P=0.01); clinical improvement correlated with biopsy evidence of new collagen formation. Dermatol Surg 28(3):231-6. https://doi.org/10.1046/j.1524-4725.2002.01129.x (PMID 11896774). Limitation: very small n; combination product (can't isolate LAA vs THD); industry context.
  • Humbert et al. (2003) — double-blind vs placebo with ultrastructural read-out; topical 5% LAA cream on photoaged neck/forearm of female volunteers, 6 months. Clinical + topographical improvement and electron-microscopy evidence of dense elastic-tissue repair (regenerated elastic fibers) on the treated side. Exp Dermatol 12(3):237-44. https://doi.org/10.1034/j.1600-0625.2003.00008.x (PMID 12895413). Limitation: small, body (not face) skin, 5% (lower than "optimal").
  • Lin et al. (2005), the C+E+ferulic photoprotection study — on human skin (plus pig-skin stability work): adding 0.5% ferulic acid to 15% LAA + 1% α-tocopherol stabilized the vitamins and doubled photoprotection (~4× → ~8×) measured by erythema and sunburn-cell formation; reduced thymine dimers and inhibited apoptosis (reduced caspase-3/-7). J Invest Dermatol 125(4):826-32. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.0022-202X.2005.23768.x (PMID 16185284). Foundation of the "CE Ferulic" category. (Note: the "p53 induction" reduction is reported in the broader CE-ferulic literature; this specific paper's primary endpoints were erythema, sunburn cells, thymine dimers and apoptosis.)
  • Murray et al. (2008) — topical C+E+ferulic reduced UV-induced erythema, thymine dimers and apoptotic sunburn cells in human skin after 4 days' application then solar-simulated UV. J Am Acad Dermatol 59(3):418-25. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jaad.2008.05.004 (PMID 18603326).
  • Oresajo et al. (2008) — topical vitamin C + ferulic acid + phloretin reduced UV-induced photodamage markers (thymine dimers, sunburn cells, Langerhans-cell depletion) in human skin. J Cosmet Dermatol 7(4):290-7. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1473-2165.2008.00408.x
  • Qin et al. (2025) and Shi et al. (2026), split-face fractional-laser + CE Ferulic RCTs — investigator-blinded, split-face controlled trials show a C+E+ferulic serum improves post-laser recovery endpoints when applied as an adjuvant after fractional laser (Qin 2025, nonablative Fraxel, n=50, J Cosmet Dermatol, PMC12104010; Shi 2026, ablative CO2 / atrophic acne scars, J Cosmet Dermatol, PMC12793811). These are adjuvant post-procedure trials, not standalone photoaging RCTs — supportive context for the C+E+ferulic system, not direct proof of leave-on anti-photoaging efficacy. (Earlier draft mis-attributed these to "Ruvolo"; corrected.)

Derivative-specific human topical trials:

  • SAP (sodium ascorbyl phosphate), 5% lotion, acne — randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled, n=50, 12 weeks: SAP statistically beat vehicle on all measured parameters (Investigator/Subject Global Assessment, lesion counts, tolerability). Woolery-Lloyd et al., J Cosmet Dermatol 2010;9(1):22-7. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1473-2165.2010.00480.x (PMID 20367669). (The specific "~20% at 4 wk / ~49% at 8 wk" inflammatory-lesion reductions are from the full-text figures, not the abstract — treat as approximate pending full-text confirmation.) Klock et al. (2005) earlier in-vivo/in-vitro acne efficacy. Note: this is an acne indication, not anti-aging; relevant to SAP's sebum/acne story, not to a wrinkle/collagen claim.
  • MAP, melasma/pigment — Kameyama (1996) clinical arm: skin-lightening significant in 19/34 chloasma/freckle patients. Combination RCTs (e.g., TCA peel + 5% MAP, n=148) show added MASI reduction vs peel alone. https://doi.org/10.1016/S0190-9622(96)90830-0
  • EAC (3-O-ethyl ascorbic acid), 10% serum — controlled cosmetic clinical evaluations report reduced dark spots, improved firmness/elasticity, fewer visible pores/wrinkles; selected as best-performing derivative in screening studies. e.g., J Clin Aesthet Dermatol / formulation studies (PMC8146548). Caveat: several are open-label or manufacturer-run, smaller and less rigorous than the LAA RCTs.

4b. Lower-tier human topical (open-label, case series, reviews)

  • Correia & Magina (2023), systematic review — 7 prospective RCTs, only 139 total volunteers: vitamin C improved photoaging texture/wrinkle appearance (biopsy-supported) and produced significant lightening in melasma; hydration improved no better than placebo. Authors: small studies, ideal concentration unsettled, long-term use likely needed. J Cosmet Dermatol 22(7):1938-45. https://doi.org/10.1111/jocd.15748 (PMID 37128827).
  • Multiple narrative reviews (Pullar/Carr/Vissers 2017 Nutrients "The Roles of Vitamin C in Skin Health"; Telang 2013; Al-Niaimi 2017 JCAD) consolidate mechanism + clinical data and consistently note the evidence base is mechanistically strong but clinically modest (small n, heterogeneous formulas).

Honest synthesis of Tier 4: the human topical RCT base is real but thin — a handful of well-designed but very small trials (n=10-50), often combination products, several industry-linked. The most robust topical findings are (a) photoprotective/antioxidant adjunct effects (C+E+ferulic) and (b) tone/brightness/pigment improvement. Visible wrinkle/texture and collagen benefits are supported but at moderate strength (biopsy-corroborated in 2-3 small studies). Barrier/hydration is not a vitamin-C win.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Pinnell et al. (2001), the definitive penetration study — in pig skin: LAA only penetrates at pH <3.5; optimal absorption at 20% (higher gives no more); tissue saturates after ~3 daily applications; tissue half-life ~4 days (so once-daily is fine and you can't "load up"). Critically, MAP, ascorbyl-6-palmitate and dehydroascorbic acid did NOT raise skin LAA levels in that model. Dermatol Surg 27(2):137-42. https://doi.org/10.1046/j.1524-4725.2001.00264.x (PMID 11207686).
  • Implication: the penetration evidence is strongest for acidified LAA. Derivatives must (i) penetrate and (ii) be enzymatically converted to LAA intracellularly. Conversion is documented (esterases for THD/palmitate; phosphatases for SAP/MAP; glucosidase for AA2G) but in-skin conversion efficiency is variable and, for several derivatives, not demonstrated to deliver meaningful LAA levels in vivo. THD and EAC have the better lipophilicity/penetration story; AA2G is limited by low human skin glucosidase activity; ascorbyl palmitate is weak.
  • Practical reading: "vitamin C derivative" on a label does not guarantee active vitamin C reaches the dermis. This is a real formulation/efficacy risk, not pedantry.

6. Formulation

  • LAA effective range: ~8-20% (below ~8% under-effective; ~20% is the absorption ceiling; >20% adds irritation, not benefit).
  • pH: LAA needs <3.5 to penetrate and to drive collagen-gene effects — this is the source of both its potency and its irritation/stability headaches.
  • Stability — the core challenge: LAA oxidizes to dehydroascorbic acid then to inert/discoloring products. Visible yellow→brown = degraded/inactive. Mitigations: low pH, anhydrous/silicone or low-water systems, antioxidant partners (vitamin E, ferulic), chelators, opaque/airless packaging, sometimes encapsulation. Derivatives (SAP, MAP, EAC, AA2G, THD-in-formula) are markedly more stable and pH-flexible.
  • Synergies: Vitamin E (α-tocopherol) + ferulic acid — the best-evidenced pairing (stability + ~doubled photoprotection). SPF in the AM (vitamin C complements, never replaces).
  • Pairings/conflicts:
  • Niacinamide: the old "they cancel / form niacin and flush" claim was based on high-heat conditions and is largely overstated for modern formulas; widely co-formulated. (Flag only if a copy claim needs to lean on co-use.)
  • Retinoids / AHAs/BHAs: don't stack high-strength LAA with strong actives in the same step on sensitive skin (additive irritation). Standard pattern: vitamin C AM, retinoid PM.
  • Benzoyl peroxide: can oxidize LAA — separate.
  • Sensory: low-pH LAA serums can sting and feel tacky; THD (oil-soluble) and modern derivative systems give the elegant, gentle, premium texture a luxury barrier-focused brand wants.

7. Safety

  • Irritation: main issue is low-pH LAA (stinging, transient erythema), concentration-dependent; derivatives are gentler. Start lower / buffer for sensitive skin.
  • Sun sensitivity: vitamin C does not photosensitize; it is photoprotective-adjunctive. Still not a sunscreen.
  • Sensitization / allergy: ascorbic acid itself is a rare allergen, but allergic contact dermatitis to vitamin C is documented since 1980, and 3-O-ethyl ascorbic acid (EAC) is flagged as an emerging contact allergen as its cosmetic use grows (Foti et al., 2022, systematic review of vitamin contact allergy, Health Sci Rep 5(5):e766, https://doi.org/10.1002/hsr2.766). Worth noting given EAC's popularity.
  • Pregnancy/lactation: topical vitamin C is generally considered low-risk (no evidence of teratogenicity; water-soluble; minimal systemic absorption). Still attach "consult your doctor" boilerplate; favor ≤10-15% to limit irritation on pregnancy-sensitive skin. (Not medical advice — see CLAUDE.md.)
  • CIR safety status: the Cosmetic Ingredient Review Expert Panel concluded ascorbic acid, Ca/Mg/Na ascorbate, MAP and SAP are safe as used (2005, reaffirmed on re-review), and that seven ethers/esters of ascorbic acid are safe in current use/concentration (CIR, 2022). https://www.cir-safety.org/sites/default/files/Ascorbic%20Acid.pdf
  • Sourcing/sustainability: LAA is industrially produced (Reichstein / two-step fermentation from glucose/sorbitol); no notable animal-derived or endangered-source concerns. Main sustainability levers are packaging (airless components are harder to recycle) and waste from degraded/returned product.

8. Regulatory & claims (US FDA framing per CLAUDE.md)

  • Cosmetic vs drug line: a cosmetic claim describes appearance; a drug claim is "intended to diagnose, cure, mitigate, treat, or prevent disease" or to affect the structure or function of the body. Collagen-building, wrinkle-treating, melasma-treating, and "prevents sun damage" claims tip toward drug territory and the FDA has issued warning letters over exactly these. Keep copy in appearance/"helps"/"visibly" language.
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "antioxidant defense against environmental stressors," "helps brighten and even the look of skin tone," "for visible radiance/luminosity," "helps improve the appearance of fine lines," "supports the look of firmer skin."
  • ⚠️ Risky / drug-like (flag for substantiation/legal): "boosts collagen [by X%]," "stimulates collagen," "prevents/repairs sun damage," "replaces sunscreen," "treats hyperpigmentation/melasma," "anti-aging" as a function claim. Quantified claims (e.g., "X% fewer wrinkles") require product-specific substantiation.
  • Substantiation reality: the published RCTs are on other formulas. Any efficacy/percentage claim must be backed by our finished-product testing, not borrowed from the literature.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • LAA raw material: cheap, commodity. The cost is in stabilization, packaging (airless/opaque), and shelf-life QC — and in the brand-risk of a product that oxidizes before use.
  • Derivatives: THD ascorbate is significantly more expensive (premium-positioning fit); SAP/MAP/EAC/AA2G are mid-tier and far more forgiving to formulate.
  • Suppliers: DSM-Firmenich, Corum, Spec-Chem, Ashland, Sabinsa and others; raw materials are widely available and second-sourceable.
  • Cost tier for the brand: ingredient cost is low-to-moderate; total cost is driven by format choice (stable THD/derivative system + airless pack = higher but on-brand).

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • "Boosts collagen" / "rebuilds skin": in-vitro and a couple of small biopsy-backed RCTs support collagen support; the loud quantified "boost" claims are overreach and a drug-claim hazard.
  • "Brightening" is the strongest real-world story — yet often undersold relative to vague "anti-aging."
  • "Vitamin C derivative = same benefit, no downside": false comfort. Per Pinnell, several derivatives don't demonstrably raise skin LAA; THD degrades fast under oxidative stress (Swindell). Derivatives trade proven potency for stability/gentleness — a reasonable trade, but not "free."
  • "It's like a sunscreen / protects from the sun": no. It's an antioxidant adjunct, not a UV filter.
  • Oxidized = fine: no. Brown serum is degraded; this is the single biggest credibility trap for an evidence-led brand.
  • Senescence / mitochondria / glycation / microbiome longevity claims: unsupported topically — do not borrow these from oral or in-vitro longevity literature.

11. Verdict for the brand

✅ HERO (AM) — with a stability-first formulation discipline. Vitamin C is the best-evidenced daytime antioxidant + radiance active and the natural counterpart to a nighttime retinoid: defend by day, renew by night. Strongest defensible claims are environmental/antioxidant defense and brightness/tone; collagen/wrinkle support is real but should stay in appearance language. Favor a stable derivative (THD or EAC) or a well-stabilized C+E+ferulic system in airless/opaque packaging over a finicky low-pH LAA serum, to match a premium, low-irritation, elegant-texture positioning — but verify actual in-skin vitamin C delivery, not just a derivative on the label.

Longevity-relevance note: vitamin C maps cleanly onto two genuine hallmarks of skin aging — oxidative/photoaging and collagen/ECM decline — and oral epidemiology (Cosgrove/NHANES) links higher vitamin C status to less wrinkling, giving an honest "skin-longevity" throughline. It does not credibly touch senescence, mitochondria, glycation or the microbiome topically; keep those out of the longevity story for this ingredient.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Citation check — what I verified

All of the load-bearing citations are real and say what the dossier claims (spot-checked against PubMed/PMC): - Pinnell 2001 (penetration): pH <3.5, 20% ceiling, ~4-day tissue half-life, saturates after 3 applications, MAP/ascorbyl-6-palmitate/DHAA did NOT raise skin LAA — all exact. - Traikovich 1999 (n=19, 3-mo, RCT, vehicle-controlled, topography endpoints) — exact. - Fitzpatrick & Rostan 2002 (n=10, split-face, 10% LAA + 7% THD, biopsy collagen correlation) — exact. - Lin 2005 (15% LAA + 1% tocopherol + 0.5% ferulic, 4× → ~8× photoprotection, human skin) — exact. - Geesin 1988 (type I/III procollagen mRNA, 3-4× synthesis / 2-3× mRNA, in-vitro) — exact. - Cosgrove/NHANES 2007 (n=4,025; wrinkling OR 0.89 [0.82-0.96]; dryness OR 0.93 [0.87-0.99]; 24-h recall) — exact. - Swindell 2021 (THDC degrades fast, poor standalone antioxidant, type-I interferon activation alone, MMP1/MMP7 repression + collagen rise after acetyl-zingerone stabilization; cell-based) — exact, including the inconvenient bits. - Woolery-Lloyd 2010 (SAP 5%, acne, n=50, RCT, double-blind, vehicle-controlled, 12 wk) — design verified. - Correia & Magina 2023 (7 RCTs, 139 total participants; hydration improved equally to placebo; pigment + photoaging benefit) — verified, including the hydration null result, which the dossier states correctly.

Main caveats (read before writing any copy)

  • The human-topical RCT base is real but small and old. The core monotherapy LAA trials are n=10-19, single-site, 1999-2003, with surface-topography or small-biopsy endpoints. Several involve combination products (can't isolate LAA) and industry context (Fitzpatrick/Rostan, the SkinCeuticals/Duke C+E+ferulic lineage via Pinnell). The science is directionally sound but would not survive being sold as "clinically proven" without our own finished-product testing.
  • Industry-funding / single-lab concentration is real. A large share of the foundational topical and penetration work traces to Pinnell and the Duke group, commercially tied to the CE-ferulic (SkinCeuticals) franchise. That doesn't make it wrong — the Swindell paper even publishes THDC's flaws — but the evidence base is not independent and not large. Treat as "plausible, supported, not settled."
  • Derivatives ≠ LAA. Pinnell 2001 explicitly shows several common derivatives failed to raise skin LAA in pig skin. The dossier's insistence on verifying actual in-skin delivery rather than trusting the label is the single most important and correctly-stated point.
  • Oral and in-vitro data must never migrate to topical copy. The NHANES wrinkle association is oral/observational; the collagen "boost" is in-vitro. Both are correctly fenced.

Claims that should stay softened (already appearance-language in the dossier — keep them there)

  • "Boosts/stimulates collagen [by X%]" → only "supports the look of firmer skin," "helps improve the appearance of fine lines." (In-vitro + 2-3 tiny biopsy RCTs ≠ a quantified collagen claim.)
  • "Prevents / repairs sun damage" / "like a sunscreen" → only "antioxidant defense against environmental stressors"; it is an adjunct, not an SPF.
  • "Treats hyperpigmentation / melasma" → "helps brighten and even the look of skin tone."
  • Any percentage efficacy claim → requires our product's substantiation; the literature percentages are for other formulas.
  • Longevity framing → keep to oxidative/photoaging + collagen/ECM; do not invoke senescence, mitochondria, glycation, or microbiome (Grade D topically).

Net: the dossier is defensible and well-disciplined after the four citation corrections above. Grade B. Build the brand's vitamin-C story on antioxidant/environmental defense and brightness/tone (strongest), with appearance-language wrinkle/firmness support as a secondary, and verify in-skin delivery and run own-product testing before any quantified claim.
