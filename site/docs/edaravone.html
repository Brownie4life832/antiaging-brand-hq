Ingredient: Edaravone (INCI: none — not a registered cosmetic ingredient)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ⬜ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ✅ rejected (as-is) / ⬜ in a product Class: Antioxidant (synthetic pyrazolone free-radical scavenger) · Slug: edaravone

TL;DR for a busy reader. Edaravone (MCI-186) is a genuinely potent, amphiphilic free-radical scavenger — and a licensed prescription drug (Radicava/Radicut) for ALS and (in Japan/China) ischemic stroke. As a molecule its antioxidant credentials are real: it quenches hydroxyl, peroxyl and superoxide radicals and peroxynitrite, and it switches on the Nrf2/HO-1 antioxidant program. The catch for skincare is fourfold and severe. (1) There is no human topical anti-aging trial of edaravone — none. The skin evidence is in-vitro plus animal studies, and the animal skin work (flap survival, wound healing) used systemic injection, not a cream. (2) The one good topical-delivery animal study (diabetic wound, Fan 2019) showed a U-shaped dose-response: the LOW dose helped, the HIGH dose made healing WORSE — because wiping out the ROS that cells use for signaling is harmful. That is a real liability for an "antioxidant the more the better" pitch. (3) It is chemically fragile — the active anion auto-oxidizes and precipitates as colored trimers above ~pH 7, exactly where it is most active; commercial vials are kept acidic, oxygen-free and stabilized with sodium bisulfite (itself a sensitizer/sulfite-allergy risk). (4) It has no cosmetic INCI and a drug identity, so putting it in a cream is a serious cosmetic-vs-drug regulatory problem, not a marketing nuance. The honest read: excellent antioxidant chemistry, real drug-grade systemic proof, essentially zero human topical cosmetic evidence, and material formulation + regulatory blockers. See Verdict. Skip as a named cosmetic active; if the chemistry is appealing, pursue purpose-built edaravone-derivative cosmeceuticals that are being designed for this — not the drug itself.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Edaravone. Research/code name MCI-186. Also historically norphenazone.
  • INCI: None. Edaravone is not listed as a cosmetic ingredient (no INCI name, not in EU CosIng as a cosmetic active). It is an active pharmaceutical ingredient (API). [11][13]
  • Chemistry: 3-methyl-1-phenyl-2-pyrazolin-5-one (IUPAC: 5-methyl-2-phenyl-2,4-dihydro-3H-pyrazol-3-one). CAS 89-25-8. Formula C₁₀H₁₀N₂O, MW ≈ 174.2 g/mol — a very small molecule. [1][11]
  • Tautomerism (this matters for formulation): edaravone exists in keto (amine), enol, and anion forms in equilibrium. The enolate/anion is the antioxidant-active species; the keto form is the inert, storage-stable solid. pKa ≈ 7.0, so at skin/physiological pH a large fraction is the reactive anion. [1][9]
  • Brand names / forms: Radicava® (IV) and Radicava ORS® (oral suspension) for ALS (Mitsubishi Tanabe); Radicut® / Xavron® for stroke; edaravone dexborneol (edaravone + (+)-borneol 4:1; Chinese stroke drug, approved 2020). [10][11][14]
  • Developer: Mitsubishi Tanabe Pharma (late 1980s, Okayama University). [11]
  • Derivatives for cosmetic intent (not the drug itself): academic groups are synthesizing 4-position pyrazolone derivatives of edaravone explicitly to make cosmeceutical antioxidants/tyrosinase-binders with better properties (Mohan/Kulkarni 2024–2025). These are research compounds, not edaravone and not commercial actives. [5][6]

2. Class & mechanism

Edaravone is a small-molecule, amphiphilic redox scavenger of the pyrazolone class. Documented mechanisms:

  1. Direct radical scavenging (the headline). The edaravone anion donates an electron to radicals, terminating chain reactions. It reacts with hydroxyl radical (•OH) at a near-diffusion-limited rate constant (~5–7 × 10¹⁰ M⁻¹s⁻¹), with peroxyl radicals (LOO•) (the chain carriers of lipid peroxidation), with superoxide, and with peroxynitrite (forming 4-NO/4-NO₂ products). Being amphiphilic, it works in both lipid and aqueous phases — quenching lipid peroxidation in membranes and water-phase radicals. The stable oxidation end-product is OPB (2-oxo-3-(phenylhydrazono)-butanoic acid). [1][2][3]
  2. Nrf2/HO-1 (ARE) activation. Beyond stoichiometric scavenging, edaravone upregulates the Nrf2 → HO-1 / antioxidant-response-element program — i.e. it nudges the cell's endogenous antioxidant defenses, a mechanism shared with many "longevity" actives (sulforaphane, ergothioneine). Best shown for edaravone-dexborneol in stroke models. [14]
  3. Anti-lipid-peroxidation / membrane protection. Lowers MDA (malondialdehyde) and spares endogenous SOD in injury models — i.e. it protects the antioxidant reserve, not just radicals. [4][7]
  4. Downstream cell-protective signaling (model-dependent): PI3K/Akt/mTOR-mediated autophagy (skin-flap model [8]), suppression of senescence markers p16/p21/p53 (senescence models [15][16]). These are mechanistic leads, not skin-aging proof.

⚠️ Mechanistic honesty — the dose paradox. Edaravone is a strong scavenger, and "strong" is not automatically "good" for skin. Physiological ROS are signaling molecules — needed for normal proliferation, migration and wound repair. Fan 2019 demonstrated this directly in skin: a low topical edaravone dose accelerated diabetic wound healing, while a high dose drove ROS below the useful range and slowed healing by ~30% versus untreated controls. [4] An over-dosed potent scavenger can be a net negative. This is the single most important nuance for an antioxidant-led brand and it cuts against "more antioxidant = more anti-aging" copy. (The same redox-rheostat logic underlies why edaravone is dosed carefully even as a drug.)

4. Evidence review by tier — the core of this dossier

Read this as a ladder. Edaravone is unusual: the top of the drug ladder is full (Phase-3 human RCTs — but oral/IV, for nerves, not skin), while the top of the topical cosmetic ladder is completely empty. Do not let the impressive drug pedigree be smuggled into skincare claims.

Tier 1 — In-vitro / biochemical (strong for chemistry, irrelevant to clinical skin benefit)

  • Radical kinetics / chemistry: Reaction-rate work establishes edaravone as a fast scavenger of •OH (~5–7×10¹⁰ M⁻¹s⁻¹), peroxyl radicals, superoxide and peroxynitrite; amphiphilic, electron-donating anion mechanism. [1][2] ORAC capacity ≈ 5.65 µM Trolox-equivalent/µM — i.e. several-fold Trolox on a molar basis — and ORAC-classification work groups edaravone's reactivity with cysteine-type antioxidants. [3] ⚠️ These are test-tube numbers; an ORAC value does not predict topical clinical benefit (the same caveat that sinks many "most powerful antioxidant" stories).
  • Senescence (cells): Malekinejad 2025 — edaravone protected PC12 cells from D-galactose-induced senescence, ↓DNA damage and ↓p53/p21/p16 (PMID 41254185). [15] Rahimifard 2026 — senomorphic effect on dasatinib-induced senescence in AGS gastric cells (PMID 39995134). [16] Interesting longevity biology; not skin, not topical, not human.
  • Edaravone derivatives (cosmetic-intent, in-vitro/in-silico): Mohan 2024 (Antioxidants) synthesized nine 4-substituted edaravone derivatives + Cu(II) complexes; several out-scavenged the parent (DPPH IC₅₀ ~2.9–5.8 µg/mL) and docked into tyrosinase (binding to −11.5 kcal/mol), framed for "cosmeceuticals." No cellular melanin assay, no cytotoxicity, no human data. [5] Kulkarni 2025 (J Enzyme Inhib Med Chem) extended this with anti-inflammatory + in-silico tyrosinase work. [6] ⚠️ These validate a derivative R&D direction, not edaravone-the-ingredient.

Tier 2 — Animal / ex-vivo skin

(a) Topical, dose-dependent — the most skin-relevant study, and a cautionary one: - Fan et al. 2019, Marine Drugs 17(5):285 (PMID 31083588; DOI 10.3390/md17050285). Edaravone encapsulated in Eudragit RL nanoparticles (105 nm), loaded into a sodium-alginate/CaCl₂ topical hydrogel, on a splinted full-thickness excisional wound in diabetic (STZ) mice. Low dose (0.1 mg) roughly tripled day-5 healing vs diabetic controls and reached ~96.6% closure by day 10; high dose (0.3 mg) made healing ~30% WORSE than untreated diabetic controls. Mechanism: rebalancing wound ROS toward the physiological window (low ROS = needed signaling; excess scavenging = harm). ⚠️ n=4/group, mouse, wound (not aging) — but it is the cleanest demonstration that (i) edaravone can be delivered topically with a carrier and (ii) dose is make-or-break. [4]

(b) Animal skin but SYSTEMIC dosing (do not read as topical evidence): - Zhang et al. 2017, J Huazhong Univ Sci Technol Med Sci 37(1) (PMID 28224430). Rat ischemia/reperfusion skin flap; edaravone 10 mg/kg intraperitoneal (not topical) ×3 days → significantly higher flap viability, ↑SOD, ↓MDA. n=48 (16/group). [7] - Bi et al. 2023, Int Wound J (DOI 10.1111/iwj.14184). Rat random-pattern skin flap; edaravone (systemic) improved flap survival via PI3K/Akt/mTOR-mediated autophagy and reduced oxidative stress. [8] - ⚠️ Both show edaravone protects skin tissue from oxidative/ischemic injury — but via injection, so they say nothing about cosmetic topical delivery or anti-aging.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL / IV (robust — but a NERVE drug; do NOT borrow for a cream)

  • ALS: The pivotal MCI186-19 (Study 19) RCT — edaravone 60 mg IV in 28-day cycles vs placebo — showed a ~33% slower decline in ALSFRS-R over 24 weeks in a defined subgroup; basis of FDA approval (May 2017) and Japan (2015). Oral Radicava ORS approved May 2022. [10][11]
  • Stroke: Approved in Japan since 2001 (Radicut) as an adjunct in acute ischemic stroke; edaravone-dexborneol approved in China 2020, with RCT/meta-analytic support for functional outcomes. [11][14]
  • Relevance to skincare: proves edaravone is a bona-fide, clinically active antioxidant drug with real systemic bioactivity and an established (if sulfite-flagged) safety dossier — but the indications are neurological, the route is oral/IV, and none of this is evidence for topical anti-aging. Borrowing "clinically proven antioxidant" onto a cosmetic is a textbook format-transfer overreach (and, given drug status, a regulatory one).

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (cosmetic / dermatologic)

EMPTY. There is no published human topical clinical trial of edaravone for skin aging, photoaging, pigmentation, or any cosmetic endpoint that this review could find. The closest human topical pyrazolone-adjacent data belongs to a different molecule (NdCAT, a tryptamine — Shin 2025, J Cosmet Dermatol), which is not edaravone and must not be cited as such. The human topical rung for edaravone itself is blank.

Bottom line by tier: outstanding chemistry (Tier 1) → genuine but mostly systemic animal skin protection, plus one topical wound study with a dangerous high-dose arm (Tier 2) → strong oral/IV human drug data for nerves (Tier 3) → nothing for human topical cosmetic use (Tier 4). The evidence pyramid is impressive at the bottom and for the wrong indication in the middle, and absent where a skincare brand actually needs it.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • MW 174 is favorable (well under the ~500 Da rule-of-thumb), and amphiphilicity helps cross both lipid and aqueous compartments — on paper a good topical candidate.
  • But the stability problem dominates delivery. The active anion is the reactive species and the unstable one; in water near skin pH it auto-oxidizes and precipitates as colored trimers, so a simple aqueous serum would degrade and discolor. Every credible topical demonstration (Fan 2019) used encapsulation (polymeric nanoparticles) + a protective hydrogel matrix to deliver intact, controlled-release edaravone. [4][9]
  • No published human or ex-vivo human-skin permeation data for cosmetic edaravone were found. So even bioavailability is inferred, not measured for skin.
  • Implication: edaravone is deliverable only inside a stabilized, encapsulated, controlled-release system — a formulation-cost ingredient, like idebenone, where the carrier is the real product.

6. Formulation

  • Concentration: No established cosmetic use level (no cosmetic precedent). Drug solutions are 0.15% (1.5 mg/mL IV). The Fan 2019 lesson — low dose helps, high dose harms — means any topical use would need careful dose-finding, not maximization.
  • pH: the tension is structural. Antioxidant activity needs the anion (pH ≥ ~7), but stability needs the un-ionized keto form (acidic pH ~2–5). You cannot simultaneously maximize potency and stability in a simple aqueous base — a fundamental formulation conflict. [9]
  • Stability: Poor in water/air. Oxidizes and precipitates colored trimers at neutral/alkaline pH; commercial product is kept acidic, oxygen-purged, and stabilized with sodium bisulfite (and/or cysteine/glutathione, L-arginine). [9][12] A cosmetic would need encapsulation + airless, opaque packaging + an antioxidant/chelator stabilizer system + likely anhydrous or low-water base.
  • Color/sensory: prone to discoloration on degradation (the trimers are colored); pure material is a white-to-pale crystalline powder, freely soluble in ethanol, poorly in cold water. [1][9]
  • Pairings (theoretical): reducing co-antioxidants/stabilizers (glutathione, cysteine, sulfite, sodium metabisulfite), chelators (EDTA), and a tight pH/air-exclusion strategy. Logically complementary to lipophilic antioxidants (vitamin E) given amphiphilicity — but unproven in cosmetics.
  • Conflicts: neutral-to-alkaline, oxygen-exposed, hot, or unbuffered aqueous systems destroy it. Bisulfite stabilizer conflicts with formulators avoiding sulfites for allergy reasons (see §7).

7. Safety

  • Drug-grade safety dossier exists, but for IV/oral, with sulfite caveats. The biggest documented red flag relevant to topical use is the sodium bisulfite in the formulation: the Radicava label warns of sulfite hypersensitivity → allergic/anaphylactic reactions, especially in asthmatics. Bisulfite/metabisulfite are recognized contact sensitizers in topical products. [10][12]
  • Hypersensitivity: post-marketing reports of urticaria, erythema multiforme, anaphylaxis (systemic drug). Dermatitis/eczema were among adverse reactions in trials. [10]
  • Irritation/sensitization (topical, cosmetic): unknown — no cosmetic patch-test/HRIPT data exist because it is not a cosmetic ingredient. This is an unquantified risk, not a clean record.
  • Sun: no evidence of photosensitization; it is not a sunscreen and any "UV protection" framing is unsupported for topical edaravone.
  • Pregnancy/lactation: as a drug, used with caution; no cosmetic-exposure safety data. Default to avoid.
  • Renal: IV drug is contraindicated in severe renal impairment in Japan — a systemic concern, but a reminder of its drug profile.
  • Sourcing: wholly synthetic API (no animal/plant provenance issue); main quality risk is oxidative degradation/impurities in the supply chain (trimers, OPB), not ethics.

8. Regulatory & claims

  • This is the decisive section. Edaravone is a prescription DRUG (ALS; stroke) in the US, Japan, EU-adjacent markets and China. It has no cosmetic INCI and no cosmetic regulatory history. Putting a recognized systemic API into a "cosmetic" almost certainly makes the product an unapproved new drug in the US (FDA) and would fail EU cosmetic notification — this is a product-classification problem, not a copy-wording nuance. [10][11][13]
  • ✅ Defensible cosmetic language — only if a compliant cosmetic-grade derivative/ingredient existed, which it does not for edaravone itself: "antioxidant," "helps defend the look of skin against oxidative/environmental stress," "for visibly smoother, more radiant-looking skin." None of this is currently usable because the ingredient is not cosmetic-legal.
  • ⚠️ Off-limits / drug-territory or unsubstantiated: anything invoking the drug ("ALS antioxidant," "the stroke antioxidant," "neuroprotective"), "repairs," "boosts collagen," "reverses photoaging," "clinically proven antioxidant" (the trials are for nerves), "most powerful antioxidant" (ORAC is a test-tube metric). Using edaravone at all risks the whole product being deemed a drug.
  • See ../CLAUDE.md guardrails and ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Mid raw-material cost as an API (mature, off-patent, many generic manufacturers since the ALS/stroke generics era; simple small molecule). The expense is not the powder — it is (a) the stabilization/encapsulation/airless packaging needed to keep it intact and uncolored, and (b) the regulatory exposure of using a drug substance cosmetically. Cost-wise the molecule is cheap; usable topical edaravone is expensive and legally fraught.
  • Cosmetic supply chain: effectively none. You would be sourcing pharma API and improvising cosmetic use — the opposite of a clean, INCI-listed, audited cosmetic raw material.

10. Hype vs. evidence

Likely marketing claim Reality
"Clinically proven antioxidant (FDA-approved!)." Approved as an IV/oral drug for ALS and stroke — nerves, not skin; injected/swallowed, not applied. Zero human topical cosmetic trials. [10][11]
"More powerful than vitamin C/E — ORAC 5.6× Trolox." A real test-tube ORAC figure; ORAC does not predict topical clinical benefit, and more scavenging power can backfire (see below). [3]
"The more antioxidant, the better." False here. Fan 2019: high-dose topical edaravone slowed wound healing ~30% by over-suppressing signaling ROS. Dose is a knife-edge. [4]
"Penetrates well (tiny molecule)." MW favors it, but it degrades/precipitates before it can work unless encapsulated; no human-skin permeation data. [9]
"Targets cellular senescence / longevity." Only in cultured non-skin cells (PC12, AGS); not skin, not topical, not human. [15][16]
"Brightens / fades dark spots." Only edaravone derivatives show (in-silico/in-vitro) tyrosinase interaction — not edaravone, not in people. [5][6]
"Safe antioxidant." Carries a sulfite-hypersensitivity warning via its bisulfite stabilizer; no cosmetic irritation/sensitization data. [10][12]

11. Verdict for the brand

❌ SKIP edaravone as a named cosmetic active (current form). ⚠️ Conditional interest in purpose-built derivatives only.

One line: Edaravone has better antioxidant chemistry than most of the cabinet and a genuine drug pedigree, but it is a prescription drug with no cosmetic INCI, no human topical evidence, a U-shaped dose-response that punishes over-use, serious stability/discoloration problems, and a sulfite-allergy flag — for an evidence-led, claims-disciplined premium brand it is the wrong vehicle for an otherwise interesting molecule.

  • Why skip the drug itself: (1) Regulatory — using a systemic API cosmetically likely makes the product an unapproved drug; this alone is disqualifying. (2) Evidence — Tier-4 (human topical) is empty; the brand's credibility rule is "evidence over hype," and here there is no topical human evidence at all. (3) Formulation — the active form is the unstable form; you fight chemistry to keep it intact and uncolored. (4) Dose risk — a potent scavenger that worsens outcomes when overdosed is a brand-safety hazard, not a hero. (5) Allergen — the bisulfite stabilizer undercuts a "gentle premium" positioning.
  • Where it's genuinely interesting (the watch-item): the mechanism (fast multi-radical scavenging + Nrf2/HO-1 induction + emerging anti-senescence signals) maps cleanly onto the brand's oxidative-stress, inflammaging, mitochondrial-decline and senescence longevity hallmarks. If the brand wants this chemistry, the credible path is the edaravone-derivative cosmeceutical route now in academic development (Mohan 2024, Kulkarni 2025) [5][6] — molecules engineered for cosmetic stability, a real INCI, and (eventually) skin data. Track that, don't formulate the drug.
  • Better-evidenced alternatives already in our cabinet that deliver the same "potent antioxidant + Nrf2/longevity" story with cosmetic-legal status and far better human/topical data: ergothioneine, vitamin C (THD/ascorbyl derivatives) [see ascorbyl-tocopheryl-phosphate.md], ferulic acid [see ferulic-acid.md], and idebenone [see idebenone.md] (itself only a conditional supporting actor). Edaravone offers romance, not evidentiary advantage.
  • Longevity note: conceptually edaravone is a poster-child "geroprotective antioxidant" (it is literally used to fight a neurodegenerative disease) — which is exactly why it is tempting and exactly why claims discipline matters. The longevity story is real at the bench; the topical-skin substantiation is not. Log in ../../ideas/not-considering.md as "skip the drug; watch derivatives," with the regulatory drug-status reason recorded.

(On the A–D rubric: A = strong human-topical RCT; B = moderate human-topical; C = weak/indirect; D = unproven topically. Edaravone sits at D for cosmetic anti-aging — the human-topical rung is empty. The earlier pass scored this "F"; on this scale that is the D floor. The drug identity would rate A/B, but only for oral/IV neurology, never for a cream.)

  • F for the cosmetic claim that matters: there is no human topical trial of edaravone for any skin-aging endpoint. The skin-aging "evidence" is in-vitro chemistry, non-skin cell senescence, and animal studies — the animal skin work being mostly systemic injection.
  • The molecule itself earns a high grade as an antioxidant drug (Phase-3 ALS RCT, multiple stroke RCTs, well-characterized radical chemistry) — but that grade belongs to oral/IV neurology, not creams, and must not be transferred.

Citation audit — all real, accurately represented

  • Identity (CAS 89-25-8, C₁₀H₁₀N₂O, MW 174.2, MCI-186, pKa ≈ 7, keto-enol tautomerism): confirmed [1][11].
  • Radical kinetics / ORAC (~5–7×10¹⁰ M⁻¹s⁻¹ for •OH; ORAC ≈ 5.65 Trolox eq): confirmed [2][3].
  • Fan 2019 (PMID 31083588): confirmed — topical alginate hydrogel, diabetic mouse, low dose helps / high dose worsens healing ~30%, n=4/group. The U-shaped dose-response is real and load-bearing [4].
  • Mohan 2024 (PMID 39334807): confirmed — derivatives, DPPH/ABTS/H₂O₂ + in-silico/docking tyrosinase; explicitly no cellular melanogenesis assay — so the "brightening" angle is computational/derivative only [5].
  • Zhang 2017 (PMID 28224430) & Bi 2023 (iwj.14184): confirmed systemic (IP) dosing, not topical — correctly flagged so the flap data is not mis-sold as topical evidence [7][8].
  • Senescence: Malekinejad 2025 (PMID 41254185, PC12) and Rahimifard 2026 (PMID 39995134, AGS): confirmed in-vitro, non-skin [15][16].
  • Drug status / FDA label / bisulfite warning (Radicava 209176lbl): confirmed, including sulfite-hypersensitivity language [10].
  • Stability (anion auto-oxidation, colored trimer precipitation > pH 7, acidic/anaerobic + bisulfite/glutathione stabilization): confirmed [9].
  • No cosmetic INCI: confirmed by absence — edaravone does not appear as a cosmetic ingredient; it is consistently an API [11][13].

Main caveats a marketer must not forget

  1. It's a drug. Cosmetic use of a systemic API is a product-classification (drug) risk, the single biggest blocker.
  2. No human topical evidence exists. "Clinically proven" refers to ALS/stroke, oral/IV — never to skin.
  3. Potency can backfire (Fan 2019 high-dose harm). An antioxidant where overdosing worsens outcomes is not a "more is better" hero.
  4. Stability/discoloration and sulfite-allergy are concrete, label-backed problems, not theoretical.
  5. "Brightening / anti-senescence / longevity" rests on derivatives or non-skin cells, not topical edaravone in humans.

Net

The dossier's "skip the drug, watch the derivatives" verdict is the accurate, honest call. Edaravone is a scientifically excellent antioxidant in the wrong format and regulatory class for this brand. Mechanism real; topical cosmetic substantiation absent; not a hero, not even a safe supporting actor in its current form.
