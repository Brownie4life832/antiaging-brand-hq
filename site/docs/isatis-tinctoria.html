Ingredient: Isatis tinctoria (Woad) extract (INCI: Isatis Tinctoria Leaf Extract)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Senotherapeutics & senolytics (botanical senolytic/senostatic; also a documented topical anti-inflammatory)

One-line honest summary: Woad is one of the very few botanical actives with direct, skin-cell senolytic data — a 70% ethanol leaf extract selectively killed senescent human dermal/foreskin fibroblasts (comparable to the reference senolytic ABT-737 on cell viability, in vitro) and shut down their SASP (IL-6, IL-8) in vitro. It is also unusually well-pedigreed as a topical anti-inflammatory (a real 20-volunteer human study + decades of Hamburger-group mechanism work). The catch: the senolytic/anti-aging evidence is 100% in-vitro cell models — and both of those pillar studies come from a single commercial developer (BioSpectrum / Shinsegae), not an independent lab; the one human topical trial tested anti-irritant (not anti-aging) endpoints and had mixed results; and "extract" is a moving target — composition, plant part, and solvent change everything. A strong, genuinely-differentiated supporting ingredient with a real mechanistic story, not yet a proof-backed hero.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Woad; dyer's woad; pastel des teinturiers (FR); in TCM the leaf is Dà Qīng Yè and the root is Bǎn Lán Gēn / Radix Isatidis (Isatidis radix).
  • INCI (cosmetic): Isatis Tinctoria Leaf Extract (the senescence research material). Other registered INCI forms: Isatis Tinctoria Leaf Powder, Isatis Tinctoria Root Extract / Root Powder, Isatis Tinctoria Seed Oil, Isatis Tinctoria Seed Extract. INCI function listed as skin conditioning. [9][10]
  • Botany: Isatis tinctoria L., family Brassicaceae (same family as broccoli/mustard — relevant to its glucosinolate chemistry and goitrogen caveat). Ancient European/Chinese dye plant (source of natural indigo before Indigofera). [4]
  • Plant part matters enormously. The skin-aging/senescence studies all used leaf extract. The pharmacopeial antiviral material (Ban Lan Gen) is the root, with different chemistry. Seed oil is a separate emollient ingredient. Do not treat these as interchangeable. [4]
  • Key bioactive constituents (leaf):
  • Alkaloids: tryptanthrin (the marquee dual COX-2/5-LOX inhibitor), indirubin, indigo, indoxyl, isatin, indolin-2-one (anti-mast-cell). [4][6]
  • Glucosinolates: glucobrassicin, neoglucobrassicin, sinigrin-type, sulfoglucobrassicin (Brassicaceae signature). [4]
  • Flavonoids: quercetin, rutin, isovitexin, vicenin-2, apigenin glucosides, linarin. [4][2]
  • Phenolic acids: chlorogenic, caffeic, ferulic, sinapic, 4-hydroxybenzoic, anthranilic, kynurenic acids. [2]
  • Fatty acid: α-linolenic acid — identified as a major lipophilic anti-inflammatory principle (often more than tryptanthrin in vivo). [7][8]
  • Carotenoids (β-carotene, lutein), polysaccharides.
  • Solvent forms in the literature: 70% ethanol leaf extract (the senescence work), lipophilic dichloromethane (DCM) and supercritical CO₂ (SFE) extracts (the anti-inflammatory pharmacology), and petroleum-ether fraction (the IL-6/IL-33 mast-cell work). These behave differently — a "woad extract" claim is meaningless without the solvent/part/spec.

2. Class & mechanism

Woad sits in the senotherapeutic class with two distinct modes, plus a separate (older, better-characterised) anti-inflammatory mode:

(A) Senolytic — selectively kills senescent cells. In senescent human dermal/foreskin fibroblasts, 70% ethanol woad extract triggered caspase-3-dependent apoptosis (↑ cleaved caspase-3, ↑ cleaved PARP; BAX unchanged), preferentially in senescent cells while sparing young/proliferating cells. Its profile was comparable to the BCL-2/BCL-xL inhibitor senolytic ABT-737. [1]

(B) Senostatic / SASP-suppressing — quiets the senescent secretome. In replicatively senescent dermal fibroblasts, woad leaf extract (ITE) lowered the SASP by acting upstream on mTOR → MAPK (JNK/ERK) → IκB/NF-κB, blocking NF-κB nuclear translocation, and restoring SIRT1 (which falls in senescence). Net result: large drops in IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α, IL-1α, and MMP-1, plus reduced senescence markers (SA-β-gal, p53, p21, p16). [2]

(C) Classic anti-inflammatory (the older Hamburger-group story). Lipophilic extracts inhibit COX-2 (preferentially over COX-1) and 5-LOX, suppress iNOS, and block IgE-mediated mast-cell degranulation; they reduce IL-6 and IL-33/ST2 signalling in keratinocyte and contact-hypersensitivity models. Tryptanthrin is a COX-2/5-LOX inhibitor, but in whole-animal models the activity tracks more with α-linolenic acid and other matrix components than with tryptanthrin alone. [6][7][8]

Honest mechanistic read: (A) and (B) are real, reproducible, and directly in human skin fibroblasts — that is rare and valuable for a botanical. (C) is the most mature pharmacology but is about inflammation/irritation, not aging per se. The active compound(s) for the senolytic effect are not identified — the authors explicitly say so. [1]

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

The single most important honesty point: Woad's anti-aging/senolytic case is entirely in-vitro (human fibroblast cultures). Its anti-inflammatory case is much deeper and even includes a real human topical trial — but that trial measured irritation/erythema, not aging, and its results were mixed-to-negative (helped induced irritant dermatitis only during the induction phase; did nothing for UVB erythema). So: strong cell-level senescence biology + a credible topical-delivery/anti-irritant track record, but zero human topical anti-aging outcome data.

Tier 1 — In vitro (cell models) — this is where the anti-aging case lives

  • Kim E.J., Woo J., Shin S. et al. (2022), Animal Cells and Systems 26(6):310–317 (DOI 10.1080/19768354.2022.2143895; PMID 36605596). Focused screen of 6 natural compounds/extracts for senolytic activity. Woad (70% EtOH, 80 °C) was the only one with senolytic activity. In bleomycin-induced and replicatively senescent human dermal (HDF), foreskin (BJ) fibroblasts it selectively killed senescent cells (caspase-3/PARP cleavage; BAX unchanged) "comparable to ABT-737, with no discernible toxicity on young cells" at 500 ppm (marginal at 200 ppm). It also acted senostatically, suppressing IL-6/IL-1A at the protein level (post-transcriptionally). Limitations: in-vitro only; effect was cell-type-specific — robust in skin fibroblasts (HDF/BJ) but did not work in lung IMR90; active compound(s) unidentified; bleomycin senescence ≠ UV/photoaging; n = 3 replicate design; the authors themselves call for in-vivo work. Conflict of interest (verified 2026-06-19): two authors are employed by Biospectrum and the study was funded by Shinsegae International Inc. — i.e. this senolytic finding comes from the ingredient's own commercial developer, not an independent lab. [1]

  • Woo J., Shin S., Ji H. et al. (2022), Nutrients 14(9):1979 (DOI 10.3390/nu14091979; PMID 35565945). Woad leaf extract (ITE, 70% EtOH) on replicatively senescent HDFs (passage 40 vs young P8–10), effective dose 200 µg/mL. Results: SA-β-gal⁺ cells −40.7%; p53 0.30-fold, p21 0.58-fold, p16 0.45-fold; IL-6 ↓>70% (baseline ~40× elevated), IL-8 ↓>70% (baseline ~600× elevated), TNF-α & IL-1α ↓, MMP-1 ↓. Mechanism: ↓ mTOR/S6 phosphorylation, ↓ JNK/ERK, ↓ IκB phosphorylation, ↓ NF-κB nuclear translocation, ↑ SIRT1. LC-MS/MS identified 4-hydroxybenzoic acid, anthranilic acid, kynurenic acid, linarin as candidate actives. Limitations: in-vitro only; single highlighted dose; triplicate assays; no penetration/finished-formula data; extract composition variability not addressed. Conflict of interest (verified 2026-06-19): all authors are employees of BioSpectrum / Shinsegae International — the same commercial group as ref [1]. The paper declares "no external funding," but it is industry-internal R&D, not independent replication. The two pillar studies behind woad's entire anti-aging case therefore share one commercial sponsor. [2]

  • Foundational COX-2 / 5-LOX activity profiling (Hamburger group): Oberthür/Danz et al. used HPLC activity profiling to pin tryptanthrin as the principal COX-2 inhibitor in leaf extract, but found the major 5-LOX inhibitory activity tracked to α-linolenic acid, not tryptanthrin. [7] Earlier work isolated/quantified tryptanthrin as a dual COX-2/5-LOX inhibitor. [6]

  • Lotts T., Kabrodt K., Hummel J. et al. (2020), Acta Dermato-Venereologica (DOI 10.2340/00015555-3476). Petroleum-ether woad extract in HaCaT/primary keratinocytes + LAD2 mast cells: ↓ IL-6 (Poly I:C-stimulated), ↓ IFN-γ-induced IL-33, ~30–40% inhibition of mast-cell degranulation at 75 µg/mL. (Also has an in-vivo arm — Tier 2.) In-vitro arm is mechanistic; human studies absent. [8]

Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

  • Recio M.C., Cerdá-Nicolás M., Potterat O., Hamburger M., Ríos J.L. (2006), Planta Medica 72(6):539–546 (DOI 10.1055/s-2006-947177; PMID 16773538). Lipophilic woad extracts in mouse models: carrageenan-induced paw oedema ED₅₀ ≈ 78 mg/kg (CO₂/SFE extract) and ≈165 mg/kg (DCM); activity in TPA ear-oedema (oral and topical) and anti-allergic models. Pure tryptanthrin showed no significant in-vivo anti-inflammatory effect, confirming the activity is a matrix property, not a single molecule. [11]
  • Lotts 2020 (in-vivo arm): In a DNFB contact-hypersensitivity mouse model, petroleum-ether extract ↓ IL-6 and ↓ IL-33/ST2 in lesional skin (ear-edema model). Acute irritated-skin model, not chronic aging skin. [8]
  • No animal study tests woad against a skin-aging endpoint (photoaging, dermal thinning, wrinkle). The in-vivo data is all anti-inflammatory/anti-allergic.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL

  • TCM uses the root (Ban Lan Gen) orally as an antiviral; indirubin has oral oncology history (CML). None of this is skin-aging relevant, and it is the root, not the leaf. No oral human data bears on topical skin longevity. [4]

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters — thin, and not about aging)

  • Heinemann C., Schliemann-Willers S., Oberthür C., Hamburger M., Elsner P. (2004), Planta Medica 70(5):385–390 (PMID 15124080). Randomized, single-blind, within-subject, n = 20 healthy volunteers. Three lipophilic woad extracts + pure tryptanthrin applied topically in two models:
  • SLS-induced irritant contact dermatitis: treatment during the eliciting/induction phase gave a significantly smaller increase in visual score and TEWL vs untreated field. Tryptanthrin alone trended better but not statistically significant. Treatment after the eliciting phase did not speed resolution.
  • UVB-induced erythema: no anti-inflammatory effect observed for any test substance.
  • This is the only controlled human topical study. It validates anti-irritant/barrier-protective behaviour (timing-dependent) and skin penetration in vivo, but it is not an anti-aging study, n is small, single-blind, and the UVB/photo endpoint was a clear negative. [3]
  • No human topical study has tested woad for senescence, wrinkles, elasticity, firmness, or any anti-aging endpoint. Every anti-aging claim today is borrowed from Tier-1 cell data.

Bottom line by tier: Senolytic + SASP-suppression are proven in human skin fibroblast cultures (genuinely notable for a botanical) and one is comparable to a pharmaceutical senolytic. Anti-inflammatory activity is proven in vitro, in animals, and in one small human topical irritant-dermatitis trial. No human topical anti-aging outcome exists, and the one photo/UV human endpoint was negative.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Tryptanthrin penetrates skin — but only as part of the extract. Dermal microdialysis on ex-vivo pig skin (Oberthür et al., 2003, Planta Medica, PMID 12802716) found a dose-dependent rise in dermal tryptanthrin for the Isatis extract but NOT for pure tryptanthrin — the pure compound crystallised out of vehicle, whereas the extract kept it amorphous and bioavailable. The human SLS study independently confirmed in-vivo activity (so something does get in). [12][3]
  • Strong formulation implication: the whole-extract matrix is the delivery system. Buying "pure tryptanthrin" or over-purifying defeats penetration. This also means efficacy is hostage to extract composition — standardize and don't strip the matrix.
  • Caveats: the senolytic actives are unidentified, so we cannot say what needs to penetrate to reproduce the Tier-1 senolysis in living skin. In-vitro cell-culture doses (200–500 ppm / 200 µg/mL in medium) are not formulation guidance and say nothing about reaching viable dermis at depth. No human study has measured woad's senolytic actives in living dermis.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: No validated topical use level for skin-aging. In-vitro actives were 200–500 ppm (senolytic screen) and 200 µg/mL (senostatic, in culture medium) — culture doses, not formula doses. Commercial cosmetic use is typically low (often fractional-percent of a standardized extract). Formulate conservatively; do not imply a validated dose-response.
  • pH / stability: Polyphenol/alkaloid-rich plant extract — keep within a normal skincare window (~pH 4.5–6.5); avoid extremes that degrade flavonoids/phenolics. Indole pigments (indigo/indirubin) and the phenolics are light/oxidation-sensitive and colored — expect blue/green/brown tint and potential color drift; use opaque/airless packaging, antioxidants/chelators, and run real-time + accelerated stability and a preservative challenge.
  • Color is a real formulation constraint. Woad is literally an indigo dye plant — leaf extracts can impart color and could stain in higher loads. This caps usable concentration on aesthetic grounds and rules it out as a "brightening/even-tone" actor.
  • Pairings (conceptual, science-forward):
  • Other senotherapeutics for a layered senescence story — fisetin, quercetin, OS-01/Peptide-14, GHK-Cu (see master index §5). Woad is the botanical member of that family.
  • Niacinamide / ceramides (barrier thesis) — complements the anti-irritant/TEWL data.
  • Antioxidants (vitamin C/E, resveratrol) to support the modest oxidative-stress angle and protect the extract.
  • Conflicts: strong oxidizers/very low-pH actives may degrade phenolics/indoles and worsen color drift. Glucosinolate-rich material can carry faint "green/cabbage" Brassicaceae notes — masking/fragrance may be needed depending on grade.
  • Sensory: depends heavily on grade; expect color contribution and possible herbaceous note at higher loads.

7. Safety

  • Irritation: No specific topical irritation signal at cosmetic levels; in the human SLS model it was anti-irritant during induction (lower TEWL/visual score). Still, generate our own patch-test/HRIPT on the finished formula — no published cosmetic HRIPT found. [3]
  • Sensitization / allergen: Brassicaceae plant with reactive glucosinolate breakdown products (isothiocyanates) — theoretical contact-sensitization consideration; no strong published cosmetic allergy signal, but verify on the specific grade. [4]
  • Sun: No photosensitization flag; conversely it offered no UVB-erythema protection — do not position it as photoprotective. [3]
  • Goitrogen caveat (sourcing/spec): glucosinolates in Brassicaceae can be goitrogenic at high oral intake. Negligible concern for topical cosmetic use, but it is a reason to (a) prefer leaf extract over crude high-glucosinolate fractions and (b) keep messaging topical-only. [4]
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: No topical-specific data. TCM root preparations carry traditional cautions; default to conservative copy ("consult your doctor"). [4]
  • Tolerability context: centuries of use as a dye/medicinal plant; oral root use noted occasional GI upset (nausea) — not relevant to topical but worth knowing. [4]

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic status: Isatis Tinctoria Leaf Extract is an established cosmetic INCI listed in EU CosIng with function skin conditioning; leaf powder, root extract/powder, and seed oil are also registered. Confirm status per target market before formulating. [9][10]
  • The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame): This is the danger zone. "Senolytic," "kills zombie/senescent cells," "clears senescent cells from your skin," "anti-inflammaging," "reduces SASP" all read as structure/function (drug) claims — and they're sourced from cell-culture data, so they're doubly unsafe. The COX-2/5-LOX and "anti-inflammatory/treats dermatitis" language is overtly drug-like (woad has even been pursued as an anti-inflammatory phytopharmaceutical) — avoid it in cosmetic copy.
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "a botanical inspired by senescence/skin-longevity science," "derived from woad, a plant studied for its effect on aging skin cells," "helps support the look of calmer, more resilient skin," "helps improve the appearance of skin stressed by environmental aggressors." Even these should be hedged given no human anti-aging data.
  • ⚠️ Risky / needs substantiation or legal review: "senolytic," "removes senescent cells," "reduces inflammation/SASP," "as effective as [a senolytic drug]," "anti-inflammatory," "treats/soothes dermatitis," "boosts collagen," any % claim. None are supported for topical human anti-aging use.
  • Substantiation reality: the published cell + small human-irritation data won't substantiate an anti-aging performance claim. Real claims need our own finished-formula human testing. See ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Forms/suppliers: Cosmetic-grade standardized leaf extracts (often the senescence-research material, e.g. work out of Biospectrum/IGC in Korea), plus European woad (cultivated for natural-indigo dye revival in France/Germany/UK — a genuine sustainability/heritage angle). Seed oil and root extract are separate supply chains.
  • Cost tier: Low–mid as a botanical extract; rises for standardized/characterised grades (e.g. tryptanthrin- or marker-standardized) — which you want, because efficacy is matrix-dependent.
  • Standardization risk (the big one): "Isatis tinctoria extract" spans different parts (leaf/root/seed), solvents (EtOH/DCM/CO₂/pet-ether), strains, harvest timing, and post-harvest treatment — and tryptanthrin content varies widely by strain and processing. Require a COA specifying part, solvent, and quantified markers (e.g. tryptanthrin); insist on the leaf, hydro-ethanolic type that matches the senescence literature if that's the story you're telling. [4][13]
  • Heritage/sustainability narrative: woad is the original European indigo and a low-input Brassicaceae crop — a credible "ancient plant, modern longevity science" story (which, per CLAUDE.md, is a brand option, not a chosen metaphor).

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • "Botanical senolytic that clears zombie cells from your skin." The senolysis is real but in a dish — senescent human fibroblast cultures, not living skin. No human (or even animal) study shows woad clearing senescent cells in vivo. [1]
  • "As effective as pharmaceutical senolytics." The ABT-737 comparison is accurate in vitro — and worth knowing — but it's a cell-viability comparison, not a clinical one. [1]
  • "Reduces inflammation / SASP in your skin." The SASP/cytokine drops are cell-culture (and mouse) data; the only human topical trial measured induced irritation, helped only during induction, and failed on UVB. [2][3]
  • "It's the active tryptanthrin." Misleading: pure tryptanthrin doesn't penetrate well and showed no in-vivo anti-inflammatory effect — the whole-extract matrix (incl. α-linolenic acid) carries activity and delivery. Over-purifying breaks it. [11][12]
  • "Anti-aging, brightening botanical." No pigment/tone data, and it's a dye plant (color/staining risk), so brightening is the wrong angle entirely.
  • Cross-format/part confusion. Marketers conflate the antiviral root (Ban Lan Gen) and the emollient seed oil with the senescence-active leaf extract. They are different ingredients.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ SUPPORTING — a genuinely differentiated botanical senotherapeutic with a real mechanistic story, but no human topical anti-aging proof (yet).

One-line: Woad is arguably the single most credible botanical in the senolytic conversation — it has direct, selective senolytic + SASP-suppressing data in human skin fibroblasts (rare for a plant) and a real topical-delivery/anti-irritant track record — so it's an excellent science-forward supporting active and education/differentiation asset, but its anti-aging evidence is in-vitro and its one human topical trial was about irritation (mixed) and photoaging (negative), so it must carry strictly honest, mechanism-framed copy, not proof-backed performance claims.

Why it beats most botanicals here: unlike fisetin/quercetin (whose senolytic fame is mostly oral/systemic) or spermidine (oral/animal autophagy), woad's headline senotherapeutic data was generated in human dermal/foreskin fibroblasts directly, with a built-in selectivity control (spared young cells; failed in lung cells — i.e., skin-tropic). That makes it a strong botanical complement to a peptide-led senolytic (OS-01/Peptide-14) in a layered "senescence" narrative.

Longevity-relevance note: Cellular senescence is a core hallmark of aging and a legitimate skin-longevity target; a botanical that selectively removes senescent skin cells and dampens their SASP fits a skin-longevity thesis exactly. The honest gaps are (1) route/format: cell-dish → living human skin, (2) identity: senolytic active(s) unknown, so we can't standardize to them, and (3) outcomes: zero human anti-aging endpoints, plus a negative UVB result. If we want real topical anti-aging claims, the path is our own finished-formula human study on a defined leaf/hydro-ethanolic extract — not the existing literature.

Conditions if we use it: (a) leaf, hydro-ethanolic extract matching the senescence literature, COA with quantified markers; (b) keep the whole-extract matrix (don't buy isolated tryptanthrin); (c) opaque/airless packaging + antioxidants for color/oxidation stability; (d) frame as "inspired by senescence science / supports calmer, more resilient-looking skin" — never "senolytic," "anti-inflammatory," or "clears senescent cells" in consumer copy without legal review and our own data.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Independent re-check performed 2026-06-19. Each headline claim was tested against three questions: (a) is the evidence HUMAN and TOPICAL, or borrowed from in-vitro / animal / oral? (b) are the cited studies real and do they say what's claimed? (c) are they industry-funded or tiny?

Overall grade: C (weak / indirect for topical anti-aging)

Woad has a genuinely interesting mechanistic story and — unusually for a botanical — direct senolytic/senostatic data generated in human skin fibroblasts, which lifts it above the typical "in-vitro botanical." But for the specific claim that matters to this brand (topical, human, anti-aging benefit) the evidence is indirect: zero human topical anti-aging outcomes, the only human topical trial measured irritation (mixed) and UVB erythema (negative), and the two studies carrying the entire anti-aging case are in-vitro and authored/funded by the ingredient's own commercial developer. It is not a D (the cell biology is real, reproducible-looking, selective, and human-skin-derived), but it is well short of B (which would need independent human topical data). C is the defensible grade.

  1. Undisclosed sponsor conflict (the biggest one).   3. "ABT-737-comparable" softened to specify it is a cell-viability comparison in vitro (the dossier already said this in §10; now consistent in the summary too).

Main caveats to carry into any copy or formulation decision

  • Route mismatch: every anti-aging data point is cell-culture; every human data point is anti-irritant. There is no human topical anti-aging evidence, and the one human photo-endpoint (UVB) was negative.
  • Single-sponsor risk: the senolytic/SASP story is not independently replicated and comes from the party that sells the ingredient.
  • Unidentified active: you cannot standardize to the senolytic compound because no one knows what it is — so even the in-vitro effect may not transfer to a different lot/spec.
  • Dye-plant constraint (color/staining) and part/solvent confusion (leaf vs root vs seed) remain real and are well-handled in the dossier.

Claims to soften / never make in consumer copy

"Senolytic," "clears/removes senescent cells from your skin," "as effective as a senolytic drug," "reduces inflammation/SASP," "anti-inflammatory," "treats dermatitis," any % figure, and any anti-aging performance claim. All are sourced from in-vitro (developer-funded) or animal/irritation data, not human topical anti-aging — and several are structure/function (drug) language. The dossier's §8 already states this; this verification confirms it and adds the sponsor-independence caveat as a further reason for restraint.
