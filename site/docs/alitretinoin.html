Ingredient: Alitretinoin / 9-cis-Retinoic Acid (INCI: none — prescription DRUG, no cosmetic INCI)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ❌ rejected for the brand's own products (prescription DRUG, not a cosmetic ingredient — no INCI, no legal cosmetic use) — kept as a benchmark/education entry in the retinoid class Slug: alitretinoin · Class: Retinoids & retinoid alternatives

Read-this-first framing. Alitretinoin is 9-cis-retinoic acid — a true retinoic ACID, in the same drug tier as tretinoin and isotretinoin, not in the cosmetic tier with retinol/retinal/retinyl esters/bakuchiol. It exists on the market in exactly two forms, both prescription drugs: Panretin (alitretinoin gel 0.1%, for AIDS-related cutaneous Kaposi sarcoma) and Toctino (oral alitretinoin 10/30 mg, for severe chronic hand eczema refractory to potent topical steroids). There is no cosmetic-grade alitretinoin, no INCI name, no legal path to put it in a cream. This dossier exists to (1) close the loop on the retinoid taxonomy so no future session mistakes it for a cosmetic option, (2) document the one small open-label topical photoaging pilot that the trade press sometimes cites, and (3) sharpen claims discipline — alitretinoin is the textbook case of "real drug data that a cosmetic brand may NOT borrow." See the gold-standard benchmark tretinoin.md, the class file retinoids.md, and the cosmetic-legal alternatives granactive-retinoid.md / hydroxypinacolone-retinoate.md.

0. The honest punchline (read this first)

  • Alitretinoin is a prescription drug, full stop. It is the active acid form of vitamin A in its 9-cis isomer — biologically as potent (and as regulated) as tretinoin, not a gentle cosmetic precursor.
  • Its unique pharmacology — it is the one retinoid that is a genuine dual RAR + RXR pan-agonist (binds both receptor families at nanomolar affinity) — is scientifically real and interesting. But that pharmacology is exploited in drug indications (Kaposi sarcoma, chronic hand eczema), not anti-aging.
  • For our purposes the only relevant human topical anti-aging data is a single open-label, uncontrolled pilot of 20 people (Baumann 2005), reporting subjective improvement and good tolerability — far below the bar we'd accept, and using a prescription gel we can't sell.
  • The strongest alitretinoin evidence (the BACH phase III, 1,032 patients) is for ORAL alitretinoin in hand eczema — a different molecule format, a different indication, a teratogen requiring a pregnancy-prevention programme. None of it transfers to a cosmetic.
  • Net for the brand: SKIP. Not because the science is weak — because it is a drug, with no INCI, no cosmetic legality, real teratogenicity, and (for the topical gel) it was even withdrawn in the EU in 2021. We treat it the way we treat tretinoin: as a benchmark and a claims-discipline lesson, never as an ingredient.

Relationship to other retinoids A geometric isomer of tretinoin (all-trans RA). The vitamin-A acids are positional isomers: all-trans (tretinoin), 13-cis (isotretinoin/Accutane), 9-cis (alitretinoin). In the body these can interconvert (alitretinoin partly isomerizes to tretinoin in vivo). It sits at the bottom of the conversion ladder — it is retinoic acid, so unlike retinol/retinal/esters it needs no enzymatic activation.

Why it is grouped with the drugs, not the cosmetics. The cosmetic-legal retinoids (retinol, retinaldehyde, retinyl esters, retinyl retinoate, HPR/granactive, bakuchiol) all sit upstream of retinoic acid and rely on skin enzymes to (slowly, inefficiently) convert them. Alitretinoin skips all of that — it is already the active acid. That directness is exactly why it is potent, irritating, teratogenic, and regulated as a medicine.

2. Class & mechanism

Class: Topical/oral retinoid (retinoic acid isomer). Nuclear-receptor agonist.

The distinctive mechanism — true dual-receptor (pan-agonist) activity. Retinoid signaling runs through two nuclear-receptor families: RARs (α, β, γ) and RXRs (α, β, γ), which act as ligand-activated transcription factors controlling hundreds of genes for proliferation, differentiation, and immune tone.

  • Tretinoin (all-trans RA) binds only RARs at nanomolar affinity.
  • Alitretinoin (9-cis RA) is the one retinoid that binds BOTH RARs and RXRs at nanomolar affinity — it is widely described as the (proposed) endogenous ligand for RXR and a genuine pan-agonist (Kislat et al. 2023 [5]; MedChemExpress monograph [2]). Reported affinities cluster in the low-nM range (e.g., RXRα Kd ~12 nM; RARα Ki ~22 nM; RXRβ Ki ~7 nM; RARβ Kd ~97 nM) [2]. It is ~40× more potent than other RA isomers at activating RXR.
  • Caveat on "endogenous": whether 9-cis RA truly circulates as an endogenous human tissue retinoid has been scientifically controversial for decades; it has been reliably detected mainly in pancreas/specialized tissues, not robustly in skin (JBC 2023 [10]; PMC3003056 [11]). So "your skin's own retinoid" framing is shaky.

What the pan-agonism does mechanistically (relevant to skin): 1. Modulates keratinocyte proliferation/differentiation — the classic retinoid epidermal-normalizing effect (basis of the Kaposi and photoaging signals). 2. Strong immunomodulation — RXR engagement gives alitretinoin a more anti-inflammatory/tolerogenic profile than RAR-only retinoids: it keeps dendritic cells immature (low CD80/CD86), raises IL-10, induces CD73→adenosine, and blunts T-cell activation; in keratinocytes it counteracts inflammation-driven barrier-gene dysregulation and upregulates hyaluronan synthase (Kislat 2023 [5]). This anti-inflammatory edge is why ORAL alitretinoin works in eczema, where RAR-only retinoids do not. 3. Anti-angiogenic / anti-tumor in Kaposi — retinoid signaling suppresses the proliferation of KS spindle cells (basis of Panretin gel). 4. (Theoretical, for photoaging) like all retinoic acids, it can in principle drive procollagen and suppress the UV→AP-1→MMP collagen-degradation cascade — but for alitretinoin specifically this is assumed by class, not demonstrated with the depth tretinoin has.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

Ordered weakest-format-first up to the format that matters for us: in-vitro → animal → human ORAL → human TOPICAL. The cruel irony of alitretinoin: its best evidence is oral (a different format we can't use), and its topical human anti-aging evidence is one uncontrolled pilot. For each tier I flag whether the benefit is proven for topical cosmetic-relevant use (it essentially never is) or borrowed.

TIER 1 — In-vitro / mechanism (do not use for efficacy claims)

  • Dual RAR/RXR receptor binding. 9-cis RA binds RARs and RXRs at nanomolar affinity, unlike all-trans RA (RAR-only) (MedChemExpress monograph + BindingDB [2]). Foundational pharmacology; not efficacy.
  • Immunoregulation of antigen-presenting cells. Kislat A, Oláh P, Kuchner M, et al. Int J Mol Sci 2023;24(11):9654 [5]. Human monocyte-derived dendritic cells + keratinocytes: alitretinoin kept DCs immature (↓CD80/CD86), ↑IL-10, induced functional CD73 (→immunomodulatory adenosine), ↓T-cell activation; in keratinocytes counteracted inflammation-driven barrier-gene dysregulation and ↑hyaluronan synthase. Limitation: isolated cells; explains eczema mechanism, not anti-aging efficacy.
  • Class collagen/MMP mechanism (borrowed from tretinoin). Retinoic acids drive procollagen and suppress MMP-1 via RAR; fibroblast collagen responses are biphasic/concentration-dependent (low-dose ↑, high-dose ↓ in culture) — see the tretinoin dossier's in-vitro section. Not specifically demonstrated for alitretinoin.

TIER 2 — Animal

  • No high-quality, alitretinoin-specific topical photoaging animal model (e.g., hairless-mouse UV) was located in this session. Photoaging animal data in the retinoid literature is dominated by tretinoin/retinol, not 9-cis RA. [Gap — treat any "animal-proven" alitretinoin anti-aging claim as unverified.]

TIER 3 — Human ORAL (the strongest data — but wrong format AND wrong indication)

This is where alitretinoin actually has rigorous trials. None of it is cosmetic; all of it is the systemic drug Toctino for hand eczema, a teratogen. Classic "borrowed format" trap.

  • BACH phase III (the pivotal trial). Ruzicka T, et al. Br J Dermatol 2008. Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, n=1,032 severe chronic hand eczema refractory to potent topical steroids; placebo vs alitretinoin 10 mg vs 30 mg daily up to 24 wk. Results: ~48% of the 30-mg group reached "clear/almost clear" hands vs placebo; dose-dependent across phenotypes; median mTLSS reduction 75% (30 mg) / 56% (10 mg) / 39% (placebo) [7][9]. Level 1b evidence, largest hand-eczema trial to date.
  • Confirmatory phase III (BAL4079). J Am Acad Dermatol 2015 (PMID 25607554) — replicated efficacy/safety of oral alitretinoin in steroid-refractory severe CHE [12].
  • Retreatment / relapse. Responders frequently relapse after stopping; median time to relapse ~5.5–6.2 months; retreatment of relapsers with 30 mg restored response in ~80% vs ~8% placebo [7]. Establishes it as a course-based drug, not a cure.
  • Head-to-head & phototherapy comparators. ALPHA RCT (alitretinoin vs PUVA, NIHR HTA / NCBI Bookshelf NBK607770) and an alitretinoin-vs-azathioprine RCT (PMC9540441, prematurely discontinued) round out the comparative picture [8][13].
  • Skin-relevance for us: essentially zero. This is a systemic prescription drug for a disease, with a mandatory pregnancy-prevention programme. Its existence does not license any cosmetic claim.

TIER 4 — Human TOPICAL

4a. Topical alitretinoin gel 0.1% — Kaposi sarcoma (a DRUG indication, not anti-aging).

Study Design n Dosing Result AEs
Bodsworth NJ, et al., Am J Clin Dermatol 2001;2(2):77–87 [3] Phase III, randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled 134 (62 alitretinoin / 72 vehicle) gel 0.1% BID ×12 wk Response (CR+PR) 37% vs 7%, p=0.00003 application-site rash/irritation ~32%
Walmsley S, et al. (Panretin Gel NA Study Group), J Acquir Immune Defic Syndr 1999/2000 [14] Phase III, vehicle-controlled ~268 gel 0.1% up to QID ×12+ wk Superior to vehicle across CD4 strata local irritation common

  • What this proves: topical 9-cis RA gel has real, vehicle-beating activity against Kaposi sarcoma lesions — an oncologic skin indication — at the cost of frequent local irritation. It does not address wrinkles, tone, or aging, and the product is a prescription antineoplastic.

4b. Topical alitretinoin gel 0.1% — PHOTOAGING (the only cosmetic-adjacent human topical data).

  • Baumann L, et al. "Open-label pilot study of alitretinoin gel 0.1% in the treatment of photoaging." Cutis 2005;76(1):69–73 (PMID 16144296). [4]
  • Design: open-label, uncontrolled pilot. n=20 with photodamaged skin; alitretinoin gel 0.1% applied over a treatment course.
  • Findings: subjective/investigator-reported improvement in photoaging signs and regression of seborrheic keratoses and actinic keratoses; well tolerated.
  • Limitations (severe): no vehicle control, no randomization, no blinding, tiny n, largely subjective endpoints, off-label use of a Kaposi-sarcoma drug. The authors themselves conclude "larger, blinded, controlled trials are needed." Those trials were never done.
  • Bottom line for topical anti-aging: the human topical anti-aging evidence for alitretinoin is one 20-person open-label pilot, two decades old, never replicated, using a prescription gel. This is weaker than the cosmetic-legal alternatives (retinaldehyde, HPR, retinyl retinoate) we already have on the shelf — and unlike them, we couldn't legally sell it.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • As a small (MW 300), lipophilic free acid, alitretinoin penetrates skin readily — Panretin gel achieves therapeutic intra-lesional concentrations for KS, and the photoaging pilot saw epidermal effects. Penetration is not the limiting factor.
  • It partly isomerizes to tretinoin in vivo, so some of any skin effect overlaps with all-trans RA activity.
  • Systemic absorption from the topical gel is low (it's a localized antineoplastic); systemic exposure and teratogenic risk are the dominant concern with the oral form, not the gel — but the gel still carries a pregnancy caution.
  • Practical point: penetration/efficacy is real; the disqualifier is regulatory status and irritation/teratogenicity, not bioavailability.

6. Formulation (academic only — we will NOT formulate this)

  • Approved concentration: topical 0.1% gel (alcohol-based; Panretin). Oral 10/30 mg capsules.
  • pH/stability: like all retinoic acids, light-, air- and oxidation-sensitive; requires opaque, airless packaging and antioxidants. The gel is an alcoholic vehicle (drying/irritating).
  • Pairings/conflicts: as a potent acid retinoid it would stack irritation with AHAs/BHAs, benzoyl peroxide, other retinoids; not a "layer freely" active. DEET interaction warning on the gel label (avoid concomitant DEET-containing insect repellents).
  • Sensory: the gel is associated with stinging, erythema, scaling, and frank dermatitis at the application site (~32% rash in the KS trial [3]) — a clinical, not cosmetic, sensory profile.
  • Reality check: none of this matters for us, because alitretinoin is not a permissible cosmetic ingredient. This section exists only to document why even the drug version is hard to use comfortably.

7. Safety

  • Local irritation (topical gel): frequent — rash/irritation ~32%, plus erythema, scaling, pain, pruritus at the application site [3]. A clinical irritant profile, harsher than cosmetic retinoids.
  • Systemic AEs (oral): the most frequent are headache (often early, can be severe), mucocutaneous dryness/flushing, and dyslipidemia (↑triglycerides, ↑cholesterol); also thyroid changes (↓TSH/T4), and lab monitoring is required [6][16][17]. Typical retinoid systemic toxicity.
  • TERATOGENICITY (the decisive safety fact): alitretinoin is a potent human teratogen. Oral use mandates a Pregnancy Prevention Programme — two effective contraception methods, monthly pregnancy testing, no pregnancy during treatment or for 1 month after. Documented retinoid-embryopathy malformations: CNS (hydrocephalus, microcephaly, cerebellar), craniofacial/cleft palate, ear and eye defects [6][16][17]. Pregnancy is an absolute contraindication. Even the topical gel carries a pregnancy caution.
  • Photosensitivity: retinoids increase sun sensitivity; sun protection required.
  • Sourcing: pharmaceutical drug substance only — there is no cosmetic supply chain to "source" from.

8. Regulatory & claims (per ../../CLAUDE.md guardrails)

This is the heart of the matter. Alitretinoin is a prescription drug in every market, not a cosmetic ingredient.

  • US: Panretin gel 0.1% — FDA-approved drug (since 2 March 1999) for AIDS-related cutaneous Kaposi sarcoma. Prescription only; no OTC/cosmetic status; no INCI.
  • EU: Panretin gel was EMA-authorized (Oct 2000) but the marketing authorization was WITHDRAWN effective 1 Jan 2021 (commercial reasons, at the holder's request) [18][19]. Oral Toctino is a licensed prescription medicine (e.g., UK from Aug 2008) under a pregnancy-prevention programme.
  • Cosmetic line: Using alitretinoin in a cosmetic would (a) lack any INCI/cosmetic-grade material, (b) deliver a pharmacologically active retinoic acid → automatically a drug by structure/function, and (c) involve a known teratogen. There is no compliant route to formulate it.

Claims discipline — what alitretinoin teaches: - ❌ Never imply our products contain or act like alitretinoin / "9-cis retinoic acid" / "prescription-strength retinoid." - ❌ Never borrow alitretinoin's drug data: "clears hand eczema," "treats Kaposi sarcoma," "clinically proven retinoic acid" → all drug claims, none transferable to a cosmetic. - ❌ The photoaging "evidence" is one open-label pilot of a prescription drug — not substantiation for any cosmetic wrinkle claim. - ✅ If alitretinoin appears anywhere in our content, it is education only (e.g., explaining the retinoid family / why we use cosmetic-legal retinoids instead), clearly labeled as a prescription drug, never as something in our formula.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • "9-cis retinoic acid is your skin's own/endogenous retinoid — the next-gen retinoid." — Mechanistically it's a real dual RAR/RXR ligand, but its endogenous status in human skin is scientifically contested [10][11], and it's a prescription drug, not a cosmetic. Overreach.
  • "Clinically proven retinoic acid for aging." — The robust trials (BACH, n=1,032) are oral, for eczema [7]; the topical anti-aging data is one uncontrolled 20-person pilot [4]. The "clinical proof" is borrowed across format and indication.
  • "Gentler/dual-receptor retinoid." — The RXR/anti-inflammatory angle is genuine [5], but the topical gel is markedly irritating (~32% rash [3]) and the molecule is teratogenic — "gentle" is the wrong word.
  • What's NOT hype: alitretinoin is a legitimately effective drug for Kaposi sarcoma (topical) and severe chronic hand eczema (oral). Its dual-receptor pharmacology is real and scientifically distinctive. None of that makes it a cosmetic ingredient.

11. Verdict for the brand

  • ❌ SKIP — closed by category, not by interest. Alitretinoin is a prescription drug with no cosmetic INCI, no cosmetic-grade supply, real teratogenicity, an irritating topical profile, and (for the gel) an EU market withdrawal. There is no legal, safe, or sourceable way to put it in our products. The only human topical anti-aging evidence is a single open-label pilot of 20 people — weaker than several cosmetic-legal actives we already shortlist.
  • Keep it as a benchmark/education entry alongside tretinoin.md: when we explain "why we use a cosmetic retinoid (retinal / HPR / retinyl retinoate) instead of a prescription acid," alitretinoin is part of that family map — and a clean illustration of the cosmetic-vs-drug line we must hold.

One-line reasoning: Real drug, real pharmacology, wrong shelf — alitretinoin belongs to dermatology prescriptions, not to a cosmetic anti-aging line; we cite it to teach the retinoid family and our claims discipline, never to formulate.

Longevity-relevance note: Its most "longevity-flavored" angle is the RXR-driven inflammaging/immune-tolerance mechanism [5], which is genuinely interesting science. But that story is fulfilled, for us, by cosmetic-legal retinoids plus anti-inflammatory actives — not by smuggling a teratogenic prescription acid into a cream. The lesson alitretinoin contributes to the portfolio is disciplinary: the gap between "impressive clinical data" and "data a cosmetic may legally claim."
