Ingredient: Niacin (Nicotinic Acid) & its lipophilic esters — Myristyl Nicotinate & Tocopheryl Nicotinate (INCI: Niacin / Myristyl Nicotinate / Tocopheryl Nicotinate)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Slug: niacin-esters · Class: NAD+ / sirtuin / AMPK / cellular-energy actives

Reading note. This file covers the acid branch of vitamin B3 — niacin (nicotinic acid) and its skin-targeted ester prodrugs, chiefly myristyl nicotinate (MN) and tocopheryl nicotinate. This is a different molecule and a different mechanism from niacinamide / NMN, which are covered in nad-niacinamide-longevity.md. The two B3 branches are constantly conflated in marketing; they should not be. Crucially, the nicotinate-ester branch has something its glamorous NMN cousin does not: actual published human topical RCTs measuring skin NAD, barrier and differentiation endpoints. Read the two files together before any "NAD/cellular-energy" positioning decision.

0. The honest punchline (read this first)

  • Niacin (nicotinic acid) is the acid form of vitamin B3. Applied to skin raw, it is a rubefacient — it triggers a prostaglandin-mediated flush (redness, warmth, tingling) via the GPR109A/HCA2 receptor on keratinocytes. That makes raw nicotinic acid a poor leave-on cosmetic active by itself.
  • The clever fix is an ester prodrug: bolt nicotinic acid onto a fatty alcohol (myristyl nicotinate) or onto vitamin E (tocopheryl nicotinate). The lipophilic ester partitions into the stratum corneum, skin esterases slowly cleave it, and nicotinic acid is released gradually inside the skin — delivering the vitamin without the flush.
  • This is the rare "NAD/cellular-energy" story active with real human topical RCT data: the Jacobson group (University of Arizona / Niadyne) showed topical myristyl nicotinate raised skin-cell NAD by ~25%, increased stratum-corneum thickness ~70%, epidermal thickness ~20%, sped epidermal renewal, reduced TEWL ~20%, and raised minimal erythemal dose, in placebo-controlled human studies of photodamaged and atopic skin (Jacobson 2007 [1]; Atopic study [2]).
  • But be honest about scale and independence: this evidence base is small, single-lineage (mostly one research group with commercial IP), and now ~15-20 years old without large independent replication. It is much stronger topical proof than NMN has, but it is not the deep, multi-lab RCT base that niacinamide enjoys.
  • Net: the nicotinate esters are a credible, mechanistically elegant, genuinely human-topical-tested barrier + NAD active — but a niche one. For our brand they are best framed as a supporting / conditional flourish on a niacinamide- and ceramide-led barrier story, not a standalone hero, and never claimed beyond the barrier/appearance lane (the skin-cancer/AK work is investigational and drug-adjacent).

Biochemical relationship to the rest of vitamin B3. Niacin (nicotinic acid) and niacinamide (nicotinamide) are the two interconvertible forms of vitamin B3; both feed NAD+ synthesis, but by different routes. Nicotinic acid enters NAD+ synthesis via the Preiss-Handler pathway (nicotinic acid → NaMN → NaAD → NAD+), whereas nicotinamide enters via the salvage pathway (→ NMN → NAD+). Only nicotinic acid is a ligand for the GPR109A (HCA2) receptor — niacinamide is not. That receptor difference is the whole reason the acid branch flushes (and the reason it has a receptor-mediated differentiation story that niacinamide lacks). See §2.

2. Class & mechanism

Class: NAD+ / cellular-energy actives (the acid-prodrug branch). Two mechanistically distinct arms:

Arm A — NAD+ precursor (the "cellular energy / longevity" arm)

  • Nicotinic acid delivered into living skin is converted (Preiss-Handler) to NAD+, the central coenzyme for mitochondrial ATP production, DNA repair, and the substrate for sirtuins (SIRT1-7) and PARPs.
  • Skin NAD+ declines with age and with UV exposure (UV/PARP activation consumes NAD+). The intervention logic: deliver a precursor → restore skin NAD+ → re-fuel sirtuins/PARPs/mitochondria → better repair, differentiation and resilience.
  • What is actually demonstrated for this format (not borrowed): topical myristyl nicotinate measurably raised skin-cell NAD by ~25% in humans (Jacobson 2007 [1]). This is a real, format-correct NAD endpoint — the single most important thing distinguishing the nicotinate esters from NMN, where no human topical NAD elevation has been shown.

Arm B — GPR109A/HCA2 receptor signalling (the differentiation & flush arm — unique to the acid)

  • Nicotinic acid (but not nicotinamide) is an agonist of GPR109A/HCA2, a Gi-coupled receptor expressed in human epidermal keratinocytes (and Langerhans cells), with little/no expression in dermal fibroblasts (Bermudez/Jacobson 2011 [3]).
  • Receptor activation → Gi-mediated inhibition of adenylyl cyclase / lower cAMP → promotes epidermal terminal differentiation (the paper reported ~30% ↑caspase-14 and ~20% ↑filaggrin markers in photodamaged skin) and supports normal barrier formation. The receptor mislocalises and becomes non-functional in squamous cell carcinoma, hinting at a differentiation-keeping/anti-carcinogenic role [3].
  • The flush is the same receptor. GPR109A on keratinocytes → phospholipase A2 → arachidonic acid → COX-2-dependent PGE2 (keratinocyte late-phase) and COX-1/PGD2 (Langerhans early-phase) → vasodilation = the niacin flush (Hanson/JCI 2010 [4]; Elawa 2020 — NSAIDs cut topical methyl-nicotinate perfusion ~82% [5]). The ester prodrug strategy blunts the flush by releasing nicotinic acid slowly below the flush threshold.

Net mechanism: the nicotinate esters are a controlled-release way to deliver nicotinic acid into skin, giving two effects niacinamide cannot fully reproduce — a measured NAD bump plus GPR109A-driven epidermal differentiation — while the slow release tames the flush that would otherwise make the acid unusable as a leave-on.

3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

Collagen / ECM loss Indirect. NAD/sirtuin support is plausible; no direct human collagen RCT for the esters. (Dermal fibroblasts barely express GPR109A [3], so the acid's receptor arm is epidermis-biased.)
Barrier decline Strongest, format-correct human data. ↑stratum-corneum thickness ~70%, ↑epidermal thickness ~20%, ↑epidermal renewal 6-11%, ↓TEWL ~20% in photodamaged skin (Jacobson 2007 [1]); ↓TEWL at 4/8/12 wk in atopic skin (5% MN [2]).
Oxidative stress Plausible via NAD/redox; tocopheryl nicotinate adds vitamin-E antioxidant capacity on hydrolysis. Limited direct skin-oxidation endpoints for the esters.
Glycation / AGEs Not a documented primary effect.
Cellular senescence Mechanistically plausible (NAD+/SIRT1) but not directly measured for the topical esters in humans.
Mitochondrial decline Core claimed mechanism (NAD+ → mito/ATP); supported by the human ↑NAD endpoint [1], not by direct mitochondrial human-skin readouts.
Inflammaging Mixed/uncertain — the acid is pro-inflammatory acutely (prostaglandin flush); slow-release esters avoid that, but no clean anti-inflammaging human data.
Photoaging Yes (human). MN improved photodamaged-skin endpoints (differentiation, barrier; ↑MED ~9-10% but only borderline-significant, P≈0.05-0.07) [1]; counteracted retinoic-acid-induced SC thinning [6]; investigational for actinic-keratosis prevention (NAD/differentiation rationale) [3][7].
Pigmentation / tone Not a primary or well-evidenced effect for the acid esters (that's niacinamide's lane via melanosome transfer). Tocopheryl nicotinate is marketed for brightening but evidence is thin.
Microbiome Not established.
Hydration / water loss Yes (human) — the ↓TEWL / ↑barrier data above is the headline benefit.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core of this dossier)

Tiers, strongest format last within the topical story: in-vitro → animal → human ORAL → human TOPICAL (RCT vs lower). For the nicotinate esters, unusually, the topical human tier is the most developed. I flag whether each claim is proven for the topical ester format or borrowed from another form/route.

TIER A — In-vitro / mechanism (cell & receptor biology)

A1. GPR109A/HCA2 receptor in human keratinocytes & epidermal differentiation. - Bermudez Y, Benavente CA, Meyer RG, Coyle WR, Jacobson MK, Jacobson EL. "Nicotinic Acid Receptor Abnormalities in Human Skin Cancer: Implications for a Role in Epidermal Differentiation." PLoS ONE 2011;6(5):e20487. DOI 10.1371/journal.pone.0020487. [3] - Findings: GPR109A and GPR109B transcribed in human skin/keratinocytes (GPR109A ~2.2× higher; below detection in dermal fibroblasts); receptor moves to the plasma membrane in more differentiated layers; nicotinic acid ↑terminal-differentiation markers (~30% caspase-14, ~20% filaggrin) in photodamaged skin; receptor over-expressed but non-functional/mislocalised in SCC (up to 16× transcript in advanced tumours). Mechanism: Gi → ↓cAMP → pro-differentiation. - Limitation: mechanistic/expression study; the differentiation-marker change is a small human-skin signal embedded in a cancer-biology paper, not an efficacy RCT. Single research lineage.

A2. Flush mechanism (why raw acid is a problem, why esters help). - Hanson J, Gille A, Zwykiel S, et al. "Nicotinic acid- and monomethyl fumarate-induced flushing involves GPR109A expressed by keratinocytes and COX-2-dependent prostanoid formation in mice." J Clin Invest 2010;120(8):2910-2919. DOI 10.1172/JCI42273. [4] — keratinocyte GPR109A → COX-2 → PGE2 drives the late-phase flush; Langerhans/COX-1/PGD2 the early phase. - Elawa S, Mirdell R, Farnebo S, Tesselaar E. "Skin blood flow response to topically applied methyl nicotinate: Possible mechanisms." Skin Res Technol 2020;26(3):343-348. PMID 31777124. [5] — human: NSAIDs cut the topical-methyl-nicotinate perfusion rise ~82% (prostaglandin-dominant), sensory-nerve block ~32%, NO pathway uninvolved. - Relevance: confirms the flush is receptor/prostaglandin-mediated and dose/rate-dependent — the rationale for slow-release long-chain esters.

TIER B — Animal

  • Sparse and not central to the cosmetic case. The skin-relevant work for this branch is essentially the human studies below; animal data exists mainly for the flush mechanism (mouse GPR109A models, A2 [4]) and general niacin pharmacology. No major rodent photoaging RCT specific to myristyl/tocopheryl nicotinate was identified — note this gap honestly. (Contrast: the NMN branch leans heavily on oral mouse data; the ester branch leans on human topical data instead — an interesting inversion.)

TIER C — Human ORAL (context only — different molecule/route)

Oral B3 has real skin data, but it is mostly nicotinamide, not nicotinic acid, and oral ≠ topical. Included only to prevent borrowing it for ester claims. - ONTRAC (oral nicotinamide 500 mg BID ×12 mo) — 23% fewer non-melanoma skin cancers in high-risk patients (Chen, NEJM 2015;373:1618-1626; PMID 26488693). [8] This is nicotinamide, oral, drug-adjacent — NOT our nicotinate ester, NOT topical. - Belardi/Pacifici/Cosio et al. "Role of Nicotinamide in the Pathogenesis of Actinic Keratosis: Implications for NAD+/SIRT1 Pathway." Biomolecules 2024;14(12):1512. DOI 10.3390/biom14121512. [9] — oral nicotinamide 1000 mg/day ×24 mo ↑serum NAD+, ↑nuclear SIRT1 activity, ↓AK lesions. Again oral nicotinamide, included to show the NAD/SIRT1/AK rationale the topical nicotinate-ester program borrows its hypothesis from — not evidence for our format. - Why it matters: the strongest "B3 prevents skin cancer/AK" headlines are oral nicotinamide. They must never be implied by a topical nicotinate-ester cosmetic.

TIER D — Human TOPICAL (the real strength of this branch)

D1. Myristyl nicotinate in photodamaged skin — the flagship topical RCT. - Jacobson EL, Kim H, Kim M, Williams JD, Coyle DL, Coyle WR, Grove G, Rizer RL, Stratton MS, Jacobson MK. "A topical lipophilic niacin derivative increases NAD, epidermal differentiation and barrier function in photodamaged skin." Exp Dermatol 2007;16(6):490-499. PMID 17518989; DOI 10.1111/j.1600-0625.2007.00553.x. [1] - Design: randomised, placebo-controlled topical MN vs placebo in photodamaged human skin (facial + arm sites; biopsy + biophysical endpoints). - Quantitative results (vs placebo): skin-cell NAD ↑~25% (p=0.001); stratum-corneum thickness ↑~70% (p=0.0001); epidermal thickness ↑~20% (p=0.001); epidermal renewal ↑6-11% (p≈0.003-0.001); TEWL ↓~20% on cheeks and arms (p=0.012, 0.017); minimal erythemal dose ↑8.9-10% (p≈0.05-0.07, i.e. borderline). - Limitations: modest sample (pilot-to-small RCT scale; exact n not stated in abstract — confirm before quoting n); single research group with commercial interest (Niadyne); MED change only borderline-significant; no long-term wrinkle/collagen endpoint; not independently replicated at scale. [confirm exact sample size from full text before using in copy]

D2. 5% Myristyl nicotinate in atopic skin — barrier RCT. - Jacobson MK / Jacobson EL et al. "Treatment effects of 5% myristyl nicotinate on the integrity of the stratum corneum in atopic patients." J Am Acad Dermatol 2004 (AAD abstract/poster, vol. 50 suppl; article ID S0190-9622(03)03649-1). [2] - Design: placebo-controlled, 12-week, subjects with atopic features and elevated baseline TEWL; 5% MN vs placebo. - Results: TEWL significantly reduced at 4, 8 and 12 weeks in the MN arm — improved stratum-corneum barrier integrity. - Limitations: appears to be a conference/abstract-grade report rather than a full peer-reviewed paper; small; same lineage. [verify whether a full peer-reviewed version exists before citing as a definitive RCT]

D3. Myristyl nicotinate + retinoic acid (tolerability/synergy). - Jacobson EL, et al. "Effect of myristyl nicotinate on retinoic acid therapy for facial photodamage." Exp Dermatol 2007;16(11) (companion paper; DOI 10.1111/j.1600-0625.2007.00616.x). [6] - Design: double-blind, placebo-controlled pilot; 1-month MN (or placebo) run-in, then 3 months retinoic acid. - Result: retinoic acid alone thinned the stratum corneum ~25%; MN ameliorated that thinning (barrier-protective alongside a retinoid). - Limitation: pilot scale; surrogate (SC thickness) endpoint; same group. Useful as a "pairs well with retinoids / cushions retinoid barrier disruption" signal, not a hard efficacy claim.

D4. Formulation compatibility (supporting, not efficacy). - Tashtoush BM, Kim H, Jacobson EL, Jacobson MK. "Formulation compatibility of myristyl nicotinate with drugs used to treat dermatological conditions involving an impaired skin barrier." Drug Dev Ind Pharm 2007;33(11):1176-1182. PMID 18058313. [12] — MN compatible & photocompatible with retinoic acid, hydrocortisone, triamcinolone, metronidazole, ketoconazole.

D5. Topical niacin derivatives for female-pattern hair thinning (adjacent, weak). - Draelos ZD, Jacobson EL, et al. "A pilot study evaluating the efficacy of topically applied niacin derivatives for treatment of female pattern alopecia." J Cosmet Dermatol 2005;4(4):258-261. DOI 10.1111/j.1473-2165.2005.00201.x. [13] - Design: double-blind, placebo-controlled; 60 women (40 active / 20 placebo), Ludwig I-III; vehicle with 0.5% octyl nicotinate + 5% myristyl nicotinate, 6 months; standardized photo grading. - Result: statistically significant ↑hair fullness vs placebo (p=0.04); mechanism unknown. - Limitations: pilot; small placebo arm; subjective fullness endpoint; not a core anti-aging claim and not replicated. Treat as a curiosity, not a hair-growth claim (note: separate reviews argue topical niacinamide does not stimulate hair growth).

D6. Tocopheryl nicotinate — the weakest-evidenced ester. - No robust independent human topical RCT identified for tocopheryl nicotinate as an anti-aging active. The CIR safety review notes it is used relatively infrequently (<0.3% of α-tocopherol-derivative uses) and that α-tocopherol uptake from the nicotinate ester is lower than from the acetate ester (the comparative uptake data CIR cites are from an oral rat study, not a topical-penetration study — so treat the "delivers less vitamin E" point as directional, not a measured topical figure); the acetate/succinate forms also carry the documented UV-protection data (Zondlo Fiume, Int J Toxicol 2002 [14]). Marketing claims (brightening, soothing, warming, UV) are largely vendor/encyclopaedia-level, not RCT-backed (Paula's Choice/Cosmetics Info [15]). - Verdict on this ester: plausible dual vitamin-E/B3 idea, but the evidence and even the penetration favour plain tocopheryl acetate for vitamin E and myristyl nicotinate for the niacin. Tocopheryl nicotinate is the weakest member of this family for us.

Bottom line by tier. The nicotinate-ester branch is, unusually, best evidenced in the correct (human topical) format — but that evidence is narrow: a handful of small, ~2004-2007, single-group studies (myristyl nicotinate), plus a thin tocopheryl-nicotinate record. It clears the "is there any human topical proof?" bar that NMN fails — but it is far below niacinamide's broad, multi-lab RCT base.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Raw nicotinic acid: small, water-soluble, penetrates — but its receptor agonism makes it a flushing rubefacient; short esters (methyl/ethyl/hexyl nicotinate) are used deliberately as warming/penetration agents and flush provocateurs (Elawa [5]). Poor choice for a calm leave-on at meaningful dose.
  • Myristyl nicotinate (the good delivery story): long C14 lipophilic chain → high log P → partitions efficiently into the stratum-corneum lipid lamellae; cutaneous esterases cleave it slowly, releasing nicotinic acid gradually intracellularly below the flush threshold. The human ↑NAD (+25%) and barrier endpoints (D1) are the proof that the acid actually reaches living skin and is converted. Long-chain nicotinate esters (log P ~10-15 region cited by the developers) are described as optimal for SC partitioning.
  • Tocopheryl nicotinate: lipophilic but, per CIR, gives lower α-tocopherol uptake than tocopheryl acetate [14] (CIR's comparative data are from an oral animal study, not a topical-penetration assay — so the "under-delivers the vitamin-E half" read is directional, not a measured topical number); the niacin-delivery half is less characterised than MN's.
  • Practical implication: if we want the niacin-into-skin-NAD effect, myristyl nicotinate is the delivery vehicle with the actual human data. Raw niacin = flush risk; tocopheryl nicotinate = under-delivers both halves.

6. Formulation

Myristyl nicotinate (the practical active): - Concentration: human studies used ~5% (photodamage [1], atopic [2], alopecia vehicle [13]). 5% is the de-facto evidence anchor. - Phase: oil-soluble — formulate into the oil phase of an emulsion (it is an emollient-like liquid), not a water serum. - pH: as an ester it is most stable near-neutral; strongly acidic/alkaline or hot conditions accelerate hydrolysis to free nicotinic acid (which would reintroduce flush risk) + myristyl alcohol. Keep formulations near-neutral and avoid prolonged heat. [specific pH-stability curve not found in public abstracts — request from supplier] - Stability/compatibility: shown compatible & photocompatible with retinoic acid, hydrocortisone, triamcinolone, metronidazole, ketoconazole (D4 [12]) — good news for pairing with a retinoid or in barrier-support regimens. - Pairings: natural fit with ceramides, cholesterol, fatty acids, niacinamide, panthenol, HA in a barrier-repair cream; cushions retinoid-induced SC thinning (D3 [6]) → attractive "retinoid companion." - Sensory: light emollient liquid; low odour at good grade; oil-soluble so it blends into rich creams without tack. - Watch-out: if the ester hydrolyses in-pack to free niacin, you get flushing/tingling on application — formulate to keep it intact.

Tocopheryl nicotinate: typically used at low levels (CIR-reported max ~0.1% rinse-off, 0.001% leave-on historically [15]) — i.e. trace antioxidant/warming use, not an active dose. Not a meaningful NAD-delivery route.

Raw niacin (nicotinic acid): avoid as a leave-on active at functional levels (flush). Acceptable only as a trace/pH context, or intentionally in warming/rubefacient products (not our use case).

7. Safety

  • Flushing (the headline issue): nicotinic acid and short esters cause prostaglandin-mediated flush (transient redness, warmth, tingling, sometimes itch) via keratinocyte GPR109A/COX-2 (Hanson [4]; Elawa [5]). Cosmetically undesirable; the ester-prodrug + slow-release design is specifically what mitigates it — MN human studies did not report problematic flushing at 5%.
  • Irritation/sensitization: the read-across toxicology category (nicotinic acid, nicotinate esters, nicotinamide) concluded the group is not genotoxic, not carcinogenic, with negative reproductive/developmental signals; methyl nicotinate was non-sensitizing in guinea-pig testing (Charehsaz 2020 [16]). CIR found tocopheryl nicotinate safe as used in cosmetics (Zondlo Fiume 2002 [14]; CIR tocopherols update 2018 [17]); not irritating to rabbit eyes.
  • Sun sensitivity: no photosensitization signal; MN actually raised MED slightly (mild photoprotective trend [1]). Daytime-compatible.
  • Pregnancy: vitamin B3 derivatives are generally low-concern topically; no specific contraindication identified, but defer to medical advice and avoid disease claims. [no pregnancy-specific topical study for MN found — flag as data-limited]
  • PDE / systemic: a pharma-manufacturing read-across set a permitted daily exposure of 10 mg/day for the category (conservative, cleaning-validation context — not a cosmetic limit) (Charehsaz 2020 [16]).
  • Sourcing/sustainability: nicotinic acid is a commodity, made at scale; myristyl nicotinate is a synthesized ester (niche cosmetic supply, fewer suppliers than niacinamide). Demand CoA: ester purity, residual free nicotinic acid (flush driver), hydrolytic stability.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

The cosmetic/drug line. Cosmetics may improve appearance but may not claim to treat/prevent disease or alter structure/function — that is drug territory (US FDA / EU).

  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "supports the skin barrier and helps reduce water loss," "helps visibly improve the resilience and smoothness of photo-aged skin," "an oil-soluble form of vitamin B3 that supports skin's natural renewal and energy (NAD) processes," "helps cushion the barrier when using a retinoid."
  • ⚠️ Risky / drug-like / unsubstantiated — flag for legal:
  • "prevents/treats actinic keratosis or skin cancer" — the AK/skin-cancer evidence is oral nicotinamide (ONTRAC [8], Belardi [9]) and the MN-AK program is investigational/drug-adjacent. Absolutely not a cosmetic claim, and not even our molecule for the proven part.
  • "recharges your skin's NAD+ / boosts cellular energy" quantified — we can say it "supports NAD" more defensibly than NMN can (because [1] showed a +25% NAD endpoint for this format), but any specific % or "recharge/boost" superlative needs our own substantiation and legal review; keep it as "supports," not "boosts by X%."
  • "reverses photoaging / erases wrinkles" — overreach; the human endpoints are barrier/differentiation/SC-thickness, not wrinkle-grade.
  • "stimulates hair growth" — only a single weak pilot (D5 [13]); do not make a hair-growth claim.
  • Compliance nuance unique to this ingredient: because this branch is literally tied to a drug-development program (topical nicotinic-acid prodrug for AK), there is real risk of importing drug-grade claims. Keep every claim in the barrier/appearance/"supports NAD" lane and keep the AK/cancer science clearly labelled as background education about oral nicotinamide, never as our product's effect.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • "NAD skincare that actually raises your skin's NAD." — Closest-to-true in the whole NAD category for a topical. Myristyl nicotinate did raise skin NAD ~25% in a human study [1]. This is the ester branch's genuine edge over NMN. But it's one small study from one group; "actually raises NAD" is defensible-with-care, not a slam-dunk superlative.
  • "Niacin without the flush." — True and clever — the ester slow-release design specifically tames the GPR109A/prostaglandin flush [4][5]. Legitimate.
  • "Clinically proven barrier repair." — Partly true: real human TEWL/SC-thickness RCT data [1][2], but small and dated; "clinically studied" is honest, "clinically proven" is a stretch given scale/replication.
  • "Prevents skin cancer / clears precancerous spots." — Hype/drug-adjacent. The proof is oral nicotinamide [8][9]; the topical-MN AK work is investigational. Do not imply.
  • "Vitamin E + B3 powerhouse (tocopheryl nicotinate)." — Weak. Lower tocopherol uptake and UV protection than tocopheryl acetate [14]; thin evidence. Marketing gloss.
  • "Boosts hair growth." — Hype. One small pilot [13]; contradicted for the related niacinamide.
  • What's NOT hype: the mechanism is real and uniquely receptor-grounded (GPR109A epidermal differentiation [3]), the delivery problem is genuinely solved by the ester, and there is real—if modest—human topical barrier + NAD data [1][2]. That combination is rare in this category.

11. Verdict for the brand

  • Myristyl nicotinate: ⚠️→✅ SUPPORTING / CONDITIONAL (strong supporting, not a solo hero). It is the most intellectually honest "NAD/cellular-energy" topical we could put in a barrier cream: it has a format-correct human NAD endpoint and human barrier RCT data — things NMN simply lacks — plus a clean "niacin without the flush" story and good retinoid compatibility. But the evidence is small, ~20 years old, single-lineage, and not independently scaled, and it is epidermis/barrier-biased rather than a broad anti-aging actor. So: an excellent co-star to a niacinamide + ceramide barrier hero, especially if we want a defensible, differentiated NAD narrative — not the headline act on its own.
  • Tocopheryl nicotinate: ❌ SKIP (for our purposes). Under-delivers both vitamins vs simpler alternatives; thin evidence. Use tocopheryl acetate for vitamin E and myristyl nicotinate for niacin instead.
  • Raw niacin / nicotinic acid: ❌ SKIP as a leave-on active (flush); only relevant as the released active inside the MN prodrug.

One-line reasoning: Among "NAD" topicals, myristyl nicotinate is the one with actual human skin-NAD and barrier data — make it a credible supporting differentiator on a niacinamide/ceramide barrier hero, dosed ~5% in the oil phase, claimed only in the barrier/appearance/"supports NAD" lane.

Longevity-relevance note. This ingredient is a clean bridge between the longevity/NAD narrative and defensible cosmetic claims — arguably cleaner than NMN, because the proof is in the right format. It pairs naturally with our barrier-repair thesis (the human wins are literally barrier/TEWL/differentiation) and gives the brand a way to tell an honest "cellular-energy/NAD" story without the NMN overreach. The discipline trap is identical: the moment we borrow the oral nicotinamide skin-cancer halo for a topical, we lose the credibility that is our core asset.

Overall grade: B− (moderate human-topical, but narrow and single-lineage)

There is genuine human-topical RCT-grade evidence here — which already lifts this branch above the bulk of "NAD skincare" (NMN especially). That is what earns the B. The minus reflects three structural weaknesses the dossier itself already names and does not hide: the efficacy data are (1) small, (2) ~2004-2007 vintage with no independent at-scale replication, and (3) almost entirely from one research group (Jacobson / Niadyne) with direct commercial IP in the molecule. A grade of A is reserved for broad, multi-lab, independently replicated human-topical RCTs (the bar niacinamide clears and this branch does not).

Borrowed-evidence check (the main integrity risk in this category) — PASSED

The single biggest way an "NAD skincare" dossier goes wrong is laundering oral nicotinamide skin-cancer data ([8],[9]) into a topical nicotinate-ester efficacy claim. This dossier does the opposite — it repeatedly, explicitly walls those off (§4 Tier C, §8, §10, and verification note 3). Likewise it correctly keeps the mouse flush mechanism [4] in the mechanism tier rather than dressing it as efficacy. The format-discipline here is genuinely good.

Main caveats (keep these visible; do not let copy erase them)

  1. One group, commercial IP. The efficacy spine ([1],[2],[3],[6],[12],[13]) is overwhelmingly Jacobson / Niadyne, who developed and patented the molecule. No independent lab has replicated the +25% NAD / barrier results at scale. This is the dominant weakness — "clinically studied," never "clinically proven."
  2. Small + old. Core efficacy is ~2004-2007, pilot-to-small-RCT scale; the flagship [1] doesn't even state n in its abstract. Quantified claims ("+25% NAD," "+70% SC") are defensible only with the citation and the "small single study" framing attached.
  3. Atopic study [2] is abstract/poster-grade, not confirmed full peer-review. Do not call it a "definitive RCT." (Dossier already flags; keep.)
  4. MED / photoprotection is borderline (P≈0.05–0.07). Now flagged in the §3 table too. Not a clean "raises sun resistance" claim.
  5. Tocopheryl nicotinate has essentially no human-topical efficacy evidence and its "delivers less vitamin E" knock rests on oral animal uptake data — directional only. The ❌ SKIP verdict is sound; the reasoning was tightened for route precision.
  6. Epidermis/barrier-biased, not whole-skin anti-aging. No human collagen/wrinkle/dermal endpoint for the esters; fibroblasts barely express the receptor [3]. The "barrier supporting actor, not anti-wrinkle hero" framing is correct.
