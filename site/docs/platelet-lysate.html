Ingredient: Platelet Lysate (PRP-derived topical) (INCI: see §1 — no single standardized INCI; commonly "Human Platelet Extract," declared via sh-polypeptide / blood-component naming)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ⬜ considering / ✅ shortlisted-as-CONDITIONAL / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Growth factors, cytokines, exosomes, conditioned media & regenerative Slug: platelet-lysate

One-line honest take: A real, mechanistically-rich cocktail of human platelet growth factors with genuinely strong in vitro and animal anti-aging data and a promising-but-thin topical human evidence base — but it is dragged down by a hard skin-penetration problem (growth factors are 6–45 kDa, the skin barrier excludes >500 Da), sourcing/biosafety/regulatory baggage (it is a human blood derivative), and a category (PRP / "vampire facial" / exosomes) that the FDA is actively policing. Conditional at best for a claims-disciplined premium brand.

1) Identity

Common names: Platelet lysate; human platelet lysate (hPL); platelet-rich plasma (PRP) derivative / PRP-derived topical; platelet releasate; platelet-released growth factors (PRGF); human platelet extract (HPE); "growth-factor serum" (marketing). In medspa marketing the injected/microneedled cousin is the "vampire facial."

What it physically is: PRP is whole blood spun down to concentrate platelets in plasma. Platelet lysate goes one step further — the concentrated platelets are deliberately ruptured (freeze-thaw cycles, sonication, or chemical activation) to dump the contents of their α-granules: a soup of growth factors, cytokines, chemokines, and platelet-derived extracellular vesicles/exosomes, after which cell debris is removed. The result is a cell-free, growth-factor-rich liquid that can be filtered, pooled, standardized, and (importantly for cosmetics) lyophilized (freeze-dried) for shelf stability — a key advantage over fresh autologous PRP, which must be made chairside from the patient's own blood.

Key actives present (representative concentrations from a characterized hPL, Li et al. 2022): PDGF ~10.5 ng/mL, TGF-β ~4.3 ng/mL, IGF-1 ~5.4 ng/mL, EGF ~1.0 ng/mL; literature also consistently reports VEGF, bFGF/FGF-2, HGF, and platelet-derived exosomes. Composition varies enormously by donor, preparation method, and platelet dose — this lack of standardization is the field's central scientific weakness.

Forms relevant to a topical brand: - Allogeneic, pooled, donor-screened human platelet lysate / human platelet extract (HPE) — the only commercially scalable, standardizable form for a packaged cosmetic. Sourced from FDA-registered blood banks; leukocyte-depleted; pathogen-tested. (This is the form the brand would actually use.) - Lyophilized hPL powder — reconstituted or formulated dry; best growth-factor stability. - Autologous PRP / "vampire facial" — chairside, patient's own blood, injected or microneedled. Not a packaged-product format; a clinical procedure. Listed here only because most human evidence comes from it. - Platelet-derived exosomes — increasingly split out as their own "ingredient"; heavily regulated (see §8).

INCI / labeling reality: There is no single clean INCI name for "platelet lysate." Human-blood-derived cosmetic actives are labeled inconsistently — some products declare engineered single growth factors as sh-polypeptide-X (recombinant, E. coli/yeast-fermented human-identical peptides — not actually platelet lysate), others use proprietary trade names ("Human Platelet Extract," "HPE") with vague INCI. A brand must get exact INCI/regulatory classification confirmed before any label is printed — see §8. [partially unverified — INCI assignment for pooled human platelet lysate is not standardized; confirm with a regulatory consultant before use]

2) Class & mechanism

Class: Regenerative / growth-factor-and-cytokine signaling complex (not a single molecule — a biologically active multi-factor cocktail).

Mechanism (well-characterized in cell/animal models): - Mitogenic + pro-migratory on fibroblasts. hPL strongly promotes proliferation and migration of human dermal fibroblasts (HDFs) — the cells that build collagen and ECM (Li et al. 2022; Burnouf foundational work on hPL as a cell-culture growth supplement). - Senescence suppression via NF-κB. The standout mechanistic finding: hPL suppresses the senescent state of HDFs by down-regulating phosphorylated p65 (p-P65) in the NF-κB pathway — confirmed bidirectionally (p65 siRNA mimicked hPL's benefit; the NF-κB agonist prostratin abolished it). Downstream, hPL lowered senescence markers p16, p21, reduced SA-β-gal⁺ cells, and suppressed SASP (IL-6, IL-8) and collagen-degrading MMPs (MMP-1/-2/-3/-9) (Li et al. 2022). - NRF2-ARE antioxidant activation. Platelet growth factors modulate the NRF2 (antioxidant) and NF-κB (inflammation) axes — a redox/anti-inflammatory mechanism reported in platelet-released growth-factor studies. - Pro-angiogenic / dermal remodeling. Increased VEGF expression, dermal thickness, and collagen fiber density in aged-skin animal models (Li et al. 2022). - Individual growth factor roles: PDGF (fibroblast mitogen, ECM synthesis), TGF-β (collagen synthesis — also a fibrosis/scar caution at high dose), IGF-1 (fibroblast survival/proliferation), EGF (keratinocyte proliferation, re-epithelialization), VEGF (angiogenesis), bFGF (fibroblast/endothelial proliferation).

The mechanism is real and coherent. The unresolved question is whether enough of this large-molecule signaling cocktail crosses intact stratum corneum from a leave-on topical to reproduce the effects seen with injected or in-vitro/applied-directly-to-cells delivery (see §5).

4) Evidence review by tier

Brutal-honesty framing: The overwhelming majority of high-quality human data is for injected PRP, not a leave-on topical platelet lysate. The dossier must not let injection data masquerade as topical proof. There is currently one notable peer-reviewed human study of a leave-on topical platelet extract, and it is single-arm and uncontrolled. Everything else topical is either in-vitro, animal, or borrowed from injection.

Tier 1 — In vitro (cells)

  • Li T, Lu H, Zhou L, et al. (2022), Cell Proliferation 55(4):e13212. Human dermal fibroblasts in a D-galactose senescence model. hPL ↑ proliferation/migration; ↓ SA-β-gal, p16, p21, LaminB1; ↓ SASP (IL-6/IL-8) and MMPs; mechanism = NF-κB (p-P65) suppression, validated by siRNA + agonist rescue. Strength: mechanistically rigorous. Limitation: applied directly to cells (bypasses skin barrier); acute 24–48 h model ≠ chronic aging; single cell type. DOI: 10.1111/cpr.13212. [PMC9055903]
  • Burnouf and colleagues (multiple, 2000s–2010s): hPL is a well-established serum substitute for culturing human cells (it is defined by its growth-factor potency on cells). Establishes biological activity but says nothing about topical skin delivery. (Foundational; e.g., the 1980 demonstration that platelet lysate supports diverse cell lines, PMID 7419237.)

Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

  • Li T, et al. (2022) (same paper): BALB/c nude mice, D-galactose aging model, hPL SUBCUTANEOUSLY INJECTED at three dilutions (0.25 mL weekly × 3 weeks) — not topically applied (corrected on verification; see verification section). Dose-dependent ↓ skin roughness & wrinkle depth, ↑ dermal thickness & collagen density, ↑ VEGF, restored SOD, normalized MDA. Strength: in-vivo skin endpoints + dose-response. Limitation: delivery was by injection, which bypasses the stratum corneum entirely — so this is NOT evidence of topical/leave-on efficacy; mouse skin ≠ human skin; small n per group; chemically-induced (not chronological/photo) aging.
  • Wound-healing animal literature (broad): PRP/platelet derivatives accelerate experimental wound healing in animals (systematic review/meta-analysis, e.g., PMC5764374). Relevant to barrier/repair pedigree, but wounded skin has no intact barrier — irrelevant to leave-on penetration on healthy skin.

Tier 3 — Human, ORAL

  • None / not applicable. Platelet lysate is not an oral ingredient. (No oral-format evidence exists or is relevant.)

Tier 4 — Human, TOPICAL

4a. Leave-on topical platelet extract (the directly-relevant tier — and it is thin): - Wyles SP, Proffer SL, Farris P, et al. (2024), J Drugs Dermatol — "Effect of Topical Human Platelet Extract (HPE) for Facial Skin Rejuvenation: A Histological Study of Collagen and Elastin." Design: prospective, single-arm, NON-randomized, evaluator-blinded. n=56 (mean age 54). Allogeneic pooled HPE serum ("Intense Serum," Rion Aesthetics) twice daily × 12 weeks, no concurrent procedures. Results: significant ↑ collagen fibril thickness on histology (Masson trichrome / EM, P≤0.0001); 87.3% subject-reported improvement; sustained pigment reduction + ↑ luminosity (P≤0.001); no serious AEs. Limitations (severe for evidence-weighting): no control/placebo arm, no randomization, short (12-wk) follow-up, subjective subject-reported primary efficacy outcomes, and a major conflict of interest — the senior author (Atta Behfar) is a CO-FOUNDER of Rion, the manufacturer of the tested serum, and Mayo Clinic holds a licensed financial interest in the platelet-exosome technology (PEP). This is not merely "industry-affiliated"; it is effectively a manufacturer-run study of its own product. This is the best topical-platelet study and it is still low on the evidence hierarchy. PMID 39231070. (Registered NCT05457491.)

4b. Topical growth-factor preparations more broadly (the category this would live in): - Quinlan DJ, Ghanem AM, Hassan H (2023), J Cosmet Dermatol — systematic review of topical GF preparations for facial rejuvenation. 33 studies (9 RCTs, 24 case series), 1,180 participants, 23 different preparations. Findings: modest improvements — skin texture (median <50%), fine lines/wrinkles (<35%), overall appearance (<20%); participant-rated > investigator-rated; 3 comparative RCTs showed NO significant difference between treatments; low AE risk; durability beyond 6 months unknown; heterogeneity in GF source/number prevents firm conclusions. Verdict of the review: "appears effective" but proof-of-concept only, not evidence-based paradigm. PMID 37222303 / DOI 10.1111/jocd.15644. (Most topical-GF products use fibroblast-conditioned media, recombinant single GFs, or plant/snail GFs — platelet-lysate-specific topicals are a small subset.) - Fabi S, Sundaram H (2014), Facial Plast Surg — review: topical GFs from fibroblast secretions/lysate, snail secretion, stem-cell media show level II–III evidence; injectable PRP/PRFM "promising" on in-vitro/animal/small case series; higher-powered data required. Flags the central delivery problem implicitly. PMID 24810127.

4c. INJECTED PRP / platelet lysate — strongest human data, but WRONG FORMAT (read as ceiling-of-biological-potential, not as topical proof): - Li T, et al. (2022) (the human arm of the same NF-κB paper, surfaced on verification): a retrospective analysis of n=30 subjects (aged 30–60, ~93% female) who received three SUBCUTANEOUS INJECTIONS of ~5 mL hPL at 2-month intervals; VISIA imaging reported reduced wrinkles, improved texture and pores, no AEs. This is injection data, not topical — and retrospective/uncontrolled — so it does NOT support a leave-on serum. (The paper's title says "clinical studies," but the clinical portion is injected and uncontrolled; do not let the paper be cited as topical human proof.) - Alam M, Hughart R, Champlain A, et al. (2018), JAMA Dermatology — the highest-quality individual RCT. Randomized, split-face, placebo (saline)-controlled, masked raters. n=27 enrolled / 19 analyzed. 3 mL intradermal PRP vs saline, single session. Result: masked dermatologists found NO significant objective difference vs saline; only patient self-assessment favored PRP for texture (P=.02) and wrinkles (P=.03) at 6 months. A sobering, well-designed negative-leaning result. (Injection, single session, small n.) - Hersant et al. — RCT, n=93, PRP vs hyaluronic acid vs combination (3 sessions); combination significantly improved facial appearance and elasticity. (Injection; combination confounds the PRP-alone effect.) [reported via secondary sources — verify primary] - Chen & Zhou (2025), Aesthetic Surg J Open Forum — meta-analysis, 9 RCTs / 358 patients, all INJECTION. Patient satisfaction RR 1.34 (95% CI 1.07–1.67, P=.01); objective efficacy RR 1.42 (1.01–2.00, P=.04); AEs no difference. Low heterogeneity. Explicitly: no topical PRP formulations included; calls for more rigorous RCTs. PMC12894766. - Systematic reviews / umbrella reviews of PRP facial rejuvenation consistently conclude promising but low-quality, non-standardized evidence: Xiao et al. 2021 (36 studies, 3,172 patients; CCID, PMID 34819739); 2023 Plast Reconstr Surg review (PMID 36728559); and a 2024 umbrella review (13 SRs, 28 primary studies, 18 uncontrolled) finding "evidence insufficient to suggest firm conclusions." - Melasma / pigmentation (injection or microneedle, often adjunctive): multiple RCTs — photoactivated vs classical PRP (PMC11626296), microneedle-RF + PRP split-face (VISIA ↓ brown spots significantly only with PRP added, PMC12905025), PRP + microneedling/microinjection (n=62), PRP + oral tranexamic acid (PMID 38934336). Signal is real but delivery is procedural, not leave-on topical.

Bottom line by tier: In-vitro = strong. Animal = moderate-strong. Human oral = N/A. Human topical leave-on = weak (1 uncontrolled study + a heterogeneous category review showing only modest effects). Human injected = moderate (real but inconsistent, small, non-standardized; best RCT was essentially negative on objective measures). The proven-for-topical-human gap is the whole story.

5) Topical bioavailability — the central problem

This is the make-or-break section and the brand must be clear-eyed about it.

  • The 500-Dalton rule. Healthy human stratum corneum is a robust barrier; passive penetration of hydrophilic molecules drops sharply above ~500 Da. Platelet growth factors are far larger — EGF ~6 kDa, PDGF ~25 kDa, TGF-β ~25 kDa, IGF-1 ~7.6 kDa, VEGF ~38–45 kDa. As a class, GFs are "unlikely to penetrate the epidermis in measurable quantities to produce pharmacologic effects" on intact skin (Fabi & Sundaram 2014 and the broader topical-GF literature).
  • Why injected/microneedled PRP "works" and a serum may not: injection and microneedling bypass the barrier entirely. Most positive human PRP data come from breaching the skin. A leave-on serum does not.
  • Plausible (but unproven-for-this-ingredient) penetration routes: follicular/appendageal route for a small fraction of large molecules; compromised/aged/very dry barrier may leak more; and secondary signaling — even barrier-trapped GFs at the surface could theoretically trigger keratinocyte cytokine cascades that propagate inward. The Wyles 2024 histology (↑ dermal collagen from a leave-on serum) is the single piece of evidence that something reaches the dermis or triggers a relay — but it is uncontrolled and manufacturer-conducted (senior author co-founded the maker), so attribution to the platelet actives (vs. the vehicle/occlusion/measurement/bias) is not secure.
  • Delivery-enhancement levers if pursued: pairing with at-home microneedling (regulatory + safety complications — see §7/§8), penetration enhancers, encapsulation/nanocarriers, or exosome-mediated delivery (platelet exosomes are small and membrane-fusogenic — but exosomes carry their own FDA problem, §8).

Conclusion: topical bioavailability of intact platelet growth factors through healthy skin is low and largely unquantified. Any efficacy claim for a leave-on platelet-lysate product rests on (a) one uncontrolled study and (b) hope about secondary signaling — not on demonstrated dermal delivery.

6) Formulation notes

  • Effective concentration: No established cosmetic use-level for "platelet lysate" as such (contrast engineered sh-polypeptide GFs at ~0.5–15%). hPL is dosed by growth-factor content/platelet-equivalent, which varies by lot — a formulation challenge.
  • pH: Proteins are pH- and ionic-strength-sensitive; growth factors generally tolerate near-physiologic, mildly acidic-to-neutral pH (~5–7). Lyophilized HPL retains non-altered pH/osmolality vs liquid (PMC11807961). Avoid extremes that denature proteins.
  • Stability (the hard part): Growth factors are labile — heat, oxidation, proteases, and time degrade them. Best practice from the regenerative-medicine literature:
  • Lyophilization preserves PDGF/TGF-β/VEGF; lyophilized hPL stable ~9 months at 4°C and ≥3 months at room temp in some preps (PMC11807961; MDPI porcine PL study).
  • Implies anhydrous or freeze-dried/reconstitute formats, cold-chain or single-use ampoules, airless/opaque packaging, antioxidants, low water activity. A standard water-based jar cream is a poor home for live growth-factor activity.
  • Pairings (rational): hyaluronic acid (humectant carrier; HA + platelet derivatives co-lyophilized for sustained GF release — PMC12457828), niacinamide (barrier/tone, well-tolerated), ceramides (barrier — central to the brand's thesis), peptides (complementary signaling). Antioxidants to protect the proteins.
  • Conflicts: avoid high concentrations of low-pH actives (L-ascorbic acid, strong AHAs), strong oxidizers, and high-percentage preservative systems that may denature proteins in the same phase/timeframe — sequence them in routine instead.
  • Sensory: plasma/blood-derived materials can have a faint biological odor and amber/straw color; serum textures fit best; needs masking/fragrance strategy consistent with claims discipline.

7) Safety

  • Irritation/sensitization: Across PRP and topical-GF studies, adverse events are low and mostly mild/local (injection-site for the procedural form). Topical HPE (Wyles 2024) and the topical-GF systematic review (Quinlan 2023) both report low AE rates. Proteins/peptides can be sensitizers in principle; patch-test data for pooled hPL leave-on are limited.
  • The biosafety elephant — it is human blood. Platelet lysate is a human-blood-derived material, so the dominant safety axis is blood-borne pathogen transmission and contamination, not classic cosmetic irritancy.
  • "Vampire facial" HIV cluster (CDC): at an unlicensed New Mexico spa, ≥3 clients contracted HIV linked to non-sterile PRP-microneedling; first CDC-documented HIV transmission via cosmetic injection services (investigation 2018–2023; 198 contacts tested; route — cross-contamination vs. mislabeled blood — never definitively established). This is the cautionary tale that defines the category's reputational and regulatory risk. (Aesthetic Society; CDC/MMWR coverage 2024.)
  • Mitigation for a packaged allogeneic product: sourcing from FDA-registered blood banks, donor screening, infectious-disease testing (HIV/HBV/HCV), leukocyte depletion, pathogen reduction/inactivation, and GMP pooling substantially de-risk vs. chairside autologous handling. Allogeneic pooled material is more standardizable and arguably safer-when-properly-manufactured than ad-hoc autologous prep — but the inherent "it's blood" perception risk remains.
  • Sun: No photosensitization signal; not a photosensitizer. (Does not replace SPF — still the #1 anti-aging step per the glossary.)
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: No specific topical-platelet safety data in pregnancy. Growth-factor actives are generally advised with caution / clinician discretion in pregnancy due to absence of data — flag "consult a clinician," do not market to pregnant users. [unverified — no pregnancy-specific topical hPL data located]
  • TGF-β caution: at high/sustained dose TGF-β is pro-fibrotic; theoretical (not reported for cosmetic topicals) concern about scarring/keloid tendency — relevant mainly to injection.
  • Theoretical proliferation concern: GFs are mitogenic; a recurring (largely theoretical, not demonstrated) question is whether chronic application near (sub)clinical lesions is wise. Recombinant PDGF-BB has a documented cosmetic safety review (PMC12754275) that is broadly reassuring, but it is a single recombinant GF, not pooled lysate.

8) Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md)

This is the second make-or-break section. Platelet lysate sits in a regulatory danger zone.

  • Cosmetic vs. drug/biologic line (US FDA): A leave-on cosmetic may only make appearance claims. Platelet lysate's whole mechanistic story — "suppresses senescence," "rebuilds collagen," "repairs," "regenerates," "boosts collagen by X%" — is structure/function language that pushes toward drug territory and would need substantiation/legal review (CLAUDE.md guardrail). The brand can say "helps improve the visible appearance of fine lines / supports a smoother, more luminous look"; it cannot say "regenerates skin," "reverses aging," "boosts collagen."
  • Human-blood-derived material = potential HCT/P / biologic classification. Depending on processing and claims, blood-derived products can fall under FDA's human cells, tissues, and cellular/tissue-based products (HCT/P) framework or be deemed unapproved biologics/drugs rather than cosmetics. This is not a settled "just a cosmetic ingredient" situation.
  • The exosome enforcement wave (directly adjacent): Platelet lysate contains platelet-derived exosomes, and many marketers blur "platelet"/"exosome." The FDA has issued multiple public safety notifications and warning letters (≥6 by Oct 2023; ongoing into 2024–2025) against exosome and stem-cell products, stating no exosome product is FDA-approved, that marketing them for skin/hair regeneration violates federal law, and citing unapproved-biologic distribution, cGMP failures, and unsubstantiated claims (e.g., Kimera Labs 2023; "EXO RNA" 2024). A platelet-lysate skincare line that leans into regenerative/exosome claims invites the same scrutiny.
  • INCI / labeling: No clean standardized INCI for pooled human platelet lysate (see §1). Mislabeling risk is real. Mandatory: regulatory-counsel review of classification, INCI, and every claim before launch — and check non-US markets (EU Cosmetics Regulation restricts human-derived ingredients; some jurisdictions ban human-blood-derived cosmetics outright). [EU/other-market specifics unverified — confirm with regulatory consultant]

Claim cheat-sheet for this ingredient: - ✅ Safer: "formulated with human platelet-derived growth factors," "helps improve the appearance of skin texture and luminosity," "supports the look of firmer skin." - ⚠️ Flag for legal/substantiation: "regenerates," "repairs," "reverses aging," "boosts/increases collagen by X%," "anti-aging," "treats," anything implying biologic/drug action — and any "exosome" hero-claim.

9) Sourcing & cost

  • Form to buy: allogeneic, leukocyte-depleted, pathogen-tested human platelet lysate / human platelet extract, ideally lyophilized, from suppliers that source from FDA-registered blood banks under GMP. (Note: most "platelet lysate" suppliers serve the cell-culture / regenerative-medicine market, not cosmetics — cosmetic-grade, claims-compliant supply is narrower.)
  • Cost tier: HIGH / premium. Human-blood-derived, donor-screened, GMP, cold-chain, lyophilized actives are expensive raw materials with batch variability and supply constraints — opposite of the brand's "buy commodity bases, invest in the brand" default. This is a hero-ingredient cost structure, not a base-cost structure.
  • Supply considerations: donor-dependent supply, lot-to-lot growth-factor variability (standardization burden), cold-chain logistics, and ethical/consent sourcing of human blood components. Diligence the supplier's donor-screening, pathogen-reduction, and regulatory documentation thoroughly.
  • Engineered alternative to consider: recombinant single growth factors (sh-polypeptide-X) or fibroblast/stem-cell conditioned media sidestep the human-blood biosafety/perception problem and are easier to standardize and label — at the cost of the "platelet lysate" story and the multi-factor synergy.

10) Hype vs evidence

Claim in the market Reality
"Growth factors from your platelets regenerate skin" True biologically at the cell level / when injected; unproven that a leave-on serum delivers them through intact skin at meaningful dose.
"Clinically proven anti-aging" The strong clinical data are injection PRP (and even the best RCT, Alam 2018, was objectively negative). The one topical human study is uncontrolled.
"Boosts collagen" One uncontrolled topical histology study showed ↑ collagen fibril thickness; not a controlled, replicated, causal result — and it's a drug-like claim regardless.
"Standardized, consistent dose" Platelet derivatives are notoriously non-standardized; this is the field's #1 methodological criticism.
"Exosome-powered regeneration" FDA: no exosome product is approved; such claims violate federal law. Major red flag.
"Safe — it's natural/from blood" "From blood" is the risk, not the reassurance (HIV transmission cluster; HCT/P/biologic classification questions). Safe only with rigorous GMP/donor-screened allogeneic sourcing.

Net: The science is genuinely interesting and the senescence/NF-κB story aligns beautifully with a skin-longevity narrative — but the topical human evidence is thin, the delivery physics are unfavorable, and the sourcing/regulatory profile is heavy. This is a high-hype, premium-priced category where evidence lags marketing.

11) Verdict for the brand

⚠️ CONDITIONAL — and leaning toward "skip for v1."

Reasoning: - For the brand thesis (premium barrier-repair, lean routine, claims discipline, evidence over hype): platelet lysate is a seductive but awkward fit. It offers a premium, differentiated, "skin-longevity"-coded story (senescence/inflammaging/NF-κB) that few mass brands can credibly tell — genuine white-space appeal. But it collides with three of the brand's stated values: (1) claims discipline — its whole appeal is drug-adjacent structure/function language that legal will gut; (2) evidence over hype — leave-on topical proof is currently one uncontrolled study; (3) buy commodity bases / invest in brand — this is an expensive, variable, cold-chain, regulatorily-fraught hero raw material, the opposite of a commodity base. - The killer trio: (a) delivery (growth factors don't readily cross intact skin), (b) regulatory (human-blood-derived; HCT/P/biologic risk; active FDA exosome enforcement nearby), (c) sourcing/perception ("it's blood"; HIV-cluster headlines attached to the category).

Recommendation: - Do NOT make it a hero of v1. It cannot carry compliant, evidence-backed claims today, and it would import outsized regulatory and reputational risk for an early-stage brand. - Conditional path if revisited later: only as a premium add-on / limited "regenerative" line if and only if the brand secures (1) a GMP, FDA-registered-blood-bank, pathogen-reduced, lyophilized, INCI-clean supplier; (2) regulatory counsel sign-off on classification + claims + target markets; (3) a delivery rationale stronger than hope (controlled penetration/efficacy data, or a microneedle-paired clinical protocol); and (4) at least one brand-funded controlled topical study to substantiate appearance claims. Until then, treat the recombinant sh-polypeptide GFs or conditioned-media actives as the lower-risk way to tell a "growth factor" story. - Default for now: ❌/⚠️ — shortlist as CONDITIONAL, do not formulate into v1. Log in ../../ideas/considering.md as a conditional/parking-lot item with the gating conditions above; if the brand decides the regulatory/sourcing burden is unacceptable, move to not-considering.md with reason "delivery physics + human-blood regulatory/biosafety burden + thin topical evidence."

Longevity note (skin-longevity framing): Of all this ingredient's properties, the senescence-suppression / SASP / NF-κB ("senomorphic") mechanism is the most on-thesis for a skin-longevity (not just "anti-wrinkle") brand — it speaks to the hallmarks-of-aging language the category is moving toward. If the brand ever wants a flagship "cellular aging" hero, this mechanism is compelling; the delivery and regulatory reality are what currently disqualify this format of it. A senomorphic story may be more safely told via better-delivered, better-regulated actives.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Conducted 2026-06-19. Method: independently re-checked the four load-bearing citations (Li 2022, Wyles 2024, Alam 2018, Quinlan 2023) against their primary sources for (a) human vs. topical applicability, (b) whether the study exists and says what is claimed, (c) industry funding / sample size. The remaining injection-PRP citations were spot-checked against the dossier's own framing, which already flags them as injection-only.

Overall grade: D → low C for a leave-on topical platelet-lysate cosmetic. There is no strong human-topical RCT (would be A) and no moderate controlled human-topical evidence (would be B). The entire leave-on human evidence base is one uncontrolled, non-randomized, manufacturer-conducted study (Wyles 2024) plus a heterogeneous category review showing only modest effects (Quinlan 2023). The strong data are in-vitro and injected (not topical). The dossier's own self-assessment ("topical leave-on = weak") is correct and, if anything, this verification makes the case slightly worse than the dossier originally framed it. Graded D as a standalone topical actor; nudged to a generous C only because the in-vitro mechanism is genuinely well-evidenced and one (biased) topical histology signal exists.

What checked out (citations real and accurately represented): - Alam 2018 (JAMA Dermatology) — verified exactly: split-face RCT, n=27 enrolled / 19 analyzed, intradermal PRP vs saline; masked dermatologist raters found no significant objective difference; only patient self-assessment favored PRP for texture (P=.02) and wrinkles (P=.03). The dossier represents this faithfully, including the "objectively negative" framing. (Injection, single session, small n.) - Quinlan 2023 (J Cosmet Dermatol) — verified: 33 studies (9 RCTs, 24 case series), 1,180 participants, 23 preparations, "modest" effects, participant- > investigator-rated, 3 comparative RCTs showed no between-treatment difference. Accurately summarized. - Wyles 2024 (J Drugs Dermatol) — design verified exactly: prospective, single-arm, non-randomized, evaluator-blinded; n=56; mean age 54; topical HPE "Intense Serum" (Rion Aesthetics) BID × 12 weeks; ↑ collagen fibril thickness (P≤0.0001); 87.3% subject-reported improvement; pigment/luminosity (P≤0.001). Real study, accurately described. - Li 2022 (Cell Proliferation, e13212) — paper is real; in-vitro NF-κB/p65 mechanism (siRNA + prostratin rescue), SASP/MMP suppression, and senescence-marker findings are accurately represented.

Corrections made to the dossier (overstatements / errors found): 1. MOUSE DATA WAS INJECTED, NOT TOPICAL (factual error, now corrected). The original Tier-2 entry said Li 2022 applied hPL to mice "topically/locally." The primary source states the mice were subcutaneously injected (0.25 mL weekly × 3 weeks). Injection bypasses the stratum corneum, so this is not evidence of topical efficacy. Corrected in §4 Tier 2 and in the §3 hallmarks table. This was the dossier's most material accuracy problem because it implied a topical animal proof that does not exist. 2. Li 2022's human arm was omitted and is INJECTION (now added, correctly placed). The paper has a human component — but it is a retrospective, uncontrolled, n=30 study using subcutaneous INJECTION (~5 mL × 3 sessions), not topical. Added to the injection tier (§4c) with an explicit warning that the paper's "clinical studies" billing must not be cited as topical human proof. 3. Wyles 2024 conflict of interest was understated (now sharpened). The dossier said "industry-affiliated." Verification shows the senior author (Atta Behfar) is a co-founder of Rion, the manufacturer of the tested serum, and Mayo Clinic holds a licensed financial interest in the underlying platelet-exosome technology (PEP). This is effectively a manufacturer-run study of its own product — a far heavier COI than "affiliated." Sharpened in §4a, §3, and §5.

Main caveats / claims to keep softened: - No human-topical RCT exists. Any efficacy language for a leave-on platelet-lysate product currently rests on one biased uncontrolled study + mechanistic hope (secondary signaling). Do not imply "clinically proven topically." - Delivery physics remain unfavorable and unquantified (growth factors 6–45 kDa vs. ~500 Da barrier limit). The dossier's §5 is sound and should not be diluted. - Best injection RCT (Alam) was objectively negative. When citing PRP's "human data," always pair it with this caveat — the dossier does, correctly. - "From blood" is the safety risk, not reassurance (HIV-cluster precedent; HCT/P-biologic classification questions; active FDA exosome enforcement nearby). Verified as accurately framed. - Hersant n=93 remains cited via secondary sources — verify against primary before any external use (unchanged caveat).

Net: The dossier was already appropriately skeptical and its bottom-line verdict (CONDITIONAL / skip-for-v1) is well-supported and unchanged. The verification did not find hype to deflate so much as two factual delivery-mode errors (mouse + human arms of Li 2022 were injected, not topical) and an understated COI on the single topical study — all now corrected. The corrected picture strengthens the dossier's caution: the topical-human evidence is even thinner and more conflicted than the original draft conveyed.
