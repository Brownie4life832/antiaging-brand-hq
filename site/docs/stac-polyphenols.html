Ingredient: STAC-class Polyphenols — Butein, Myricetin, Delphinidin, Cichoric Acid (INCI: Butein; Myricetin; Delphinidin (usually supplied as Delphinidin Chloride / anthocyanin extract); Chicoric Acid / Echinacea Purpurea Extract)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: NAD+ / sirtuin / AMPK / cellular-energy actives (sirtuin-activating polyphenols — chalcone, flavonol, anthocyanidin, caffeic-acid dimer) Slug: stac-polyphenols

Read this first (the one-paragraph honest version). "STAC" = Sirtuin-Activating Compound, a label minted by the Sinclair lab. Three of these four molecules (butein, and the flavonols that myricetin sits beside; the anthocyanidin class delphinidin belongs to) trace directly to the founding Howitz/Sinclair 2003 Nature screen that also crowned resveratrol — and that whole screen was later shown to be substrate-artifact-dependent: the "activation" appeared with a fluorophore-tagged peptide and vanished with native substrates (Pacholec 2010, Beher 2009). A 2013 Science paper rehabilitated the idea as a real but substrate-selective allosteric effect, so "STAC" is scientifically alive but contested, and — critically — no study shows any of these four raises NAD⁺ or activates sirtuins in living human skin. Where each molecule does have legs is as a classic topical antioxidant/anti-photoaging polyphenol: butein is a potent tyrosinase inhibitor (brightening) and anti-inflammatory; myricetin blocks UVB→Raf→MMP-9 wrinkle formation in mice; delphinidin has the best data of the four — HaCaT + topical-mouse UVB photoprotection (Afaq 2007); cichoric acid is an unstable but strong antioxidant/anti-inflammatory with fibroblast cytoprotection data. The catch across the board: abundant in-vitro/animal data, essentially zero human topical RCTs, and three of the four are chemically fragile (delphinidin and cichoric acid especially). Verdict preview: a genuinely good supporting antioxidant/brightening story (delphinidin or butein as the lead) with an on-brand but claims-hazardous sirtuin/longevity narrative — not a proven topical-longevity hero. Detail below.

This dossier covers four chemically distinct polyphenols grouped only by the marketing/longevity label "STAC." They are not interchangeable and their data must not be pooled.

  • Delphinidin vs. anthocyanins. Free delphinidin (the aglycone) is what most cell studies use, but it is so unstable that commercial actives are usually the glycosides (anthocyanins) or whole berry extracts standardised to delphinidin/anthocyanin content. "Delphinidin extract" on an INCI list almost always means a glycoside-rich botanical, not pure aglycone.
  • "Chicoric" = "cichoric." Same molecule, two spellings; both common in the literature.
  • Butein ≠ butin ≠ butrin. Butein is the chalcone; "butin" is its flavanone cyclization product. Verify CAS on any raw material.

2. Class & Mechanism (how STACs are claimed to work — and the honest version)

2.1 The sirtuin / NAD⁺ / AMPK story (the "longevity" mechanism)

  • Sirtuins (SIRT1–7) are NAD⁺-dependent deacylases. SIRT1 deacetylates targets (p53, FOXO3, PGC-1α, NF-κB p65) to promote mitochondrial biogenesis, autophagy, stress resistance and genomic stability — the canonical "longevity gene" axis. A STAC is a small molecule that allosterically makes SIRT1 deacetylate faster.
  • The founding claim (Howitz/Sinclair 2003, Nature): a screen of polyphenols found resveratrol, butein, piceatannol, isoliquiritigenin, fisetin and quercetin activated SIRT1 and extended yeast lifespan. Reported fold-activations included resveratrol ~13×, piceatannol ~8×, butein ~8.5×, fisetin ~7×, quercetin ~4–5× [1]. This is where butein (and, by class, the flavonols including myricetin) earn the "STAC" badge.
  • AMPK is the cellular energy sensor (low energy → AMPK on → autophagy + mitochondrial biogenesis). Several of these polyphenols also engage AMPK — cichoric acid raises pAMPK and SIRT1 in inflammatory/metabolic models [4][10]; myricetin drives SIRT1→PGC-1α with increased mitochondrial density in muscle [12].

2.2 The honest mechanistic caveats (this is the crux of the dossier)

  1. The original assay was artifact-prone. Pacholec et al. 2010 (JBC) and Beher et al. 2009 (Chem Biol Drug Des) showed resveratrol (and the synthetic STACs) only "activate" SIRT1 when the peptide substrate carries a covalently attached fluorophore (Fluor-de-Lys); with native peptides or full-length proteins, the activation disappears [2][3]. This casts the same shadow over the polyphenol STACs found in the same kind of assay.
  2. Stability/metabolism wrecks the cell-level story. de Boer et al. 2006 is the most important paper for this group: myricetin stimulated recombinant SIRT1 ~3.2-fold under stabilising conditions but strongly inhibited SIRT1 without stabilisation (via H₂O₂ formation), and quercetin stimulated recombinant SIRT1 yet could NOT stimulate SIRT1 inside live cells because its human metabolite (quercetin-3-O-glucuronide) inhibits the enzyme [16]. Conclusion in their words: "extrapolation of in-vitro SIRT1 stimulation to physiological effects should be done with caution." Butein's 8.5-fold figure is from the same fragile in-vitro context.
  3. It is not all artifact, though. Hubbard et al. 2013 (Science) rescued the concept: STACs do activate SIRT1 by a real allosteric "assembly activation" mechanism, but only for substrates bearing large hydrophobic residues C-terminal to the acetyl-lysine, and the effect requires a single residue, Glu230, in SIRT1's N-terminal domain [9]. So sirtuin activation by these scaffolds is real but substrate-selective and context-dependent — not the universal "switch on your longevity genes" the marketing implies.
  4. None of this is shown in human skin. Even granting genuine SIRT1 activation in a tube, no study demonstrates that topical butein/myricetin/delphinidin/cichoric acid raises NAD⁺, activates sirtuins, or extends cell lifespan in living human skin. The sirtuin mechanism is education/story, not a topical efficacy claim.

2.3 What these molecules actually do in skin models (the defensible mechanisms)

Independent of the sirtuin debate, each is a legitimate antioxidant/anti-photoaging polyphenol with skin-relevant actions:

  • Butein: potent direct antioxidant; potent tyrosinase inhibitor (monophenolase IC₅₀ ~10.9 µM, diphenolase ~15.2 µM) → brightening/anti-pigment [7]; anti-inflammatory (NF-κB, COX-2, TNF-α suppression — chalcone class effect) [8]; Nrf2 induction.
  • Myricetin: blocks the UVB→Raf→MEK/ERK→AP-1→MMP-9 cascade by binding Raf directly (ATP-non-competitive) → less collagen/elastin breakdown [11]; strong Nrf2/antioxidant flavonol; ↓ JNK and ROS in keratinocytes [11][13].
  • Delphinidin: direct + Nrf2 antioxidant; ↓ UVB-induced ROS, lipid peroxidation, DNA damage (8-OHdG, CPDs) and apoptosis in keratinocytes; ↓ MMP-1; modulates Bax/Bcl-2 [5][6]; anti-inflammatory, antiangiogenic.
  • Cichoric acid: strong DPPH/ROS scavenger + Nrf2/SOD↑; NF-κB↓ / cytokine↓ anti-inflammatory; fibroblast cytoprotection (↑ proliferation, ↑ GSH, ↓ caspase-3/7/9, ↓ ROS) [10][15]; SIRT1/AMPK in metabolic models [4]; C. elegans lifespan extension via AAK-2(AMPK)/SKN-1(Nrf2) [4].

Class fit (NAD+/sirtuin/AMPK): these are arguably the purest "STAC story" ingredients after resveratrol — butein literally co-founded the category. That is exactly why the claims discipline in §8 matters: the narrative is stronger than the topical-human evidence.

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core)

The single biggest honesty trap here is borrowing the sirtuin/longevity halo (in-vitro + oral) to imply a topical benefit. Read each tier's format label.

TIER 1 — In vitro (cell & enzyme) — abundant, mechanistically rich, lowest translational weight

Sirtuin / enzyme assays (the contested foundation): - Howitz, Bitterman, Sinclair et al. 2003 (Nature 425:191). Polyphenol screen: butein ~8.5× SIRT1 activation; resveratrol, piceatannol, fisetin, quercetin, isoliquiritigenin also active; lowered SIRT1's K_m for substrate and NAD⁺; extended S. cerevisiae lifespan. The category's origin — and the source of the artifact controversy. Source [1]. - de Boer et al. 2006 (Mech Ageing Dev). Myricetin ~3.19× recombinant-SIRT1 stimulation under stabilising conditions — but inhibited SIRT1 without stabilisation (H₂O₂ artifact); quercetin failed to stimulate SIRT1 in live HT29 cells (its glucuronide metabolite inhibits SIRT1). The most important caveat paper for this class. Source [16]. - Pacholec et al. 2010 (JBC 285:8340); Beher et al. 2009. Resveratrol & synthetic STACs activate SIRT1 only with fluorophore-tagged peptides, not native substrates → the original assays were artifact-prone. Sources [2], [3]. - Hubbard et al. 2013 (Science 339:1216). STAC activation is real but substrate-selective (needs hydrophobic residues C-terminal to acetyl-Lys; requires SIRT1-Glu230) — an allosteric "assembly activation" mechanism. Source [9].

Butein (skin-relevant): - Antioxidant + anti-tyrosinase kinetics. Butein is a potent radical scavenger and tyrosinase inhibitor (uncompetitive monophenolase IC₅₀ ~10.88 µM; mixed diphenolase ~15.20 µM) — a credible brightening mechanism, chalcone-class. Source [7]. - Anti-inflammatory. Chalcone butein suppresses NF-κB, COX-2, TNF-α, CCL2 across cell models — relevant to inflammaging. Source [8].

Myricetin (skin-relevant): - UVB→Raf→MMP-9 blockade. Myricetin binds Raf (ATP-non-competitive), blocking MEK/ERK and MMP-9 induction in keratinocytes; ↓ MDA, H₂O₂, JNK. Source [11]. - Myricetin-3-O-galactoside reversed UVA-induced MMP-1↑/collagen↓ in HaCaT + dermal fibroblasts (MAPK/AP-1↓, TGFβ/Smad↑). Source [13].

Delphinidin (skin-relevant): - HaCaT keratinocytes (Afaq 2007). 1–20 µM delphinidin pretreatment before UVB (15–30 mJ/cm²): ↓ lipid peroxidation, ↓ 8-OHdG + CPDs (DNA damage), ↓ caspase activation, modulated Bax/Bcl-2/Bcl-xL → protection from UVB apoptosis. Source [5]. - Anthocyanin-class reviews corroborate delphinidin's antioxidant/anti-MMP/anti-apoptotic skin actions. Source [6].

Cichoric acid (skin-relevant): - Fibroblast cytoprotection. Against oxidative (doxorubicin) damage in fibroblasts: ↑ proliferation, ↑ GSH, ↓ ROS, ↓ caspase-3/7/9 (time/dose-dependent). Source [15]. - Antioxidant/anti-inflammatory/SIRT1-AMPK & C. elegans lifespan via AAK-2/SKN-1. Sources [4], [10], [14].

TIER 2 — Animal (mouse) — the strongest tier these molecules reach for topical skin

  • Delphinidin — topical mouse (Afaq 2007). Topical delphinidin (1 mg/0.1 mL DMSO) on SKH-1 hairless mouse skin before UVB → ↓ apoptosis, ↓ DNA-damage markers in vivo. The best in-vivo topical-skin datapoint of the four. Source [5].
  • Myricetin — topical mouse (Jung 2010). 1 or 5 nmol myricetin (in acetone) topically, 3×/week × 15 weeks of chronic UVB on SKH-1 mice → ↓ wrinkle formation, ↓ epidermal thickening, ↓ MMP-9, ↓ Raf activity. ⚠️ Note the nanomolar dose — efficacy at trace amounts in a solvent vehicle does not predict performance in a real cosmetic emulsion. Source [11].
  • Butein / cichoric acid — no dedicated topical-skin animal study found (systemic/oral animal data exist for other endpoints). [unverified for topical skin]
  • Translational caveat: hairless-mouse skin penetration and biology differ markedly from human; these support plausibility, not efficacy claims.

TIER 3 — Human ORAL / systemic (the longevity fame — NOT topical)

  • Myricetin oral/systemic: SIRT1/PGC-1α–driven mitochondrial benefits, endurance and metabolic effects — animal (mouse) and mechanistic, not human-skin. Source [12].
  • Delphinidin oral: notoriously poor oral bioavailability (t₁/₂ ≈ 30 min; degrades to gallic acid + phloroglucinaldehyde); most "delphinidin" exposure in humans is actually metabolites. Sources [17], [18].
  • Cichoric acid oral: antioxidant/anti-inflammatory/antihyperglycaemic, immunostimulant (its Echinacea context) — preclinical/early; unstable, limited PK. Sources [4], [14].
  • Butein oral: anticancer/anti-inflammatory preclinical; Rhus verniciflua (butein-containing) extracts tolerated in small oncology series. Source [8].
  • Relevance to skin: none of this transfers to a topical claim. It is systemic pharmacology.

TIER 4 — Human TOPICAL (what actually matters for a cream)

  • There is no published human topical RCT — and, to the depth of this search, no controlled human topical clinical study at all — for butein, myricetin, delphinidin (as an isolated active), or cichoric acid as standalone cosmetic actives, as of June 2026. [unverified — absence of evidence after multi-source search, not a confirmed null]
  • The closest human-relevant data are: (a) delphinidin-rich berry/anthocyanin extracts appearing in cosmetic anti-aging formulas (efficacy attributable to the blend/extract, not isolated delphinidin — see anti-age-berry-extracts.md); (b) Echinacea purpurea (cichoric-acid-bearing) extracts with cosmeceutical/wound-healing positioning, again as whole extracts. Neither isolates the named molecule's contribution.
  • Bottom line by tier: Enzyme/in-vitro = rich but artifact-shadowed. Animal (topical mouse) = genuinely supportive for delphinidin and myricetin photoprotection. Oral/systemic = the longevity fame, non-transferable. Human topical = none. Treat all four as mechanistically promising, topically unproven in humans.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • The shared problem: these are polar, multi-hydroxyl polyphenols that penetrate the stratum corneum poorly and unevenly. More -OH groups = more antioxidant punch but worse permeation and worse stability.
  • Delphinidin — worst stability of the four: stable only at acidic pH, degrades rapidly at neutral/alkaline pH (t₁/₂ ≈ 30 min) to gallic acid; needs acidic formula + encapsulation (cyclodextrin, liposome, nanoemulsion) or use as the glycoside/anthocyanin form. Sources [17], [18].
  • Cichoric acid — explicitly described as "unstable," soluble in ethanol/methanol/hot water; hydrolyses to caffeic + tartaric acid; reviews recommend structural stabilisation. Source [14].
  • Myricetin — very low aqueous solubility, oxidation-prone (6 -OH groups); benefits from glycoside forms or encapsulation; the mouse efficacy used a solvent (acetone) vehicle, not a cosmetic base. Sources [11], [13].
  • Butein — lipophilic chalcone, more skin-compatible than the others, but oxidation-prone and strongly coloured (yellow-orange) which limits use level. Source [7].
  • Implication: any real formulation needs acidic-to-neutral pH, antioxidant network, opaque/airless packaging, and ideally encapsulation or a stabilised glycoside/derivative. Free aglycones in a simple cream will likely degrade before they work.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: no validated topical use-levels exist (no human studies). By analogy to similar polyphenols, expect ~0.05–0.5% for the colored/potent actives (butein, delphinidin) and up to ~1% for myricetin/cichoric, but this is inference, not data. Butein and delphinidin add visible color — formulate low.
  • pH: keep mildly acidic (~4–5.5) — essential for delphinidin stability (flavylium cation is acid-stable) and helpful for the rest. Avoid alkaline systems entirely.
  • Stability (the central challenge): all four oxidize; delphinidin and cichoric acid are the most fragile. Mitigations: antioxidant network (vitamin C/E, ferulic acid), chelators, opaque airless packaging, low water activity or anhydrous bases, and encapsulation (liposome/cyclodextrin/SLN). Consider glycoside forms (anthocyanins for delphinidin; myricitrin for myricetin) for stability at the cost of penetration.
  • Pairings (rational synergy): classic antioxidant-network partners (ferulic acid + vitamin E ± C); niacinamide and tranexamic acid alongside butein for a brightening stack; berry-extract delivery for delphinidin. Compatible with peptides.
  • Conflicts / cautions: alkaline actives; high-water "clear gel" formats (color + instability); strong oxidisers. Not a substitute for SPF.
  • Sensory: color is the practical limiter — butein (yellow), delphinidin (purple/blue→red, pH-shifting), myricetin (yellow) will tint a cream and can stain. Cichoric acid is comparatively low-color. Plan for product color and possible textile staining at higher loads.

7. Safety

  • General tolerability: as dietary polyphenols (berries, tea, chicory, echinacea) all four have a benign oral safety reputation. Dedicated topical human safety/sensitization data are lacking for the isolated molecules. [unverified for isolated topical use]
  • Butein — the main flag: butein is a Michael-acceptor chalcone (reactive α,β-unsaturated ketone) and a documented pro-apoptotic/cytotoxic agent in cancer-cell studies, with high doses cytotoxic to normal cells (e.g., Neuro2A) [8][19]. It is also sourced from lacquer-tree (Rhus/Toxicodendron) family plants — relatives of poison ivy/sumac — so sourcing must guarantee absence of urushiol-type sensitizers. Treat butein as low-concentration only and screen for sensitization.
  • Delphinidin: generally low-concern antioxidant; degradation product is gallic acid (also benign). Main issue is instability/staining, not toxicity.
  • Myricetin: flavonol with a pro-oxidant face — under non-stabilised conditions it generates H₂O₂ (the very effect that flipped its SIRT1 result negative) [16]; argues for antioxidant-network co-formulation and PM use. Some flavonols are weak photosensitizer candidates in vitro — keep daytime use SPF-paired. [unverified for myricetin specifically]
  • Cichoric acid / Echinacea: Echinacea is a known contact allergen / Asteraceae-family sensitizer — anyone with ragweed/daisy/chrysanthemum allergy can react. If delivered as echinacea extract, flag Asteraceae cross-reactivity. Isolated cichoric acid is lower-risk but under-studied topically.
  • Sun sensitivity: no compound here is an SPF; myricetin's pro-oxidant potential and delphinidin's role as a UV-absorbing pigment both argue for PM positioning and/or SPF pairing, and never marketing "sun protection."
  • Pregnancy/lactation: no topical-specific data; echinacea in particular has cautionary oral-pregnancy literature. Use conservative, non-specific copy ("consult your doctor"); do not affirmatively market to pregnant users. [unverified — no dedicated topical-pregnancy study]
  • Sourcing/sustainability: butein from lacquer/dahlia (urushiol caution); delphinidin from berry by-streams (good upcycling story) or synthesis; myricetin from bark/tea; cichoric acid from echinacea/chicory (renewable). Specify purity, isomer/glycoside form, and allergen screening in supplier specs.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic vs. drug line: as topical antioxidants for appearance (defense, tone, fine-line appearance), all four sit on the cosmetic side. They cross toward drug (US FDA structure/function) the moment copy says they "activate sirtuins / longevity genes," "raise NAD⁺," "extend skin-cell lifespan," "boost collagen by X%," "prevent/treat photoaging or skin cancer," or "sun protection."
  • The STAC/longevity trap (the #1 hazard for this group): the entire appeal is the sirtuin-activating story — which is (a) contested even in vitro (Pacholec/Beher; de Boer's metabolism caveat), (b) never demonstrated in human skin, and (c) for myricetin/quercetin literally reversed by their own metabolism. Marketing a topical "switches on your longevity sirtuins" cream is therefore both a compliance risk and a scientific overreach. Keep sirtuin/NAD⁺/AMPK strictly as mechanism education, clearly framed as "how these molecules behave in research."
  • ✅ Lower-risk cosmetic language: "antioxidant-rich polyphenols," "helps defend the look of skin against environmental/oxidative stress," "helps visibly even tone and brightness" (butein/delphinidin), "helps protect the appearance of firmness," "inspired by the science of cellular-energy pathways." Naming the STAC/sirtuin research as storytelling is acceptable if benefits stay appearance-level.
  • ⚠️ Avoid without substantiation + legal: "sirtuin-activating skincare," "topical NAD⁺ activation," "turns on longevity genes," "boosts collagen," "anti-glycation that reverses sugar damage," "DNA repair / prevents DNA damage" (the delphinidin 8-OHdG/CPD data are mouse/cell), "sun protection," any cancer/disease implication.
  • Substantiation reality: with zero human topical RCTs, numeric or efficacy claims must be reserved for in-house consumer-perception or instrumental testing on the final formula. Mechanism ≠ proof.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: mid to mid-high for purified single molecules (butein, delphinidin chloride, myricetin, cichoric acid are specialty fine chemicals, often >95% from research-grade suppliers; cosmetic-grade tonnage is limited). Lower if delivered as standardised botanical extracts (bilberry/maqui anthocyanins for delphinidin; echinacea for cichoric acid; tea/berry for myricetin) — which is also the more practical, better-stabilised route.
  • Supply: isolated cosmetic-grade single STAC actives are not mainstream commodity raws — expect to source via extract suppliers or specialty houses; verify CAS, purity, isomer/glycoside form, color, and allergen screening (urushiol for butein; Asteraceae for echinacea-derived cichoric acid).
  • Practical recommendation: for a premium, claims-disciplined product, lead with delphinidin (as a stabilised berry-anthocyanin extract) or butein (low %, for brightening), inside a ferulic/vitamin-E/C antioxidant network, acidic pH, opaque airless packaging, ideally encapsulated. Treat "STAC/sirtuin" as the story layer, not the substantiation.

10. Hype vs. evidence (marketing overreach to avoid)

  • "Sirtuin-activating skincare that switches on your longevity genes." Overreach. The mechanism is contested in vitro and never shown in human skin; for myricetin/quercetin it can even reverse in live cells/metabolism [16].
  • "Topical NAD⁺ boost." No evidence any of these raises NAD⁺ in skin (and NAD⁺ biology is a precursor story, not an STAC story).
  • "Clinically proven anti-aging polyphenols." There are no human topical clinical trials of these four as actives.
  • "Repairs / prevents DNA damage." Delphinidin's 8-OHdG/CPD data are mouse + HaCaT, and "DNA repair" is drug-adjacent language.
  • "Reverses sun/sugar damage." In-vitro/animal anti-photoaging and class anti-glycation don't license a "reverses damage" promise on human skin.
  • "Powerful brightener" (single-active claim). Butein's tyrosinase data are in-vitro only; no human depigmentation trial.
  • Honest claim available: "antioxidant-rich polyphenols — drawn from the same family of compounds studied in cellular-energy/sirtuin longevity research — that help defend skin's appearance against daily oxidative stress, with delphinidin and butein lending tone/brightness support." Defensible.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ SUPPORTING / CONDITIONAL — a strong story ingredient and a credible antioxidant-brightening supporting cast, but NOT a proven topical-longevity hero.

One-line reasoning: These four are real antioxidant/anti-photoaging polyphenols with a uniquely on-brand "sirtuin/cellular-energy longevity" narrative (butein literally co-founded the STAC category), but their topical-human evidence is nonexistent (no RCTs), their sirtuin mechanism is scientifically contested and never shown in human skin, and three of the four are chemically fragile and/or coloring — so they earn a supporting role at best, ideally as stabilised delphinidin/anthocyanin or low-dose butein inside an antioxidant network, with sirtuin/NAD⁺ used as education, never as a claim.

  • Longevity-relevance note: This group is the brand's best pure "STAC / sirtuin" storytelling material after resveratrol — and therefore demands the most claims discipline. Use it to teach the sirtuin/AMPK/NAD⁺ longevity mechanism (clearly as research, not promise) while delivering only appearance-level benefits. The cleanest substantiation play is delphinidin (best in-vivo topical data of the four) or butein (clearest single mechanism: brightening) — not the whole quartet at once.
  • Pick-one guidance: if a single lead is wanted, delphinidin (stabilised berry-anthocyanin form) for antioxidant/photo-defense storytelling, or butein (low %) for a brightening angle. Myricetin and cichoric acid are better as network supporting actives than headliners.
  • Next steps if pursued: (1) choose lead molecule + stabilised/glycoside vs. free form; (2) lock acidic pH + antioxidant network + opaque airless + encapsulation; (3) screen sourcing for urushiol (butein) and Asteraceae (echinacea-cichoric) allergens; (4) PM positioning + SPF pairing; (5) reserve all efficacy claims for in-house testing on the final formula. Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified: 2026-06-19. Skeptical pass checking (a) whether each headline claim is genuinely human + topical or borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral data, (b) whether cited studies are real and say what is claimed, and (c) the strength of the sirtuin-activation foundation.

Overall evidence grade: C− / D+ for topical human efficacy (mechanistically interesting, topically unproven)

These are real antioxidant polyphenols whose topical human anti-aging case is essentially empty — no human topical RCTs were found for any of the four as isolated actives. The strongest data is animal topical photoprotection (delphinidin, myricetin) and rich in-vitro work, all of which is plausibility, not proof. The defining "STAC/sirtuin" mechanism is contested at the enzyme level (Pacholec 2010, Beher 2009), partially rehabilitated but substrate-selective (Hubbard 2013), and for the flavonols can reverse in live cells (de Boer 2006). This grade reflects the ingredients' thin topical evidence, not a defect in the write-up, which is deliberately conservative.

What was independently verified (and held up)

  • Howitz/Sinclair 2003 (Source [1]) — confirmed. Butein is a genuine original STAC (~8.5× SIRT1); resveratrol/piceatannol/fisetin/quercetin/isoliquiritigenin co-listed. Real paper, Nature 425:191.
  • Pacholec 2010 [2] & Beher 2009 [3] — confirmed real and accurately characterised (fluorophore-substrate artifact; activation vanishes with native substrates).
  • Hubbard 2013 [9] — confirmed (Science; substrate-selective allosteric activation; Glu230 required) — correctly used to show the mechanism is real-but-conditional, not debunked-or-universal.
  • de Boer 2006 [16] — confirmed and load-bearing. Myricetin 3.19× under stabilising conditions but inhibitory without (H₂O₂); quercetin fails in live cells. Accurately reported as the key caveat.
  • Afaq 2007 [5] — confirmed to the detail (HaCaT 1–20 µM; SKH-1 topical 1 mg; UVB; ↓8-OHdG/CPD/apoptosis). The strongest topical-skin datapoint, correctly labeled mouse/cell.
  • Jung 2010 [11] — confirmed (myricetin, SKH-1, 1–5 nmol topical, 15 wk UVB, ↓MMP-9/wrinkles via Raf). The nanomolar/solvent-vehicle caveat is correctly flagged.

Main caveats (kept honest in the body)

  1. No human topical evidence for any of the four as isolated actives (Tier 4 is empty). Marked clearly; the §11 verdict does not pretend otherwise.
  2. The sirtuin story is the trap — contested in vitro, never shown in human skin, metabolism-reversible for the flavonols. §2.2, §8, §10 all hold this line.
  3. Two molecules are chemically fragile (delphinidin t½≈30 min at neutral pH; cichoric acid "unstable") and three are coloring — real formulation limiters, not hidden.
  4. Butein safety flag (Michael-acceptor chalcone, cytotoxic at high dose, lacquer-tree sourcing/urushiol) and echinacea/Asteraceae allergen flag for cichoric acid are genuine and disclosed.
  5. Cichoric-acid skin data is the thinnest — fibroblast cytoprotection [15] is oxidative-(doxorubicin) not UV/skin-aging-specific; the "UVA fibroblast senescence/MMP-3" line that surfaced in early searching could not be pinned to a confirmable primary source and was therefore not asserted as fact in the body. The §3 collagen/ECM table line attributing MMP-3↓ to cichoric acid has now been softened to "caffeic-acid class generally, not confirmed for cichoric acid in skin." [unverified]

Independent re-verification (second adversarial pass, 2026-06-19)

Every load-bearing citation was re-checked against primary sources / PubMed in a fresh skeptical pass; all held up to the stated detail:

  • [1] Howitz/Sinclair 2003 — confirmed (Nature 425:191; butein an original STAC; resveratrol/piceatannol/fisetin/quercetin/isoliquiritigenin co-listed).
  • [16] de Boer 2006 — confirmed to the number (myricetin 3.19× under stabilising conditions, inhibitory/H₂O₂ without; quercetin fails in live HT29 cells via its 3-O-glucuronide; authors' own "extrapolate with caution" conclusion).
  • [2] Pacholec 2010 — confirmed (JBC 285:8340; activation is an artifact of fluorophore-tagged peptides; vanishes with native p53 peptide and full-length p53 / AceCS1).
  • [9] Hubbard 2013 — confirmed (Science 339:1216; Glu230 critical across 117 STACs; hydrophobic substrate motifs e.g. PGC-1α/FOXO3a; activation-defective SIRT1 blocks STAC effects in cells).
  • [5] Afaq 2007 — confirmed to the detail (HaCaT 1–20 µM; SKH-1 hairless-mouse topical; ↓8-OHdG/CPD/apoptosis). Genuinely the best in-vivo topical datapoint of the four; correctly labeled mouse/cell, not human.
  • [11] Jung 2010 — confirmed including the dose: "200 µl acetone containing 0, 1, or 5 nmol myricetin," UVB 0.18 J/cm² 3×/wk × 15 wk, SKH-1. The nanomolar / solvent-vehicle caveat in the body is well-founded.
  • [7] butein tyrosinase — confirmed (monophenolase IC₅₀ ~10.88 µM, diphenolase ~15.20 µM; PMC10525132).
  • [4] cichoric acid → AMPK/SIRT1 + C. elegans lifespan — confirmed; there is even a dedicated primary paper (AAK-2/SKN-1, J. Agric. Food Chem. 2019, PMID 30835107) beyond the cited review. Correctly kept as oral/model-organism context, not a topical claim.

No fabricated or misrepresented citations were found. No industry-funded efficacy study is being leaned on (the Sinclair-lab papers are used to establish the contested origin of the STAC concept, not to support an efficacy claim — appropriate). The only correction needed this pass was the MMP-3/cichoric table line above.

Net

The dossier is adversarially honest: it grants the on-brand longevity story, refuses the unearned topical claims, grades the topical-human evidence as effectively absent, and points to delphinidin (best in-vivo data) or butein (clearest single mechanism: brightening) as the only defensible leads. Grade C−/D+ reflects the ingredients, not the write-up.
