Ingredient: Celyscence™ — Silybum marianum (milk thistle) FLOWER extract, a topical senotherapeutic (INCI: Glycerin (and) Aqua/Water (and) Silybum Marianum Extract)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Senotherapeutics & senolytics (botanical) · Slug: celyscence-milk-thistle

Exhaustive, brutally-honest literature review. Two things to internalize before reading further: 1. The slug calls this "milk thistle senolytic," but the active is NOT silymarin/silibinin (the famous milk-thistle seed compound). Celyscence is a seedless FLOWER extract deliberately made to contain <0.1% silymarin; the proposed actives are flavonoids led by apigenin. So the large classical "milk thistle protects skin from UV" literature — which is about the seed/silymarin — does not transfer to this ingredient. Treat that as a borrowed-evidence trap. 2. Essentially all efficacy data are generated by the patent holder and its development partner (Clariant / Lucas Meyer Cosmetics + Biospectrum) — the peer-reviewed paper is co-authored by three Clariant employees, two of whom (Fréchet, Chajra) are the named patent inventors. (Note: the funding line on the paper is actually a South Korean government grant [Ministry of Trade, Industry & Energy, to the Biospectrum author], not Clariant money — so "Clariant-funded" overstates it; the conflict is authorship/commercial-interest, not declared funding.) The one peer-reviewed paper is in-vitro only; the human data are an unpublished company marketing study. No independent replication exists as of 2026-06.

1. Identity

  • Trade name: Celyscence™ — a branded cosmetic active from Clariant Active Ingredients / Lucas Meyer Cosmetics (co-developed with Biospectrum / BioSpectrum Life Science Institute, South Korea). Launched April 2021. [1][2][3]
  • Botanical source: Silybum marianum (milk thistle), FLOWER part, seeds removed. This is the defining feature. Traditional milk-thistle skincare uses the seed (rich in silymarin ~70–80%); Celyscence uses the flower, processed to contain <0.1% (down to ~0%) silymarin. [4][5][7]
  • INCI: Silybum Marianum Extract, supplied in a carrier of Glycerin (and) Aqua/Water (a water-soluble, glycerin/water botanical extract). [6][13] (Note: "Silybum Marianum Extract" is the generic INCI; it does not by itself signal flower-vs-seed or silymarin content — identity must be confirmed against the supplier spec.)
  • Physical form: yellow to slightly brown water-soluble liquid. [13]
  • Naturality: 100% natural origin (ISO 16128) and COSMOS-approved. [13] A genuine plus for a "clean/natural longevity" angle.
  • Proposed active constituents: apigenin (reported >0.24% w/w in the flower extract) plus a flavonoid/phenolic mix (taxifolin, luteolin, quercetin, kaempferol, myricetin, chlorogenic acid, scopoletin/scopolin, etc.). Critically, the authors state no silymarin peak was detected in the flower extract. [5][13]
  • Extraction (per patent): hydroethanolic (≈70% ethanol/water), ambient temp, from seedless flowers; final actives delivered in glycerin/water. [4]

Nomenclature caution. "Milk thistle senolytic" is marketing shorthand. The molecule doing the (claimed) senolytic work is not the classical milk-thistle hepatoprotective compound (silibinin/silymarin) — it is the flower's flavonoid fraction (apigenin-led). Anyone importing "silymarin is a proven skin antioxidant/photoprotectant" data onto Celyscence is making a category error. See §4 and §10.

2. Class & mechanism

Celyscence is positioned as a topical botanical senotherapeutic with a claimed dual mechanism — both senolytic AND senomorphic (most actives are only one or the other). [1][2][4][5]

  • Senolytic = selectively kills existing senescent ("zombie") cells. Celyscence claims to do this by inducing apoptosis selectively in senescent dermal fibroblasts via the caspase-3 / PARP-cleavage pathway, sparing healthy cells (claimed >1.5-fold selectivity for senescent vs. normal cells). [4][5]
  • Senomorphic = suppresses the senescent phenotype (the SASP) without necessarily killing the cell. Celyscence claims to down-regulate SASP factors — IL-6, TNF-α, MMP-1 (collagenase) — and to "reprogram" senescent fibroblasts back toward a younger, collagen-producing phenotype. [1][2][5]

Net cell-level story (as claimed): clear a fraction of senescent fibroblasts + quiet the SASP of those remaining → less collagen-degrading/inflammatory signalling → the surviving young fibroblast pool proliferates faster and restores type-I collagen synthesis → (downstream, hypothesized) firmer, more even, more hydrated skin. The proposed molecular driver is apigenin and related flavonoids, not silymarin. [5]

A caution worth stating plainly: "kills zombie cells AND reprograms them back to young" is a best-case in-vitro narrative. In a real cream on real skin, how many senescent cells are actually reached and cleared is unknown (see §5).

4. Evidence review by tier ← core of this dossier

Overarching honesty flags: 1. The single peer-reviewed paper is in-vitro only; its three Clariant co-authors are company employees, two of whom (Fréchet, Chajra) are the named patent inventors (the other co-authors are at Biospectrum, the development partner). The paper declares "No authors have competing interests" — which, given the patent and Clariant employment, is a disclosure worth scrutinizing. (Its stated funding is a South Korean government grant to the Biospectrum author, not Clariant — so the conflict here is commercial authorship, not undisclosed industry funding.) 2. There is no published, peer-reviewed human (topical) RCT. The human data are a company marketing/clinical study reported only in press releases and trade media — design and statistics are not independently verifiable. 3. No independent (non-Clariant/Biospectrum) replication exists as of 2026-06.

Tier 1 — In-vitro & ex-vivo (the only real evidence base)

Woo J, Shin S, Garandeau D, Chajra H, Fréchet M, Jung E (2021). "Senotherapeutic-like effect of Silybum marianum flower extract revealed on human skin cells." PLoS ONE 16(12):e0260545. [5] (Open access; this dossier read the full PMC text.) The seminal paper behind Celyscence. - Models: replicative-senescent human dermal fibroblasts (HDFs) (≥40 passages), plus stress-induced senescence (H₂O₂ 200 µM, doxorubicin 100 nM, etoposide 10 µM); paracrine effects tested on normal keratinocytes via conditioned medium. - Concentrations: 50–200 µg/mL flower extract (SMFE), 3-day treatment. Senolytic apoptosis appeared at 100–200 µg/mL; senomorphic (SASP suppression) effects at lower ~50 µg/mL. - Senescence markers ↓: SA-β-gal+ cells −40.3% (100–200 µg/mL); p16^INK4A reduced (4.27-fold → 0.54-fold); p21, p53, and γ-H2A.X (DNA damage) all dose-dependently reduced. - Senolysis (selective): Annexin-V apoptosis rose to 29.87% at 200 µg/mL in senescent cells only; cleaved caspase-3 ↑ and PARP degradation ↑ — i.e., apoptosis via the caspase pathway, selective for senescent cells. Positive control: ABT-737 (a reference senolytic). - SASP ↓: IL-6 −59% and MMP-1 −73% (~100 µg/mL). - Collagen: type-I procollagen (which had fallen −67.3% in senescent HDFs) rose to +140% at 200 µg/mL; positive control TGF-β. - Function: in young+senescent co-culture, removing senescent burden increased young-cell proliferation and shortened doubling time. - Paracrine: conditioned medium from SMFE-treated senescent HDFs reduced SA-β-gal in normal keratinocytes (to ~33–36% of control) — i.e., quieting fibroblast SASP benefited neighbouring cells in vitro. - Key identity finding: "We did not detect any silymarin peak in SMFE"; active flavonoid identified as apigenin (>0.24% w/w). - Authors' OWN limitations (important and candid): "the effective concentration of SMFE in vitro is too high for in vivo application" (100–200 µg/mL vs ~10 µg/mL typical for active plant compounds); they recommend identifying/isolating the actual active compound rather than applying the crude extract as a senolytic drug. This is a striking admission directly from the source paper.

Earlier company-group work: the same Clariant team's senescence program built on prior plant-extract senescence work in the field (e.g., Solidago virgaurea / goldenrod, Lämmermann et al., npj Aging Mech Dis 2018 — a different plant, useful only as class context, not evidence for Celyscence). [12]

Patent disclosures (same in-vitro dataset, broader claims): WO2022073709A1 / US20230330003A1, "Compositions comprising Silybum marianum extract as a senotherapeutic agent," assignee Clariant International Ltd, inventors Fréchet & Chajra. Confirms: seedless flower, <0.1% silymarin; HDFs + hair-follicle dermal papilla cells; SA-β-gal, p21, caspase-3/PARP, TNF-α/IL-6/MMP-1, type-I collagen; 50–200 ppm, 3 days; ABT-737 control. The patent itself states testing was in-vitro only — no in-vivo data. US application status: pending; the PCT/WO is listed as ceased. [4][7]

Bottom line, Tier 1: a genuinely competent in-vitro senolytic/senomorphic dataset on human fibroblasts — selective apoptosis, SASP suppression, restored collagen synthesis in a dish. But (a) it's a crude extract at concentrations the authors themselves call too high to be realistic in vivo, (b) it's company-authored, and (c) it is the source of all "clears zombie cells / restores collagen" marketing — none of which has been shown to occur in living human skin.

Tier 2 — Animal

None identified. No published rodent/in-vivo animal efficacy or tox studies for the flower extract / Celyscence. The in-vitro→human gap is unbridged by animal work.

Tier 3 — Human, ORAL

Not applicable to this product. Celyscence is a topical cosmetic active; there is no oral Celyscence format or oral study. (Oral milk-thistle/silymarin supplements exist for liver health, but that is a different plant part and compound and is irrelevant to topical skin efficacy here.)

Tier 4 — Human, TOPICAL

Company "clinical" study (split-panel, vs placebo) — reported only in press releases / trade media; no peer-reviewed publication located. [3][8] - Design: split-panel; one group applied a 3% Celyscence cream, the other a placebo, on the neck and chest (décolletage); instrumental measures at 14, 28, 56 days. A shorter 14-day facial arm was also run. - N: 30 women, aged 50–60, with visible neck/décolletage wrinkles. - Reported results: - Hydration: +8.6% at 14 d, +14% at 56 d (placebo −12.6%). The most defensible result. - Décolletage brightness/luminosity: "significantly increased," reported +3.2% at 56 d. - Facial melanin: −5% at 14 d. - Firmness / collagen / contour: described qualitatively ("visible firming," "remodeled contours") — not quantified for wrinkles or actual collagen in the public materials. - Self-assessment: 81% reported "healthy-looking skin" at 28–56 d (subjective). - Limitations: unpublished; company-run; small N; single demographic; outcomes are largely hydration + appearance/pigmentation, the two least specific endpoints; the "senolytic/collagen-restoration" headline is not directly measured in human skin (no biopsy, no in-vivo senescence marker, no collagen quantification reported).

What is actually proven for TOPICAL human use: a 3% cream improved skin hydration and self-perceived radiance, and modestly reduced facial melanin, in a small unpublished company study — i.e., it behaves like a pleasant, glycerin-rich brightening/hydrating botanical. The marquee claims — "clears senescent cells," "restores collagen," "erases zombie cells" — are demonstrated only in cultured cells, at concentrations the authors call unrealistically high, and are borrowed into the human story, not earned in it.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • The proposed active, apigenin (MW ~270 Da), is small and lipophilic — in principle a better penetrant than peptides/proteins; but Celyscence is delivered as a glycerin/water (hydrophilic) extract, and apigenin's aqueous solubility and skin partitioning are poor, so how much functional apigenin reaches dermal fibroblasts (the site of action) is unquantified.
  • The authors' own concentration concern is the crux: senolysis required 100–200 µg/mL in a dish [5], a level they call too high for in vivo application. A 3% cosmetic dose of a dilute glycerin/water extract is orders of magnitude below that at the target cell — making it unlikely the in-vitro senolytic effect is reproduced in living dermis. The human hydration/brightness benefits are far more plausibly explained by glycerin + topical flavonoid antioxidant/anti-inflammatory action than by actual senescent-cell clearance.
  • No published human/ex-vivo penetration or PK data for Celyscence exist. Penetration of the actual senolytic compound into living dermis is the central unproven assumption of the whole product story.

6. Formulation

  • Use level: supplier-recommended 1–5%; the human study used 3%. [13] Water-soluble — goes into the water phase.
  • pH: formulate within standard skincare range (~5–6); no published narrow pH window, but flavonoid actives are happiest mildly acidic. Confirm against supplier spec.
  • Stability: flavonoid/phenolic actives are oxidation- and light-sensitive; the glycerin/water matrix needs robust preservation and protection from light/air. Apigenin and related flavonoids can discolour (yellow→brown) — color drift is a sensory/QC consideration.
  • Pairings (well-matched): humectants (glycerin — already the carrier — HA), barrier lipids (ceramides, squalane), niacinamide, antioxidants (vitamin E/ferulic), other "longevity narrative" actives. Sits comfortably in a calm, sensitive-skin-friendly barrier cream.
  • Conflicts: avoid harsh oxidants and strong acids in the same phase at high % (can degrade flavonoids); standard "layer at different steps / different products" guidance applies. No specific documented incompatibility.
  • Sensory: water-soluble botanical extract; low odour; lends a light, "natural/clean" feel. The glycerin carrier itself contributes hydration/slip.

7. Safety

  • Irritation/sensitization: no irritation signal reported in the small company study; positioned as gentle/COSMOS-natural. No published HRIPT/sensitization or patch-test data located — sensitization can't be ruled out for any botanical with multiple phenolics. [unverified] beyond marketing assurances.
  • Sun sensitivity: none identified (not an exfoliant or known photosensitizer). Note this is separate from the (unrelated) silymarin photoprotection literature — Celyscence has no demonstrated photoprotective benefit.
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: no specific contraindication identified, but no safety data in pregnancy — default to conservative "consult your doctor" copy; do not market as pregnancy-safe without data. (General note: high-dose apigenin has theoretical hormonal/uterine activity in lab models; at cosmetic topical levels this is not an established concern, but it argues against overdosing and for caution in pregnancy copy.)
  • Botanical/allergen note: Silybum marianum is an Asteraceae/Compositae (daisy/ragweed family) plant; people with Asteraceae allergy may react to botanical extracts from this family — worth a disclosure line.
  • Sourcing/sustainability: plant-derived, COSMOS, 100% natural origin (ISO 16128) [13] — a clean provenance story (better than synthetic-biology exosomes/growth factors on a "natural" axis). Uses the flower (a part often discarded), which can support an upcycling/whole-plant narrative if substantiated with the supplier.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

The line: under US FDA, intended use decides cosmetic vs drug. Claiming a product alters the structure/function of skin (e.g., "removes senescent cells," "restores/boosts collagen," "repairs DNA," "reverses aging") makes it a drug requiring approval — there is no OTC anti-aging/anti-wrinkle monograph. Cosmetic-safe claims are limited to appearance and moisturization. [14]

  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "inspired by senescence ('zombie cell') science," "helps skin look more radiant and even-toned," "visibly improves luminosity," "deeply hydrates" (hydration is the best-supported human endpoint), "supports skin's healthy, youthful appearance," "natural-origin / COSMOS-approved active."
  • ⚠️ Risky / drug-like — avoid or send to legal:
  • "senolytic" / "clears / kills / erases zombie (senescent) cells" in your skin — pharmacological/structure-function and unproven in human skin (in-vitro only).
  • "restores / boosts collagen," "repairs DNA," "reverses aging / biological age" — structure/function; in-vitro-only basis.
  • "milk thistle's proven antioxidant/UV protection" — a borrowed-evidence error: that data are silymarin/seed, which this flower extract does not contain. Using it would be misleading substantiation.
  • "Senotherapeutic / senolytic / senomorphic" are scientific terms, not regulated cosmetic claims — usable educationally but must be paired with cosmetic-appropriate benefit language; never let them imply disease treatment or actual cell clearance in the consumer's skin.
  • EU/UK: "Silybum Marianum Extract" is a recognized INCI; claims must meet the EU Cosmetics Claims Regulation (evidential support, no misleading "removes cells / reverses aging"). The company's own in-vitro data + small in-vivo study would be the substantiation file — thin, and not yours unless you license/buy the active and its dossier.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Supplier: Clariant Active Ingredients / Lucas Meyer Cosmetics (the branded active Celyscence™). This is a single-supplier, patent-protected commercial ingredient — you buy the finished active, not a generic. [1][6][13]
  • Generic alternative reality: plain "Silybum Marianum (seed) Extract" is widely and cheaply available — but it is the wrong part/compound (silymarin), with none of the senescence data. You cannot DIY Celyscence from a commodity milk-thistle extract; the flower-specific, low-silymarin, validated material is the product.
  • Cost tier: branded active-ingredient pricing (specialty, MOQ-bound), used at 1–5% — moderate ingredient cost contribution; far cheaper to formulate-with than to develop a senolytic from scratch.
  • IP: Clariant holds the composition/use patents (flower extract as senotherapeutic) [4][7]. Buying Celyscence = licensed use of their active and (their) substantiation; building an independent milk-thistle-flower senolytic would need freedom-to-operate review.
  • Substantiation transfer: if we buy Celyscence, we can reference the supplier's claims dossier (subject to their guidance); a different/generic extract inherits none of the data and creates our own substantiation burden.

Marketing claim Reality
"Milk thistle — proven skin antioxidant / UV protector" Category error. That's silymarin from the SEED; Celyscence is the FLOWER with <0.1% silymarin [4][5]. The classic UV/silymarin data [9][10][11] do not apply.
"Senolytic — clears the zombie cells in your skin" Selective apoptosis shown only in cultured fibroblasts at 100–200 µg/mL [5] — a level the authors call too high for in vivo [5]. Not shown in living human skin.
"Restores collagen / reverses aging" Type-I collagen ↑ in a dish [5]; no human collagen/wrinkle measurement in the public clinical data [3][8]. Structure/function claim.
"Clinically proven anti-aging" Human proof = +hydration, +self-perceived radiance, −facial melanin in a small, unpublished, company split-panel study [3][8] — appearance/moisturization endpoints, not senescence.
"Dual senolytic + senomorphic" True as an in-vitro description [4][5]; unverified as an in-skin effect.
Independent validation None located (2026-06). All efficacy authorship traces to Clariant/Biospectrum.

Independent context: dermatology reviews agree senotherapeutic skincare is scientifically promising but clinically immature — most senolytic skin evidence is preclinical, and human topical proof (even for the most-studied agents like rapamycin/D+Q) is early. [15][16] Celyscence is a real, competently-studied in-vitro botanical senolytic, but its consumer-facing story runs well ahead of the human evidence.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ Conditional — a credible, natural-origin "longevity-science" supporting active with a great story, but NOT an evidence-backed hero, and the name must not lean on milk-thistle/silymarin photoprotection.

  • Why it's tempting: it's a COSMOS / 100%-natural-origin, single-supplier, patent-protected senotherapeutic with an actual peer-reviewed in-vitro paper and a (small) human study — a cleaner-than-average "skin longevity" hook, and it maps neatly onto a repair/renewal thesis (../../brand/positioning.md). The natural-origin angle differentiates it from synthetic peptide senotherapeutics (cf. senolytics-os01-peptide.md).
  • Why it's only conditional: (1) the honest human-topical story is "natural brightening + hydrating botanical," not "clears zombie cells"; (2) the senolytic data are in-vitro, company-authored, at concentrations the authors say are unrealistic in vivo; (3) no independent replication; (4) the silymarin/UV photoprotection evidence does not transfer and must never be used as substantiation.
  • Longevity relevance: High in narrative, modest-and-unproven in topical effect. Excellent education/differentiation asset; weak as a stand-alone efficacy claim.
  • Gating conditions before any "hero" use: (1) license Celyscence specifically (not a generic milk-thistle extract) and obtain the supplier substantiation file; (2) lock claims to hydration/radiance/even-tone + "senescence-science-inspired" cosmetic language — never "senolytic/clears cells/restores collagen"; (3) ideally pair with an active that carries real human-topical proof (e.g., a retinoid or niacinamide) so the routine isn't resting on an in-vitro story.
  • Best role: a supporting "natural senotherapeutic" character ingredient in a barrier/renewal cream — sells the longevity narrative honestly while a proven active does the heavy efficacy lifting.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Independent adversarial re-check performed 2026-06-19 against the primary sources (PLoS ONE full text via PMC, patent WO2022073709A1 via Google Patents, Premium Beauty News / Cosmetics & Toiletries trade coverage). Goal: confirm the cited studies are real, that they say what the dossier claims, that human-vs-in-vitro and topical-vs-oral lines are honest, and that conflicts/funding/sample sizes are stated correctly.

Overall evidence grade: C (weak / indirect for the headline claims)

  • The senolytic / "clears zombie cells" / "restores collagen" headline claims rest on a grade of C–D: the data are in-vitro only, in a crude extract, at concentrations the authors themselves call too high for in-vivo use, with no animal bridge, no human biopsy, no in-vivo senescence or collagen measurement, and no independent replication.
  • The only human-topical (Tier 4) claims that earn a grade are hydration (≈B−) and radiance/even-tone/melanin (≈C) — and even those come from a single small (n=30), unpublished, company-run split-panel study, not an independent peer-reviewed RCT.
  • Net: the dossier's own verdict ("credible in-vitro botanical senolytic; honest human story is hydrating/brightening botanical, NOT zombie-cell clearance") is well-supported and appropriately skeptical. The headline marketing story is essentially unproven topically (D); the modest cosmetic benefits are weak human evidence (C, hydration ~B−).

What checked out (citations are real and accurately represented)

The PLoS ONE paper's declared funding is a South Korean government grant (Ministry of Trade, Industry & Energy, to the Biospectrum author EJ), not Clariant. The real conflict is commercial authorship (three Clariant employees, two of them the patent inventors) and the declared "No authors have competing interests" statement — not undisclosed industry funding. Corrected in the header callout and §4.
  2. Inventor count corrected from "three" to "two." The patent names Fréchet & Chajra only. The dossier had conflated "three Clariant co-authors" with "named inventors." Corrected.
  3. Conflict-of-interest critique stands — but should be framed precisely: it is an authorship/commercial-interest problem (the people who showed the effect sell the ingredient and hold its patent) plus a "no competing interests" declaration that is hard to credit, not a hidden-funding problem.
