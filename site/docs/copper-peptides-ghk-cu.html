Ingredient: Copper Tripeptide-1 (GHK-Cu) & Tripeptide-1 (GHK)

Slug: copper-peptides-ghk-cu · Class: Peptides Researched: 2026-06-19 (exhaustive upgrade) · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product

How to read this dossier. This is an evidence-graded literature review, not marketing copy. The core is the tiered evidence review (§4): in-vitro → animal → human oral → human topical. The single most important honest finding: GHK-Cu has deep, decades-old mechanistic and in-vitro/animal science, but the human topical evidence is thin — a handful of small trials, several industry-sponsored or conference-presented, none a large independent RCT, and most studies use complex formulations or delivery systems rather than a simple "1% GHK-Cu" cream. Treat mechanism and clinical efficacy as separate questions.

1. Identity

  • Common names: Copper peptide; copper tripeptide; GHK-Cu; "the blue peptide."
  • GHK (free peptide): glycyl-L-histidyl-L-lysine — a naturally occurring human tripeptide (sequence Gly-His-Lys). INCI: Tripeptide-1. CAS 49557-75-7 (GHK), 72957-37-0 (GHK-Cu).
  • GHK-Cu (the copper complex): GHK chelated to a copper(II) ion (Cu²⁺), which forms spontaneously. INCI: Copper Tripeptide-1. This is the form with the characteristic blue color.
  • Origin in the body: GHK is liberated proteolytically from the parent matrix glycoprotein SPARC (Secreted Protein Acidic and Rich in Cysteine) and from collagen during tissue injury/remodeling [3]. It circulates in plasma; reported plasma GHK levels decline with age — commonly cited as ~200 ng/mL at age 20 falling to ~80 ng/mL by age 60 [4]. (This "decline with age" stat is real and frequently cited, but it underpins a narrative of replacement that topical data do not directly prove — see §10.)
  • Key forms / derivatives:
  • Copper Tripeptide-1 (GHK-Cu) — the workhorse cosmetic form.
  • Tripeptide-1 (GHK) — free peptide, sometimes used without added copper.
  • Palmitoyl Tripeptide-1 (Pal-GHK) — lipidated GHK with a 16-carbon fatty-acid tail for better skin penetration; the GHK component of Matrixyl® 3000 (Pal-GHK + Pal-GQPR). Note: Pal-GHK is a derivative, and clinical data on Matrixyl 3000 is not interchangeable with GHK-Cu data.
  • Various encapsulated / nano-carrier GHK-Cu systems developed specifically because the bare peptide penetrates poorly (§5).

2. Class & Mechanism

GHK is classed as both a signal peptide (it tells fibroblasts and keratinocytes to remodel the extracellular matrix) and a carrier peptide (it ferries copper — a cofactor for matrix-building enzymes — into cells) [3][7].

Mechanisms supported by the literature (note: mechanism evidence is strong; whether each translates to visible topical benefit in humans is a separate, weaker question):

  • ECM stimulation. In fibroblast culture and animal skin, GHK/GHK-Cu increases synthesis of collagen, elastin, proteoglycans, glycosaminoglycans (GAGs), and decorin [1][3][5][6].
  • Copper delivery for crosslinking enzymes. Copper is an essential cofactor for lysyl oxidase, the enzyme that crosslinks collagen and elastin; GHK-Cu supplies it [7].
  • MMP/TIMP rebalancing. GHK-Cu shifts the protease balance toward matrix preservation — it raised TIMP-1/TIMP-2 (tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases) relative to MMPs in human dermal fibroblasts, and that ratio correlated with collagen/elastin output [6].
  • Broad gene modulation. Connectivity-Map / microarray analyses report GHK altering expression of ~4,000+ human genes (≥50% change in ~31% of genes assessed in one dataset), resetting age-shifted patterns toward "younger" profiles, with the most-affected pathways being antioxidant defense, DNA repair, ubiquitin-proteasome (protein clearance), and TGF-β signaling [1][2][8]. Big caveat: these are cultured-cell (often cancer cell line) transcriptomics at micromolar doses — they do not prove clinical outcomes in living skin (§10).
  • Antioxidant. Blocks copper-dependent LDL oxidation, quenches reactive aldehydes (4-HNE, acrolein, MDA), reduces iron-driven oxidation [2].
  • Anti-inflammatory. Lowers TNF, IL-6; inhibits NF-κB and p38 MAPK signaling in models [2].
  • Wound-healing / regenerative. The original and best-evidenced biological role: accelerates wound closure, angiogenesis, and granulation in animals and clinical wound use [3].

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (core of this dossier)

Reading guide: Benefits proven for topical human use are limited to the human-topical tier below. Everything in the in-vitro, animal, and oral tiers is mechanism or other-format evidence and must not be presented as proof of a topical cosmetic result.

Tier 1 — In-vitro (strongest, most numerous evidence; least relevant to consumer claims)

  • Badenhorst et al. 2016, J Aging Sci 4:166 — Human adult dermal fibroblasts (HDFa) treated with GHK-Cu at 0.01, 1, 100 nM: significantly increased collagen and elastin production at all concentrations, raised TIMP-1 at all doses and MMP-1/MMP-2 at the lowest dose; a higher TIMP:MMP ratio tracked with more collagen/elastin. (Same paper's human arm is in Tier 4.) Verified DOI 10.4172/2329-8847.1000166 [6].
  • Pickart, Vasquez-Soltero & Margolina 2017, Brain Sci 7(2):20 (PMC5332963) — Connectivity Map analysis: GHK altered ≥50% expression in ~31% of assessed genes (≈4,000+ of ~13,400), with marked DNA-repair (PARP3, POLM, MRE11A), antioxidant (MT3, PON1), and ubiquitin-proteasome upregulation. Cell lines (PC3 prostate-cancer, MCF7 breast-cancer) at 1 µM [8]. Note the format honestly: this paper's framing is nervous-system / cognitive-decline gene expression, not skin — the "~4,000 genes" figure that gets recycled into skin marketing comes from a non-skin, cancer-cell-line transcriptomics dataset. (Verified 2026-06-19: source is the Brain Sci cognition paper; 31.2% / cancer-cell-line / 1 µM details confirmed — the earlier "Int J Mol Sci" label was wrong.)
  • Pickart & Margolina 2018, IJMS (PMC6073405) — synthesis of fibroblast/keratinocyte studies: GHK-Cu boosts fibroblast viability, integrin/basal-cell stimulation, decorin, angiogenesis [2][3].
  • Limitation across this tier: cultured cells (often cancer lines) at controlled doses; transcriptomic "rejuvenation" does not predict visible skin change. The TrimRX/Peptide-Garden critiques flag exactly this overreach [11][12].

Tier 2 — Animal

  • Maquart / Pickart-era rat studies (cited in [3][6]) — dose-related increases in skin dry weight, total protein, collagen, and GAG content in rat skin; accelerated wound healing, increased collagen synthesis (reported up to ~9-fold in healthy-rat wound models) [2][3].
  • Limitation: rat ≠ human skin (penetration and fibroblast response differ; Badenhorst explicitly notes rat-vs-human fibroblast differences). Wound-healing models are injury, not cosmetic anti-aging.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL / systemic

  • Essentially absent for skin endpoints. GHK-Cu is not approved for ingestion; oral bioavailability of the bare peptide is considered negligible without advanced (e.g., liposomal) encapsulation [4][12]. No credible human oral RCT shows a skin anti-aging benefit. Injectable/systemic GHK-Cu has zero published human efficacy trials and is sold in a grey/research-chemical market [11][12]. Any benefit borrowed from oral/injectable claims is not transferable to a topical cosmetic.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters for the brand)

RCT / controlled: - Badenhorst et al. 2016 (human arm), J Aging Sci — Randomized, double-blind trial, n=40 women aged 40–65, 8 weeks, twice daily. The tested active was a specific commercial product ("New Radiance Face Serum") containing GHK-Cu encapsulated in a lipid-based nano-carrier, benchmarked against (a) the serum vehicle without GHK-Cu/carrier and (b) a commercial Matrixyl® 3000 product (StriVectin SD). Result: GHK-Cu reduced wrinkle volume by 31.6% vs Matrixyl 3000 (p=0.004) and by 55.8% vs vehicle (p<0.001), plus wrinkle depth −32.8% vs vehicle (p=0.012). Strengths: randomized, double-blind, instrumental endpoints. Limitations: small, single-site, only 8 weeks; the active was encapsulated in a nano-carrier (tests "GHK-Cu delivered effectively," not a generic cream); and it evaluated a named branded serum, so a commercial/sponsor interest cannot be excluded — the published paper carries no clear independent-funding statement [6]. Best-designed topical study available, but not an independent, generic-formulation RCT. (Verified 2026-06-19: design, n, age range, nano-carrier delivery, and the 31.6% / 55.8% figures confirmed against the source.) - Leyden et al. (facial) / companion (eye) — facial-cream trial widely cited as n=71 women with photoaging, 12 weeks: reported improved skin density/thickness, reduced fine lines/laxity, improved clarity; companion eye-cream trial n=41 photodamaged women, reported to outperform placebo and a vitamin-K cream on periorbital lines/density. Major limitations (two layers): (1) the most-cited "71 women" facial data were industry-sponsored and largely conference-presented (poster/abstract), not independently peer-reviewed in full — repeated across reviews but never independently scrutinized; and (2) the primary citation for this work could not be cleanly verified. The publication year and venue are reported inconsistently across secondary sources, and the Wiley DOI carried in source [10] (der2.70067) actually resolves to the unrelated 2026 Sarbaziha review [16], not a Leyden trial — so the exact reference is uncertain and partly mis-linked. Treat the underlying data as low quality and the citation itself as unconfirmed [10][11][12].

Lower-tier human (open-label / comparative / pilot): - Abdulghani et al. 1998 (Disease Management & Clinical Outcomes) — small pilot comparing copper-peptide cream vs vitamin C vs melatonin vs tretinoin on normal (non-photoaged) skin, assessed by biopsy / immunohistology / ultrastructure. The endpoint was procollagen synthesis by papillary-dermis fibroblasts, not a visible wrinkle outcome: a significant increase was seen in ~70% of copper-peptide subjects vs ~50% (vitamin C) and ~40% (tretinoin). Limitations: very small pilot, open-label, older, biopsy-based surrogate endpoint on normal skin, non-blinded interpretation. The "outperforms retinoic acid" line rests almost entirely on this single tiny study and refers to a biochemical marker, not measured anti-wrinkle efficacy — handle with care [1][3][11]. (Verified 2026-06-19: study real; 70/50/40% procollagen figures and "normal skin" design confirmed.) - Assorted small manufacturer studies report improved density/elasticity/moisture over ~12 weeks; consistently small, frequently sponsor-run [3][10].

Honest topical bottom line. Proven for topical human use: small, mostly short trials show visible reduction in fine lines/wrinkles and modest improvement in skin density/firmness, with effect sizes generally judged smaller than prescription retinoids [11]. Everything else (DNA-repair, "resetting the genome," senescence reversal, systemic regeneration) is borrowed from in-vitro/animal/non-topical data and is not established for a topical cosmetic.

5. Topical Bioavailability / Penetration

This is GHK-Cu's quiet weak spot and is decisive for formulation.

  • GHK-Cu is highly hydrophilic — far from the logP ≈ 1–3 "sweet spot" for passive skin permeation, so as a small, charged, water-loving molecule it does not cross the stratum corneum efficiently on its own. (Caveat on the exact number: a logD ≈ −2.49 value is cited in some reviews [3], but the Hostynek penetration paper [9] reports a markedly more hydrophilic pseudo log Pw ≈ −4.5 for the copper tripeptide. The two figures are not from the same method and the −2.49 value should not be attributed to [9]. Either way the conclusion — strongly hydrophilic, poor passive permeation — holds.)
  • Penetration study — Hostynek, Dreher & Maibach 2010, Inflammation Research (PMC3016279): in-vitro human skin, flow-through (Franz-type) diffusion cells, 0.68% aqueous copper tripeptide. Copper-as-tripeptide did distribute into stratum corneum (large reservoir, ~290 µg/cm²), epidermis (~67 µg/cm²) and dermis (~28 µg/cm²) over 48 h, and the authors concluded delivery could reach "therapeutically effective amounts." But this is 48 h, infinite dose, isolated-skin conditions — generous relative to real wear-time, and it measures copper, not intact functional peptide [9].
  • Implication: much consumer GHK-Cu likely sits in the upper layers. The strongest topical result (Badenhorst) required a nano-carrier, and the Mortazavi review's central conclusion is that GHK/GHK-Cu efficacy in real products is gated by permeation and is best improved with lipidation (Pal-GHK), encapsulation, cell-penetrating peptides, or microneedle pretreatment [3]. A plain aqueous GHK-Cu serum may underperform its mechanism.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: Suppliers and formulators commonly cite ~0.05–1% Copper Tripeptide-1 (with much consumer marketing around 1%). Note the disconnect: the CIR safety dossier recorded actual industry use up to only ~0.0016% as of its assessment [13] — i.e., historical real-world use levels were often far below the "1%" now marketed. Effective dosing in skin is uncertain because penetration limits how much active reaches fibroblasts.
  • pH: Best held ~5.5–7.0. Below ~5.0 the peptide bonds/complex are destabilized; above ~7.5 copper can precipitate. This narrow window constrains co-formulation.
  • Stability: Water-soluble; the Cu²⁺ complex is redox-sensitive. Needs careful chelator/antioxidant balance and pH control.
  • Color/sensory: Distinctive blue tint (copper) — can read as a hero "blue serum" feature or as a formulation constraint (can tint skin/textiles at higher loads). Light texture, water-phase.
  • Pairings (good): humectants (HA, glycerin), niacinamide, ceramides/barrier lipids, gentle/non-acidic peptides. Niacinamide + GHK-Cu is a clean, well-tolerated combo.
  • Conflicts (real, not just folklore):
  • Direct L-ascorbic acid (vitamin C): genuinely problematic in the same layer/product — ascorbic acid reduces Cu²⁺, which can degrade both the peptide and the vitamin C and drive pro-oxidant chemistry. Low-pH vitamin C also pushes the formula out of GHK-Cu's stable window. Separate by routine step/time (e.g., vitamin C AM, copper peptide PM), or use a pH-compatible vitamin C derivative [14]. This is a real chemical interaction, not a myth.
  • Strong exfoliating acids (AHA/BHA), high-strength actives at low pH: same pH-destabilization logic — don't co-formulate; layer separately.
  • Retinoids: no hard chemical incompatibility, but stacking strong actives can irritate; sequence sensibly.

7. Safety

  • Overall: Strong real-world safety record; widely used in cosmetics for ~4 decades without notable serious adverse events [2][13]. Clinical trials up to 12 weeks reported no irritation in the main cohorts [6][10].
  • Irritation/sensitization: Generally low. A minority of users report transient redness/itch; sensitive skin should patch test. Copper itself is a weak sensitizer — positive copper patch tests are uncommon (≈4% of suspected cases) and usually of low clinical relevance, frequently co-occurring with nickel/cobalt allergy rather than standing alone [metal-allergy literature]. Real copper contact allergy exists but is rare.
  • CIR (Cosmetic Ingredient Review) conclusion: Copper Tripeptide-1 is "safe in the present practices of use and concentration in cosmetics" (assessment c. 2012–2014), with copper levels in formulations judged not to present a safety concern as used [13].
  • Sun sensitivity: No established photosensitization; not a photosensitizer. (Still pair any anti-aging routine with SPF — sun is the #1 driver of photoaging.)
  • Pregnancy/nursing: No specific topical red flags identified; data are limited, so use conservative copy and defer to a clinician — do not make pregnancy-safety claims without review.
  • Contraindication to flag for systemic/oral use (not topical cosmetics): Wilson's disease (copper-overload genetic disorder) is an absolute contraindication for copper supplementation; high-dose zinc can antagonize copper. These belong to the ingestible/injectable conversation — note them only to avoid the brand being associated with unsafe off-label use.
  • Sourcing/sustainability: Synthetic peptide + copper salt — no animal/biological-provenance concerns (unlike growth factors/exosomes). Suppliers must document purity and heavy-metal content [13].

8. Regulatory & Claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Regulatory status: Cosmetic ingredient, not a drug. Copper Tripeptide-1 / Tripeptide-1 are legal cosmetic skin-conditioning ingredients in the US (CIR-reviewed safe) and EU (not restricted under Annex II–VI of Reg. EC 1223/2009; each finished formula still needs its own safety assessment) [13][15]. Not approved for ingestion or injection — those uses are off-label/grey-market and must be kept entirely separate from any brand messaging.
  • The cosmetic vs. drug line. GHK-Cu's strongest scientific story (wound healing, tissue regeneration, gene modulation, collagen synthesis) is exactly the story that crosses into drug/structure-function claims. This is the central tension for this ingredient.
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "supports the appearance of firmer, denser-looking skin," "helps visibly smooth the look of fine lines," "supports skin's natural renewal processes," "comforting," "a copper peptide with decades of study behind it."
  • ⚠️ Risky / drug-like — needs substantiation or legal review: "boosts collagen by 70/28%," "regenerates skin," "heals," "repairs tissue," "reverses aging," "resets your genes," "increases skin thickness" (structure/function). The percentage figures originate in tiny studies and as marketing claims would require substantiation; "heals/regenerates/remodels" is drug territory. Prefer "supports/visibly/appearance of" over quantified "boosts." Do not import oral/injectable "longevity" claims into topical copy.
  • See ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & Cost

  • Cost tier: Low-to-mid. Copper Tripeptide-1 is a mature, widely available commodity peptide (e.g., CAS 49557-75-7) from many cosmetic-peptide suppliers; raw cost is modest at cosmetic use levels.
  • Supply: Easy, stable, multi-supplier — a real practical advantage over rarer/proprietary "longevity" actives (exosomes, growth factors, branded senotherapeutics). Encapsulated/nano-carrier versions cost more but address the penetration problem.
  • Provenance: Fully synthetic; no ethical/biological-sourcing baggage. Specify heavy-metal/purity testing in supplier QC.

10. Hype vs. Evidence

Where marketing overreaches the data:

  1. "Affects ~4,000 genes / resets your DNA to younger." True only as cultured cancer-cell transcriptomics at micromolar doses — and the most-cited dataset [8] comes from a paper on nervous-system / cognitive-decline gene expression (PC3/MCF7 cancer lines), not skin. It is not demonstrated to happen meaningfully in living human skin from a topical cream, and gene-expression shifts in a dish don't predict visible outcomes [11][12]. This is the #1 overreach.
  2. "Boosts collagen 70% / outperforms retinoic acid." Rests on a single ~20-person 1998 pilot [1] and small sponsor studies; retinoids have a far larger, more robust evidence base. Honest framing: GHK-Cu's effect sizes are modest and smaller than prescription retinoids [11].
  3. "Clinically proven" anti-aging. The flagship "71 women" data are industry-sponsored / conference-presented, and the best controlled trial (n=40) used a nano-carrier, not a plain cream — so "clinically proven" overstates both the independence and the generalizability of the evidence [6][10][11][12].
  4. "Regenerates / heals skin." Borrowed from wound-healing and animal literature, not from cosmetic topical anti-aging endpoints — and it's drug-claim language.
  5. "Longevity peptide." The plasma-decline-with-age stat [4] fuels a replacement narrative, but topical application restoring systemic GHK or extending cellular lifespan in skin is not demonstrated. Injectable/oral "longevity" claims have no human efficacy trials [11][12].
  6. Penetration glossed over. Marketing rarely admits the bare peptide barely crosses the barrier (logD ≈ −2.49) [9] — yet penetration is the gating variable for any real effect [3].

What's genuinely good: decades of mechanistic depth, a real (if small) human topical signal on lines/firmness, excellent safety, clean sourcing, and a credible "well-studied copper peptide" story that doesn't require exotic claims.

11. Verdict for the Brand

✅ Supporting (conditional on smart delivery) — the most defensible "regenerative peptide" in the longevity set, but not a hero on the strength of its human topical data alone.

One-line reasoning: GHK-Cu offers the best evidence-to-risk-to-cost ratio of the longevity-adjacent actives — real (if small/short) human topical signal, four decades of mechanism, clean safety, open low-cost sourcing — provided the brand (a) formulates for penetration (encapsulation/lipidation or pairs it with Pal-GHK), holds pH ~5.5–7, and keeps it out of the same step as direct vitamin C/acids, and (b) disciplines claims to "supports/visibly/appearance of," never importing in-vitro gene or oral/injectable "longevity" claims.

Longevity-relevance note: Its longevity story is mechanistically rich but topically unproven — it's a credibility anchor and narrative bridge to the regenerative-aesthetics conversation, not a demonstrated skin-longevity intervention. Best used as a supporting active or co-headliner in a repair/renewal product, with an explicit internal rule that the marketing leans on its study pedigree and safety, not on overstated efficacy. 13. Evidence verification (adversarial)

Independent adversarial pass, 2026-06-19. Method: for each headline efficacy claim, checked (a) whether the evidence is human + topical or quietly borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral data; (b) whether the cited studies are real and say what is claimed; (c) whether the key studies are industry-funded or very small. Primary sources re-fetched: Badenhorst 2016 [6], Pickart/Brain Sci 2017 [8], Hostynek 2010/11 [9], Abdulghani 1998 [1]. Leyden [10] could not be verified at primary level.

Overall evidence grade: C (weak / indirect for topical anti-aging)

The dossier itself already lands near this grade, and that honesty is its strongest feature. Human-topical anti-aging efficacy rests on essentially one small, short, industry-adjacent RCT of a branded encapsulated product (Badenhorst, n=40, 8 wk) plus older, unverifiable or surrogate-endpoint studies (Leyden "71 women"; Abdulghani biopsy pilot). Mechanistic and in-vitro/animal evidence is genuinely deep (that part would grade A–B), but it does not transfer to a topical cosmetic claim. Net: a real but small human-topical signal on fine lines/firmness sitting on top of a much larger, less relevant mechanistic literature. Not B (no independent, moderate-sized, generic-formulation human-topical RCT exists); not D (there is a genuine, replicated-direction human-topical signal and excellent safety). C is the defensible grade.

What held up under scrutiny

  • Badenhorst 2016 [6] is real and accurately summarized. n=40, women 40–65, 8 weeks, randomized/double-blind; GHK-Cu in a lipid nano-carrier; the 31.6% vs Matrixyl 3000 (p=0.004) and 55.8% vs vehicle wrinkle-volume figures are correct. Newly surfaced caveat: the tested active was a named commercial serum ("New Radiance Face Serum") and the published paper carries no clear independent-funding statement — a commercial interest cannot be excluded. (The dossier's earlier "split-face" descriptor was not confirmable and has been removed.)
  • Abdulghani 1998 [1] is real; the 70% / 50% / 40% figures check out — but they are procollagen synthesis on normal skin (a biochemical surrogate), not measured wrinkle improvement. The "outperforms tretinoin" line is therefore weaker than it sounds. Corrected in §4.
  • Hostynek penetration [9] is real; copper was delivered into epidermis/dermis in vitro — but it measured copper, not intact peptide, under 48 h infinite-dose isolated-skin conditions. The "highly hydrophilic, poor passive permeation" conclusion holds.
  • The ~4,000-gene transcriptomic claim is real in the data [8] — and is appropriately quarantined to the in-vitro tier.

Main caveats (the load-bearing ones)

  1. One real RCT, and it's a branded, encapsulated product with no clear independent funding. "Clinically proven" overstates both independence and generalizability. A plain 1% GHK-Cu serum is not what Badenhorst tested.
  2. Mechanism ≠ topical outcome. The deepest, most impressive evidence (gene modulation, ECM stimulation, wound healing) is in-vitro/animal/wound, i.e. the wrong format for a cosmetic anti-aging claim. Keep the two questions separate (the dossier does this well).
  3. The "~4,000 genes" headline comes from a non-skin paper. Source [8] is a nervous-system / cognitive-decline transcriptomics study in cancer cell lines at 1 µM. The number is real; the context is borrowed. (§4 / §10 corrected.)
  4. "Outperforms retinoic acid" = one tiny biopsy pilot on normal skin. Surrogate endpoint, n small, open-label, 1998. Not a basis for a comparative efficacy claim.
  5. The plasma-GHK-decline stat (200→80 ng/mL) traces to Pickart's own narrative. It motivates a "replacement" story that topical data do not prove. Fine as background; not evidence of topical benefit.
  6. Citation hygiene defect (now fixed-as-flagged): source [10] (Leyden) carried a Wiley DOI (der2.70067) that actually belongs to source [16] (Sarbaziha 2026). The Leyden primary reference is unverified and was mis-linked. The logD ≈ −2.49 figure was mis-attributed to [9], which reports pseudo log Pw ≈ −4.5; corrected in §5.

Claims that should be softened / were edited in this pass

  • "Best-designed topical study" → kept, but reframed as a branded, encapsulated-product RCT without a clear independent-funding statement (not a generic-cream proof). (§4)
  • "Split-face" descriptor on Badenhorst → removed (not confirmable). (§4)
  • Abdulghani "increased collagen in ~70% … outperforms retinoic acid" → reframed to procollagen synthesis on normal skin, a surrogate biochemical endpoint. (§4)
  • Leyden "2002 (facial) / 2005 (eye), Dermatologic Surgery" → softened to inconsistently dated, primary-source-unverified, and mis-linked; quality downgraded from "low–moderate" to low / unconfirmed. (§4, §12)
  • "~4,000 genes" → labeled as coming from a non-skin cognition paper in cancer cell lines. (§4, §10)
  • "logD ≈ −2.49 [9]" → corrected; number de-attributed from Hostynek (which reports ≈ −4.5). (§5)

Net guidance for the brand

The §11 verdict ("supporting active / co-headliner, lean on study pedigree + safety, not on efficacy") survives this audit and is the right posture. Marketing must not say "clinically proven anti-aging," must not import the gene/longevity story, and should treat the one RCT as suggestive of a delivery-dependent effect, not as proof a generic copper-peptide serum works. Grade C = "promising, well-tolerated, mechanistically rich, but topically under-proven."
