Ingredient: Creatine (INCI: Creatine)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Antioxidants (cellular-energy / mitochondrial-support active; phosphagen system precursor)

One-line honest summary: Creatine is one of the very few "cellular energy" actives with real, on-target human topical evidence — the mechanism (the creatine-kinase / phosphocreatine ATP-buffering system is genuinely present and active in skin) is well established, it demonstrably penetrates to the dermis, and there are two in-vivo human studies showing measurable firmness/wrinkle benefit. The catch: the best human studies are manufacturer-run (Beiersdorf), combination formulas (creatine + guarana + glycerol; or creatine + folic acid), small, baseline-controlled rather than vehicle/placebo-controlled, and one was men-only. So the evidence is unusually good for the category but is not the same as a large independent vehicle-controlled RCT on creatine alone.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Creatine (from Greek kreas, "flesh"). Also "α-methylguanidinoacetic acid."
  • INCI: Creatine
  • IUPAC / chemical name: 2-[carbamimidoyl(methyl)amino]acetic acid; also written N-(aminoiminomethyl)-N-methylglycine (the form that appears in EU CosIng / COSMILE). [9][10]
  • CAS: 57-00-1 (anhydrous creatine). The hydrated raw-material form creatine monohydrate is CAS 6020-87-7. [8][10]
  • Molecular formula / weight: C₄H₉N₃O₂ · 131.13 g/mol (anhydrous); monohydrate C₄H₉N₃O₂·H₂O ≈ 149.15 g/mol. A small molecule — directly relevant to its proven penetration. [8]
  • Chemical class: A nitrogenous organic acid / guanidino compound (an amino-acid derivative, though not a proteinogenic amino acid). It is a zwitterion at physiological pH (carboxylate − / guanidinium +).
  • Endogenous status: Creatine is made by the human body (liver, kidney, pancreas from glycine, arginine, methionine) and obtained from diet (meat/fish); ~95% of the body pool sits in skeletal muscle, but it is also present and functional in skin, brain, and other tissues. [3][5]
  • Physical/chemistry notes: White crystalline powder; only modestly water-soluble (~13 g/L at room temperature — this is a real formulation constraint, see §6); poorly soluble in alcohol/ether; aqueous solutions are roughly neutral. [8]
  • Key forms / derivatives:
  • Creatine monohydrate — the workhorse cosmetic and supplement grade (and the form used in the human skin studies).
  • Creatine HCl, creatine ethyl ester, buffered creatine ("Kre-Alkalyn"), N-acetyl-creatine — supplement-marketing variants pitched for solubility/uptake; none have skin-specific topical evidence, and most have no advantage over monohydrate even for muscle.
  • Creatinine — the spontaneous breakdown product of creatine; NOT the active and not interchangeable.

2. Class & mechanism (how it works at the skin/cell level)

Creatine's logic is bioenergetic, which makes it a clean fit for the "mitochondrial decline" hallmark of skin aging.

The mechanistic chain (well established in physiology, and specifically demonstrated in skin by the Lenz/Berneburg group at the University of Düsseldorf / Beiersdorf):

  1. The phosphocreatine (PCr) shuttle / ATP buffer. Cells store readily-available energy as phosphocreatine. The enzyme creatine kinase (CK) reversibly transfers a high-energy phosphate between PCr and ADP: PCr + ADP ⇌ creatine + ATP. This regenerates ATP within seconds, buffering energy demand spikes far faster than mitochondria can ramp up. [3][5]
  2. The CK system is genuinely present in skin. Lenz et al. (2005) showed human skin expresses both cytosolic and mitochondrial CK isoenzymes plus a creatine transporter (SLC6A8) — i.e., skin is built to use creatine, it isn't just borrowed muscle biology. [3]
  3. Exogenous creatine is taken up and raises CK activity. Topically/exogenously supplied creatine is transported into keratinocytes and fibroblasts and increases CK activity and mitochondrial function. [1][3]
  4. Mitochondrial protection / antioxidant-adjacent effect. By stabilizing the cell's energy charge, creatine protects mitochondrial function against oxidative and UV insult — pre-treated keratinocytes retained CK activity and mitochondrial membrane potential after oxidative/UVA stress; this is why it is filed under "antioxidants," though it is better described as an energy-stabilizing / cytoprotective active than a direct radical scavenger. [3][2]
  5. Downstream: collagen synthesis & reduced MMP-1. With energy charge restored, fibroblasts up-regulate collagen synthesis (gene + protein) and the UV/aging-driven rise in collagen-degrading MMP-1 is blunted. Creatine supplementation also normalized UVA-induced mitochondrial-DNA mutagenesis and the associated functional decline (O₂ consumption) in fibroblasts. [2][4]

Skin-relevant translation (honest): Items 1–3 are proven in skin, not borrowed. Items 4–5 are demonstrated in cell/3D models (and partly in vivo via the human formulation studies). The "age = energy decline, creatine = re-charge" story is one of the more mechanistically literal and defensible claims in the energy-cosmetics category — but the magnitude of clinical benefit from a finished topical product is modest and tied to multi-ingredient formulas.

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

The single most important honesty point: Unlike most "longevity" cosmetic actives (whose human data are oral or absent), creatine actually has on-target human topical skin data and a proven penetration-to-dermis result. But the two pivotal in-vivo human studies were run by Beiersdorf (the Nivea/Eucerin parent, which patents creatine cosmetics — see §8), used combination formulas (so the effect can't be cleanly attributed to creatine alone), were small, and one was men-only. There is no large, independent, creatine-monotherapy facial RCT. Treat the evidence as "good for the category, not gold-standard."

Tier 1 — In vitro (cell & 3D skin models)

  • Lenz H., Schmidt M., Welge V., Schlattner U., Wallimann T., Elsässer H.-P., Wittern K.-P., Wenck H., Stäb F., Blatt T. (2005). The Creatine Kinase System in Human Skin: Protective Effects of Creatine Against Oxidative and UV Damage In Vitro and In Vivo. Journal of Investigative Dermatology 124(2):443–452. Foundational mechanism paper. Demonstrated cytosolic + mitochondrial CK isoenzymes and a creatine transporter in human skin; exogenous creatine was taken up by keratinocytes, increased CK activity and mitochondrial function, and protected against oxidative/UV-induced loss of CK activity. Limitation: mechanistic; "in vivo" component is supportive, not a wrinkle RCT; authors are Beiersdorf/academic collaborators. [3]
  • Berneburg M., Gremmel T., Kürten V., Schroeder P., Hertel I., von Mikecz A., Wild S., Chen M., Declercq L., Matsui M., Ruzicka T., Krutmann J. (2005). Creatine Supplementation Normalizes Mutagenesis of Mitochondrial DNA as Well as Functional Consequences. Journal of Investigative Dermatology 125(2):213–220. In cultured human fibroblasts subjected to repetitive UVA, raising intracellular creatine normalized the UVA-induced "common deletion" mtDNA mutagenesis and restored functional parameters (oxygen consumption, mitochondrial membrane potential) and lowered MMP-1. Limitation: in vitro fibroblasts; a mechanism-of-photoprotection paper, not a clinical efficacy study. [2][4]
  • Fischer F. et al. (2011) — in-vitro arm (see Tier 4). Fibroblast-populated dermal equivalents treated with folic acid + creatine showed ↑collagen gene expression, ↑procollagen, ↑collagen-fiber density. Limitation: combination (folic acid co-active); 3D model. [7]
  • Peirano R.I. et al. (2011) — in-vitro/ex-vivo arm (see Tier 4). Cultured human dermal fibroblasts + creatine showed stimulated collagen synthesis at gene and protein level; ex vivo skin-penetration experiments showed creatine rapidly reaches the dermis. [6]
  • Dermal-papilla (hair) cells, creatine dose-response (vendor/in-vitro data). Creatine 0.25–1 mM increased DNA synthesis in human dermal papilla cells (≈+36% at 0.25 mM, dose-dependent). Relevance to facial anti-aging is indirect (hair model); source rigor is lower — treat as supportive only. [11] [partially unverified]

Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

  • There is no major creatine topical anti-aging animal model that adds beyond the human and cell data. (Murine work on CK distribution after growth-factor application exists but is tangential.) The field jumped from cell/ex-vivo mechanism straight to human formulation studies — a point in creatine's favor for relevance, but it means we have no rodent dose-ranging to lean on. (No tier-2 citation claimed.)

Tier 3 — Human ORAL (context only — not the skin claim)

Oral creatine is one of the most-studied sports-nutrition supplements in existence, which matters here only for systemic safety context (§7), not for facial skin claims.

  • Kreider R.B. et al. / Buford T.W. et al. (2007). International Society of Sports Nutrition position stand: creatine supplementation and exercise. JISSN 4:6 (and the 2017 update, Kreider et al., JISSN 14:18). Conclusion: creatine monohydrate is effective and safe; up to 5 years of supplementation showed no adverse effect on renal function in healthy people. Oral/muscle; included only as a safety anchor. [13]
  • Antonio J. et al. (2021). Common questions and misconceptions about creatine supplementation. JISSN 18:13. Debunks dehydration/cramping/kidney myths; reaffirms safety in healthy adults. [13]
  • de Souza e Silva A. et al. / recent pooled safety analyses (2025), JISSN. Analysis of adverse-event prevalence across clinical trials found side-effect rates comparable to placebo at recommended doses. [13]
  • Oral creatine for skin/anti-aging: essentially no evidence. Despite heavy 2024–2025 "creatine for skin" marketing, there is no human trial showing oral creatine improves facial wrinkles, firmness, or photoaging. Any "ingestible creatine for skin" claim is currently extrapolation. (Honesty flag.)

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters for a skincare brand)

This is creatine's differentiator versus most longevity actives — it actually has two in-vivo human topical studies. Read the fine print.

  • Peirano R.I., Achterberg V., Düsing H.-J., Akhiani M., Koop U., Jaspers S., Krüger A., Schwengler H., Hamann T., Wenck H., Stäb F., Gallinat S., Blatt T. (2011). Dermal penetration of creatine from a face-care formulation containing creatine, guarana and glycerol is linked to effective antiwrinkle and antisagging efficacy in male subjects. Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology 10(4):273–281. PMID 22151935. The pivotal in-vivo study. Design: combined ex-vivo penetration + single-center in-vivo clinical on 43 male Caucasian subjects, 6 weeks twice-daily use of a creatine + guarana + glycerol face cream. Results: topical creatine penetrated to the dermis; significant reduction in jowl sagging vs baseline; significant reduction in crow's-feet and under-eye wrinkles vs baseline. Limitations: (a) combination formula — guarana (caffeine, vasoactive/antioxidant) and glycerol (humectant) plausibly drive part of the visible benefit, so the effect can't be attributed to creatine alone; (b) men only; (c) baseline-controlled, not vehicle/placebo-controlled in the published design for the efficacy arm; (d) manufacturer-run (Beiersdorf); (e) n=43, single center, 6 weeks. [6]
  • Fischer F., Achterberg V., März A., Puschmann S., Rahn C.-D., Lutz V., Krüger A., Schwengler H., Jaspers S., Koop U., Blatt T., Wenck H., Gallinat S. (2011). Folic acid and creatine improve the firmness of human skin in vivo. Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology 10(1):15–23. In-vivo arm: 15 female volunteers, 35–53 y, twice-daily application of a folic acid + creatine formulation. Results: significantly improved skin firmness, reduced crow's-feet wrinkle volume, and improved epidermal cell turnover; in-vitro dermal equivalents showed ↑collagen. Limitations: (a) combination (folic acid co-active); (b) very small (n=15), women only; (c) manufacturer-run (Beiersdorf); (d) short; (e) baseline-referenced. [7]
  • "Topical application of creatine is multibeneficial for human skin" — Blatt T. et al., conference abstract, Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology (2004) Vol. 50/Issue Suppl. A meeting abstract summarizing the multi-benefit topical creatine thesis. Limitation: conference abstract, not a peer-reviewed full study — supportive citation only. [1] [partially unverified — abstract]

Bottom line by tier: For creatine specifically, the proven facts are: the CK/PCr energy system operates in human skin (Tier 1), creatine protects mitochondria/mtDNA against UV in skin cells (Tier 1), it penetrates to the human dermis (Tier 4), and two small manufacturer-run human studies show firmness/wrinkle improvement from creatine-containing combination creams (Tier 4). What is NOT established: a large independent RCT on creatine alone, female + male generalizability for the wrinkle data, any oral-creatine skin benefit, and any glycation/pigment/microbiome effect.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • In favor — and this is creatine's strongest single asset: It is small (131 g/mol) and the dedicated creatine transporter (SLC6A8) is expressed in skin [3], so uptake is active, not just passive diffusion. The Peirano (2011) ex-vivo work directly showed topically applied creatine rapidly reaches the dermis [6] — a level of penetration proof most "energy"/peptide actives lack.
  • Caveats: Creatine is a polar zwitterion with limited lipid solubility, so getting it across the stratum corneum depends on formulation (humectants like glycerol — present in the Peirano formula — and propanediol help; high water activity helps). Modest water solubility (~13 g/L) [8] caps how much you can dissolve, which constrains achievable concentration in simple aqueous systems.
  • Open questions: No published study quantifies the intracellular creatine rise or PCr/CK flux increase in living human dermis after cosmetic use — the inference "penetrates → recharges cells → firmer skin" is mechanistically reasonable and partly supported, but the full causal chain in vivo isn't fully measured.
  • Practical read: Penetration is better-evidenced than for almost any peer energy active, but still formulation-dependent; pair with humectants and keep water activity high.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: No single "validated %" exists for creatine alone. Cosmetic use is typically ~0.25–5% (commonly ~0.5–2% in finished products); the Beiersdorf studies used finished combination creams rather than disclosed single-ingredient %. Formulate within solubility limits and do not imply a precise validated dose-response.
  • Solubility constraint (the main practical hurdle): ~13 g/L in water [8] means a plain aqueous serum tops out near ~1–1.3% before saturation; higher loads need heat-assisted dissolution, humectant co-solvents (glycerol, propanediol, betaine), or accepting a partly suspended system. Watch for recrystallization on cooling/storage.
  • pH: Creatine is most stable in mildly acidic-to-neutral aqueous solution and is happy in the skin-friendly pH ~5–6 range; avoid strongly acidic or strongly alkaline systems and avoid prolonged heat, because creatine degrades to creatinine (inactive) faster at low pH and high temperature. Add late/cool where possible, and confirm finished pH ~5–6.
  • Stability: Solid creatine monohydrate is stable dry; the liability is in-solution conversion to creatinine with time, heat, and acidity. Run real-time and accelerated stability with a creatinine check; favor anhydrous-leaning or low-water-activity formats, or encapsulation, if conversion is an issue.
  • Pairings (rational):
  • Glycerol / humectants — used in the human studies; aid penetration and hydration. [6]
  • Niacinamide — complementary energy/NAD + barrier story; pH-compatible.
  • Antioxidants (vitamin E, ferulic, idebenone) — complement the mitochondrial-protection narrative.
  • Peptides / matrikines — collagen-support stack.
  • Folic acid — literal precedent (Fischer 2011 formula) though folic acid's own topical evidence is weak. [7]
  • Caffeine/guarana — the Peirano precedent (de-puffing/microcirculation), but it complicates attribution. [6]
  • Conflicts: Strong acids (low-pH vitamin C, high-strength AHA peels) accelerate creatinine conversion and may compete for the formula's water — keep creatine in mild-pH systems or separate from acid steps.
  • Sensory: At cosmetic levels, creatine is odorless, colorless, non-tacky, and easy to formulate into light emulsions/serums; the powder can feel slightly draggy if under-dissolved (another reason to fully solubilize).

7. Safety

  • Irritation/sensitization (topical): Creatine is endogenous to skin, non-acidic, and has a clean tolerability record in the human topical studies and in market use across moisturizers, eye creams, and post-sun products; no notable irritation or sensitization signal at cosmetic levels. There is no dedicated published HRIPT for creatine monotherapy, so we should still generate our own finished-formula patch/HRIPT data. [6][7]
  • Sun sensitivity: None expected — mechanistically creatine is photo-protective (stabilizes mitochondria/CK and mtDNA under UVA in vitro). [2][3] It does not replace sunscreen, and copy must not imply so.
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding (topical): No specific topical pregnancy data. Creatine is endogenous and the topical dose is tiny, so the theoretical risk is low; for oral creatine, supplement guidance generally notes a lack of pregnancy data and advises caution — so default to conservative copy ("consult your doctor") rather than a pregnancy-safe claim. [13]
  • Systemic creatine safety (context, from the oral literature): Extensively studied; no adverse renal effect in healthy people across studies up to 5 years, and dehydration/cramping/kidney-damage claims are not supported. Elevated serum creatinine from supplementation reflects metabolism, not kidney injury. Caution only for pre-existing renal disease. This is reassuring background for a topical, where systemic exposure is negligible. [13]
  • Sourcing/allergen: Modern cosmetic/supplement creatine is synthetic (from sarcosine + cyanamide) — vegan/vegetarian-friendly and not animal-derived, despite the "flesh" etymology. Specify a creatinine-controlled, low-heavy-metal, low-dicyandiamide (DCD) grade on the COA; cheap industrial creatine can carry impurities.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic ingredient status: Listed as INCI Creatine (chemical name N-(aminoiminomethyl)-N-methylglycine), EU CosIng/COSMILE function "skin conditioning." No EU Annex restriction noted; confirm legality per target market and rely on supplier regulatory docs. [9][10]
  • The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame): The danger zone is the mechanism story. "Recharges cell energy / boosts mitochondria / repairs collagen / reverses photoaging at the cellular level" all drift toward structure/function (drug) claims. The firmness/wrinkle-appearance endpoints from the human studies can support cosmetic appearance claims if worded carefully and substantiated on our own finished formula.
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "supports skin's natural energy," "helps improve the appearance of firmness," "visibly reduces the look of fine lines," "energizing," "supports skin's resilience to daily environmental stress," "a small molecule skin can take up" (penetration is genuinely supported). Even firmness/wrinkle wording should be substantiated on our formula, since the literature is on other combination creams.
  • ⚠️ Risky / needs substantiation or legal review: "boosts collagen by X%," "repairs mitochondria," "reverses aging," "increases cellular ATP in your skin," "anti-aging at the cellular/DNA level," "protects DNA from UV" (the mtDNA result is in vitro), "replaces sunscreen." Importing the oral sports-nutrition evidence or the in-vitro DNA results into topical consumer claims is the central trap.
  • Substantiation reality: The published human data are on competitor (Beiersdorf) combination formulas, not ours — so for any performance claim we should run our own finished-formula human testing. See ../../brand/glossary.md.
  • IP note: Beiersdorf holds patents on topical creatine cosmetic compositions (e.g., US 6,413,552; US 6,730,331; "creatine for skin preservation" filings). Some may be expired or expiring given 2000s priority dates, but clear freedom-to-operate before launch. [14]

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Forms/suppliers: Creatine monohydrate is a globally commoditized raw material (the supplement industry produces it at enormous scale). Cosmetic-grade and USP/pharma-grade available from mainstream fine-chemical and nutraceutical suppliers; cosmetic distributors list "Creatine" directly.
  • Cost tier: Low. Creatine monohydrate is one of the cheapest actives per kg — its cost-to-credibility ratio is excellent. Specialty derivatives (HCl, ethyl ester) cost more with no topical upside.
  • Supply/standardization risk: Low supply risk; the quality risk is purity — specify ≥99.9% with controlled creatinine, DCD, and heavy metals (most reputable supply is "Creapure"-type German-made or audited Chinese material). Confirm vegan/synthetic origin for clean-beauty positioning.

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • "Creatine for skin is brand-new science" — false. The core skin work is 2004–2011 (Lenz, Berneburg, Peirano, Fischer). The 2024–2025 wave is a TikTok/marketing revival of old data, not new evidence.
  • "Oral creatine gives you younger skin" — unsupported. The viral "ingestible creatine for skin" trend has zero human skin trials. All credible skin data are topical (or in-vitro). Flag any oral-skin claim as extrapolation.
  • "Clinically proven to boost collagen / reverse wrinkles" — overstated. The human studies are small, manufacturer-run, combination formulas, baseline-referenced (and one men-only). They show real appearance improvement, but not clean creatine-alone, vehicle-controlled proof.
  • The combination-formula problem. Both pivotal human studies bundled creatine with glycerol/guarana or folic acid — so part of the visible benefit is plausibly humectant/caffeine/co-active, not creatine. Don't claim creatine as the cause.
  • "Protects your DNA from the sun." True in cultured fibroblasts (mtDNA common-deletion normalization) — not demonstrated as consumer photoprotection, and absolutely not a sunscreen substitute.
  • Pigmentation / glycation / microbiome claims — invented. No creatine-specific evidence exists for these hallmarks; AGE/pigment links in the literature are about glycation generally, not creatine. [12]
  • Derivative hype (creatine HCl/ethyl ester "better absorbed"). Marketing borrowed from supplements; no topical skin advantage shown — monohydrate is the evidenced form.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️→✅ SUPPORTING, trending toward a credible secondary hero in an "energy/resilience" product. One-line: Creatine is a rare energy active with genuine, on-target human topical evidence and proven dermal penetration — cheap, well-tolerated, mechanistically literal, and easy to story-tell — but its best clinical data are small, manufacturer-run, combination-formula studies, so it earns a strong supporting role (or co-hero in a deliberately "cellular-energy" SKU), not a stand-alone "clinically proven" hero claim without our own testing.

Why it fits a skin-longevity brand: Creatine maps directly onto the "mitochondrial decline / cellular energy" hallmark of aging — a hallmark few cosmetic actives address with real skin biology. The CK/PCr system is provably present and functional in human skin, which lets us tell an honest, differentiated "skin runs out of energy as it ages; this helps it keep its charge" story without resorting to hype. It pairs naturally with NAD/niacinamide and antioxidant narratives the brand is already building, and its low cost + clean safety + small-molecule penetration make it low-risk to include.

The honest gap & path forward: To make performance claims, we need our own finished-formula human study (ideally creatine-forward, vehicle-controlled, both sexes) because the literature substantiates competitor combination creams, not ours. Use mechanism-framed, appearance-level copy until then; never import the oral sports-nutrition or in-vitro DNA results into consumer claims.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19 by adversarial fact-check against primary sources (PubMed, JID, J Cosmet Dermatol, JISSN, supplier chemistry data). Method: for each headline claim, asked (a) is the evidence HUMAN and TOPICAL or borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral; (b) are the cited studies real and do they say what is claimed; (c) industry-funded or tiny.

Overall grade: B− (moderate human-topical, with heavy caveats)

Creatine is a genuine outlier in this research base: most "longevity" actives are graded C/D because their human data are oral or absent. Creatine has two real in-vivo human topical studies with measurable wrinkle/firmness endpoints plus proven dermal penetration — that earns a B. It is held below a clean B (and well below A) because both pivotal human studies are small, manufacturer-run (Beiersdorf), combination formulas, and baseline-controlled rather than vehicle/placebo-controlled, and one is men-only. There is no independent, vehicle-controlled, creatine-monotherapy facial RCT. The dossier was already unusually honest about this; verification confirmed its caveats rather than overturning them.

Citation check (all primary citations verified REAL and accurately characterized)

  • Peirano et al. 2011 [6] — VERIFIED via PubMed (PMID 22151935) and Wiley. Real. n=43 male Caucasians, 6 weeks, creatine + guarana + glycerol cream. All efficacy endpoints (jowl sagging, crow's-feet, under-eye wrinkles) reported "as compared to baseline" — confirmed NOT vehicle/placebo-controlled. Lead author affiliation: Beiersdorf AG R&D, Hamburg — confirmed manufacturer-run. Dossier's description is accurate.
  • Fischer et al. 2011 [7] — VERIFIED via Wiley/Semantic Scholar. Real. Topical folic acid + creatine; in-vivo cutometric firmness improvement + crow's-feet wrinkle reduction in mature skin; in-vitro collagen ↑ in dermal equivalents. Combination formula, Beiersdorf authors. Dossier's "n=15 women, 35–53 y" is consistent with the published study and widely cited; the headline conclusions are accurately represented.
  • Lenz et al. 2005 [3] — VERIFIED via JID (124:443–452). Real. Demonstrated cytosolic + mitochondrial CK isoenzymes and a creatine transporter in human skin; exogenous creatine uptake ↑ CK activity and mitochondrial function and protected against oxidative/UV stress. IMPORTANT: the paper's title says "In Vitro and In Vivo," but the "in vivo" component is a demonstration of creatine's protective/energetic effects in skin — NOT a wrinkle/firmness clinical efficacy trial. The dossier already flags this correctly ("'in vivo' component is supportive, not a wrinkle RCT"). Do not let the paper's title be used to imply human clinical efficacy.
  • Berneburg et al. 2005 [2][4] — VERIFIED via PubMed (PMID 16098029) and JID (125:213–220). Real. Creatine (1 mM) in cultured human fibroblasts abolished UVA-induced mtDNA "common deletion" and normalized O₂ consumption and MMP-1. This is in-vitro fibroblast data — correctly tiered. The "protects DNA from UV" story is in-vitro only; the dossier already bars it from consumer copy.
  • Chemistry [8] — VERIFIED. Creatine monohydrate water solubility ~13–14 g/L at 20–25 °C; degrades to creatinine; MW 131.13 (anhydrous). Accurate; the solubility-as-formulation-constraint point is sound.
  • ISSN safety position stand (Kreider 2017) [13] — VERIFIED via JISSN (14:18). Confirms "up to 30 g/day for 5 years is safe and well-tolerated in healthy individuals." Dossier's "no adverse renal effect up to 5 years in healthy people" is accurate. This is ORAL/systemic context only — correctly walled off from topical efficacy claims.
  • Tier-1 hair / dermal-papilla [11] and conference abstract [1] — already self-flagged "[partially unverified]" in the dossier; not load-bearing. Left as-is.

Evidence by claim type

  • HUMAN + TOPICAL (the claim that matters): YES, exists — but only as small, combination-formula, baseline-controlled, manufacturer-run studies. This is the basis for the B-, not an A.
  • Dermal penetration: Genuinely human/ex-vivo supported (Peirano) — one of creatine's strongest, cleanest assets. Defensible.
  • Mechanism (CK/PCr system in skin): Genuinely proven in human skin (Lenz) — defensible, but is mechanism, not efficacy.
  • Mitochondrial/mtDNA/collagen/MMP-1: In-vitro (cell + 3D) only. Borrowed into the story but correctly tiered.
  • Oral creatine for skin: No human skin evidence — extrapolation. Dossier already flags this hard.
  • Glycation / pigmentation / microbiome: No creatine-specific evidence — dossier correctly calls these invented.

Main caveats (the things to keep front-of-mind)

  1. No vehicle/placebo-controlled creatine-monotherapy facial RCT exists. Every human efficacy result is baseline-referenced and from a creatine-containing combination cream.
  2. Attribution problem: guarana (caffeine) + glycerol, or folic acid, co-drive the visible benefit. Creatine cannot be claimed as the cause.
  3. Industry funding + small n: both pivotal studies are Beiersdorf-run; n=43 (men) and n=15 (women). Not independent, not large.
  4. Title trap: Lenz 2005's "in vivo" and Berneburg 2005's mtDNA result are NOT human wrinkle data — guard against citing them as clinical efficacy.

Claims to soften / already softened in this pass

  • Edited the one-line summary and the collagen/ECM hallmark row to state explicitly that the human studies are baseline-controlled, not vehicle/placebo-controlled (this was implied later but not at the top).
  • No claim in the dossier rises to the level of fabrication or unsupported hype; the §10 "Hype vs evidence" and §8 "Regulatory & claims" sections already pre-empt the main overreach traps (oral-for-skin, DNA protection, "boosts collagen by X%," sunscreen substitution). These were verified as appropriately conservative and left intact.

Net: The dossier is honest and well-evidenced for the category. Grade B−. Safe to use mechanism-framed, appearance-level cosmetic copy and penetration claims; any performance claim still requires our own finished-formula, ideally vehicle-controlled, both-sex human testing.
