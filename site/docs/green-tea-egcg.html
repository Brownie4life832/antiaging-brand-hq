Ingredient: EGCG / Green Tea Polyphenols (INCI: Camellia Sinensis Leaf Extract; Epigallocatechin Gallate)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Slug: green-tea-egcg · Class: NAD+ / sirtuin / AMPK / cellular-energy actives (longevity-signaling antioxidant)

Reading note. The brief files EGCG under the "NAD+ / sirtuin / AMPK / cellular-energy" longevity class. That filing is defensible but needs an honest caveat up front: EGCG's sirtuin/AMPK/FOXO mechanism is real and well-published, but it is demonstrated mainly in model organisms (C. elegans), cell culture, and rodents — not in living human skin under a cream. EGCG's strongest, most replicated dermatology evidence is actually as a topical antioxidant / photoprotective / anti-MMP polyphenol, which is a different (overlapping) story. This dossier keeps those two stories separate and is explicit about which format each claim was proven in.

0. The honest punchline (read this first)

  • Green tea polyphenols (the catechin family — EGCG, ECG, EGC, EC) are among the best-studied plant antioxidants in dermatology. The mechanistic and photoprotection literature (in-vitro + animal + some human) is genuinely deep: ROS scavenging, MMP suppression (collagen-sparing), anti-inflammatory NF-κB/AP-1 blockade, DNA-damage reduction, and reduced UV-induced "sunburn cells" / Langerhans-cell protection.
  • The longevity-class framing (AMPK/SIRT1/FOXO, mitohormesis, autophagy) is real biology but format-borrowed. The cleanest causal demonstration is a C. elegans lifespan study where EGCG extended healthy lifespan dependent on AMPK, SIRT1 and FOXO (Xiong 2018 [16]). That is mechanism, in a worm — not proof a human face cream activates skin sirtuins.
  • For the format we actually sell — topical, human, appearance — the RCT picture is mixed and modest. The single best-known human anti-aging RCT (Chiu 2005, n=40, topical+oral green tea, 8 wk [5]) found NO significant clinical improvement vs placebo and more irritation in the green-tea arm; only a histologic elastic-tissue change reached significance. A 2-year oral RCT (Janjua 2009, n=56 [6]) was also negative for clinical and histologic photoaging. A smaller Phase II 2% EGCG gel study (Syed/Hsu 2003 [7]) reported positive damaged-skin improvement.
  • Net: EGCG is a credible, evidence-grounded supporting antioxidant with a legitimate longevity-signaling halo — not a proven hero "wrinkle eraser." Its biggest practical liabilities are chemical instability/oxidation (it browns and degrades) and the gap between mechanism and human topical appearance outcomes. Treat it as a formulation-quality and story asset, formulated carefully, with disciplined claims.

Why the longevity class fits (the kernel of truth): EGCG is an activator of the AMPK → SIRT1 → FOXO energy-sensing axis and a modulator of mTOR/autophagy and Nrf2. SIRT1 is NAD+-dependent, which is the bridge to the "NAD+/sirtuin/cellular-energy" taxonomy. The connection is mechanistically genuine; the human-skin-topical proof is the missing piece (see §4).

2. Class & mechanism

Class: Longevity-signaling polyphenol / antioxidant. Primary documented mode of action in skin is antioxidant + anti-inflammatory + anti-protease (MMP); the longevity-class hook is its AMPK/SIRT1/FOXO + autophagy + Nrf2 signaling.

Mechanistic threads (with the format each was shown in):

  1. Direct antioxidant / ROS scavenging. EGCG's eight phenolic hydroxyls make it a potent radical scavenger; it spares glutathione (GSH) and suppresses UV-driven H₂O₂ and lipid/protein oxidation (in-vitro + mouse; Vayalil/Katiyar [9]). Caveat: the same redox activity makes EGCG auto-oxidize and brown in aqueous formula (see §5–6).
  2. MMP suppression → collagen/ECM sparing. EGCG inhibits UV- and TNF-α-induced MMP-1, -2, -3, -7, -9 by blocking ERK/p38 MAPK and NF-κB / AP-1 signaling, reducing collagen-degrading protease load (in-vitro fibroblasts + mouse skin [1][9]).
  3. Anti-inflammatory (inflammaging). Downregulates IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α, COX-2; blocks NF-κB p65/p50 nuclear translocation and JAK/STAT signaling (in-vitro; some human [1]).
  4. Photoimmune / DNA protection. Topical green tea reduces UV-induced "sunburn cells," protects epidermal Langerhans cells, and lowers cyclobutane pyrimidine dimer (CPD) DNA damage (human + mouse; Elmets 2001 [4]; Katiyar [1][8]).
  5. Longevity signaling (the class hook). AMPK/SIRT1/FOXO-dependent lifespan extension via mitohormesis (a small ROS pulse that upregulates endogenous defenses) — shown causally in C. elegans (Xiong 2018 [16]); EGCG also modulates mTOR/autophagy and activates Nrf2 antioxidant-response genes (review [17]).
  6. Anti-glycation (AGEs). Catechins inhibit advanced-glycation-end-product formation and collagen cross-linking; green tea extract reduced age-related collagen crosslinking/pentosidine in mice and AGE/cross-linking in diabetic-rat tendon (animal; Rutter/Babu [14][15]).
  7. Anti-melanogenic (pigment/tone). EGCG/ECG/GCG downregulate the cAMP/CREB → MITF → tyrosinase/TRP-1/TRP-2 cascade and directly inhibit tyrosinase, lowering melanin (in-vitro melanocytes/melanoma [11][12]).
  8. Sebum/antimicrobial/barrier-adjacent. Reduces sebum output (small human study [1]); antibacterial vs C. acnes; oral catechins improved barrier/TEWL in one human study [1].

4. Evidence review by tier (the core of this dossier)

Tiers, weakest-to-strongest format relevance for a topical cosmetic: in-vitro → animal → human ORAL → human TOPICAL (RCT vs lower). For each claim I flag whether it is proven for topical human use or borrowed from another format. Brutal-honesty rule applies.

TIER A — In-vitro (cell / biochemistry)

A1. MMP suppression & collagen sparing. EGCG inhibits UV/TNF-α-induced MMP-1, -2, -3, -7, -9 in human dermal fibroblasts by blocking ERK/MEK and NF-κB/AP-1 (multiple in-vitro studies summarized in MDPI 2024 review [1]; mechanism corroborated in [9]). Mechanistic basis for "collagen protection," not appearance proof.

A2. Antioxidant / GSH-sparing / lipid-protein oxidation. EGCG and green tea polyphenols reduce UVB-induced ROS, prevent GSH depletion, and lower lipid/protein oxidation in human HS68 fibroblasts and keratinocytes (Vayalil/Katiyar work [9]).

A3. Anti-melanogenic. EGCG/ECG/GCG downregulate cAMP/CREB→MITF and inhibit tyrosinase/TRP-1/TRP-2 and melanin in B16 melanoma & melanocyte models; some catechins out-performed arbutin in cell models (ScienceDirect / vetsci / PMC sources [11][12]). Cell-model only.

A4. Longevity signaling. EGCG activates AMPK/SIRT1, modulates mTOR/autophagy and Nrf2 in cell models; reviewed as a "sirtuin/AMPK aging-pathway" compound alongside resveratrol/curcumin (Immunity & Ageing 2025 review [17]).

A5. Stability liability (formulation-relevant). In aqueous systems EGCG auto-oxidizes (transparent→brown) and epimerizes to GCG faster at higher temperature / lower pH (narrative review [2]; co-antioxidant stability study [3]). This is a negative in-vitro finding that drives formulation choices (§6).

Limitations across Tier A: cultured cells bathed in EGCG ≠ skin under a cream; gallated catechins are unstable; many "stronger than arbutin / tretinoin-like" claims are cell-model extrapolations.

TIER B — Animal (mostly topical/oral mouse — supportive, not human)

B1. Topical green tea polyphenols prevent UV photoaging in mouse skin. Vayalil, Katiyar et al., J Invest Dermatol 2004;122(6):1480–1487 (PMID 15175040 [9]). Topical/oral GTP in mice ↓UV-induced MMP-2/-3/-7/-9, ↓protein/lipid oxidation, ↓MAPK phosphorylation, preserved antioxidant enzymes. Strong mechanistic photoaging support — but mouse.

B2. Topical green tea polyphenols protect against acute UVB photodamage (hairless mice). Green tea polyphenols emulsified in carboxymethyl cellulose reduced acute UVB damage (Photochem Photobiol Sci 2016 [18]).

B3. Anti-glycation in vivo (animal). Green tea extract suppressed age-related collagen crosslinking and fluorescent products in C57BL/6 mice (Rutter et al., Int J Vitam Nutr Res 2003; PMID 14743550 [14]); reduced AGE/cross-linking of tail-tendon collagen in diabetic rats (Babu et al., Food Chem Toxicol 2008 [15]). Borrowed format + species; supports an anti-glycation story, not a human-skin claim.

TIER C — Human ORAL (real RCTs — but mostly negative for skin appearance / photoprotection)

Oral green tea has multiple genuine human RCTs. They are the honest cautionary tale: systemic absorption is confirmed, skin-appearance/photoprotection benefits are inconsistent-to-negative.

C1. Negative — oral green tea did NOT improve photoaging over 2 years. Janjua R, Chang ALS, et al., Dermatol Surg 2009;35(7):1057–1065 (PMID, [6]). Double-blind RCT, n=56 women (25–75), 250 mg GTP BID vs placebo ×24 months, blinded scoring + histology at 0/6/12/24 mo. No superiority over placebo in clinical or histologic photoaging. Longest, cleanest oral skin-aging RCT — and it's negative.

C2. Negative — oral catechins did NOT protect against UV inflammation. Farrar MD, Rhodes LE, et al., Am J Clin Nutr 2015;102(3):608–613 (PMC4548173 [10]). RCT, n=50 (45 per-protocol), 1080 mg GTC + 100 mg vit C daily ×12 wk. No significant change in UV-erythema threshold (p=0.47), dermal leukocyte infiltration, or eicosanoids — despite confirmed systemic absorption (urinary EGC-glucuronide rose sharply). Absorption ≠ efficacy.

C3. Negative — oral catechins do not replace sunscreen for DNA damage. Companion/related RCT: oral GTC did not protect against direct DNA damage from higher-dose solar-simulated radiation (Rhodes group, PMC5785335 [10b]).

C4. Positive (modest) — oral green tea polyphenols improved skin photoprotection/microcirculation. Heinrich U, et al., J Nutr 2011;141(6):1202–1208 ([13]). RCT, n=60 women, beverage ~1402 mg GTC/day ×12 wk: ↓UV-induced erythema, ↑skin blood flow/microcirculation, improved elasticity/roughness/density/hydration. One of the more positive oral results; beverage format, modest effect sizes.

C5. Mixed across the literature. A systematic review of oral green tea in skin ailments (Sharma/Katiyar-adjacent; Nutrients 2022;14(15):3149 [19]) concludes evidence is heterogeneous and condition-dependent, strongest for inflammatory/photoprotective endpoints, weak/inconsistent for anti-aging appearance.

TIER D — Human TOPICAL (the format we sell)

D1. Negative for clinical appearance — the landmark anti-aging topical RCT. Chiu AE, Kimball AB, et al., Dermatol Surg 2005;31(7 Pt 2):855–860 (PMID 16029678 [5]). Double-blind, placebo-controlled, n=40 women with moderate photoaging; 10% green tea cream + 300 mg BID oral green tea vs placebo ×8 wk; clinical grading + skin biopsies. - Clinical result: NO significant difference vs placebo — except higher subjective irritation in the green-tea arm. - Histologic result: significant ↑ elastic tissue content in treated biopsies (p<0.05). - Interpretation: a real but sub-clinical (microscope-only) tissue signal, no visible benefit in 8 weeks, at the cost of more irritation. The most-cited "green tea anti-aging" study is, honestly, clinically negative.

D2. Positive (low-confidence) — Phase II 2% EGCG ("Polyphenon") gel for damaged/aged facial skin. Syed/Hsu et al., "Phase II clinical evaluation of 2% polyphenone (green tea extract) in a hydrophilic gel…," J Am Acad Dermatol 2003/2004 (S0190-9622(03)03434-0 [7]). Placebo-controlled double-blind; 2% EGCG gel BID; reported as safe, tolerable, and significantly more beneficial than placebo for damaged/prematurely-aged facial skin. Treat as weak evidence: sample size, duration, and effect sizes are not publicly verifiable (paywalled, byline not confirmed this session); the positive conclusion appears in the JAAD record and secondary summaries but the primary methodology could not be checked. A lone, thin, hard-to-verify positive is not a reliable counterweight to the larger, fully-verified negative Chiu 2005 — do not lean on it for claims.

D3. Positive — topical green tea reduces UV erythema & sunburn cells (human). Elmets CA, et al., J Am Acad Dermatol 2001;44(3):425–432 (PMID 11209110 [4]). Dose-dependent inhibition of UV-induced erythema; gallated catechins (EGCG, ECG) most effective; histology showed fewer sunburn cells, protected Langerhans cells, less UV DNA damage. Strong topical photoprotection signal — but an acute UV-challenge model, not long-term anti-aging.

D4. Mechanistic human topical — caspase-3 / apoptosis of UV-damaged keratinocytes. Katiyar SK, et al., J Am Acad Dermatol 2005 (S0190-9622(04)03380-8 [8]). Topical EGCG on UVB-irradiated human skin increased caspase-3 in keratinocytes (pro-apoptotic clearance of UV-damaged cells). Mechanistic human topical support for photoprotection.

D5. Topical for other dermatoses (supportive context). EGCG/green-tea topicals show genuine efficacy in anogenital warts (Polyphenon E 10–15% ointment, FDA-approved sinecatechins/Veregen, n=503 RCT, ~50% clearance [1]) and radiation dermatitis (EGCG solution, n=49 [1]). These confirm topical EGCG does something on human skin — but they are drug indications, not cosmetic anti-aging proof.

Topical bottom line: EGCG topically has good human evidence for acute photoprotection (erythema/sunburn-cell/DNA endpoints) and weak/mixed evidence for visible anti-aging — one fully-verified negative landmark appearance RCT (Chiu 2005) against one small, paywalled, hard-to-verify positive Phase II (D2). There is no large, long-term topical RCT proving an EGCG cosmetic visibly reverses wrinkles in humans. The anti-aging case is mechanistic + photoprotective + histologic, not appearance-RCT-proven.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Penetration is plausible but format-dependent. Dvorakova/Dorr et al., Cancer Chemother Pharmacol 1999;43(4):331–335 (PMID 10071985 [20]): 10% EGCG in hydrophilic ointment achieved substantial intradermal uptake (~1–20% of applied dose) with negligible systemic absorption in human/mouse skin in vitro. Good news: it reaches viable skin without going systemic.
  • Formulation steers depth. In comparative human-skin work, ~35–36% of applied EGCG permeated the stratum corneum; gels concentrate EGCG in superficial layers, emulsions push it deeper (but emulsions were less stable) (penetration studies in narrative review [2]).
  • The real limiter is instability, not penetration. EGCG auto-oxidizes (transparent→brown) and photodegrades rapidly. Under simulated sunlight, EGCG in cream lost ~77% within exposure; thermally, ~10% lost in 2 days at 37 °C (review [2]; stability study [3]). The molecule you carefully formulate may be partly gone — or oxidized — before it acts. (Partially oxidized EGCG retains some antioxidant capacity, which softens but does not erase the problem.)
  • Delivery upgrades (liposomes, nano-transfersomes, EGCG3″Me / GCG epimers, PLGA, co-antioxidant systems) measurably improve stability and skin delivery in lab models — relevant if EGCG is a feature ingredient rather than a trace botanical.

6. Formulation

  • Concentration: Cosmetic green-tea extract is commonly used at low % (often <1% standardized extract). Purified EGCG efficacy studies cluster at 2% (gel, [7]) up to 10% (cream, [5]); the higher end (Chiu) tracked with more irritation and no clinical benefit, so "more is better" is false here. A standardized extract at modest % with strong antioxidant protection is the pragmatic target.
  • pH: EGCG is most stable at mildly acidic pH (~4–5.5) and degrades faster as pH rises toward neutral/alkaline; at low pH + heat it epimerizes to GCG (which is actually more heat-stable). Avoid high-pH systems.
  • Stability (the dominant constraint):
  • Highly prone to auto-oxidation and photodegradation → browning, loss of activity.
  • Stabilizers that work: BHT 0.1% dramatically extended EGCG shelf life (≥90% retained at 130 days/37 °C vs lost in 2 days without it; Dvorakova [20]); co-antioxidants (vitamin C/E, sodium metabisulfite, ascorbyl derivatives) improve photochemical and functional stability ([3]); chelators (EDTA) to remove metal ions that catalyze oxidation; opaque/air-tight, low-headspace packaging; reduced water activity / anhydrous or encapsulated systems; cool storage.
  • Pairings (synergistic / sensible):
  • Vitamin C + E — classic antioxidant network; also helps stabilize EGCG ([3]); anti-glycation co-story.
  • Niacinamide — barrier/tone partner; both well-tolerated, pH-compatible.
  • Sunscreen / SPF — EGCG complements but does not replace sunscreen ([10][10b]); pair, never substitute.
  • Ferulic acid, resveratrol, other polyphenols — antioxidant-network and longevity-story coherence.
  • Conflicts / watch-outs: high pH, prolonged heat/light, oxidizers, and (in some systems) interaction with proteins/metal ions. Don't co-formulate where browning would be cosmetically unacceptable.
  • Sensory: Green-tea extracts can carry a faint plant color/odor; oxidized EGCG browns the product — the #1 cosmetic-elegance risk. Purified EGCG is less colored but less stable. Tinting/packaging strategy required.

7. Safety

  • Topical, cosmetic levels: generally well tolerated. The Cosmetic Ingredient Review (CIR) safety assessment of Camellia sinensis-derived ingredients found leaf extract not irritating or sensitizing in human repeat-insult patch tests (HRIPT) at tested cosmetic levels (e.g. 0.86%) (CIR 2014 [21]).
  • Irritation signal at higher actives. In the Chiu 2005 topical RCT, the 10% green tea arm reported more subjective irritation than placebo ([5]) — a real, in-human caution against pushing concentration. EGCG isolate caused minor dermal irritation in some rodent models and was a moderate sensitizer in the guinea-pig maximization test; rare occupational contact sensitization to tea is documented ([21], sensitization sources). Eye irritation reported with EGCG instillation in a rabbit — keep out of eye-area products at high %.
  • Photosensitivity: No evidence EGCG is a photosensitizer; it is studied as photoprotective. Daytime-compatible (and synergistic with SPF).
  • Pregnancy: Topical green-tea polyphenols at cosmetic levels are low-concern; no specific topical contraindication identified. (Distinct from oral high-dose concerns below.) Defer to medical advice; avoid disease claims.
  • Oral high-dose hepatotoxicity (NOT our format, but disclose honestly). EFSA 2018 concluded green-tea-catechin supplements providing ≥800 mg EGCG/day are associated with signs of liver injury (ALT/AST elevations); the EU now caps EGCG in food supplements and requires a warning (EFSA Journal 2018;16(4):5239 [22]). Brewed tea is considered safe; the risk is concentrated oral supplements. This is irrelevant to a topical product but must never be implied away — and is a reason to keep any "drink-it-too" cross-selling cautious.
  • Sourcing/sustainability: Camellia sinensis is an abundant agricultural crop; standardized extracts are widely available. Quality varies enormously (total polyphenol %, EGCG %, heavy metals, pesticide residue, oxidation state) — demand a CoA: EGCG/total-catechin assay, oxidation/color spec, heavy-metals and pesticide limits, microbial spec.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

The cosmetic/drug line. Cosmetics may improve appearance; they may not claim to treat/prevent disease or alter the body's structure/function (US FDA; analogous EU rules). EGCG is doubly sensitive because it has actual drug approvals (sinecatechins/Veregen for genital warts) and chemoprevention research — that science must never bleed into our cosmetic copy.

  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "antioxidant-rich green tea polyphenols," "helps defend the skin's appearance against environmental/oxidative stress," "supports a more even-looking tone," "helps protect the look of skin from pollution/daily aggressors," "formulated with EGCG, a green-tea polyphenol studied for its antioxidant and skin-soothing properties," "complements your daily SPF."
  • ⚠️ Risky / drug-like / unsubstantiated — flag for legal:
  • "boosts collagen" / "rebuilds collagen by X%" — MMP/collagen data is in-vitro/animal; quantified claims need our own substantiation.
  • "activates sirtuins / longevity genes / cellular energy in your skin" — the AMPK/SIRT1/FOXO data is from worms/cells, not human skin in vivo; mechanism, not efficacy.
  • "reverses aging / erases wrinkles" — structure/function + overreach; the landmark human topical RCT was clinically negative ([5]).
  • "sun protection / replaces sunscreen / SPF" — explicitly contradicted by human RCTs ([10][10b]); pairing is fine, substitution is a compliance landmine.
  • "treats / prevents skin cancer / warts / rosacea / dermatitis" — these are drug indications (Veregen, radiation-dermatitis, etc.), absolutely off-limits for a cosmetic.
  • "anti-glycation / stops sugar-aging" — animal-only; keep as soft "helps defend the appearance against…" if used at all.
  • Key compliance nuance: EGCG's most impressive evidence (genital-wart clearance, UV-DNA protection, skin-cancer chemoprevention research) is the part we can least use. Keep the science as internal rationale and education; let "antioxidant defense / tone / soothing appearance" carry the on-label story.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • "Green tea reverses aging / a wrinkle-erasing antioxidant." — Overreach. The landmark human topical RCT (Chiu 2005, 10% cream + oral, 8 wk) was clinically negative and more irritating; only a microscope-level elastic-tissue change reached significance ([5]). The 2-year oral RCT was negative too ([6]).
  • "EGCG activates your skin's sirtuins / longevity genes / cellular energy." — Mechanism real, format borrowed. The clean AMPK/SIRT1/FOXO lifespan demonstration is in C. elegans ([16]); no human-skin in-vivo proof. Strong story, weak claim.
  • "Natural sun protection / antioxidant SPF." — Contradicted in humans. Oral catechins did not prevent UV erythema or DNA damage in RCTs ([10][10b]); even topical photoprotection is an adjunct to, not a replacement for, sunscreen.
  • "Clinically proven anti-aging." — Usually leaning on in-vitro MMP, mouse photoaging ([9]), or the drug indications (warts/radiation dermatitis) — not on a positive human anti-aging appearance RCT.
  • "Anti-glycation / stops sugar-aging." — Animal/in-vitro only ([14][15]); not human-skin-demonstrated.
  • What's NOT hype: EGCG is a genuinely potent antioxidant with real human topical photoprotection signals (↓UV erythema, ↓sunburn cells, ↓DNA damage; Elmets [4], Katiyar [8]), a deep, coherent mechanism (ROS/MMP/NF-κB + AMPK/SIRT1/FOXO longevity hook), and good cosmetic-level tolerability ([21]). It is a credible, defensible supporting antioxidant — provided it's formulated for stability and claimed with discipline.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️→✅ SUPPORTING antioxidant (conditional on formulation). Not a hero.

  • Why supporting, not hero: The human topical appearance RCT evidence is mixed-to-negative (Chiu 2005 clinically negative [5]); the dazzling parts of the dossier are either mechanism in worms/cells (the longevity hook) or drug indications we can't claim. EGCG cannot anchor an anti-aging hero claim on its own data.
  • Why still a yes (conditional): It's a real, well-studied antioxidant with human topical photoprotection signals, a coherent longevity-signaling mechanism that fits the brand's science narrative, good tolerability at sensible %, and low-to-mid cost. It earns a place as a supporting antioxidant in a serum/day product, paired with SPF and vitamin C/E/niacinamide, in a stability-protective formula (BHT/EDTA/co-antioxidants, opaque airless packaging, mildly acidic pH).
  • The two hard conditions: (1) Formulate for stability or it browns and dies — this is the single biggest practical risk. (2) Claims discipline — antioxidant/tone/environmental-defense language only; no sirtuin/collagen-boost/SPF/disease claims.

One-line reasoning: EGCG is a legitimate supporting antioxidant with a genuine longevity-mechanism halo — keep the AMPK/SIRT1/FOXO biology as education, let "antioxidant environmental defense + even tone" carry the claims, solve the stability problem, and never let it imply sun protection or collagen-boosting.

Longevity-relevance note: Of the "NAD+/sirtuin/AMPK/cellular-energy" class, EGCG is one of the most mechanistically credible longevity-signaling actives (causal AMPK/SIRT1/FOXO lifespan data exists — rare in this class), but like the rest of the class its human-skin-topical proof lags the mechanism. It pairs naturally with the niacinamide NAD+ story (see nad-niacinamide-longevity.md) and with spermidine/autophagy actives — a coherent "cellular-energy & cleanup" narrative, all of which must be told as mechanism/education, not efficacy.
