Ingredient: Altheostem™ — Hollyhock Stem-Cell Extract (INCI: Althaea Rosea Flower Extract [as supplied])

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Senotherapeutics & senolytics (botanical) · Slug: altheostem-hollyhock

Exhaustive literature review. Brutally honest evidence flag up front: all efficacy data for Altheostem are generated and owned by the supplier (Provital S.A.) and exist only as a 2020 IFSCC conference poster + marketing/technical leaflets — there is NO peer-reviewed, PubMed-indexed publication of the Altheostem data, and no independent replication. The headline "−3.26 years apparent age" rests on the supplier's own AI age-estimation model scoring its own trial. Treat every number below as supplier-reported, single-source, unverified by independent science. This is one notch weaker in provenance than even OS-01 (which at least has peer-reviewed papers). Read accordingly.

1. Identity

  • Common / trade name: Altheostem™ (Provital S.A., Barcelona; "CareActives™" range). Trade/marketing code "73540." Marketed under Provital's coined category "Dermohacking cosmetics."
  • Source: Cell-culture (callus/suspension) of Althaea rosea — the hollyhock (Malvaceae). Note the botanical naming tangle: Althaea rosea = Alcea rosea = common hollyhock. (Do not confuse with Althaea officinalis, marshmallow root, a different, mucilage-rich species.) Provital frames the name around "Althaea," a Greek mythological figure — pure marketing, not science.
  • INCI (as actually declared on the raw material): Propanediol, Water (Aqua), Glycerin, Althaea Rosea Flower Extract, Xanthan Gum, Pentylene Glycol, Levulinic Acid, Glyceryl Caprylate. [1][4]
  • Identity caveat (important): the active is declared simply as "Althaea Rosea Flower Extract" (CosIng ref 54313; CAS 90045-76-4; EC 289-940-2) [5][6] — the same INCI used for an ordinary hollyhock-flower extract. There is no separate registered INCI such as "Althaea Rosea Flower Cell Culture Extract" or "…Meristem Cell Culture Extract" in CosIng. [5] The master-index label "Althaea Rosea Flower Cell Culture Extract" is therefore descriptive, not a registered INCI — flag this when sourcing/labelling.
  • Active molecules / standardization: standardised in total polyphenols. [1] The "stem cell" is a production method (plant cell culture); what ends up in the bottle is a standardised polyphenol-bearing botanical extract, not living cells (see §10).
  • Form: transparent liquid, water-soluble; preserved with pentylene glycol / levulinic acid / glyceryl caprylate; preservative-free positioning. [1][3]

2. Class & mechanism

Provital positions Altheostem as a botanical senolytic — i.e. an extract claimed to selectively kill existing senescent ("zombie") dermal fibroblasts by inducing apoptosis, then let the rebalanced (younger) fibroblast pool restore the extracellular matrix (ECM). [1][2][7] This is a senolytic claim (kill the cell), which is mechanistically stronger/bolder than the senomorphic claim made for OS-01 (suppress the phenotype) — and therefore carries a heavier burden of proof.

Supplier-stated mechanism (from leaflet + poster) [1][2]: - Selective, dose-dependent apoptosis of senescence-induced human dermal fibroblasts (HDF) vs normal HDF, evidenced by ↓ β-galactosidase-positive cells, ↓ ATP/viability, ↑ phosphatidylserine externalisation, and ↑ Caspase-3/7 activation (apoptosis biomarkers). - "Rebalances the senescent cell population to an earlier age" and "restores the ECM" — the senolysis is claimed to translate downstream into pro-ECM gene expression (see §4). - The leaflet's mechanism diagram correctly lists the canonical senescence triggers (genotoxic/oxidative stress, telomere shortening, DNA injury, mitochondrial dysfunction, replicative exhaustion) and senescent hallmarks (irreversible growth arrest, apoptosis resistance, SASP release). The claim is that Altheostem overcomes senescent cells' characteristic apoptosis resistance. [1]

Honest mechanistic read: the direction is biologically coherent and aligned with the legitimate, fast-moving topical-senolytics field (ABT-263/navitoclax, dasatinib+quercetin, fisetin, procyanidin C1). [12][13] But "a polyphenol-standardised hollyhock extract selectively triggers caspase-mediated apoptosis only in senescent fibroblasts" is an extraordinary, specific claim resting on a single unpublished in-vitro dataset. No molecular target (no Bcl-2/Bcl-xL inhibition data, no specific senolytic compound identified) is disclosed — unlike navitoclax (a defined Bcl-2-family inhibitor). The "how" is a black box.

4. Evidence review by tier ← core of this dossier

Overarching honesty flag: the entire evidence base is Provital-generated and Provital-owned, disseminated as a 2020 IFSCC Congress poster ("Senolysis, a cutting-edge strategy for healthy skin ageing, is activated by Althaea rosea stem cells" — Top-10 of 367 posters) plus technical/marketing leaflets. [1][2] No peer-reviewed journal article was located on PubMed or elsewhere. [7] A conference poster is not peer-reviewed primary literature; it is the weakest tier of "published" evidence. There is zero independent replication.

Tier 1 — In-vitro (the only mechanistic data)

Provital IFSCC 2020 poster / leaflet — senolytic assay on human dermal fibroblasts (HDF). [1][2] - Model: senescence-induced HDF vs normal HDF (control). Induction method not specified in public materials (likely oxidative/replicative). Donor specifics not disclosed. - Dose-dependent senolytic activity: β-galactosidase-positive (senescent) cells reduced up to −42.5% at 1% Altheostem. [1] - Selective viability reduction: senescent-cell viability −44.5% at 1% (p<0.01), described as selective vs normal HDF. [1] (This −44.5% / "44%" is the widely-repeated marketing headline.) - Apoptosis: induction of apoptosis "only observable on formerly apoptosis-resistant senescent HDF," +36% at 0.75% vs control (via Caspase-3/7 imaging). [1] - ECM gene expression (naturally-aged HDF from an old donor, treated at 0.025% Altheostem vs control): [1] - Up (ECM biosynthesis): ADAMTS2 +247%, COL1A2 (collagen I) +203%, FBN2 (fibrillin-2) +260%, HAS3 (hyaluronan synthase 3) +259%. - Down (ECM degradation): MMP7 −? (reduced), MMP9 −? (reduced) — leaflet shows reduction bars (the +24%/+29%/+54% figures on that panel attach to other genes; GM-CSF was reported up +54%, which is a pro-inflammatory SASP-type signal and slightly awkward for a "less inflammaging" story). Read the gene panel cautiously — it is a small, single-donor, single-experiment qPCR snapshot. - Limitations: single source; unpublished; no replication count, no statistics on most genes, no donor n disclosed, induction method opaque, no identified active molecule/target, no positive senolytic comparator (e.g. D+Q, navitoclax). Conflict: 100% supplier-authored, commercial purpose.

Tier 2 — Animal

None. No animal efficacy or toxicology studies for Altheostem located.

Tier 3 — Human, ORAL

Not applicable / none. Altheostem is a topical cosmetic active; no oral form or oral evidence. (Unlike NAD precursors/spermidine, there is no "borrowed-from-oral" data here — but also no oral safety база.) Note: edible/dietary hollyhock has a long history of safe consumption, which is leveraged only for systemic-safety argumentation (§7), not efficacy. [11]

Tier 4 — Human, TOPICAL

Provital in-vivo study (supplier-run). [1][2] - Design: double-blind, placebo-controlled, split-panel. N = 70 healthy Caucasian women, aged 45–65: 35 used a formula with 2% Altheostem, 35 used placebo. Measurements at D28 and D56. Single source; no registration; no independent oversight; investigators = manufacturer. - Instrumental results at D56 (2% Altheostem vs baseline; asterisks = supplier-reported significance): - Biological elasticity (Cutometer) +17.9%*; elastic recovery +16.8%*; "less skin sagging" −18.6%*; skin redensification (ultrasound) +7.8%***; healthy glow/radiance (colorimeter) +8.5%*. - Periocular wrinkle depth −13.8%*** and wrinkle volume −6.7%* (image analysis). (*p<0.05, ***p<0.001.) - "Perceived age" (the marketing centerpiece): - Periocular: "−5.7 years" in evaluated age (method = appearance grading of the eye area). [1] - AI "Visual Apparent Age": −3.26 years (mean) vs placebo over 56 days, derived from Provital's own Convolutional-Neural-Network age-estimation model run on 223,560 images from volunteer videos. Trajectory: D0 0 → D28 −1.28 → D56 −2.42 "years less" vs placebo's near-zero; mean apparent age 55.38 → 52.95 in the active arm. [1][3]

What is actually proven for TOPICAL human use: in one small (n=35/arm), 8-week, supplier-run trial, a 2% Altheostem formula produced modest, instrument-measured improvements in elasticity, firmness/sagging, periocular wrinkles, density, and radiance — i.e. it performs like a competent botanical anti-aging extract. The "reverses ~3–6 years of aging" language is the supplier's own AI model (and subjective eye-area grading) scoring the supplier's own trial — not an independently validated clinical endpoint. Critically, no human endpoint actually measures senescence (no p16/p21/SA-β-gal in skin biopsies); the "senolytic" mechanism is demonstrated only in vitro and the human benefits are conventional anti-aging metrics that many non-senolytic actives also hit. The senolysis→human-benefit causal chain is assumed, not shown.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • The active is a water-soluble, polyphenol-standardised botanical extract (small-molecule polyphenols/anthocyanins/flavonoids), not a peptide or a cell — so penetration is more plausible than for "plant stem cell" marketing implies. What penetrates (if anything functional does) are secondary metabolites, not stem cells (see §10). [10]
  • No published human penetration/PK data for Altheostem exist. The in-vitro senolytic doses (0.025%–1%) were applied directly to cultured fibroblasts — bypassing the stratum corneum entirely. Whether those concentrations reach living dermal fibroblasts after topical application is unaddressed. This is the central unproven link.
  • Hollyhock is also mucilage-rich; mucilage polysaccharides sit on the surface (film-forming, soothing, water-binding) and do not penetrate — a plausible contributor to immediate "smoothing/glow" sensory effects independent of any senolytic action.

6. Formulation

  • Use level: 2% (supplier recommended dose; the clinical study used 2%; in-vitro actives ranged 0.025–1%). [1]
  • pH: not published; as an aqueous polyphenol extract it suits mildly acidic to near-neutral systems. Polyphenols/anthocyanins are pH- and light-sensitive (anthocyanin color shifts with pH) — formulate and package to protect (opaque/airless).
  • Solubility: water-soluble; transparent — easy to add to the water phase of serums, lotions, emulsions, essences. [1]
  • Stability: polyphenols are oxidation-prone; pair with antioxidants (tocopherol) and chelators; avoid prolonged heat/light. The supplier demo formula ("Skin Reviving Antidote," an emulsifier-free emulsion) co-uses tocopherol, hazelnut & grapeseed oils, magnolia bark extract, beta-glucan/pectin, and a Black Baccara rose extract. [1]
  • Pairings (sensible): humectants (glycerin, HA), antioxidants (vitamin C/E, ferulic), niacinamide, peptides, other gentle botanicals — fits a "longevity/well-aging" stack. As a senolytic-positioned active it co-narrates with carnosine (anti-glycation) and barrier lipids.
  • Conflicts: strong oxidizers and very high/low pH may degrade the polyphenols; standard "don't co-formulate with harsh actives in the same phase" caution. No specific incompatibilities published.
  • Sensory: transparent, water-thin, easy; mucilage may add slight slip/film. Fragrance-free-friendly.

7. Safety

  • Independent-of-Provital safety support (a modest plus): a 2025 peer-reviewed assessment (Cosmetics/MDPI; Ewha Womans University, Korea) evaluated Althaea rosea Flower Extract systemic safety via TTC + in-silico dermal absorption + history-of-safe-use: identified 48 constituents (92.6% common food components); cosmetic exposure 0.0045–5.380 mg/kg/day, far below dietary intake; all systemic exposure doses below TTC thresholds → modelled as systemically safe for cosmetic use. [11] (Caveats: this is a desk/in-silico modelling paper, not new lab toxicology; it covers the generic flower-extract INCI, not Altheostem itself; and it is a systemic-toxicology argument only — no irritation/sensitization testing. "Independent" here means not authored by Provital, not that it independently tested this material.)
  • Irritation/sensitization: no Altheostem-specific HRIPT, patch-test, or irritation data located. The 70-subject efficacy study reported no tolerability problems (none detailed). Botanical extracts can sensitize idiosyncratically; a finished-product patch/HRIPT would be needed before tolerability claims.
  • Sun sensitivity: none indicated; not an exfoliant or known photosensitizer. (Anthocyanin polyphenols are generally antioxidant, not phototoxic.)
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: no specific data. Default to conservative "consult your doctor" copy; do not market as pregnancy-safe without data. (Topical botanical, low systemic exposure per [11] — but absence of data ≠ proof of safety.)
  • Sourcing/sustainability (a real strength): plant cell culture avoids wild-harvest/seasonality; Provital claims COSMOS-approved, vegan, 100% Natural Origin (ISO 16128), full traceability, lower water use, preservative-free. [1][3] This is the cleanest sustainability story in the senolytic class (vs animal-derived exosomes/PRP it is explicitly pitched against as a "natural PRP alternative"). [3]

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

The line: under US FDA, intended use decides cosmetic vs drug. Claiming a product alters the structure/function of skin ("kills senescent cells," "removes zombie cells," "boosts collagen," "reverses biological age," "reduces wrinkles" as a physiological claim) pushes it into drug territory — there is no OTC anti-aging/anti-wrinkle monograph. Cosmetic-safe claims are limited to appearance, look/feel, and moisturization. FDA has issued warning letters for such overreach. [14]

  • ✅ Safe cosmetic language: "helps visibly improve firmness and elasticity," "helps the look of skin density," "visibly smooths the appearance of fine lines around the eyes," "supports a healthy-looking, radiant complexion," "inspired by skin-longevity / senescence science," "sustainably produced from plant cell culture."
  • ⚠️ Risky / drug-like — avoid or legal-review:
  • "senolytic" / "kills / clears / removes senescent (zombie) cells" — pharmacological structure/function claim; for Altheostem it is also only shown in a petri dish, never in human skin.
  • "reverses X years of aging / −3.26 years younger / −5.7 years" — structure/function + relies on the supplier's own unvalidated AI model, not a recognized clinical endpoint. High substantiation/legal risk if repeated as our claim.
  • "boosts collagen," "rebuilds the ECM," "reverses biological age" — structure/function.
  • "Dermohacking," "well-aging," "longevity" are marketing terms, fine educationally but must not imply disease treatment.
  • EU/UK: "Althaea Rosea Flower Extract" is a recognized INCI; claims must meet the EU Cosmetics Claims Regulation (655/2013) — evidential support, no misleading "biological age" promises. The "stem cell" descriptor must not mislead (no live cells reach skin — see §10); regulators and watchdogs scrutinize "stem cell" cosmetic claims.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ Conditional / supporting — appealing narrative + clean sustainability, but the evidence is the weakest-provenance senolytic in our file. Do NOT make it a hero on current data; do NOT repeat the supplier's senolytic/AI-age claims as ours.

  • Why it's tempting: it is a vegan, COSMOS, plant-cell-culture, "natural PRP alternative" that slots perfectly into a science-forward skin-longevity / senescence story (../../brand/positioning.md), with a ready-made (if overhyped) marketing kit. Sustainability/traceability are genuinely best-in-class for the class.
  • Why only conditional/supporting: the whole efficacy case is one supplier, one unpublished poster + a 70-person supplier-run, unregistered placebo-controlled trial, headlined by a proprietary AI age model, with no peer review and no independent replication — provenance is below OS-01, fisetin, or PCC1. The senolytic mechanism is petri-dish-only; human benefits are ordinary anti-aging metrics. Single-supplier dependency adds risk.
  • Longevity relevance: high in narrative, low-to-moderate in proven topical effect, and unproven specifically as "senolysis" in human skin. The honest human story is "a sustainable botanical that modestly improves elasticity, firmness and periocular wrinkles at 2%."
  • If used: position as a supporting "well-aging botanical" (not the senolytic hero), claim only the cosmetic/appearance benefits we can defend, never repeat "kills zombie cells / −3.26 years / senolytic" as brand claims, and require a finished-product tolerability test. Prefer pairing with better-evidenced longevity actives (peptides, retinoids, vitamin C) rather than resting a longevity hero on it.
  • Stronger alternatives in-class for a hero: the broader topical-senolytic field (procyanidin C1, fisetin, or — for narrative — OS-01) carries peer-reviewed data; revisit Altheostem for hero status only if Provital (or independents) publish biopsy-level senescence endpoints.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified: 2026-06-19 (independent re-check of every headline claim and citation against source/primary literature).

Overall evidence grade: D (essentially unproven topically as marketed; the senolytic / "−3.26 years" story is hype)

Rationale for D over C: a "C" would require at least weak but real human-topical evidence for the headline mechanism. Here the headline mechanism (senolysis — selectively killing senescent fibroblasts) has zero human evidence (in-vitro only, single unpublished dataset), and the dramatic human-facing numbers (−3.26 years AI apparent age, −5.7 years periocular, "44% fewer senescent cells") come from the supplier's own proprietary AI model and petri-dish assays scoring the supplier's own un-peer-reviewed trial. What is human-topical (a single n=35/arm, 8-week, supplier-run, unregistered split-panel study showing modest, conventional elasticity/firmness/wrinkle gains) would on its own merit a low C — but it is conventional anti-aging instrumentation that does not measure senescence and does not validate the marketed mechanism. The brand-relevant question is "is the senolytic / age-reversal claim proven topically in humans?" — and the answer is no (D). Honest one-liner: a competent, sustainable botanical anti-aging extract sold inside an unproven senolytic costume.

(a) Is the evidence HUMAN and TOPICAL, or borrowed?

  • Senolysis / apoptosis / selective senescent-cell killing (the −42.5% β-gal, −44.5%/"44%" viability, +36% apoptosis, ECM-gene panel): IN-VITRO only — cultured human dermal fibroblasts, actives applied directly to cells (0.025–1%), bypassing the stratum corneum. Not human, not topical-in-use. Single unpublished source. ✅ The dossier already states this plainly.
  • Elasticity / firmness / wrinkle / density / radiance gains: HUMAN + TOPICAL, but a single small supplier-run study (n=35 active / 35 placebo, 8 wk, 2%), unregistered, no independent oversight. Real but weak/low-tier.
  • "−3.26 years" / "−5.7 years": human + topical in collection but the metric is supplier-proprietary (in-house CNN apparent-age model; subjective eye-area grading) — not a validated clinical endpoint. No human endpoint measures senescence (no p16/p21/SA-β-gal in skin biopsy). The senolysis→benefit causal chain is assumed, not shown.
