Ingredient: OS-01 / "Peptide 14" — Senotherapeutic Peptide (INCI: Decapeptide-52)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Peptides (signal/senomorphic) · Slug: senolytics-os01-peptide

Exhaustive literature review. Brutally honest on evidence quality: nearly all efficacy data are generated and funded by OneSkin (the patent holder), with author conflicts of interest, small samples, and active-comparator (not placebo) controls. Treat every "biological age reversal" line as marketing-grade until independently replicated.

1. Identity

  • Common / trade names: OS-01 (OneSkin's trade name); "Peptide 14" / "Pep 14" (the lab/screening name used in the peer-reviewed papers); marketed under umbrella terms "skin longevity" and "senotherapeutic."
  • INCI: Decapeptide-52. This is the publicly registered INCI name for OneSkin's OS-01 peptide and is what appears on the product label. [3][6][7]
  • Structure: A synthetic 10-amino-acid (deca-) peptide built from alanine, glutamic acid, glycine, histidine, isoleucine, leucine, lysine and tryptophan. [7] The exact sequence is not disclosed in the peer-reviewed literature; the composition/use is protected by OneSkin's patent portfolio. [3][7]
  • INCI functional category: "skin conditioning agent." [7]
  • Key forms / derivatives: No ester or salt derivatives in commerce. The same Decapeptide-52 active appears across OneSkin's FACE, EYE, BODY, and HAIR/scalp lines. [3] "Decapeptide-52" raw material is also offered by third-party (mostly Chinese) cosmetic-peptide suppliers — see §9, with a strong identity caveat.

Nomenclature caution — three names, one INCI. OS-01 = Peptide 14 = Decapeptide-52 all refer to the same molecule. (Do not confuse with unrelated peptides that share a numbering convention.) One AI-generated secondary source describes OS-01 as an "AMPK activator"; the primary peer-reviewed paper attributes the mechanism to PP2A, not AMPK (§2). Trust the primary paper.

2. Class & mechanism

OS-01 is positioned as a topical senotherapeutic, the flagship of the "skin-longevity" category. Critically, the company's own peer-reviewed work classifies it as a senomorphic, NOT a senolytic — and says so explicitly:

"Pep 14 does not act as a senolytic molecule, failing to induce apoptosis and autophagy-related mRNA expression." [1]

  • Senolytic = selectively kills existing senescent ("zombie") cells.
  • Senomorphic / senopreventive = suppresses the senescent phenotype and slows cells entering senescence, without killing them.

OS-01 is the latter. Marketing that implies it "clears/kills zombie cells" overstates what the science claims.

Proposed mechanism (from the npj Aging 2023 paper [1]): - Identified by phenotypic screening of a 164-peptide library in progeria (HGPS) fibroblasts; hits that cut senescent (SA-β-gal+) cells >25% were optimized by amino-acid scanning (764 variants → 5 leads → Pep 14). - Acts via protein phosphatase 2A (PP2A) — upregulates the PPP2R1A scaffold subunit, modulating Akt phosphorylation and RBL2/E2F → CDK2 regulation → controlled cell-cycle arrest that permits DNA repair. - Reduces CDK2AP1 mRNA across fibroblast subpopulations, limiting progression to "late" senescence. - Reduces the SASP (senescence-associated secretory phenotype) chiefly by reducing the number of SASP-secreting cells, not by silencing SASP genes per cell. [1]

Net cell-level story (as claimed): fewer cells tip into senescence → less inflammatory SASP signalling → a younger-behaving fibroblast pool → (downstream, hypothesized) better collagen/ECM and barrier support.

4. Evidence review by tier ← core of this dossier

Overarching honesty flag: every efficacy study below is authored by OneSkin staff/founders, who are named patent inventors and company co-founders, and is company-funded (the 2023 npj paper adds Brazilian academic-agency support; the 2025 trial states "no specific funding") — in no case independently funded or independently run. There is, as of June 2026, no fully independent (non-OneSkin) clinical replication of OS-01's topical benefits. Read accordingly.

Tier 1 — In-vitro & ex-vivo (the deepest evidence)

Zonari et al. (2023), npj Aging — "Senotherapeutic peptide treatment reduces biological age and senescence burden in human skin models." [1] (Seminal paper; open access.) - Models: HGPS (progeria) fibroblasts; chronologically aged fibroblasts (donors 30, 71–90 yr); UVB-induced (0.1 J/cm²) and etoposide-induced (20 µM) senescence; 3D skin equivalents; ex-vivo human skin biopsies (donors 35, 55, 79 yr). - Senescence markers ↓ with Pep 14 (12.5 µM, 48 h): SA-β-gal+ cells, p16 (CDKN2A), p21 (CDKN1A), γH2A.x, H2A.J+ cells, abnormal nuclei, ATRX foci; SASP cytokine secretion by ELISA. - Ex-vivo skin mRNA (5-day topical-style exposure): significant ↓ CDKN2A and ↓ CXCL8 (IL-8); trend ↓ IL6; ↓ B2M in dermis. - Headline "biological age" claim: in 79-yr ex-vivo skin, the skin-specific DNA-methylation clock (MolClock) fell ~2.6 years (p<0.01) after 5 days; several other clocks (Horvath, Hannum, PhenoAge, etc.) trended the same way. - Comparator claim: the primary npj paper itself reports Pep 14 gave "superior results compared to retinol" on its in-vitro/ex-vivo markers (e.g. epidermal thickness, senescence markers); earlier company communications echo this. [1][5] Note this is a company in-vitro/ex-vivo marker comparison, not a human wrinkle/photoaging head-to-head. - Replication: bulk RNA-seq 3–4 experiments in triplicate; scRNA-seq 8,616 cells; ex-vivo methylation = one experiment, 4 biological replicates; histology 3 pieces/donor. - Limitations (authors' own): "limited to in vitro human skin cells and equivalents"; calls for human trials. Conflicts: authors are OneSkin founders + named patent inventors; funded by OneSkin plus Brazilian academic agencies (FAPESP, CAPES, FAPDF, FUNDECT, CNPq) — i.e. the agency support is academic infrastructure funding, not independent industry validation. Peptide made by CPC Scientific (>95% purity). - Author Correction (2024), npj Aging [8]: an unintended duplicated electron-microscopy image (no-treatment 79-yr ex-vivo skin) in Fig. 6a was corrected. Authors state it did not affect conclusions — but an image-duplication correction in the marquee paper is worth flagging for credibility hygiene.

Bottom line, Tier 1: mechanistically rich and genuinely interesting; the "−2.6 biological years" figure is a single ex-vivo experiment on lab-treated tissue, not a clinical outcome in living people. It is the source of essentially all "reverses your skin's age" marketing.

Tier 2 — Animal

None identified. No published rodent/in-vivo animal efficacy or tox studies for OS-01. (Not unusual for a cosmetic peptide, but means the in-vitro→human gap is unbridged by animal work.)

Tier 3 — Human, ORAL

Not applicable. OS-01 is a topical-only cosmetic peptide; there is no oral/ingestible format or oral evidence. (Useful contrast vs. NAD precursors/spermidine, where benefits are often "borrowed" from oral studies — here that borrowing problem doesn't arise, but neither is there an oral safety/efficacy база.)

Tier 4 — Human, TOPICAL

(a) RCT-grade — Zonari et al. (2024), J Cosmetic Dermatology — split-face vehicle-controlled. [2] (Wiley; abstract/secondary sources used — full text paywalled.) - Design: ~12-week, double-blind, split-face, vehicle-controlled (OS-01 formula vs identical peptide-free vehicle on the other side). This is the most rigorous design in the file — vehicle control isolates the peptide. - N: 22 women, ~47–65 yr. - Results (per abstract/secondaries): OS-01 side showed significantly lower TEWL (one barrier measure) and superior skin texture vs both baseline and vehicle, by instrumental measurement + expert grading. Other endpoints (radiance, firmness, wrinkles) reported as improved but with weaker/mixed support; much rides on which were instrument-confirmed vs self-report. - Conflicts: OneSkin authors/inventors. Limitations: very small N; single site; company-run.

(b) Lower-tier RCT — Zonari et al. (2025), J Cosmetic Dermatology — "Pilot 12-Week Clinical Trial." [4] (Open access via PMC; this dossier read the full PMC text.) - Design: randomized, double-blind, 12-week; active comparator, NOT placebo — control = Atopalm MLE Lotion (a commercial barrier-supporting moisturizer). Applied to forearm/upper arm (a body study, despite "skin" framing). - N: 60 enrolled, 52 completed (27 OS-01, 25 control); ages 62–82 (OS-01) and 61–85 (control). - Barrier (TEWL): OS-01 forearm −41.5% (p=0.001); upper arm −34.7% (p=0.027). But the active moisturizer control did equally well on forearm (−40.0%, p<0.001) — i.e., OS-01 was not shown superior to a good moisturizer on the primary barrier site. - Hydration (corneometer): OS-01 forearm +38.3% (p<0.001) and upper arm +38.4% (p=0.002); control forearm only +15.3% — here OS-01 looked better. - Cytokines (systemic, blood): OS-01 group ↓ IL-8 (p=0.028) and ↓ IL-10 (p=0.026); control group increased TNFα (p=0.002) and IFN-γ. (Systemic cytokine shifts from a forearm cream are biologically surprising and warrant skepticism.) - "Biological age" (GlycanAge, a blood glycan clock): OS-01 −0.07 yr vs control +0.72 yr over 12 wks. Tiny absolute effect; exploratory. - Perception: 70% (OS-01) vs 42% (control) reported improved general appearance — subjective. - Key control finding: mass-spec found NO OS-01 peptide in plasma at baseline or after treatment — consistent with the peptide staying local (good for a systemic-safety story; awkward for the "reduces systemic inflammation" headline). - Limitations (authors' own): lifestyle/diet not standardized; small; calls for larger, broader, longer studies. Conflicts: OneSkin founders/inventors.

What is actually proven for TOPICAL human use: modest barrier (↓TEWL) and hydration improvement, plus better skin texture vs vehicle in a 22-person face study — i.e., it behaves like a good moisturizing peptide. Reductions in senescence markers, p16, and "biological age" are demonstrated in lab tissue/cells, not in living human skin in a controlled topical trial. Any "clears senescent cells in your skin" or "reverses your skin's age" claim is borrowed from in-vitro/ex-vivo data, not earned in human topical use.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • As a 10-mer (~1,100–1,300 Da depending on exact sequence), OS-01 sits above the oft-cited ~500 Da "rule-of-500" for easy stratum-corneum passage; meaningful delivery of intact peptide into living epidermis/dermis is the central open question for all "longevity peptides."
  • OneSkin's case rests on ex-vivo skin showing marker changes after surface application, implying some functional penetration in that model. [1] No published human penetration/PK profile.
  • The 2025 trial's absence of peptide in plasma [4] suggests it does not pass into systemic circulation — neutral-to-good for safety, but says nothing about how much reaches living dermal fibroblasts (its site of action).
  • Formulation/delivery is doing real work; penetration enhancers, vehicle, and intact-peptide stability all matter and are not independently characterized for off-the-shelf Decapeptide-52.

6. Formulation

  • Use level: OneSkin does not publish the %. On the FACE INCI, Decapeptide-52 sits ~13th (after emollients/humectants, before the HA complex) — consistent with a low, typical active-peptide level (commonly ~0.1–1% for branded peptides; unverified for OS-01). [6]
  • pH: near-neutral to mildly acidic, like most signal peptides; avoid extremes.
  • Stability: peptides are vulnerable to hydrolysis/oxidation, heat, and microbial growth; needs robust preservation (OneSkin uses phenoxyethanol/sorbate/caprylyl glycol system) and cool, low-oxidation conditions.
  • Pairings (well-matched): humectants (glycerin, HA — co-formulated in OS-01 FACE), barrier lipids (ceramides, squalane), niacinamide (also in OS-01 FACE), allantoin, antioxidants (tocopherol). [6]
  • Conflicts: avoid co-formulating with high concentrations of strong acids (high % AHA/BHA, L-ascorbic acid) or harsh oxidants in the same anhydrous-vs-aqueous environment that could cleave/denature the peptide; standard "layer at different steps" guidance applies.
  • Sensory: the OS-01 FACE base is a fragrance-free emulsion (botanical oils + HA) marketed as suitable for sensitive skin — pleasant, lightweight cream feel. [6]

7. Safety

  • Irritation/sensitization: reported as well tolerated; low-% peptide, fragrance-free positioning; no notable irritation signal in the small trials [2][4]. No published HRIPT/sensitization data located.
  • Systemic exposure: plasma mass-spec found no detectable peptide after 12 weeks of topical use [4] — supports a low-systemic-risk profile.
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: no specific contraindication identified, but no safety data in pregnancy — default to conservative "consult your doctor" copy; do not market as pregnancy-safe without data.
  • Sun sensitivity: none identified (not a photosensitizer; not an exfoliant).
  • Sourcing/sustainability: fully synthetic peptide → no animal/biological-provenance issues; cleaner provenance story than exosomes/growth factors. Synthesis (e.g., CPC Scientific for the research peptide) is standard SPPS.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

The line: under US FDA, intended use decides cosmetic vs drug. Claiming a product alters the structure/function of skin (e.g., "increases collagen," "removes wrinkles," "clears senescent cells," "reverses biological age") makes it a drug requiring NDA/approval — there is no OTC monograph for anti-aging/anti-wrinkle. Cosmetic-safe claims are limited to appearance and moisturization. FDA has issued warning letters for unapproved drug claims on cosmetics. [9]

  • ✅ Safe cosmetic language: "supports skin's natural renewal," "helps visibly improve firmness, hydration and barrier resilience," "helps reduce trans-epidermal water loss," "improves the look and feel of skin texture," "inspired by skin-longevity science."
  • ⚠️ Risky / drug-like — avoid or legal-review:
  • "senolytic" / "kills / clears senescent (zombie) cells" — pharmacological + the science says it's senomorphic anyway → doubly wrong.
  • "reverses your skin's biological age by X years" — structure/function + relies on an ex-vivo lab clock, not a validated clinical endpoint.
  • "boosts collagen," "repairs DNA," "reduces systemic inflammation" — structure/function and/or systemic (drug-adjacent) claims.
  • "Senotherapeutic" is a science term, not yet a regulated cosmetic claim — usable educationally but pair with cosmetic-appropriate benefit language; don't let it imply disease treatment.
  • EU/UK: Decapeptide-52 is a recognized INCI; claims must meet the EU Cosmetics Claims Regulation (evidential support, no misleading "biological age" promises). [7]

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Finished-product benchmark: OneSkin OS-01 FACE retails roughly $90–$120 (15–50 mL); BODY ~$120+ — premium/luxury tier. [3]
  • Raw-material reality (important correction to prior dossier): OneSkin's OS-01 is the same INCI, Decapeptide-52, and "Decapeptide-52" raw material is offered by third-party cosmetic-peptide suppliers (e.g., Bolise, Chengdu Youngshe). [10][7] So the INCI is buyable — this is not a wholly locked-up molecule the way the earlier dossier implied.
  • BUT three hard caveats before treating it as sourceable: 1. Sequence/identity unverified. OneSkin's specific patented peptide/use is proprietary; whether a generic "Decapeptide-52" from a Chinese supplier is the same sequence and purity as OneSkin's clinically-tested material is [unverified]. INCI names a function class, not a guaranteed identical molecule/sequence. 2. IP exposure. OneSkin holds patents on the composition/use; building a competing senotherapeutic-peptide hero on Decapeptide-52 needs freedom-to-operate / IP review. 3. Substantiation gap. All efficacy data attach to OneSkin's peptide+formula. A generic Decapeptide-52 inherits none of that evidence; we'd own our own (modest) substantiation burden.
  • Cost tier for the raw material: specialty-peptide pricing (high per-gram, MOQ-bound), but far cheaper than the finished competitor product.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ Conditional — best strategic fit for a "signature longevity hero," but pursue only with sourced/IP-cleared peptide + rigorously honest claims.

  • Why it's tempting: OS-01 is the category-defining "skin longevity" active; it maps perfectly onto a repair/renewal thesis and gives the most credible science-forward narrative of any active in this set (../../brand/positioning.md).
  • Why it's only conditional: the independent human evidence is thin and the strongest "wow" data are in-vitro/ex-vivo and entirely company-generated (with COIs and a corrected figure). The honest human-topical story is "good moisturizing peptide that supports barrier/hydration/texture" — not "reverses aging."
  • Longevity relevance: High in narrative, modest in proven topical effect. It is the cleanest hook for a longevity story, but our differentiation (claims honesty) means we must sell the science-inspired angle, not the overreach.
  • Gating conditions before hero status: (1) confirm a sequence-verified, FTO-cleared Decapeptide-52 (or licensed/alternative senomorphic peptide); (2) build our own modest substantiation; (3) lock a claims strategy that uses "supports/visibly" cosmetic language and never "senolytic/reverses biological age."
  • Alternative: co-headline with GHK-Cu (copper-peptides-ghk-cu.md) — openly sourced, decades of (small) human data — to get a defensible "longevity peptide" hero without resting solely on a competitor's proprietary, lightly-replicated molecule.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19 by independent re-reading of the primary sources (npj Aging 2023 full text [1]; the 2024 Author Correction [8]; the 2025 JOCD pilot full text [4]; PubMed/DOI confirmation of the 2024 split-face study [2]). Goal: catch any claim quietly borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral data, any fabricated or misrepresented citation, and any small/conflicted study sold as proof.

Overall evidence grade: C (weak / indirect for the headline "longevity" claims; borderline B only for the narrow "moisturizing-peptide" benefits)

  • B-grade applies only to the modest, mundane endpoints: ↓TEWL and ↑hydration and better texture in two small, company-run human topical trials [2][4]. Even here the strongest design (2024 split-face vehicle-controlled, N=22) is tiny and single-site, and in the 2025 trial OS-01 did not beat a good moisturizer on the primary barrier site.
  • C/D-grade applies to every claim that makes OS-01 interesting — "reduces biological age," "reduces senescence burden," "senotherapeutic," "outperforms retinol." Those rest on in-vitro and ex-vivo lab tissue only, not on living human skin in a controlled topical trial. The marquee "−2.6 biological years" number is a single ex-vivo experiment, 4 replicates on excised cadaver-style skin treated in a dish — not a clinical outcome.
  • The grade is held down further by total absence of independent replication: every efficacy study traces to OneSkin founders/patent inventors, and an independent search (June 2026) found no non-OneSkin clinical confirmation.

What I verified as TRUE and accurately represented

The dossier is unusually honest; its core factual claims hold up to source-checking: - Mechanism = PP2A, NOT AMPK, and senomorphic, NOT senolytic — both confirmed verbatim in the npj paper [1] ("does not act as a senolytic molecule"; "functions via modulation of PP2A"). The dossier correctly debunks the "AMPK activator" secondary-source error. - −2.6-year MolClock result, at 12.5 µM, 5 days, one experiment / 4 biological replicates — confirmed exactly in [1]. Correctly framed as ex-vivo, not clinical. - 2024 split-face study [2] is a real paper (JOCD 2024; PMID 38400612; Vol 23(6):2135-2144; N=22, double-blind vehicle-controlled). Citation and characterization accurate. - 2025 pilot [4] numbers all confirmed against the PMC full text: 60 enrolled / 52 completed (27 OS-01, 25 control); ages 62–82 / 61–85; Atopalm MLE active (not placebo) control; body/forearm site; TEWL −41.5% (p=0.001) vs control −40.0% (p<0.001), between-group p=0.924 (i.e. not superior to moisturizer); hydration +38.3% vs +15.3%; ↓IL-8 (p=0.028), ↓IL-10 (p=0.026); control ↑TNFα (p=0.002); no peptide detected in plasma; GlycanAge −0.07 vs +0.72 yr. - Author Correction [8] = duplicated EM image in Fig. 6a (untreated 79-yr ex-vivo skin), conclusions stated unchanged — confirmed. Worth flagging for hygiene as the dossier does. - No independent replication / no animal data / no oral data — confirmed; nothing was borrowed from oral or animal studies (there are none to borrow). The "borrowing" risk here is in-vitro/ex-vivo → human, and the dossier already names it.

Main caveats (the load-bearing ones)

  1. The whole "longevity" thesis is lab-tissue evidence. Senescence reduction, p16/p21 drop, and "biological age reversal" are demonstrated in cultured cells and excised ex-vivo skin — never in a controlled trial in living people. Treat as mechanism, not proven topical outcome.
  2. Zero independent evidence. 100% of efficacy authorship = OneSkin founders + patent inventors; all studies company-funded; the flagship paper carries an image-duplication correction. This is the single biggest credibility discount.
  3. Tiny, often non-superior trials. N=22 and N=52. The better-controlled body trial shows OS-01 ≈ a good moisturizer on the primary barrier endpoint. "Clinically proven anti-aging" is not earned.
  4. The "systemic inflammation" headline is internally contradictory. Blood IL-8/IL-10 fell, yet mass-spec found no peptide in plasma — a forearm cream changing systemic cytokines is biologically implausible and should be treated as a chance/exploratory finding, not a benefit to advertise.
  5. Sourceability ≠ equivalence. Generic "Decapeptide-52" shares an INCI but not verified sequence/purity or any of OneSkin's (already thin) evidence; IP/FTO and our own substantiation burden remain.

Claims that should be softened (and were, in this pass)

  • "outperformed retinol" was attributed only to a secondary blog [5]; corrected to note it is in the primary paper [1] but is a company in-vitro/ex-vivo marker comparison, not a human head-to-head — neither inflate nor under-cite it.
  • §4 funding flag and the Tier-1 conflicts line were made precise: the 2023 paper had OneSkin + Brazilian academic-agency funding (not "no external funding"); none of it is independent.
  • For brand copy, the following should never be used as stated and remain on the "avoid" list: "senolytic," "clears/kills senescent cells," "reverses your skin's biological age by X years," "reduces systemic inflammation," "boosts collagen," "repairs DNA," "clinically proven anti-aging." Defensible ceiling = "science-inspired skin-longevity peptide that supports barrier, hydration and the look of texture."

Bottom line

The dossier does not overstate the evidence — if anything it is appropriately brutal. The only fixes needed were citation precision (retinol comparison source) and funding nuance, both applied. The honest verdict is unchanged: C-grade for the longevity story, behaves like a good moisturizing peptide in humans, and any "reverses aging" language is unearned and regulatorily risky.
