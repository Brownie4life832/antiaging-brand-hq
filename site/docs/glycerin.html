Ingredient: Glycerin (INCI: Glycerin)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ shortlisted (workhorse base humectant) · ⬜ considering · ⬜ rejected · ⬜ in a product Class: Humectants & hydration actives · Slug: glycerin

One-line take: Glycerin is the best-evidenced, cheapest, safest humectant in cosmetic science — a near-mandatory base ingredient for a barrier-repair cream — but it is a hydration/barrier supporting actor, not an anti-aging "hero." It does not meaningfully build collagen, fight glycation, clear senescent cells, or reverse photoaging. Treat it as foundational plumbing, not as a marketing centerpiece.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Glycerin (US/cosmetic), Glycerol (chemistry/pharma), Glycerine (UK spelling).
  • INCI: Glycerin. (Note: USP/pharma monograph name is "Glycerin"; chemical/biological literature uses "glycerol" — they are the same molecule, propane-1,2,3-triol.)
  • CAS: 56-81-5. Molecular formula: C₃H₈O₃. MW: 92.09 g/mol.
  • Structure: A small, three-carbon polyol (sugar alcohol) with three hydroxyl (–OH) groups — the source of its strong water-binding and its hygroscopicity.
  • Forms / grades: USP/EP (pharmaceutical-grade, ≥99.5%), cosmetic-grade, food-grade (E422), and "vegetable glycerin" (plant-derived, vs. synthetic or tallow-derived). Available as anhydrous (≥99.5%) or as aqueous solutions (e.g., 96%, 86.5%).
  • Endogenous form: Glycerol is also a native skin metabolite — released in the epidermis from sebaceous triglyceride lipolysis and phospholipid breakdown, and transported into keratinocytes via aquaporin-3 (see §2). So topical glycerin tops up a molecule the skin already uses.
  • Related / derivative actives (distinct ingredients — do not conflate):
  • Glyceryl glucoside (gluco-glycerol) — glycerol + glucose; an aquaporin-3 inducer (Eucerin "Aquaporin Active" technology). Different mechanism, separate evidence (see §4).
  • Glyceryl monolaurate / glyceryl monocaprylate — fatty-acid monoesters of glycerol; antimicrobial/emollient, NOT humectants.
  • Polyglyceryl-X esters — emulsifiers derived from polyglycerol; not relevant to humectancy.
  • Glyceryl stearate — an emollient/emulsifier, not glycerin.

2. Class & mechanism

Glycerin is the archetypal humectant — it binds and holds water. Its activity in skin is multi-mechanistic and unusually well characterized:

  1. Water-binding / NMF-mimetic. Glycerol's three hydroxyls hydrogen-bond water, drawing moisture from the dermis and the environment into the stratum corneum (SC) and creating a humectant reservoir. It behaves like a component of the skin's own Natural Moisturizing Factor (NMF) and substitutes for NMF washed out by surfactants (Bettinger et al. 2010 [#9]).
  2. Aquaporin-3 (AQP3) transport. AQP3 is an aquaglyceroporin — a transmembrane channel that moves water, glycerol, urea, and H₂O₂ across keratinocyte membranes. Endogenous glycerol delivery into the epidermis depends on AQP3. AQP3-null mice have ~3-fold lower SC glycerol, ~50% lower SC water, reduced elasticity, and impaired barrier recovery — and glycerol replacement corrects all of these defects, proving glycerol (not water per se) is the operative molecule (Hara & Verkman, JBC 2002 [#3]; PNAS 2003 [#4]).
  3. SC lipid-phase modulation. Glycerol keeps intercellular SC lipids in a more fluid liquid-crystalline state (inhibits crystalline phase transition), which supports a flexible, functional barrier (Fluhr et al. 2008 review [#1]).
  4. Plasticizer / mechanical softening. Glycerol acts as an intracellular plasticizer of corneocytes, keeping them turgid and pliable; dry, stiff SC becomes softer and more elastic (Fluhr 2008 [#1]; in-vivo plasticizing data, Pedersen et al. [#11]).
  5. Pro-desquamation. Glycerol accelerates corneodesmosome (desmoglein-1) degradation and reduces inter-corneocyte cohesion, promoting orderly shedding of dead cells and reducing the scaly, "dry-skin" look — a humidity-dependent enzymatic effect (Rawlings et al., desmosome degradation [#6]; Harding 2000 corneodesmolysis review [#7]).
  6. Anti-irritant / barrier-recovery support. Topical glycerol speeds recovery of barrier-damaged skin and reduces irritation in experimental models (Fluhr "Glycerol accelerates recovery of barrier function in vivo" [#8]; Bettinger 2010 [#9]). Caveat: this is partly rehydration/NMF replacement rather than true lipid-barrier reconstruction (see §4 honesty note).

3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

Bottom line: Glycerin maps cleanly onto one aging axis that matters — barrier/hydration decline (aged skin has lower NMF, lower glycerol, higher TEWL, stiffer SC) — and supports a second (mechanical pliability). It does not touch the dermal-remodeling hallmarks (collagen, photoaging, glycation, senescence) that "anti-aging" marketing usually implies.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

Grading convention: In-vitro → animal → human ORAL → human TOPICAL (RCT > controlled > uncontrolled). For a topical cosmetic, human topical evidence is what counts; everything above it is supporting/mechanistic only.

Tier 1 — In-vitro / ex-vivo (mechanism, not proof of benefit)

  • Rawlings et al. — glycerol & corneodesmosome degradation (ex-vivo human SC). Glycerol-treated SC showed desmosomes in more advanced degradation and significantly lower immunoreactive desmoglein-1 vs. control; glycerol increased corneocyte loss in a desquamation assay and dramatically reduced inter-corneocyte mechanical forces (extensiometry). Effect was humidity-dependent. Establishes the pro-desquamation/softening mechanism. [#6]
  • SC lipid biophysics. Glycerol inhibits the crystalline→ordered lipid phase transition and helps maintain liquid-crystalline lipid lamellae in model/ex-vivo SC (Fluhr review summarizing biophysical work [#1]; barrier-lipid hydration studies [#2]).
  • Glyceryl glucoside (a derivative, not glycerin) — AQP3 induction. In cultured human keratinocytes, glyceryl glucoside significantly increased AQP3 mRNA and protein (Dumas/Boury-Jamot et al., Skin Pharmacol Physiol 2012). Relevant to the AQP3 story but is a different ingredient. [#12]

Limitations: in-vitro/ex-vivo work proves how glycerol could help; it cannot establish clinical benefit, dose, or durability.

Tier 2 — Animal (causal mechanism in vivo)

  • Hara & Verkman, J Biol Chem 2002 [#3] and Hara-Chikuma & Verkman, PNAS 2003 [#4]. AQP3-knockout mice have selectively reduced SC glycerol (~3-fold), reduced SC hydration (~50%), reduced skin elasticity (~50% by suction/cutometry), and impaired barrier recovery after tape-stripping. Orally or topically administered glycerol corrected each defect, while water alone did not — isolating glycerol as the causal molecule for SC hydration, elasticity, and barrier recovery. This is the strongest mechanistic causal evidence that glycerol drives these properties. SC structure, protein/lipid composition, and ion content were unchanged, showing the effect is glycerol-specific.

Limitations: mouse skin differs from human (thinner SC, different barrier kinetics); KO models are extreme (glycerol-depleted) and may overstate effect size in normal human skin.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL

  • No meaningful oral-glycerin skincare literature. Glycerol is used orally/IV for very different purposes (osmotic diuretic for intracranial/intraocular pressure; "glycerol loading" for athletic hyperhydration). There is no credible evidence that oral glycerin improves skin in humans, and none should be borrowed for topical claims. (The AQP3 oral-replacement data are mouse, Tier 2 — do not present as human.)

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the decisive tier)

A) Hydration — STRONG, consistently positive.

  • Lodén & Wessman, Int J Cosmet Sci 2001 [#5]. Bilateral, double-blind, n=17 healthy volunteers; 20% glycerin cream vs. its identical vehicle, 10 days. Glycerin significantly increased corneometer hydration vs. vehicle. BUT the study could not demonstrate any effect on TEWL or on reducing SLS irritant potential. Honest read: glycerin reliably hydrates; the "barrier/anti-irritant" claims were not confirmed in this controlled design.
  • Breternitz / Fluhr — "Glycerol accelerates recovery of barrier function in vivo" [#8] and follow-on work: glycerol-treated sites showed faster TEWL normalization after tape-strip and repeated-SLS barrier damage (3- and 7-day follow-up). Supports barrier-recovery, but note the conflict with Lodén on TEWL.
  • Bettinger et al. (Atrux-Tallau et al.), Arch Dermatol Res 2010 [#9]. Acute SLS significantly raised TEWL (the exact 5.9→21.4 g·m⁻²·h⁻¹ figures are [unverified] against the primary text — confirm before quoting); low-dose glycerol aided recovery via a saturable, hygroscopic mechanism — but authors concluded glycerol largely substitutes for washed-out NMF and rehydrates rather than truly restoring barrier (TEWL stayed elevated; verified verbatim against the abstract). A model of honest mechanism: hydration ≠ barrier repair.

B) Xerosis / clinical dry-skin — STRONG (in formulated emollients).

  • Szepietowski et al., Dermatol Ther 2024 (V0034CR, glycerol 15% + paraffin 10%) [#10]. Randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled Phase 3, n=235 (uremic xerosis), once-daily, 14 European sites. Treatment response 60.2% vs 41.0% vehicle (p=0.0041); squame surface area significantly reduced (5997 vs 8783 mm², p=0.001). 12 treatment-related AEs (5.1%), all local/mild–moderate. High-quality RCT — but the active is glycerol + paraffin (an occlusive), so benefit is attributable to the combination, not glycerin alone. Funding caveat: sponsored by Pierre Fabre (the formulation's manufacturer) — industry-funded, so interpret the effect size with the usual sponsor-bias discount; the design (randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled) is nonetheless rigorous.
  • "High-glycerin vs 16 other moisturizers," 5-year severe-xerosis dataset (~394 patients, referenced second-hand in reviews [#1]). High-glycerin products were reported to restore hydration faster and with longer-lasting effect than comparators including petrolatum preparations. Consistent with glycerin being a strong humectant, but this is an observational, uncontrolled dataset cited only second-hand (no primary RCT located, and the source is manufacturer-associated) — treat as suggestive, not as proof glycerin is "the best."
  • Comparative caveat — ISDIN Ureadin trial [#13]. Randomized, assessor-blinded, n=40 (diabetic foot xerosis): a urea/arginine/carnosine cream beat a glycerol-based emollient (89% vs 47% DASI reduction; final DASI 0.2 vs 1.0, p=0.048). Glycerin is excellent but not always the single best humectant for severe xerosis — urea can outperform it in some settings. Conflict note: study drugs were supplied by ISDIN (maker of the winning urea cream), so the head-to-head is not a neutral comparison.

C) Elasticity / mechanical — MODERATE.

  • In-vivo plasticizing studies show water + glycerin soften human SC and improve pliability (Pedersen et al., plasticizing effect on human skin in vivo [#11]); consistent with the AQP3 animal elasticity data. Real but modest, and largely an SC-surface (not dermal) effect — distinct from "firming."

D) Collagen / wrinkles / photoaging / pigment — NO direct topical evidence. No credible human topical RCTs show glycerin reduces wrinkles, builds collagen, fixes photoaging, or evens pigment. Any such claim would be unsubstantiated for glycerin specifically.

What is PROVEN for human topical use vs. borrowed: - ✅ Proven (human topical): increases SC hydration; improves clinical xerosis (in formulated emollients); softens/plasticizes SC; helps barrier recovery after damage (with one notable negative on TEWL). - ⚠️ Borrowed / mechanistic only: elasticity-via-AQP3 and "corrects barrier defects" rest heavily on mouse (Hara/Verkman) and ex-vivo data — credible but not human-proven at those effect sizes. - ❌ Not supported for glycerin: collagen synthesis, antioxidant/photoprotection, anti-glycation, senescence, microbiome remodeling, wrinkle reduction.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Glycerol is small (92 Da) and hydrophilic; it partitions readily into the hydrated SC and forms a humectant reservoir there. Most cosmetic action is at the SC level, where it is needed.
  • Endogenous transport deeper into living epidermis is AQP3-mediated (aquaglyceroporin); topical glycerol can reach viable epidermis but the dominant, evidenced benefit is SC hydration/plasticization, not dermal delivery.
  • It does not require penetration enhancers to work as a humectant; its benefit is largely surface-and-SC, which is exactly where hydration is measured.

6. Formulation

  • Effective / typical concentration: Hydration benefit is dose-related and seen broadly from ~3% to 20%+. Common leave-on use 3–10%; clinical xerosis emollients use 15–20% (often with an occlusive). CIR reports use up to 78.5% in leave-on products (formulation/processing, not all "active"). For a face cream, ~4–10% balances efficacy with sensory.
  • pH: Stable across the full cosmetic range (~3–10); does not require or shift pH. Compatible with low-pH actives.
  • Stability: Outstanding. Chemically inert, non-volatile, oxidation-resistant, long shelf life. Also functions as a freeze-thaw stabilizer and co-solvent/preservation booster (lowers water activity at high % — a formulation bonus, not a standalone preservative).
  • Solubility: Fully water-miscible; miscible with alcohols; poorly soluble in oils.
  • Pairings (synergistic):
  • Occlusives (petrolatum, paraffin, squalane, dimethicone, plant butters) — the classic, evidence-backed combo; the occlusive seals in the water glycerin attracts (directly relevant to a barrier-repair cream). The Phase 3 xerosis RCT [#10] is glycerol + paraffin.
  • Other humectants / NMF — hyaluronic acid, urea, panthenol, betaine, amino acids, PCA (multi-humectant systems hydrate more robustly than any single one).
  • Barrier lipids — ceramides, cholesterol, fatty acids (humectant + lipid replenishment = the strongest barrier-repair logic).
  • Plays well with niacinamide, retinoids, vitamin C, exfoliating acids — often used to buffer their irritation by maintaining hydration.
  • Conflicts: Few. At high % in low-humidity, occlusive-free formulas it can feel tacky/sticky and (theoretically) draw water from deeper layers to the surface where it evaporates — mitigated by capping % and pairing with an occlusive (see §10). No meaningful chemical incompatibilities.
  • Sensory: Slight sweetness if tasted; smooth, slightly draggy/tacky skin-feel at high %; can be "softened" with esters/silicones.

7. Safety

  • Irritation: Among the gentlest cosmetic ingredients; widely used in products for sensitive/compromised skin and even to reduce surfactant irritation. In the Phase 3 xerosis trial, treatment-related AEs were 5.1%, all local/mild–moderate (and partly attributable to the formula, not glycerin) [#10].
  • Sensitization / allergy: Genuine allergic contact dermatitis to glycerin is rare. CIR classified it safe at current use and concentration [#14, #15].
  • Sun sensitivity: None. Not photosensitizing; no sun-avoidance needed.
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: No known concern at cosmetic topical use; non-mutagenic, non-reproductive-toxicant in available data. (Standard "not medical advice" caveat per CLAUDE.md.)
  • Comedogenicity: Very low / non-comedogenic at typical use.
  • Eye/mucous membrane: Well tolerated; used in eye/oral care.
  • Sourcing / sustainability: Three origins — (1) plant-derived "vegetable glycerin" (saponification/transesterification of vegetable oils; often a biodiesel co-product), (2) synthetic (from propylene), (3) tallow-derived (animal — relevant for vegan positioning). For a premium brand, specify plant-derived, RSPO-aware glycerin (palm-derived glycerin carries deforestation scrutiny; non-palm or certified-sustainable options exist).

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Status: Cosmetic ingredient in all major markets (US FDA cosmetic, EU CosIng, etc.). Also a recognized OTC skin-protectant drug active in the US (it appears in the FDA skin-protectant monograph) — but using it as a cosmetic humectant keeps us on the cosmetic side. CIR: safe as used [#14].
  • ✅ Defensible cosmetic claims (evidence-backed):
  • "Hydrates / deeply hydrates," "helps the skin hold onto moisture," "draws moisture into the skin's surface."
  • "Helps soften and smooth," "improves the look/feel of dry, rough skin."
  • "Supports the skin's moisture barrier," "helps replenish moisture lost to cleansing/environment."
  • "Helps soothe / comfort dry, tight skin" (supportable from anti-irritant + xerosis data).
  • ⚠️ Risky / would need substantiation or are not supportable for glycerin:
  • "Boosts collagen," "anti-aging," "reduces wrinkles," "firms," "lifts" — unsupported for glycerin; drug-adjacent and would mislead. Avoid attributing these to glycerin.
  • "Repairs the barrier" — soften to "supports/helps restore"; the human TEWL data are mixed (Lodén negative [#5]), so "repairs" overreaches.
  • "Reduces TEWL / locks in moisture" — the occlusive in the formula does this; don't credit glycerin alone (Lodён found no TEWL effect [#5]).
  • "Anti-bacterial / protects the microbiome" — that's glyceryl monolaurate (a derivative), not glycerin.
  • Net: Glycerin is a claims-safe workhorse — easy to substantiate "hydration/softness/comfort," but it should never carry the brand's anti-aging headline.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: Very low (commodity). Bulk vegetable glycerin is one of the cheapest functional cosmetic ingredients (single-digit USD/kg range at scale; pennies per unit of finished product). Cost is effectively a non-issue.
  • Suppliers: Ubiquitous — every cosmetic raw-material distributor (Croda, BASF, Cargill, Emery Oleochemicals, IOI, plus countless regional suppliers). Easy to source USP/EP, plant-derived, RSPO/non-palm grades.
  • Supply considerations: Abundant (biodiesel co-product → large, stable supply). Main strategic choice is provenance (plant vs. palm vs. tallow) for vegan/sustainability storytelling, not availability or price.

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • "The most powerful hydrator" / "better than hyaluronic acid." Glycerin is genuinely an outstanding general humectant for SC hydration, and may be the best (the 5-year/394-patient comparison favored high-glycerin formulas [#1] — but that is observational, uncontrolled, second-hand, and manufacturer-associated, so it is suggestive, not proof) — and even if so, "best humectant" ≠ "anti-aging hero," and head-to-head it can be beaten by urea in severe xerosis [#13]. Don't over-rank it.
  • "Repairs / rebuilds the barrier." Overstated for glycerin alone. It supports recovery and replaces NMF, but Lodén found no TEWL benefit [#5] and Bettinger explicitly framed it as rehydration, not barrier reconstruction [#9]. The occlusive and lipids in a formula do the heavy "barrier repair" lifting.
  • "Boosts collagen / anti-aging / smooths wrinkles." No evidence for glycerin. Pure marketing overreach — and a claims-compliance risk.
  • "Glycerin dehydrates skin in dry climates / pulls water out of your skin." A partial truth weaponized into a myth. At high % in an occlusive-free formula in very low humidity, a humectant can theoretically move water surfaceward where it evaporates — which is exactly why glycerin is formulated with occlusives and capped at sensible %. In real, properly built creams this is a non-issue; the net clinical effect is hydration, not dehydration.
  • "Antimicrobial / microbiome-friendly." That property belongs to glyceryl monolaurate, a different molecule. Glycerin itself is, at most, a weak water-activity-lowering preservation aid.
  • "Aquaporin-boosting." The AQP3 marketing story is real but rests on mouse causal data (Hara/Verkman [#3,#4]) plus a derivative (glyceryl glucoside [#12]) — not on topical glycerin upregulating AQP3 in human skin. Don't imply glycerin "activates aquaporins" in humans.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️/✅ SUPPORTING (foundational base humectant) — not a hero.

One-line reasoning: Glycerin is the cheapest, safest, best-evidenced humectant in skincare and belongs in essentially every hydrating/barrier formula we make — but its evidence is for hydration, softness, and barrier-recovery support, not the dermal-remodeling hallmarks that define "anti-aging," so it should anchor the base, never the marketing headline.

  • For the planned barrier-repair cream: include glycerin as a core supporting actor, paired with occlusives (petrolatum/paraffin/squalane) and barrier lipids (ceramides/cholesterol/fatty acids) — the exact humectant-plus-occlusive-plus-lipid logic the Phase 3 xerosis RCT validates [#10]. This is "buy commodity bases, invest in the brand" in its purest form: glycerin is the commodity base done right.
  • Longevity-relevance note: Maps to the barrier/hydration-decline axis of skin aging (aged skin has lower glycerol/NMF, higher TEWL, stiffer SC) and modestly to SC mechanical pliability — both real but surface-level aging signs. It does not address collagen loss, photoaging, glycation, oxidative stress, or senescence. Use glycerin to make the skin feel and look hydrated and supple today; rely on retinoids, vitamin C, SPF/photoprotection, peptides, and ceramides for the deeper longevity story.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19. Each headline claim was checked against primary sources for (a) human-topical vs borrowed in-vitro/animal/oral evidence, (b) citation accuracy, (c) study size and industry funding.

Overall evidence grade: B (moderate human-topical), narrowly defined

  • Grade A for the hydration claim alone (increases SC water content): multiple human topical studies, replicated, including a controlled bilateral design (Lodén [#5]) and a Phase 3 RCT [#10]. If this dossier were only about "does glycerin hydrate," it would earn an A.
  • Grade B overall because the broader headline set the dossier actually makes — barrier-recovery support and mechanical/elasticity benefit — rests on a thinner and partly conflicting human base (Lodén found no TEWL benefit; Bettinger framed the effect as rehydration, not barrier repair) plus mouse/ex-vivo mechanism. The single strongest topical RCT [#10] tests glycerol + paraffin, not glycerin alone, and is industry-funded (Pierre Fabre).
  • This is not an A-grade anti-aging ingredient and the dossier already says so — it correctly grades collagen/photoaging/glycation/senescence as None. That honesty is the dossier's strongest feature.

What held up under scrutiny (the dossier is well-disciplined)

  • Tiering is honest. The mouse AQP3 data (Hara/Verkman [#3, #4]) are explicitly labeled Tier 2 / animal and are not smuggled into human claims.
