Ingredient: Metformin (topical) (INCI: Metformin HCl)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Slug: topical-metformin · Class: NAD+ / sirtuin / AMPK / cellular-energy actives (a biguanide AMPK activator / geroprotector)

One-line honest summary: Metformin is the single most-decorated "anti-aging drug" in geroscience — it hits more hallmarks of aging than almost any other molecule and is the subject of the TAME longevity trial. But essentially all of that pedigree is oral, systemic, and in diabetics. For the skin specifically, the proof is in-vitro + animal (mostly oral/injected/needle-free, a little genuinely topical-to-animal), and the only human topical RCTs are for melasma and acne — not wrinkles, firmness, or "skin aging." It is also a drug in every major market and poorly skin-permeable. Treat it as a powerful narrative/education asset and a cautionary regulatory case study, not a cosmetic hero we can put in a cream.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Metformin (also "topical metformin"). Originally derived from Galega officinalis (French lilac / goat's rue) guanidine chemistry.
  • INCI: No established cosmetic INCI in routine use. Cosmetic/pharma listings appear as Metformin or Metformin Hydrochloride (Metformin HCl). It is a drug substance (API), not a recognized cosmetic ingredient — see §8.
  • CAS: Metformin base 657-24-9; Metformin hydrochloride 1115-70-4. [13]
  • Chemical class: Biguanide — 1,1-dimethylbiguanide. Empirical formula of the free base C₄H₁₁N₅; HCl salt C₄H₁₂ClN₅. [13]
  • Molecular weight: base ~129.16 g/mol; HCl salt ~165.62 g/mol — a very small molecule. [13]
  • Physicochemistry (this is the crux for topical use): Extremely hydrophilic. logP (octanol/water, pH 7.4) ≈ −1.43 — i.e., strongly water-loving and lipid-avoiding. It is a strong base (it carries a positive charge — exists as a hydrophilic cation at physiological pH; pKa ~12.4). Freely soluble in water, practically insoluble in lipids/organic solvents. [13] Small but charged-and-hydrophilic = the worst-case profile for crossing the lipid stratum corneum (see §5).
  • Forms/derivatives in market:
  • Metformin HCl — the standard salt (oral tablets; the form used in nearly all topical experiments: gels, lotions, creams, ointments).
  • Compounded topicals — pharmacist-compounded metformin gels/creams at 0.5%–30% used in the dermatology trials below.
  • Delivery-enhanced experimental forms — hydrogel-forming microneedle arrays, dissolving microneedles + iontophoresis, microemulsions — all developed precisely because plain topical metformin barely penetrates. [12][14]

2. Class & mechanism (how it works at the cell/skin level)

Metformin is the prototypical geroprotector / caloric-restriction mimetic. Its core biochemical action is mild inhibition of mitochondrial complex I of the electron-transport chain, which raises the cellular AMP/ATP ratio and thereby activates AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK), the master cellular energy sensor. From AMPK, a cascade of aging-relevant downstream effects follows. [1][2]

Mechanistic pillars (most established in non-skin systems): 1. AMPK activation → mTOR inhibition. Restores energy balance, suppresses mTORC1, and stimulates autophagy (the recycling pathway that clears damaged proteins/organelles) — the same axis as rapamycin and spermidine. [1][2] 2. Senomorphic / anti-SASP action via NF-κB. Metformin suppresses the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) — the inflammatory cytokine cocktail senescent cells secrete — by interfering with IKK/NF-κB activation. Critically, this effect is AMPK-independent in the foundational study (Moiseeva 2013): metformin blocked NF-κB even in AMPK-null fibroblasts. So metformin is a senomorphic / senostatic (it quiets senescent cells) rather than a senolytic (it does not kill them). [3] 3. Anti-inflammatory. Via NF-κB inhibition it lowers IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-6 (in many models), FGF2, and C/EBPβ — directly relevant to "inflammaging." [2][7] 4. Antioxidant / anti-ferroptotic. Reduces ROS and mitochondrial ROS; in skin photoaging models it preserves GPX4/NRF2 and inhibits ferroptosis (iron-dependent cell death). [9][10] 5. Mitochondrial quality control. Modulates mitophagy (Drp1/PINK1/Parkin) and mitochondrial function. [9] 6. Indirect anti-glycation. Systemically lowers glucose and induces glyoxalase-1 (GLO1), reducing methylglyoxal, a key precursor of AGEs that crosslink dermal collagen. (This is largely a systemic/metabolic mechanism, not a demonstrated topical one.) [2][16] 7. Melanogenesis suppression (the basis of the human melasma data). Topical metformin lowers cAMP → downregulates MITF (master melanocyte transcription factor) → reduces melanin synthesis. This is its best-substantiated human topical skin mechanism. [4][5]

Skin-relevant honesty: pillars 1–6 are well-proven biology in general and demonstrated for skin cells/animals; whether a topical cosmetic dose reaches living human dermis and triggers them there is largely undemonstrated. Pillar 7 (pigment) is the one with real human topical RCT support.

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

The single most important honesty point: Metformin's overwhelming evidence base is ORAL, SYSTEMIC, and largely in people with diabetes — for lifespan, cardiometabolic and cancer endpoints, not skin appearance. For skin, the data is strongest in-vitro and in animals (and even the animal "topical" work is mostly oral, injected, or needle-free jet-injected). The only human TOPICAL skin RCTs are for melasma and acne — pigmentation and lesion counts, not wrinkles, firmness, elasticity, or general "skin aging." No human topical anti-wrinkle/photoaging RCT exists. [11]

Tier 1 — In vitro (cell & biochemistry)

  • Moiseeva, Deschênes-Simard et al., 2013, Aging Cell (DOI 10.1111/acel.12075). Foundational senomorphic paper: metformin inhibits the SASP by interfering with IKK/IKKβ → NF-κB; suppressed multiple senescence-associated inflammatory cytokines; conditioned medium from metformin-treated senescent cells lost its pro-tumor effect. Effect was AMPK-independent (worked in AMPK-null fibroblasts and LPS-stimulated macrophages). Limitation: cultured cells (incl. non-skin); establishes mechanism, not skin efficacy. [3]
  • Chen et al., 2022, Int. J. Mol. Sci. 23(13):6960 (DOI 10.3390/ijms23136960) — in-vitro arm. Human foreskin fibroblasts (HFFs) + UVA (5 J/cm²/day ×3 d), metformin 100 µM: attenuated UVA-induced loss of viability and "skin aging," suppressed the PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway, reduced mitophagy markers (Drp1/PINK1/Parkin), eased mitochondrial oxidative stress. Limitation: n=3 technical replicates; direct medium dosing, not transdermal. [9]
  • Achadiyani et al., 2024, Biomed. Pharmacol. J. 17(3) (DOI 10.13005/bpj/2977). Replicative-senescence human fibroblast model, metformin 100 µM: did NOT reduce IL-6 mRNA — in fact IL-6 was significantly elevated at passage 5 (0.836 vs 0.158 control, p<0.001). Important counterweight: cuts against a clean "anti-inflammaging" story; mRNA only, not protein. Single model. [17]
  • "Metformin reverses high-glucose effects in aged-skin dermal fibroblasts via ↓RELA/p65" 2021, J. Physiol. Biochem. (DOI 10.1007/s13105-021-00823-y). In human dermal fibroblasts from aged skin under high glucose, metformin downregulated RELA/p65 (NF-κB) and reversed glucose-driven damage — a glycation/inflammaging-adjacent mechanism. Limitation: in vitro; full text paywalled — findings per abstract/secondary [partially unverified]. [15]
  • Li W. et al., 2013, Exp. Ther. Med. (DOI 10.3892/etm.2013.1416). HaCaT human keratinocytes, metformin 50 mM (very high): strongly anti-proliferative (viability fell to ~10–36%), ↑p-AMPK and ↑p-ERK1/2. Relevance/caution: this is the basis for psoriasis/acne use — metformin SUPPRESSES keratinocyte proliferation at high dose, which is the opposite of a "renewal/turnover" anti-aging story and a reason to respect dose. [18]
  • Metformin senomorphic activity broadly — multiple non-skin in-vitro models (IMR90 fibroblasts, endothelial cells, MSCs, lens/bronchial epithelium) show ↓SASP/↓ROS at therapeutic concentrations. Supports mechanism class-wide; not skin-specific. [3][19]

Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

  • Zhao, Chai et al., 2017, Aging Cell (DOI 10.1111/acel.12635) — the key genuinely-TOPICAL animal study. Aged (18-month) and young Sprague-Dawley rats (also mice/rabbits), metformin applied topically to wounds at 2 µM in ethanol, chronic daily. Metformin accelerated wound healing in both young and aged skin, prevented age-related AMPK suppression, drove a >3-fold increase in wound-bed vasculature (angiogenesis via endothelial AMPK), promoted proliferation and suppressed senescence markers p16/p21. Outperformed topical resveratrol; rapamycin inhibited healing. Limitation: rodent acute-wound model (not chronic facial photoaging); a very low topical dose in a penetration-enhancing ethanol vehicle; authors caution about translation. [6]
  • Chen et al., 2022, IJMS — in-vivo arm. Kunming mice, chronic UVA (8 wk), metformin 10 mg/kg/day ORALLY: ↑epidermal thickness, ↓collagen degradation, suppressed PI3K/AKT/mTOR. Limitation: ORAL, not topical; mouse; acute-ish model. [9]
  • "Needle-Free Injection of Metformin Ameliorates Skin Photoaging Through Inhibition of Ferroptosis and Oxidative Stress," 2024, Discov. Med. (PMID 38798266). UVB rat photoaging model + UVB-treated human fibroblasts; metformin delivered by needle-free jet injection into rat dorsal skin: reduced photoaging, preserved GPX4/NRF2, lowered ROS, inhibited ferroptosis. Limitation: rat; delivery was JET-INJECTION (bypasses the stratum corneum) — explicitly NOT a topical-cream scenario; confirms metformin's poor passive permeability by needing injection. [10]
  • Metformin lifespan/healthspan in model organisms — extends lifespan in C. elegans and (dose-dependently) some mouse strains; broad healthspan effects. Systemic; not skin. [1][2]

Tier 3 — Human ORAL / systemic (where the deep evidence is — and it isn't about skin appearance)

  • Bannister et al., 2014, Diabetes Obes. Metab. (DOI 10.1111/dom.12354). Retrospective cohort, 78,241 metformin-treated T2D vs 90,463 matched non-diabetic controls: metformin-monotherapy diabetics had longer median survival than matched non-diabetics (~15% survival-time difference). The famous "diabetics on metformin outlive non-diabetics" finding that launched the longevity hype. Limitation: observational, confounded (healthy-user, immortal-time, severity biases). A 2022 independent reanalysis (Keys et al., PMID 36287641) replicated the method in a fresh cohort and found the opposite — significantly higher mortality in metformin-monotherapy diabetics than non-diabetic controls (HR ≈1.39), with no survival advantage in any analysis. Treat the original finding as not just contested but contradicted by the best replication. Not a randomized longevity result; not skin. [20]
  • TAME (Targeting Aging with Metformin) — landmark planned ~3,000-person RCT (Barzilai et al.) designed to test metformin against composite age-related disease endpoints; funding-dependent and not yet reported. The reason metformin is the face of "drugs that target aging." Aspirational; no results; not skin. [21]
  • Oral metformin in dermatology — systematic reviews show benefit in insulin-/androgen-linked skin disease: hidradenitis suppurativa (≈106/133 patients improved across 6 studies), hormonal acne, acanthosis nigricans, psoriasis (adjunct), PCOS hirsutism. Real, but these are metabolic/endocrine mechanisms in disease, oral route — not cosmetic anti-aging. [22][23]
  • Oral NMN/NAD comparison note: like the NAD precursors (see nad-niacinamide-longevity.md), metformin's human data raises systemic biomarkers but the leap to "younger-looking skin" is unproven for any route.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters for a skincare brand — narrow, and not about wrinkles)

Per the 2024 systematic review (Afshar et al., Health Sci. Rep., DOI 10.1002/hsr2.70281), topical metformin in humans has been studied in dentistry (periodontitis, ~20 RCTs) and dermatology — melasma, acne, hair regrowth, wound healing. Across ~30 studies / ~1,220 participants, no human study examined topical metformin for photoaging, wrinkles, firmness, or general anti-aging. [11]

Melasma (pigmentation — the strongest human topical signal): - Mongkhon et al., 2023, Front. Pharmacol. (DOI 10.3389/fphar.2023.1281050) — systematic review + meta-analysis. 3 RCTs, 140 patients. 15% metformin beat placebo on MASI at 8 wk (MD −0.56, p=0.034) and 12 wk (MD −0.73, p=0.007); 30% metformin was equivalent to Kligman's triple-combination cream (MD 0.19, p=0.39) with fewer side effects (less burning/redness). Mechanism: ↓cAMP → ↓MITF. Limitation: small; all three RCTs rated HIGH risk of bias (poor randomization/blinding documentation). [11] - Banavase Channakeshavaiah & Andanooru Chandrappa, 2020, J. Cosmet. Dermatol. (DOI 10.1111/jocd.13145). Preliminary clinical trial of topical metformin for melasma — one of the primary RCTs feeding the meta-analysis. Small, preliminary; full text paywalled — design details per abstract/secondary [partially unverified]. [8]

Acne (lesion counts — a second human topical signal): - EL-Komy et al., 2023, Exp. Dermatol. 32(10):1663–1673 (DOI 10.1111/exd.14868). Topical metformin 30% gel, split-face, placebo-controlled, 27 women, 12 weeks. Treated side: significant reduction in comedones, papules and nodules (not pustules); benefit partially persisted 1 month after stopping (comedones/papules still lower); no side effects reported. Limitation: small, women-only, single-center; 30% is a very high concentration. [24]

Other human topical (not anti-aging): wound healing (scar vascularity/pigmentation/pliability improved; small studies), CCCA hair regrowth (case reports, 10% cream), periodontitis (dental gels). [11]

Bottom line by tier: Proven for metformin specifically → systemic longevity/cardiometabolic/cancer signals (oral, diabetics) and model-organism lifespan. For skin: mechanism + animal/cell photoaging protection, with genuine human topical RCTs only for melasma (pigment) and acne (lesions). No human topical evidence for wrinkles, elasticity, firmness, or "anti-aging." Any topical anti-aging claim rests entirely on borrowed (oral/animal/cell) or off-target (pigment/acne) evidence.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration (the deal-breaker)

  • The problem is physicochemical and severe. Metformin is small (MW ~165) — good — but it is extremely hydrophilic (logP ≈ −1.43) and a permanently charged cation at skin pH. The stratum corneum is a lipid barrier; charged, water-loving molecules cross it very poorly. Size helps; charge + hydrophilicity dominate and hurt. [13]
  • The literature openly concedes this. Reviews note topical metformin's "poor transdermal permeability," with topical efficacy "typically requiring relatively high concentrations (~6–30%)" — which is exactly why the human skin studies used 15–30%. [9][11]
  • The "tell" in the animal data: the topical wound study used an ethanol vehicle (a penetration enhancer) and an open wound (no intact barrier) [6]; the photoaging study used needle-free jet injection [10] — both bypass or breach the stratum corneum. Whole programs exist for microneedles, dissolving-microneedle + iontophoresis, and microemulsions specifically because passive metformin delivery through intact skin fails. [12][14]
  • No human transdermal/penetration data quantify how much topical metformin reaches viable epidermis/dermis from a conventional cream, or whether it raises intracellular AMPK signaling in living human skin.
  • Practical read: Penetration through intact skin is poor and concentration-hungry; meaningful delivery likely needs 15–30% loads, enhancers, or device-assisted delivery — none of which fit a clean, elegant, claim-safe cosmetic.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: No cosmetic-validated level; no anti-aging dose exists at all. Human topical efficacy (pigment/acne) was seen at 15–30% — pharmaceutical, not cosmetic, loads. In-vitro actives were 100 µM (fibroblasts) and 50 mM (keratinocytes, anti-proliferative); the topical animal dose was 2 µM in ethanol. These are not formulation guidance for a cosmetic. Do not imply a validated anti-aging dose-response.
  • pH: Metformin HCl is acidic in solution but the molecule is a strong base (pKa ~12.4), present as a cation across the cosmetic pH range; very water-soluble. High loads will affect formula pH/osmolality and need buffering toward skin-friendly pH ~5–6.
  • Stability: Metformin itself is a robust, stable small molecule (stable as an oral API for years). Formulation challenge is delivery, not degradation. At 15–30% it dominates the water phase and constrains the rest of the formula.
  • Pairings (conceptual, if ever pursued): mechanistically rhymes with other AMPK/autophagy/geroprotector stories — niacinamide (NAD axis; see nad-niacinamide-longevity.md), spermidine (autophagy; see spermidine.md), resveratrol/berberine. For pigment, it has been benchmarked against hydroquinone/Kligman's. Penetration enhancers (ethanol, microemulsion) would be needed to do anything.
  • Conflicts/sensory: At high % it's bulky in the water phase. Practical sensory data for cosmetic textures is essentially absent (the trials used compounded pharmacy gels/lotions). Note: at high dose in keratinocytes it is anti-proliferative [18] — relevant when reasoning about "turnover."
  • The honest formulation verdict: to deliver enough to matter you need pharma-grade concentrations and/or a delivery device — which pushes the product squarely into drug territory (§8) and away from an elegant cosmetic.

7. Safety

  • Topical irritation/sensitization: The human topical trials (melasma, acne) report it as well-tolerated with minimal irritation — generally gentler than hydroquinone/triple-combination cream, with occasional mild burning (~10% in one melasma arm) and no significant AEs in the acne study. No published HRIPT/patch-test on a finished cosmetic — generate our own if ever used. [11][24]
  • Sun sensitivity: No photosensitivity signal; mechanistically it appears photo-protective (anti-UVA/UVB in models). [9][10]
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: Oral metformin is used in pregnancy (GDM/PCOS) under medical supervision and is generally considered relatively safe systemically — but it crosses the placenta, and a cosmetic should never position a drug for pregnant users. Topical pregnancy data: none. Default to "consult your doctor."
  • Systemic safety (oral context, for honesty): Most common AE is GI upset; long-term use is associated with vitamin B12 deficiency; the rare serious risk is lactic acidosis (chiefly in renal impairment). These are oral/systemic and not expected from topical cosmetic exposure, but they underline that this is a drug with a real pharmacology, not an inert cosmetic. [1][2]
  • Anti-proliferative caution: strong keratinocyte growth suppression at high dose [18] — fine for acne/psoriasis intent, but a reason not to hand-wave high concentrations as benign "skin food."
  • Sourcing/allergen: synthetic API; no botanical-allergen concern.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails) — the headline risk

  • Metformin is a DRUG, not a cosmetic ingredient — full stop. It is a prescription antidiabetic API worldwide (e.g., US FDA-approved Rx drug; EU prescription medicine). It has no established cosmetic INCI/notified cosmetic status, and the human "topical metformin" studies are pharmaceutical/compounding investigations, not cosmetic ones.
  • Putting metformin in a marketed skincare product would almost certainly make the product an unapproved drug in the US (intended to affect structure/function and/or treat disease — melasma/acne are treatment claims), and a medicinal product in the EU/UK by ingredient + intended use. This is a bright-line legal problem, not a phrasing nuance. Do not formulate metformin into a cosmetic without specialist regulatory/legal counsel.
  • The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame): "activates AMPK," "targets cellular senescence," "the longevity drug for your skin," "anti-aging at the cellular level" are textbook structure/function (drug) claims — and here the ingredient is literally a drug, compounding the exposure.
  • ✅ Safer (education-only) language — even this needs care because the ingredient is a drug: "inspired by geroscience and the AMPK/longevity research," "metformin is the molecule behind the TAME longevity trial." Use as brand-science storytelling, explicitly not as a product we sell.
  • ⚠️ Off-limits without it becoming a drug: any claim that our product contains metformin to treat melasma/acne, reduce wrinkles, "activate longevity pathways," or "boost cellular energy" in skin.
  • Substantiation reality: even the legitimate human topical wins (pigment, acne) are disease/treatment endpoints with high-risk-of-bias trials [11] — not cosmetic claims and not anti-aging.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Forms/suppliers: Metformin HCl is a globally abundant, dirt-cheap generic API (one of the most-produced drugs on earth); pharma-grade readily available from API manufacturers. Microneedle/microemulsion delivery systems are R&D-stage, not off-the-shelf.
  • Cost tier: Very low as raw material — but irrelevant, because the binding constraint is regulatory status, not cost. The real "cost" is legal exposure and the need for drug-grade manufacturing/registration if pursued.
  • Supply risk: none for the raw API; total risk for using it compliantly in a cosmetic.

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • The format-and-population switch (the big one). Marketing borrows metformin's oral, systemic, diabetic longevity halo (Bannister, TAME) and implies it transfers to a topical cosmetic on healthy skin. It doesn't, automatically. The human longevity signal is observational and disputed, and TAME hasn't reported. [20][21]
  • "Activates AMPK / longevity pathways in your skin." Not demonstrated for a topical cosmetic dose in living human skin; the in-skin AMPK data are cells, ethanol-vehicle wounds, oral mice, and jet-injected rats. [6][9][10]
  • "Clinically proven topical metformin." The human topical RCTs are for melasma and acne (disease, pigment/lesions) — not wrinkles or aging — and are small and high risk of bias. [11]
  • "It penetrates and recharges skin cells." It's a charged, hydrophilic cation that crosses intact skin poorly; the science needed 15–30% loads, enhancers, microneedles, or injection to do anything. [9][10][12][14]
  • The inconvenient counter-data. One human-fibroblast aging model showed metformin raised IL-6 [17]; high-dose metformin suppresses keratinocyte proliferation [18] — neither fits a tidy "rejuvenation" narrative.
  • What's NOT hype: metformin's geroscience pedigree is real and exceptional (broadest hallmark coverage of any candidate drug; senomorphic/anti-SASP mechanism is solid [3]). It is a fantastic story — just not a cosmetic ingredient.

11. Verdict for the brand

❌→⚠️ SKIP as a formulation ingredient; CONDITIONAL/optional as an education-only narrative asset. One-line: Metformin is geroscience's flagship "anti-aging drug," which makes it irresistible story material — but it is a regulated drug with poor skin penetration and zero human topical anti-aging evidence (its only human topical RCTs are disease treatments — melasma and acne — at pharmaceutical concentrations). Do not put it in a cosmetic. At most, use it as brand-science storytelling about the longevity field, clearly separated from anything we sell.

Why not a hero/supporting ingredient: 1. Regulatory bright line — it's a drug; formulating it likely makes the product an unapproved drug/medicinal product (§8). This alone is disqualifying for a cosmetic. 2. Penetration — charged + hydrophilic; needs 15–30% / enhancers / devices to act (§5). 3. Evidence mismatch — human topical proof is pigment/acne (treatment), not anti-aging; the longevity data is oral/systemic/diabetic and partly disputed (§4).

Longevity-relevance note: For a skin-longevity brand, metformin is arguably the best "geroscience credibility" reference molecule in existence — the drug that defined "targeting aging itself" (TAME), with the broadest hallmark coverage. That makes it superb educational/positioning material ("the science of aging, applied to skin") and a perfect illustration of our claims discipline: we can explain why we admire the geroscience yet won't sneak a prescription drug into a cream. The honest path to anything topical here would be a registered drug development program, not a cosmetic — which is out of scope. Lean on the senomorphic/AMPK/autophagy mechanism family through cosmetic-legal actives instead (niacinamide's NAD story, spermidine's autophagy story), and let metformin be the story we tell, not the molecule we sell.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Independent re-check of every headline claim against the primary literature, 2026-06-19. Method: for each claim, asked (a) is the evidence HUMAN and TOPICAL, or borrowed from in-vitro / animal / oral? (b) is the cited study real and does it say what's claimed? (c) is it industry-funded or tiny? Primary sources fetched/verified where accessible.

Overall grade: D (for topical anti-aging — the only claim that matters for a cosmetic)

There is zero human topical evidence for wrinkles, firmness, elasticity, or photoaging. The strongest human topical data (melasma, acne) is off-target (disease/pigment/lesions) and graded high risk of bias. Any anti-aging story rests entirely on borrowed (oral/systemic/diabetic) or non-human (cell/animal) evidence. Sub-grades by claim type: - Topical anti-aging (wrinkles/firmness): D — unproven topically; no study exists. - Topical pigment (melasma): C+ — real human RCTs but small (140 pts), all high RoB. - Topical acne (lesions): C — one small (n=27) split-face RCT. - Oral/systemic longevity pedigree: B- as biology / C as a longevity outcome — observational, healthy-user/immortal-time confounded, and the flagship survival claim is now contradicted by replication (see below). - Mechanism (AMPK / senomorphic): B — solid biology, but mostly non-skin cells and not shown to occur in living human skin from a topical dose.

Verdict: the dossier's own conclusion (skip as an ingredient; education-only narrative) is correct and well-supported. The dossier is honest and accurately hedged — it does not overstate. The grade is low because the ingredient's topical anti-aging case is weak, not because the write-up is.
