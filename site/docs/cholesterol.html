Ingredient: Cholesterol (+ cholesteryl esters) (INCI: Cholesterol; Cholesteryl Esters e.g. Cholesteryl Oleate, Cholesteryl Stearate; related: Cholesteryl Sulfate)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Class: Barrier lipids & skin-identical actives · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product

Brutally honest one-liner: Cholesterol is one of the three skin-identical lipids that build the stratum-corneum barrier (with ceramides + free fatty acids), and it is the single most under-rated of the trio for aging skin specifically — because the one lipid that demonstrably falls fastest with age is cholesterol synthesis, and the dermatology literature (Elias/Feingold/Ghadially, UCSF) shows that a cholesterol-DOMINANT physiological-lipid blend repairs the aged barrier faster than the famous ceramide-dominant "3:1:1" blend. That is a genuinely strong, primary-literature-backed story. The honest caveats: (1) cholesterol works only as part of the correct three-lipid blend — alone or as a two-lipid mix it delays barrier recovery; (2) almost all the head-to-head "cholesterol-dominant beats the rest in aged skin" data is murine + small human barrier-recovery (TEWL) studies, not large independent anti-wrinkle RCTs; (3) the marketed "anti-aging" wrinkle/smoothness numbers come from a manufacturer (SkinCeuticals) study, not independent trials; and (4) cholesterol is a double-edged molecule — essential in the barrier, but its UV-oxidation products (oxysterols) are pro-aging, so an unprotected cholesterol-rich film is not automatically "good." Treat cholesterol as a near-mandatory co-star of the barrier hero, not a standalone hero.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Cholesterol (from Greek chole- bile + stereos solid). The brand's "skin-identical sterol."
  • Chemistry: A sterol — a four-fused-ring (cyclopentanoperhydrophenanthrene/steroid) lipid with a 3β-hydroxyl group, a C5–C6 double bond in the B-ring, and an 8-carbon iso-octyl side chain. C₂₇H₄₆O, MW ≈ 386.7 Da. CAS 57-88-5. It is amphipathic (a polar -OH "head" + a rigid hydrophobic body), which is why it sits at lipid–water interfaces and tunes membrane/bilayer fluidity.
  • INCI: Cholesterol (cosmetic functions: skin-conditioning, emollient, emulsion stabilizer, viscosity/structure agent).
  • Key forms / derivatives relevant to skin:
  • Free cholesterol — the bioactive barrier lipid; ~25% by mass of the stratum-corneum (SC) intercellular lipid matrix. This is the main subject of the dossier.
  • Cholesteryl esters (CEs) — cholesterol esterified to a fatty acid (e.g., Cholesteryl Oleate, Cholesteryl Stearate, Cholesteryl Nonanoate). ~10% of SC lipids; act as emollients/structural lipids and a cholesterol reservoir; common cosmetic conditioning agents and liquid-crystal/texture modifiers.
  • Cholesteryl Sulfate (cholesterol sulfate, CS) — a minor but functionally important sterol metabolite. It regulates desquamation (it inhibits the serine proteases that dissolve corneodesmosomes, so cells don't shed prematurely) and modulates differentiation. Its enzymatic desulfation to cholesterol is a required barrier-maturation step. Caveat: CS also fluidizes the SC lipid phase and can increase permeability if in excess (see §4) — so it is not interchangeable with free cholesterol.
  • 7-Dehydrocholesterol (7-DHC) — cholesterol's immediate biosynthetic precursor and the UVB substrate for vitamin-D synthesis; pro-oxidant under UVA (relevant to the photoaging caveat, §3/§7).
  • Oxysterols / cholesterol oxidation products (COPs) — e.g., 25-hydroxycholesterol, 7-ketocholesterol, cholesterol hydroperoxides. These are oxidized cholesterol and are damage signals, not actives (see §3 — the dark side of the molecule).
  • Lanosterol / phytosterols — adjacent sterols sometimes used as cholesterol-like emollients or vegan substitutes (phytosterols are not identical to human cholesterol; see §9 sourcing).

2. Class & mechanism

Cholesterol is filed under barrier lipids & skin-identical actives because it is literally one of the three molecules the body uses to construct the permeability barrier. Its mechanism is structural physiology, not "signaling-active" pharmacology.

The brick-and-mortar barrier

The stratum corneum is "bricks (corneocytes) and mortar (intercellular lipid)." The mortar is a highly ordered set of lamellar (sheet-like) lipid bilayers made of roughly: - ~50% ceramides (the structural backbone), - ~25% cholesterol (the fluidity/spacing regulator and lamellar-phase organizer), - ~15% free fatty acids (acidify the SC, co-organize lamellae), - ~10% cholesteryl esters + cholesterol sulfate + trace lipids.

These three classes self-assemble into two coexisting lamellar phases (the "short" ~6 nm and "long" ~13 nm periodicity phases) that make the barrier both tight (low water loss) and flexible. Cholesterol's specific job is to sit alongside the ceramide sphingoid backbone, modulate the packing/fluidity of the lamellae, and prevent the lipid matrix from becoming either too rigid/cracked or too fluid/leaky. Remove cholesterol and the lamellae don't organize properly; barrier function fails.

Why cholesterol matters most for AGING skin (the key mechanistic argument)

  1. Epidermal lipid synthesis falls with age, and cholesterol synthesis is among the lipids that falls. Aged skin shows a drop in total stratum-corneum lipids (~30% in the murine model of Ghadially 1995 [4]; note that paper found total lipid down but the relative proportions of ceramide:cholesterol:FFA broadly unchanged), alongside reduced epidermal cholesterol synthesis and lower HMG-CoA reductase activity (the rate-limiting enzyme of the cholesterol/mevalonate pathway) reported across the Elias-school literature [5][6][17]. Aged keratinocytes are also less responsive to the growth-factor/hormone signals that drive mevalonate→cholesterol production. (Honesty note: the strong "cholesterol synthesis falls the most / disproportionately" framing is an interpretation built on the body of work — including the functional finding that aged skin specifically needs cholesterol weighted up [5] — rather than a single quantitative measurement; state it as "cholesterol synthesis declines and aged skin behaves as if cholesterol-limited," not as a precise "falls the most.")
  2. Cholesterol synthesis is required for barrier repair. Blocking it (topical lovastatin, an HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor) impairs barrier recovery; co-applying mevalonate or cholesterol rescues it (Feingold 1990). So cholesterol isn't just a passive filler — its synthesis is causally gating.
  3. Therefore aged skin behaves as if relatively cholesterol-limited, and a cholesterol-dominant top-up repairs it fastest in the available models (Zettersten/Elias 1997 [5]; the basis of the "2:4:2" cholesterol-dominant commercial concept). This is the mechanistic reason a longevity-oriented barrier product should arguably weight cholesterol up, not just pile on ceramides. (Evidence is acute barrier-recovery / TEWL in aged mice + a small aged-human cohort, not a wrinkle outcome — see §4.)

The "you can't use it alone" rule

Cholesterol applied by itself, or in a two-lipid mix, delays barrier recovery (Man 1993). Only the complete three-lipid blend in an appropriate molar ratio restores normal repair; over-weighting any one of the three (up to ~3:1:1) then accelerates repair. This is the single most important formulation fact about cholesterol and the reason "cholesterol" is best thought of as a team player, never a solo hero.

Cholesterol's honest lane = barrier repair + hydration/TEWL, especially in aged (cholesterol-depleted) skin, as the dominant lipid in a 3-lipid blend. The "anti-wrinkle / firmness / radiance" benefits are real-ish but are downstream barrier/hydration halo effects, not direct anti-aging pharmacology.

The dark side (must not be ignored)

Cholesterol's C5–C6 double bond makes it readily oxidized by UV/ROS into oxysterols (25-hydroxycholesterol, 7-ketocholesterol) and cholesterol hydroperoxides, which accumulate in sun-exposed SC, are implicated in matrix-metalloproteinase (MMP) activation → collagen breakdown [14], drive abnormal keratinocyte differentiation, and have been shown to trigger pyroptosis/apoptosis in human skin models [13]. Translation: cholesterol is essential inside an organized, protected barrier, but a cholesterol-rich film without adequate antioxidant/photoprotection context is not unambiguously "anti-aging." This nuance is exactly the kind of evidence-honesty the brand should own.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

Read this first. Cholesterol's evidence is unusual: the mechanistic and barrier-physiology evidence is excellent and from top-tier primary literature (JCI, JID, JAAD; the UCSF Elias/Feingold school), but it is overwhelmingly murine barrier-recovery (TEWL) work plus small human barrier-recovery studies, and it is almost always about the three-lipid blend, not cholesterol in isolation. There is no large, independent, double-blind anti-wrinkle RCT of cholesterol (or of a cholesterol-dominant blend); the consumer "anti-aging" numbers come from a manufacturer study of a finished product. So: barrier/TEWL benefit = well-proven; cosmetic anti-aging = plausible halo effect with weak independent proof. Cholesterol also has no meaningful topical-skin oral-format story (dietary cholesterol does not "feed" the skin barrier — see Tier 3).

Tier 1 — In-vitro / biophysics / mechanism (strong, foundational)

  • Feingold KR, Man MQ, Menon GK, Cho SS, Brown BE, Elias PM. (1990), J Clin Invest 86(5):1738–45. doi:10.1172/JCI114899; PMID 2243142. [1] — Foundational causal study. Topical lovastatin (HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor) blocked epidermal cholesterol synthesis and impaired barrier recovery after acetone disruption in hairless mice; co-application of mevalonate or cholesterol normalized recovery. First proof that cholesterol synthesis is required for barrier function. Limitation: murine; pharmacologic-inhibition model.
  • Man MQ, Feingold KR, Elias PM. (1993), Arch Dermatol 129(6):728–38. PMID 8507075. [2] — The "blend rule" study. In acetone-perturbed murine skin, applying cholesterol (or ceramide, or FFA) alone, or as a two-component mix, DELAYED barrier recovery; only the complete three-lipid mixture allowed normal recovery. Foundational reason cholesterol must be co-formulated, never solo.
  • Man MQ, Feingold KR, Thornfeldt CR, Elias PM. (1996), J Invest Dermatol 106(5):1096–101. doi:10.1111/1523-1747.ep12340135; PMID 8618046. [3] — Optimization study. With the complete blend, an equimolar (1:1:1) ratio gave normal recovery, and increasing any one of the three up to ~3-fold accelerated recovery. Acylceramide+cholesterol combos "dramatically" improved repair. Established that ratio is tunable and that the dominant lipid can be chosen to suit the skin state. Glycosylceramides/triglycerides could substitute for ceramides/FFAs, but phospholipids and cholesteryl esters could not substitute for their free counterparts — i.e., cholesteryl esters are not a stand-in for free cholesterol in barrier repair. Limitation: mostly murine; human data described as preliminary.
  • SC lipid-model biophysics (e.g., Cholesterol sulfate fluidizes the sterol fraction and increases permeability, Biophys J 2022; doi:10.1016/j.bpj.2021.12.041; PMID 35143845 [9]; and cholesterol/SC lipid-model membrane studies, BBA Biomembranes 2008 [10]) — show cholesterol's role in lamellar phase behavior and packing, and that cholesterol sulfate increases fluidity/permeability (why CS ≠ free cholesterol functionally). Confirms cholesterol's structural mechanism and the form-specificity.

Verdict (Tier 1): Mechanism is rock-solid: cholesterol is required for barrier assembly/repair, works only in the correct blend, and the ratio is purposefully tunable. Form matters (free cholesterol ≠ cholesteryl ester ≠ cholesterol sulfate).

Tier 2 — Animal (strong for barrier recovery; this is where the "cholesterol-dominant for aged skin" finding originates)

  • Ghadially R, Brown BE, Sequeira-Martin SM, Feingold KR, Elias PM. (1995), J Clin Invest 95(5):2281–90. doi:10.1172/JCI117919; PMID 7738193. [4] — The "aged barrier" paper. Compared young (20–30 y) vs aged (>80 y) humans + a senescent murine model. Aged SC had subnormal baseline TEWL but was perturbed far more easily and recovered far more slowly, with ~30% lower total SC lipids and reduced cholesterol synthesis. This is the biochemical basis for "aged skin is lipid- (especially cholesterol-) depleted."
  • Zettersten EM, Ghadially R, Feingold KR, Crumrine D, Elias PM. (1997), J Am Acad Dermatol 37(3 Pt 1):403–8. doi:10.1016/S0190-9622(97)70140-3; PMID 9308554. [5] — THE keystone "cholesterol-dominant" study. In chronologically aged mice (>18 mo) and aged humans (n≈6, ~80 y), a single application of an optimal lipid mixture (cholesterol:ceramide:palmitate:linoleate = 3:1:1:1, i.e., cholesterol-DOMINANT) significantly accelerated barrier recovery at 6 h after tape-stripping; the FFA-dominant mix significantly delayed recovery at 3/6/24 h in aged skin even though it helped young skin — i.e., aged skin specifically needs cholesterol weighted up. This is the single most important paper for justifying a cholesterol-forward aging product. Limitations: very small human cohort (n≈6), TEWL/barrier-recovery endpoint only (not wrinkles/long-term), single-application/acute-perturbation design, single research school.
  • Haratake A, Ikenaga K, Katoh N, Uchiwa H, Hirano S, Yasuno H. (2000), J Invest Dermatol 114(2):247–53. doi:10.1046/j.1523-1747.2000.00855.x; PMID 10651982. [6] — Topical mevalonic acid (a cholesterol precursor) stimulated de novo epidermal cholesterol synthesis + HMG-CoA reductase and improved barrier resistance/recovery in aged mice (no effect in young mice); cholesterol itself worked too but required a much higher dose. Reinforces that the aged-skin deficit is specifically a cholesterol-synthesis deficit, and that driving cholesterol production (or supplying cholesterol directly) repairs it.

Verdict (Tier 2): The "cholesterol-dominant blend repairs aged skin faster" claim is genuinely evidence-based — but the evidence is acute barrier-recovery / TEWL in mice + small aged-human studies, from one dominant research school (UCSF). It is barrier science, not anti-wrinkle science.

Tier 3 — Human, ORAL / dietary (essentially irrelevant — important for honesty)

  • No credible evidence that dietary or oral cholesterol improves skin barrier or appearance. The skin makes its own cholesterol in situ (the entire mechanism above is about local epidermal synthesis, gated by HMG-CoA reductase); circulating/dietary cholesterol is not a meaningful route to SC barrier lipids. There is no oral-cholesterol skincare literature to borrow from. (This is the opposite of ingredients like collagen or NAD precursors where an oral-vs-topical debate exists.) Do not make any oral/ingestible claim.
  • Adjacent note (not a skin benefit): systemic statins (which lower cholesterol synthesis) and very-low-cholesterol states are associated in some reports with drier/itchier skin in the elderly — consistent with the barrier role, but not evidence for oral cholesterol as a cosmetic. Mention only as mechanistic color, never as a claim.

Verdict (Tier 3): No topical-relevant oral evidence, by design — cholesterol's barrier action is local/synthetic, so there is nothing to import from an oral format. This is a clean, honest "N/A."

Tier 4 — Human, TOPICAL (the tier that matters for a product)

Two sub-tiers: (a) rigorous barrier-recovery human data (good), and (b) finished-product anti-aging data (manufacturer-funded, lower tier).

  • (a) Human barrier-recovery data — within Zettersten 1997 [5] and Ghadially 1995 [4]: the cholesterol-dominant blend accelerated barrier recovery in aged human skin (TEWL endpoint, small N, acute tape-strip model). This is real human topical evidence, but for barrier kinetics, not cosmetic appearance, and at small scale.

  • (b) SkinCeuticals "Triple Lipid Restore 2:4:2" — the commercial cholesterol-dominant cream. Formulated at 2% ceramides : 4% cholesterol : 2% fatty acids by weight (cholesterol-dominant), corresponding to a ~1:3:2 molar ratio, explicitly built on the Zettersten/Elias cholesterol-dominant-for-aged-skin rationale. Manufacturer-reported results (Modern Aesthetics 2016 supplement [7]; SkinCeuticals/L'Oréal materials [8]):

  • 8-week, single-center study, n≈55 women aged 55–75 with moderate photodamage, twice-daily, used alongside a cleanser and an SPF 30 (so effects are not cholesterol-isolated): ~66% improvement in smoothness, ~44% in pore appearance, ~35% in laxity, ~26% in radiance, ~20% in evenness.
  • A lipidomic study reported the cream raised SC ceramides ~57%, cholesterol ~50%, fatty acids ~12% — i.e., it measurably replenished barrier lipids.
  • Limitations (significant): manufacturer-funded; not independently replicated; confounded by concurrent SPF + cleanser regimen; appearance endpoints are investigator/instrument-scored on a finished multi-ingredient product, not a placebo-controlled isolate of cholesterol. Useful as proof the cholesterol-dominant concept can be commercialized and shows replenishment + cosmetic improvement, not as clean proof that "cholesterol reduces wrinkles."

What is actually proven for human TOPICAL cholesterol: - Well-supported (barrier/TEWL, esp. aged skin): as the dominant lipid in a complete 3-lipid blend, cholesterol accelerates barrier recovery and replenishes SC lipids in aged human skin [4][5][7][8]. - Plausible halo (cosmetic appearance): smoothness/laxity/radiance improvements from a cholesterol-dominant finished cream — manufacturer data only, regimen-confounded [7][8]. - NOT established: cholesterol as a standalone anti-ager; direct collagen/wrinkle pharmacology; any antioxidant benefit (it is an oxidation target); any oral/ingestible benefit.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • It doesn't need to go deep — and mostly shouldn't. Cholesterol's job is to integrate into the stratum-corneum lipid lamellae, which is a surface/SC event. The foundational studies show topically applied cholesterol (in the correct blend) is taken up into the SC and incorporated into the lamellar matrix, where it does its structural work [2][5]. It does not need to reach living dermis to repair the barrier, which is why the barrier-repair claim is among the most physically defensible in skincare — the target tissue is the outer film.
  • Penetration is intrinsically limited (it is a lipophilic ~387 Da sterol; the SC it's joining is itself the rate-limiting barrier), but for barrier replenishment that's a feature, not a bug.
  • Delivery/formulation nuance: because cholesterol self-assembles into the same lamellae it's repairing, co-delivery with ceramides + FFAs in lamellar/multilamellar emulsions (the "skin-identical" or "lamellar cream" format) improves incorporation vs. a crude oil dispersion. Some products use pseudo-ceramide/lamellar systems or liposomal carriers to optimize uptake.
  • It can modestly influence the penetration of other actives by integrating into (and locally re-ordering) the SC lipid matrix — but this is a barrier-normalizing effect, not a strong penetration-enhancer effect (unlike cholesterol sulfate, which fluidizes and increases permeability [9]). Don't market cholesterol as a "delivery booster."

6. Formulation

  • Use level: Cosmetic creams typically use ~0.1–1% cholesterol (CIR survey notes a max of ~0.25% in many face/neck leave-ons, but emulsion patents/formulae range 0.05–10%, commonly 0.1–3%) [11][12]. In a dedicated cholesterol-dominant barrier hero, higher loading is the point — e.g., SkinCeuticals uses 4% cholesterol (with 2% ceramide + 2% FFA) [7][8].
  • The ratio is the active ingredient. Evidence-based targets:
  • Equimolar 1:1:1 ceramide:cholesterol:FFA = "normal" repair (floor) [3].
  • Up to ~3:1:1 weighting of one lipid = accelerated repair [3].
  • Cholesterol-DOMINANT (e.g., 3:1:1:1 chol:cer:palmitate:linoleate, or ~1:3:2 cer:chol:FFA molar) = best for aged/chronologically aged skin [5][7]. This is the brand-relevant target for a longevity barrier cream.
  • Avoid FFA-dominant blends for aging skin — they delay recovery in aged skin even though they help young skin [5].
  • Solubility / phase: oil-phase ingredient; melts/disperses in the lipid phase; helps structure emulsions (acts as a co-emulsifier/stabilizer and bodying agent), which is a useful formulation bonus.
  • pH / stability: chemically stable in well-made emulsions across normal skincare pH (~5–6, the SC's acidic optimum, which also favors FFA function). Key vulnerability = oxidation: the C5–C6 double bond oxidizes to oxysterols on UV/air/heat exposure [13][14] → must be protected with antioxidants (e.g., tocopherol/vitamin E), opaque/airless packaging, and chelators, and the brand's refillable-packaging plan should account for keeping the lipid phase from going rancid.
  • Pairings (synergy):
  • Ceramides + free fatty acids — the non-negotiable trio; cholesterol is only as good as its blend partners [2][3].
  • Niacinamide — upregulates endogenous synthesis of all three barrier lipids (ceramides, cholesterol, FFAs) — a strong "supply-side + replenishment" combo with topical cholesterol.
  • Phytosphingosine / cholesterol-precursor angles (mevalonate) — mechanistically interesting "drive your own synthesis" co-stars [6] (mevalonate is niche/not common cosmetically).
  • Occlusives/emollients, glycerin/HA, panthenol — complementary barrier+hydration system.
  • Tolerance buffer for actives — a cholesterol-replete barrier tolerates retinoids/AHAs better, so it pairs as the "repair base" beneath irritating actives.
  • Antioxidants (vitamin E, etc.) — both for formula stability and to counter cholesterol's oxysterol liability.
  • Conflicts: none chemically aggressive; the real "conflict" is using cholesterol without its lipid partners (counterproductive [2]) and leaving it unprotected from oxidation.
  • Sensory: absorbs into skin without an oily/greasy slip (a noted advantage over plain oils [11]) — gives a rich-but-non-occlusive "cushioned" finish; supports a premium cream texture.

7. Safety

  • Strong safety profile. CIR Expert Panel: "safe as used in cosmetics" (first reviewed 1986; reaffirmed) [15]. It is an endogenous human lipid, non-irritating and non-sensitizing at cosmetic levels; suitable for sensitive, dry, eczema-prone, post-procedure, and aged/compromised skin (indeed it is used to repair such skin).
  • Irritation / sensitization: negligible at cosmetic concentrations.
  • Sun sensitivity: cholesterol itself is not a photosensitizer in the classic sense, but it is photo-oxidized to oxysterols/hydroperoxides that are biologically harmful and pro-aging [13][14]. Practical implication: a cholesterol-rich product should be antioxidant-buffered and the brand should co-message daily SPF — not because cholesterol "burns," but because the molecule degrades into damaging species under UV. (Do not claim cholesterol provides any sun protection.)
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: no known topical contraindication (endogenous lipid). General "consult your clinician" brand stance; no specific flag.
  • Oxysterol caveat (formulation, not consumer): rancid/oxidized cholesterol = oxysterols. This is a stability/quality issue (shelf life, packaging, antioxidant load), and a reason to QC the lipid phase.
  • Sourcing / sustainability (this is cholesterol's real complication — see §9): historically animal-derived (lanolin/wool grease; formerly bovine spinal cord). Wool-grease cholesterol is not vegan; bovine-derived cholesterol carries a (now-negligible but optics-sensitive) prion/BSE concern [16]. Modern synthetic / plant-steroid-derived ("phyto-route") cholesterol exists and is the ethically and reputationally cleaner option for a premium brand. Specify sourcing.

8. Regulatory & claims (cosmetic vs. drug)

  • Status: a cosmetic ingredient (skin-conditioning/emollient/emulsion stabilizer). Using it as a barrier-replenishing moisturizer lipid is squarely cosmetic. However, "barrier repair / restores the barrier" sits on a watch-line: widely used as cosmetic language, but "repair" + an implied physiological change can drift toward drug territory if over-asserted — keep it tied to appearance, feel, and support.
  • ✅ Defensible cosmetic language:
  • "Replenishes skin-identical lipids (cholesterol, ceramides, fatty acids) that deplete with age," "helps support and strengthen the look and feel of the skin barrier," "helps lock in moisture / reduce the appearance of moisture loss," "cushions and comforts dry, mature-looking skin," "formulated with a cholesterol-rich, age-tuned lipid ratio."
  • The "aging skin loses cholesterol synthesis" education angle is factually well-supported and a strong, differentiated story — frame it as ingredient/skin biology with citations, not a treatment promise.
  • ⚠️ Risky / drug-like or unsubstantiated — flag for legal/substantiation:
  • "Repairs/heals the skin barrier," "treats eczema/dermatitis/dryness as a condition," "restores your skin's cholesterol levels," "rebuilds collagen," "reverses/erases wrinkles," "boosts collagen by X%." The barrier-recovery data are TEWL kinetics in disrupted skin, and the appearance numbers are manufacturer, regimen-confounded — none supports a hard wrinkle/collagen claim.
  • Any antioxidant / "fights free radicals" claim for cholesterol — false-to-mechanism (cholesterol is an oxidation target; oxysterols are damaging). Do not say it.
  • Any oral/ingestible benefit. None exists (§4 Tier 3).
  • Specific "%" efficacy numbers from the SkinCeuticals study must not be borrowed for our product (different formula) and would need our own substantiation.
  • Bottom line: sell cholesterol as the age-appropriate, skin-identical barrier lipid with a credible "skin makes less of it as you age" education story; route any "repair/anti-aging" wording through substantiation review. See ../../brand/glossary.md and the claims guardrail in ../../CLAUDE.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: LOW–MODERATE. Cholesterol is a commodity sterol; pure cosmetic-grade cholesterol is inexpensive relative to ceramides, peptides, or exotic longevity actives. A cholesterol-dominant blend's cost is driven mostly by the ceramide fraction and the lamellar-emulsion manufacturing, not the cholesterol itself. Good value for a central barrier story.
  • The sourcing decision is the real issue (and a brand-values flag):
  • Lanolin / wool-grease-derived cholesterol — the dominant commercial source; animal-derived, not vegan [16]. Cheapest, widely available.
  • Bovine-derived (legacy) — largely phased out; BSE/prion optics make it unsuitable for a premium modern brand [16].
  • Synthetic / plant-steroid-derived ("identical cholesterol" from phytosterols, e.g., from yam/soy diosgenin routes) — bio-identical to human cholesterol, vegan-compatible, no prion concern. This is the recommended source for a premium, claims-clean, ethically-defensible product, and supports a "skin-identical and cruelty-free" narrative. Confirm with suppliers that it is true cholesterol (not just "phytosterol," which is a different, non-identical sterol).
  • Suppliers: widely available from fine-chemical and cosmetic-lipid houses (e.g., Croda, Evonik, NK/Nippon Fine Chemical, and others that also supply ceramides — convenient for buying the trio together). Low supply risk. Specify vegan/synthetic grade in the spec sheet.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • The hype: marketed loosely as a barrier "rebuilder"/"anti-aging lipid," often lumped under generic "ceramide barrier creams," sometimes with implied antioxidant or collagen benefits, and with finished-product "% improvement" numbers presented as if cholesterol-the-molecule did it.
  • The reality: 1. The strong, true claim is specific and under-told: aging skin's biggest lipid deficit is cholesterol synthesis, and a cholesterol-DOMINANT 3-lipid blend repairs the aged barrier faster than the default ceramide-dominant blend [4][5][6]. That's a genuinely differentiated, primary-literature-backed angle — and most "ceramide" marketing misses it by over-weighting ceramides. 2. But the proof is barrier/TEWL science, not anti-wrinkle science. It's mostly murine + small aged-human acute-recovery studies; the cosmetic-appearance numbers are manufacturer-funded and regimen-confounded [7][8]. 3. Cholesterol is a team molecule. Alone or as a duo it backfires (delays recovery) [2]. "Cholesterol serum" as a hero makes no sense; "cholesterol-weighted barrier cream" does. 4. Cholesterol is NOT an antioxidant — it's an oxidation target that yields pro-aging oxysterols [13][14]. Any antioxidant framing is wrong. 5. No oral/ingestible angle — the action is local epidermal synthesis/replenishment.
  • Honest summary: cholesterol is under-hyped for its real, specific value (the age-appropriate dominant barrier lipid) and at risk of being over-hyped via borrowed wrinkle stats and false antioxidant/collagen framing. The disciplined play is to tell the true and differentiated story (cholesterol-loss-with-age) and resist the false ones.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️→✅ SUPPORTING / CONDITIONAL — but a near-mandatory co-star of the barrier-repair hero, and arguably the signature twist for an aging-skin barrier cream.

  • One-line reasoning: Cholesterol is a skin-identical barrier lipid with excellent primary-literature support for restoring the aged barrier — and uniquely, the evidence says aging skin needs cholesterol weighted up (a cholesterol-dominant 3-lipid ratio out-repairs the default ceramide-dominant blend) — but it only works inside the correct ceramide+FFA blend, its cosmetic anti-aging proof is manufacturer-grade, and it must be antioxidant-protected and ethically sourced (synthetic/plant route); therefore it is the right-hand co-hero of the barrier cream, not a standalone hero.
  • Longevity-relevance note: This is one of the cleanest "skin-longevity" mechanistic stories in the whole ingredient set: a measurable, age-driven decline in a specific endogenous lipid's synthesis (cholesterol, via HMG-CoA reductase) that directly degrades barrier function, replenished by a skin-identical, age-tuned ratio. For a brand whose settled thesis is a premium barrier-repair cream, a cholesterol-DOMINANT ceramide:cholesterol:FFA system is a credible, differentiated, evidence-backed signature — pairs naturally with ceramides (the named hero), free fatty acids, niacinamide (drives endogenous synthesis of all three), and vitamin E (stability + oxysterol defense). Specify a vegan/synthetic cholesterol grade. Log alongside ceramides in ../../ideas/considering.md.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19. Every primary citation [1]–[6], [8], [13], [15] was independently checked against PubMed/journal abstracts; the SkinCeuticals numbers were cross-checked against multiple secondary sources. This dossier was already written with unusual evidence-honesty — the corrections below are refinements, not a teardown.

Overall evidence grade: B− (moderate human-topical, for a narrow endpoint only)

  • Barrier-repair / TEWL benefit in aged skin (cholesterol-dominant 3-lipid blend): solid B — real human-topical data, but acute barrier-recovery kinetics in a very small cohort from a single research school, plus mouse work.
  • Cosmetic "anti-aging" (wrinkle/laxity/radiance) benefit: C/D — manufacturer (SkinCeuticals) pilot study only, regimen-confounded (concurrent SPF 30 + cleanser), not a placebo-controlled cholesterol isolate, not independently replicated.
  • Antioxidant / collagen-building / oral benefit: D / N-A — the dossier correctly says these don't exist; cholesterol is an oxidation target, not an antioxidant.
  • The dossier's own headline grade ("barrier/TEWL = well-proven; cosmetic anti-aging = plausible halo with weak independent proof") is accurate and well-calibrated.

What checked out (no change needed)

  • All six primary studies are real and accurately described: Feingold 1990 [1] (lovastatin blocks barrier recovery, mevalonate/cholesterol rescues — mice); Man 1993 [2] ("blend rule" — incomplete mixes delay recovery); Man 1996 [3] (equimolar = normal, up-to-3× one lipid accelerates; cholesteryl esters can't substitute for free cholesterol); Ghadially 1995 [4] (~30% lower total SC lipids in the aged murine model); Zettersten 1997 [5] (cholesterol-dominant 3:1:1:1 accelerates aged-skin recovery; FFA-dominant delays it — confirmed n≈6 humans ~80 y, TEWL endpoint); Haratake 2000 [6] (topical mevalonate drives cholesterol synthesis in aged mice).
  • SkinCeuticals 2:4:2 numbers verified exactly: n≈55 women 55–75, 8 wk, single/dermatologist-controlled, concurrent cleanser + SPF 30; smoothness ~66% / pore ~44% / laxity ~35% / radiance ~26% / evenness ~20%; lipidomic ceramide +57% / cholesterol +50% / FFA +12%. The dossier's "manufacturer-funded, regimen-confounded, lower-tier" caveat is correct and appropriately weighted. (The published paper is framed as a pilot evaluation — reinforcing its exploratory status.)
  • Molar-ratio math is correct: 2% cer : 4% chol : 2% FFA by weight ≈ 1 : 3 : 2 cer:chol:FFA molar.
  • CIR "safe as used" (1986, reaffirmed 2006) [15] confirmed.
  • Oxysterol "dark side" confirmed: UV raises 25-hydroxycholesterol in keratinocytes and drives differentiation changes/pyroptosis [13]; oxysterols are implicated in photoaging/MMP pathways [14].

Main caveats (what a skeptic should hold onto)

  1. Single-school dependency. Essentially the entire mechanistic + aged-skin case traces to the UCSF Elias/Feingold/Ghadially group. Strong work, but not independently triangulated by rival labs.
  2. Tiny human N. The keystone aged-human result (Zettersten [5]) rests on ~6 subjects, single application, acute tape-strip model — proof of barrier kinetics, not durable cosmetic outcome.
  3. Borrowed-tier risk on appearance claims. The only "looks better" numbers are a manufacturer pilot on a multi-ingredient finished product used with SPF + cleanser. They cannot be attributed to cholesterol-the-molecule and must never be ported to our (different) formula.
  4. Mostly murine for the deeper mechanism. The causal HMG-CoA/lovastatin and mevalonate-rescue work is in mice.
  5. The molecule is double-edged. Real, and the dossier flags it: unprotected cholesterol oxidizes to pro-aging oxysterols.

Claims that were softened (edited in the body above)

  • §3 — "cholesterol synthesis falls the most / drop driven disproportionately by cholesterol" → softened. Ghadially 1995's abstract reports total SC lipid down ~30% in mice with the ceramide:cholesterol:FFA proportions broadly unchanged; the "cholesterol falls most / disproportionately" framing is an interpretation across the literature plus the functional finding (aged skin needs cholesterol weighted up), not a single quantitative measurement. Reworded to "aged skin behaves as if cholesterol-limited," with the ~30% figure tagged as murine.
  • §3 / §4 — "cholesterol-starved" → "cholesterol-limited"; added explicit "TEWL/acute-recovery, not wrinkle" qualifier where the aged-skin benefit is asserted.
  • §3 dark side — "can activate MMPs → collagen breakdown" → "implicated in MMP activation," with citations attached.
  • §4 keystone — Zettersten human cohort made precise (n≈6, ~80 y) and flagged as "very small … single research school."
  • §4 Tier 1 — fixed author-name inconsistency (Feingold 1990 listed "Mao-Qiang M" in-text vs "Man MQ" in Sources; both are the same author, Mao-Qiang Man — standardized to "Man MQ").

Already-correct honesty the dossier deserves credit for

It does not claim cholesterol is an antioxidant, does not invent an oral/ingestible angle, explicitly flags the manufacturer data as lower-tier and regimen-confounded, and explicitly routes "repair/heal/restores cholesterol levels/boosts collagen" wording to substantiation review (§8). No fabricated citations were found. The net verdict — a near-mandatory barrier co-star with a genuinely differentiated "aging skin loses cholesterol" story, but B-grade evidence confined to barrier kinetics, not anti-wrinkle proof — stands.
