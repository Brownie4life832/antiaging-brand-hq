Ingredient: Retinyl Retinoate (INCI: Retinyl Retinoate)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Class: Retinoids & retinoid alternatives · Status: ⬜ considering (leaning ⚠️ conditional — see verdict)

One-line honest summary: A clever, photostable "hybrid" retinoid ester (retinol + retinoic acid) with genuinely promising early human data — but the entire efficacy and tolerability evidence base traces back to one Korean research group in small, short, periorbital-only trials, with no independent replication and no long-term safety data. Real molecule, thin and conflicted evidence.

1. Identity

  • Common / trade names: Retinyl Retinoate; sometimes marketed as "Retinol-8" or "the 8x retinol" (a marketing label, not a measured potency — see §10).
  • INCI: Retinyl Retinoate.
  • CAS: 15498-86-9. [verified] — corroborated by PubChem (CID 10303376; molecular formula C40H56O2, MW ~568.9) and multiple independent chemical suppliers (Santa Cruz Biotechnology, BOC Sciences, ChemicalRegister), not just cosmetic-ingredient databases. [11]
  • Chemistry: A single covalent molecule — the ester of all-trans retinoic acid (RA) and all-trans retinol (ROL). The carboxylic-acid group of retinoic acid is esterified to the hydroxyl of retinol. It is not a blend of two ingredients; it is one hybrid compound. [1]
  • Class: Vitamin A derivative (retinoid). Functionally a retinyl ester / pro-retinoid (like retinyl palmitate or retinyl propionate), but uniquely carrying a retinoid (retinoic-acid) acyl group rather than a fatty acid. [1]
  • Key related forms / siblings in this class: retinol, retinaldehyde (retinal), retinyl palmitate, retinyl propionate, hydroxypinacolone retinoate (HPR / "granactive retinoid"), and prescription tretinoin (retinoic acid). See retinoids.md, bakuchiol.md.

2. Class & mechanism

The "retinoid ladder" frame (essential context). All topical retinoids must ultimately become retinoic acid (RA) to bind nuclear retinoic-acid receptors (RAR/RXR) and switch on gene programs that drive keratinocyte renewal and dermal-matrix remodeling. The classic conversion ladder is: retinyl esters → retinol → retinaldehyde → retinoic acid. Each step down is more potent and generally more irritating. Retinyl esters sit at the gentle/weak end; they must be hydrolyzed and then oxidized twice to reach RA. [12, 13]

Where retinyl retinoate is designed to be different. The design intent (per the inventors) is a molecule that: 1. Carries a retinoic-acid moiety already attached, so that hydrolysis of the ester bond liberates retinoic acid directly (plus retinol) — in principle skipping the slow oxidative steps that bottleneck retinol/retinaldehyde. This is the rationale for claims of higher potency than retinol. [1] 2. Masks the irritating free carboxyl group of retinoic acid behind the ester bond, so the intact molecule is far less irritating than free RA until it is cleaved in situ. This is the rationale for "retinoic-acid–like results with retinol-like tolerability." [1] 3. Is more photostable and thermally stable than retinol, because the conjugated structure resists UV-driven and oxidative degradation that destroys free retinol. [1]

Mechanistic caveat (be honest): This is a plausible prodrug/ester hypothesis, well grounded in retinoid biochemistry — keratinocytes do possess retinyl-ester hydrolases and the RA-synthesis machinery. [13, 14] But the specific in-skin hydrolysis kinetics of retinyl retinoate, how much intact RA vs. retinol it actually liberates, and the in-vivo metabolic fate have not been independently mapped in published human data. The mechanism is inferred from in-vitro receptor/collagen assays plus the parent-molecule biochemistry, not from direct human pharmacokinetics.

In-vitro mechanistic findings (single group, Kim/Jung et al., 2008): In cell assays, retinyl retinoate showed (a) RAR transactivation / stronger inhibition of c-Jun than retinol (c-Jun/AP-1 drives MMP collagen breakdown, so inhibiting it is anti-photoaging), (b) greater procollagen/collagen synthesis than retinol, (c) lower cytotoxicity than retinol, and (d) enhanced thermal and photo-stability. [1] A follow-up (2010) reported it upregulates hyaluronan synthase (HAS2) and CD44 and induces more hyaluronan than other retinoids while being less irritating; that study combined human keratinocyte (in-vitro) work with hairless-mouse (in-vivo animal) data — it is therefore in-vitro + animal, not purely in-vitro, but it is not a human hydration trial. [2]

3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

Hallmark Plausibility / evidence for retinyl retinoate specifically
Collagen / ECM loss Primary target. In-vitro collagen synthesis > retinol [1]; human periorbital wrinkle/roughness improvement [3,4,5]. Strongest claim, but mechanism-level + small human trials.
Photoaging Direct target of the human RCTs (periorbital photoaging/wrinkles) [3,4,5].
Hydration / water-loss Plausible via hyaluronan/HAS2 upregulation shown in vitro + in hairless mice [2]; not measured as TEWL/hydration endpoints in any human trial. Borrowed from cell/animal data — do not state as a human hydration claim.
Pigmentation / tone Class-level retinoid effect (RA normalizes melanocyte/keratinocyte turnover); not a primary measured endpoint for retinyl retinoate. Borrowed from class, weak for this molecule.
Barrier decline Indirect / mixed. Retinoids can transiently impair barrier early (retinization). Lower-irritation profile claimed [2], but no barrier-repair efficacy data.
Oxidative stress Marginal. Greater molecular photo/oxidative stability protects the active; not demonstrated as antioxidant skin protection.
Inflammaging Indirect via c-Jun/AP-1 / MMP suppression in vitro [1]; not measured clinically.
Cellular senescence / mitochondrial decline / glycation (AGEs) / microbiome No evidence specific to retinyl retinoate. Do not claim. (Mild anti–C. acnes activity was noted in the acne study [6], but that is not a microbiome-aging claim.)

4. Evidence review by tier (core of the dossier)

Overarching caveat — read this before the table. Essentially all primary literature on retinyl retinoate originates from one group (Hyungil Jung's lab and collaborators, Yonsei/Sejong, Korea, ~2008–2013), often with commercial ties to the molecule. Sample sizes are tiny (n≈11–24 per study), durations short (8–12 weeks), populations narrow (Korean women, periorbital area), and there is no independent or Western replication and no meta-analysis. Grade everything below accordingly: "promising, unreplicated."

Tier 1 — In vitro

  • Kim H, Kim B, Kim H, Um S, Lee J, Ryoo H, Jung H. "Synthesis and in vitro biological activity of retinyl retinoate, a novel hybrid retinoid derivative." Bioorg Med Chem 2008;16(12):6387–6393. [1]
  • Synthesized via condensation of retinol + retinoic acid. Cell-based assays: RAR activity present; stronger c-Jun inhibition than retinol; superior collagen synthesis vs retinol; lower cytotoxicity than retinol; enhanced thermal + photo stability. Conclusion: candidate cosmetic anti-aging additive.
  • Limitations: in-vitro only; comparator mainly retinol (not retinaldehyde or RA at matched conditions); author group = inventors.
  • Kim JE, Kim B, Kim H, et al. "Retinyl retinoate induces hyaluronan production and less irritation than other retinoids." J Dermatol 2010;37(5):448–454. PMID 20536650. [2]
  • Upregulates HAS2 / hyaluronan and CD44 (human keratinocytes); topical RR boosted hyaluronan staining in hairless mouse skin and raised TEWL less than retinol, retinoic acid and retinaldehyde. So this paper is in-vitro + animal, not purely in-vitro.
  • Limitations: same group; mechanistic + animal, not human clinical irritation/hydration data under real use.

Tier 2 — Animal

  • No standalone animal anti-aging efficacy RCT for retinyl retinoate was located. However, in-vivo hairless-mouse data do exist embedded in the 2010 hyaluronan/irritation paper [2] (hyaluronan staining ↑, TEWL ↑ less than other retinoids). So the animal tier is thin and mechanistic (hydration/irritation surrogate, single group), not strictly absent — but there is no animal wrinkle/photoaging efficacy study. Do not lean on it.

Tier 3 — Human, ORAL

  • None. Retinyl retinoate is a topical cosmetic ingredient; there is no oral human literature, and none is expected or appropriate. (Oral high-dose vitamin A is a known teratogen — relevant only as background for the pregnancy caution in §7.)

Tier 4 — Human, TOPICAL (the tier that matters for the brand)

RCT / controlled split-face trials (all single-group, small):

  1. Kim H, et al. "Improvement in skin wrinkles from the use of photostable retinyl retinoate: a randomized controlled trial." Br J Dermatol 2010;162(3):497–502. PMID 19849696. [3] - Design: two split-face controlled studies; n = 46 total Korean women with periorbital wrinkles. Study 1: n=24, 12 wk, 0.06% retinyl retinoate vs placebo. Study 2: n=22, 8 wk, 0.06% retinyl retinoate vs 0.075% retinol. - Result: retinyl retinoate significantly improved wrinkles/roughness vs placebo and vs retinol (replica/visiometer roughness, investigator + subject assessment). No severe side-effects. - Limitations: small; short; periorbital only; investigator group = inventors; exact effect sizes sparsely reported in abstract.

  2. Kim H, et al. "Retinyl retinoate, a novel hybrid vitamin derivative, improves photoaged skin: a double-blind, randomized-controlled trial." Skin Res Technol 2011;17(3):380–385. [4] - Design: double-blind RCT, n = 11 Korean women >30 with periorbital wrinkles; 0.06% retinyl retinoate cream; assessments q4wk to 12 wk; replicas, visiometer, Dermascan C. - Result: statistically significant wrinkle improvement (P<0.05); reported visual-wrinkle & max-roughness improvement ~22% higher than 0.075% retinol at 12 wk. - Limitations: n=11 is very small; single group; the "22% higher than retinol" figure is the most-quoted stat and rests on this tiny study.

  3. Kim H, et al. "Novel anti-wrinkle effect of cosmeceutical product with new retinyl retinoate microsphere using biodegradable polymer." Skin Res Technol 2012;18(1):70–76. PMID 21443756. [5] - Design: n = 44 Korean women >30, periorbital; 12 wk. Cream A = 3% PLA microsphere encapsulating 2% retinyl retinoate; Cream B = 0.06% free retinyl retinoate. - Result: microsphere cream gave significantly greater wrinkle/roughness improvement (e.g., ~6–8% higher visual-wrinkle & roughness rates by wk 4, P<0.05); PLA microsphere improved permeation of retinyl retinoate. - Limitations: compares two retinyl-retinoate formats (not vs retinol/RA/placebo); confounds active dose with delivery; single group.

  4. Kim H, et al. "Retinyl retinoate, a retinoid derivative improves acne vulgaris in double-blind, vehicle-controlled clinical study." Tissue Eng Regen Med 2012/2013. [6] - Design: double-blind, vehicle-controlled split-face, n = 15 women, mild–moderate facial acne, 8 wk, retinyl retinoate (~0.05%). - Result: significant reduction in inflammatory + non-inflammatory lesions and sebum (P<0.05); mild anti–C. acnes activity; no local side-effects reported. - Limitations: tiny (n=15); single group; acne (not aging) endpoint — relevant to tolerability/turnover story, not anti-aging proof.

What is PROVEN for topical human use vs. borrowed: - Reasonably supported (low-tier human): modest periorbital wrinkle/roughness improvement over 8–12 weeks, outperforming placebo and (in small studies) 0.075% retinol; acceptable short-term tolerability. Caveat: all from one group, small n, unreplicated. - Borrowed from the retinoid class (NOT demonstrated for retinyl retinoate in robust human trials): broad photoaging reversal across the face, firmness, pigmentation/even-tone, "collagen boosting" by a quantified amount, long-term anti-aging. These rely on retinol/tretinoin evidence and mechanistic retinyl-retinoate data, not on RR-specific clinical proof. - Versus the gold standard: there is no head-to-head vs tretinoin, and the only retinol comparisons are tiny. So "as good as / better than retinol" is suggestive at best, and "comparable to tretinoin" is unsupported.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • As a retinyl ester, retinyl retinoate is lipophilic and must penetrate, then be hydrolyzed by skin esterases to release its active retinoid(s); the design claim is that hydrolysis yields retinoic acid directly (faster route to the active receptor ligand than retinol's two oxidation steps). [1, 13, 14]
  • Photostability is a real, repeatedly reported advantage over free retinol (resists UV/oxidative degradation), which improves the delivered dose by keeping the active intact in the jar and on skin. [1]
  • Penetration of the parent molecule is improvable with delivery systems: PLA microsphere encapsulation increased permeation and efficacy vs the free form. [5] Liposomal/nanoparticle delivery for retinoids generally is an active formulation area. [10]
  • Honest gap: no independent human pharmacokinetic / penetration-depth study quantifying how much retinyl retinoate (or its liberated RA/retinol) actually reaches the dermis, or the conversion ratio in vivo. Penetration/conversion is inferred, not measured in published human PK.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration (per the trials): 0.05–0.06% free retinyl retinoate; or ~2% active inside microspheres in a 3% microsphere cream. [3,4,5,6] No dose-ranging beyond these.
  • pH: Retinoids are not acid-dependent; formulate near skin-neutral (~5–6). Avoid extreme pH that could hydrolyze the ester prematurely.
  • Stability: More photo- and thermally stable than retinol — a genuine formulation selling point [1]. Still a vitamin-A derivative: protect from air/light/heat; use opaque, airless packaging + antioxidants as standard retinoid hygiene. Encapsulation further protects and modulates release. [5]
  • Pairings: Compatible with niacinamide, peptides, ceramides, hyaluronic acid (buffering/hydration; HA synergizes with the molecule's own HAS2 upregulation [2]). Best used PM.
  • Conflicts / cautions: Standard retinoid pairing caution — avoid stacking in the same routine with strong AHAs/BHAs, benzoyl peroxide, high-strength L-ascorbic acid to limit irritation; alternate instead.
  • Sensory: Oil-soluble, yellow-orange tint typical of retinoids (can color a formula); manage in pale/clear products.

7. Safety

  • Irritation: Reported lower than retinol / much lower than retinoic acid, with no severe side-effects across the small trials [1,2,3]. But "low irritation" rests on the same small, single-group studies — not on large post-market or independent safety data. Expect typical retinization (dryness/flaking) is possible, especially with the microsphere/higher-active versions.
  • Sun sensitivity: As a retinoid that can liberate retinoic acid, treat as photosensitizing — direct to PM use and pair with daily SPF as the non-negotiable retinoid-routine standard. (The molecule's own photostability protects the ingredient, not the user's skin.)
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: Avoid as a precaution. Topical retinoids broadly are advised against in pregnancy, and retinyl retinoate is explicitly engineered to deliver retinoic acid to skin — the precautionary case is stronger, not weaker, than for inert esters. Use a clear pregnancy caution + "consult your doctor." Oral vitamin A is a known teratogen (background only). [9]
  • Sensitization / allergen: No notable allergen flags identified; synthetic, low fragrance-allergen risk. Long-term sensitization data: absent.
  • Sourcing / sustainability: Synthetic (lab condensation of retinol + retinoic acid); no wild-harvest or biodiversity concern. Sustainability profile is unremarkable (typical synthetic active).

8. Regulatory & claims

  • Cosmetic vs drug line: In the US (FDA), retinyl retinoate in a leave-on cosmetic is a cosmetic ingredient; but because it is built to release retinoic acid (a drug, tretinoin), claims must stay strictly cosmetic/appearance-based. Any wording that implies it acts like/contains prescription retinoic acid, "treats" a condition (incl. acne as a disease), or alters skin structure/function risks reclassification as an unapproved drug. The acne data [6] is interesting internally but must not become an acne-treatment claim in US copy.
  • EU: EU tightened vitamin-A rules (Reg. 2024/996): retinol, retinyl acetate, retinyl palmitate capped at 0.05% RE (body lotion) / 0.3% RE (other leave-on & rinse-off), with mandatory "Contains Vitamin A…" labeling, in force from Nov 2025. [7, 8] Retinyl retinoate is not explicitly named in that list, but it is a vitamin-A derivative and the regulatory direction of travel is total-vitamin-A-exposure control — assume scrutiny, contributes to "retinoid equivalents," and confirm current status with a regulatory consultant before any EU launch. [partly unverified — RR's specific EU classification needs legal confirmation]
  • Safe cosmetic claim language: "helps visibly smooth the look of fine lines," "supports skin's natural renewal," "improves the appearance of uneven texture," "a next-generation, more stable vitamin A derivative," "gentle on skin in studies."
  • Risky / avoid or substantiate: "8x stronger than retinol," "as effective as prescription retinoids/tretinoin," "boosts collagen by X%," "treats acne," "delivers retinoic acid," "clinically proven anti-aging" (the clinical base is too thin/single-source to carry a hard claim). Flag all of these for legal/substantiation review. See ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: High — a specialty, patented-origin synthetic retinoid, niche supply, used at low % but premium-priced; costlier than commodity retinol or retinyl palmitate, and effective use (encapsulation, airless packaging, antioxidant system) adds formulation cost.
  • Suppliers: Niche/specialty actives suppliers (originally Korean-developed; appears in K-beauty and some Western brands — e.g., Verso, Medik8, Medi-Peel formulations per ingredient databases [11]). Verify supplier purity, photostability data, and a current regulatory dossier (esp. for EU vitamin-A rules).
  • Supply consideration: Narrower sourcing base than retinol/HPR; confirm second-source availability before committing it to a hero SKU.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • "8x more potent than retinol" / "Retinol-8": Marketing, not measured potency. The inventors' own framing places its activity between retinol and retinoic acid, not at 8× retinol. The "8" appears to be a brand/label flourish. Do not repeat. [11]
  • "Retinoic-acid results without irritation": Overstated. Mechanistically attractive and supported by small studies, but no head-to-head vs tretinoin exists, and tolerability data are small/single-group.
  • "Clinically proven" anti-aging: Soft-pedal. There are RCTs, which beats many cosmetic actives — but they're tiny (n≈11–46), short (≤12 wk), periorbital-only, Korean-women-only, and all from the inventing group with commercial interest, with zero independent replication. That is "early clinical signal," not "clinically proven."
  • vs retinol: Better stability is well-supported; "better efficacy than retinol" rests mainly on one n=11 study reporting ~22% greater improvement — directionally encouraging, not robust.
  • vs HPR / retinaldehyde / bakuchiol: No direct comparisons exist. Claims that RR is the "best" next-gen retinoid are unsupported.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ CONDITIONAL (supporting at most; not a hero).

One-line reasoning: A scientifically elegant, photostable hybrid retinoid with genuinely promising early human data — but its entire evidence base is small, short, periorbital-only, and from a single commercially-interested group with no independent replication, which is a poor fit for a brand staking its credibility on robust, citable science. For a hero "renewal" active, encapsulated retinol or retinaldehyde (deeper, replicated evidence — see retinoids.md) is the safer flagship; retinyl retinoate is a conditional supporting option / premium point-of-difference if we can independently verify supplier data, hold strict cosmetic claims, and are comfortable leaning on thin literature.

Longevity-relevance note: Mechanistically on-thesis for "repair & renew" — it engages the RAR pathway and (in vitro) drives collagen and hyaluronan, hitting collagen/ECM loss, photoaging, and hydration hallmarks. But "skin-longevity" framing should be cautious: there is no senescence, mitochondrial, or long-term-aging data for this specific molecule, only short-term wrinkle endpoints. Its longevity story is retinoid-class plausibility, not RR-specific proof.

Log in ../../ideas/considering.md as a conditional/supporting retinoid option; revisit if independent replication or larger trials appear.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19 by adversarial fact-check of every headline claim against external sources (PubMed, journal pages, PubChem, EUR-Lex). This section records what survived scrutiny, what was corrected, and what must still be softened in copy.

Overall evidence grade: C — weak / indirect (but real human-topical signal)

Rationale: the efficacy claims are based on human, topical data (not quietly borrowed from oral or animal work) — which would normally push toward B — but the human evidence is so small, short, narrow, unreplicated, and conflicted that it cannot bear a confident "moderate human-topical" grade. It is a genuine early clinical signal, not proof. Grade C, sitting just above D because real RCTs (not just in-vitro) do exist and outperformed both placebo and retinol.

  • Why not A/B: No large, independent, or Western trial; no head-to-head vs the gold standard (tretinoin); pivotal efficacy figures rest on an n=11 study; total human n across all anti-aging trials ≈ 81 (46 + 11 + 44 microsphere, the last comparing RR-vs-RR only).
  • Why not D: The molecule is real (CAS 15498-86-9, verified via PubChem + suppliers), the chemistry is sound, and the human wrinkle/roughness improvements over placebo and retinol are real, statistically significant, and topical — this is not pure hype.

Citation integrity — what I checked and found

All six primary citations [1–6] are REAL and say what the dossier claims. Specifically confirmed against external sources: - [1] Bioorg Med Chem 2008;16(12):6387–6393 — exists; in-vitro only; condensation synthesis, photostability, collagen > retinol. ✔ - [2] J Dermatol 2010;37(5):448–454, PMID 20536650 — exists. Correction applied: this paper is in-vitro + hairless-mouse (animal), not purely in-vitro as originally implied. The hyaluronan/HAS2/CD44 and lower-TEWL findings include mouse data; still no human hydration/irritation endpoint. Dossier §2, §3, and Tiers 1–2 updated to reflect this. - [3] Br J Dermatol 2010;162(3):497–502, PMID 19849696 — exists; n=46 (24 vs placebo / 22 vs 0.075% retinol); 8–12 wk; periorbital; significant improvement, no severe side-effects. ✔ (DOI 10.1111/j.1365-2133.2009.09483.x.) - [4] Skin Res Technol 2011;17(3):380–385 — exists; n=11; the "~22% higher than 0.075% retinol at 12 wk" figure is quoted verbatim in the source. ✔ The dossier already flags this as resting on a tiny study; that flag is correct and must stay. - [5] Skin Res Technol 2012;18(1):70–76, PMID 21443756 — exists; n=44; 3% PLA microsphere (2% RR) vs 0.06% free RR; ~6–8% higher at wk 4; RR-vs-RR only, no retinol/placebo/RA arm. ✔ - [6] Tissue Eng Regen Med 2013;10(5):260–265 — exists; n=15; double-blind, vehicle-controlled, split-face; 8 wk; ~0.05%; significant lesion + sebum reduction. ✔ Acne, not aging — already correctly fenced off from anti-aging claims.

RR is not named in the Annex — the dossier's "assume scrutiny, confirm with a consultant" stance is the correct, honest reading. ✔

Main caveats (the load-bearing ones)

  1. Single-source evidence base. Every primary study traces to one Korean group (Kim / H. Jung, Yonsei/Sejong, ~2008–2013) with commercial ties to the molecule. This is the single most important caveat. Independent and Western replication: none found. A 2025 photoaging network meta-analysis merely cites the existing Kim RCT — it is not new evidence.
  2. Tiny, short, narrow human data. n≈11–46/study; ≤12 weeks; periorbital only; Korean women only. No full-face, no diverse skin types/ages, no long-term (>12 wk) data.
  3. No gold-standard comparison. Zero head-to-head vs tretinoin. Retinol comparisons are tiny (the key one is n=11).
  4. Mechanism is inferred, not measured in humans. No published human pharmacokinetics: how much intact retinoic acid vs retinol RR actually liberates in skin, and at what depth, is unmeasured. The "delivers retinoic acid directly" story is a plausible prodrug hypothesis, not a demonstrated in-vivo fact.
  5. Safety/tolerability rests on the same small studies. "Low irritation" and "no severe side-effects" come from the same tiny single-group trials — no large post-market or independent long-term safety data; no sensitization data.
  6. Hydration/hyaluronan is cell+mouse, not human. Do not present HA/hydration benefits as clinically demonstrated in people.

Claims that should be softened or avoided (copy guardrails)

These survived the original dossier's own §10 and are reaffirmed/extended here — treat as hard guardrails: - "8x stronger than retinol" / "Retinol-8" — marketing label, not measured potency. Do not repeat. (Inventors place activity between retinol and RA.) - "Delivers retinoic acid" / "retinoic-acid results" — overstated and regulatorily risky (implies drug action); in-vivo conversion is unmeasured. Avoid. - "Clinically proven anti-aging" — downgrade to "early clinical signal" or "shown in small early studies." The base is too thin/single-source for "proven." - "Better than / as good as retinol" — at most "comparable to or modestly better than retinol in small studies." The efficacy edge rests largely on one n=11 trial. - "Comparable to prescription tretinoin" — unsupported (no head-to-head). Do not claim. - Hydration / "boosts hyaluronic acid" — only cell + mouse data; do not state as a human benefit. - Acne / "clears breakouts" — real data exist [6] but this is a drug claim in the US; keep strictly out of cosmetic copy. - Any specific "+X% collagen" figure — in-vitro only; not a human-measured collagen quantum. Avoid numeric collagen claims.

Net for the brand

The dossier was already unusually honest and its ⚠️ CONDITIONAL (supporting, not hero) verdict is endorsed. Nothing in the fact-check upgrades the molecule; the corrections (animal data in [2], CAS now verified) are minor and do not change the strategic call. For a credibility-first, science-led brand, encapsulated retinol or retinaldehyde remains the safer flagship; retinyl retinoate stays a premium point-of-difference supporting option, only if supplier data are independently verified, claims stay strictly cosmetic, and the brand is comfortable that its science story for this active leans on thin, single-source literature. Revisit and re-grade upward only if independent or larger trials appear.
