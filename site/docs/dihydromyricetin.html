Ingredient: Dihydromyricetin (INCI: Dihydromyricetin)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Anti-glycation & related (plant flavanonol; RAGE antagonist + DNMT1 / epigenetic-clock modulator + antioxidant) Also known as: Ampelopsin, DHM (DMY), the active of "vine tea" (Ampelopsis grossedentata)

One-line honest summary: Dihydromyricetin (DHM/ampelopsin) is unusually interesting for a longevity brand because it hits aging from two well-publicized angles at once — it is a measured RAGE antagonist / anti-glycation flavonoid AND a DNA-methylation ("epigenetic clock") modulator with the rare distinction of actual human-topical mechanistic data (suction-blister epidermis, 0.15%) plus a 2026 in-use clinical study. BUT: the strongest skin-aging evidence is animal + cell + ex-vivo, the one in-use human trial was a 60-person, single-cohort, no-vehicle-control study of a multi-ingredient serum (so DHM cannot be isolated), and DHM has a notorious bioavailability/penetration problem. Genuinely promising and on-trend; not yet a clean topical-proven hero.

1. Identity

  • Common names: Dihydromyricetin; Ampelopsin; DHM; DMY. ("Ampelopsin" and "dihydromyricetin" are the same molecule.)
  • INCI: Dihydromyricetin (the cosmetic INCI is the molecule name itself). When delivered as a botanical extract it may also appear under Ampelopsis Grossedentata Leaf/Stem Extract (vine-tea extract standardized to DHM). [9][10][14]
  • CAS: 27200-12-0
  • Molecular formula / weight: C₁₅H₁₂O₈ · ~320.25 g/mol. [7][8]
  • IUPAC / structure: (2R,3R)-3,5,7-trihydroxy-2-(3,4,5-trihydroxyphenyl)-2,3-dihydro-4H-chromen-4-one — i.e., the dihydro (flavanonol) form of myricetin, with an extra pyrogallol (3,4,5-trihydroxyphenyl) B-ring. The pyrogallol B-ring + multiple hydroxyls are why it is such a strong antioxidant and metal/dicarbonyl chelator (relevant to anti-glycation). [7][8]
  • Chemical class: Flavanonol (a flavonoid sub-class; the saturated 2,3 form of the flavonol myricetin).
  • Physical/chemistry notes: Pale-yellow crystalline solid. Poorly water-soluble (~0.2 mg/mL); classified as a BCS Class IV compound (low solubility + low permeability). Stable in acidic and neutral aqueous solution; degrades/oxidizes irreversibly on prolonged heating above ~80–100 °C. Phenolic, so prone to oxidative browning/discoloration in formula — a real stability watch-item. [7][8][13][15]
  • Natural source: Predominantly the leaves/stems of vine tea (Ampelopsis grossedentata) — a Chinese herbal tea ("Teng Cha," "Moyeam") that can be 20–40%+ DHM by dry weight, making it an unusually concentrated single-flavonoid botanical. Also found in Hovenia dulcis (Japanese raisin tree). [12][13]
  • Key forms/derivatives: Purified DHM (≥98% HPLC, supplement/cosmetic grade from vine tea); vine-tea extract standardized to DHM; and a research zoo of delivery-enhanced forms (self-emulsifying systems, nanoparticles, cocrystals, salt cocrystals, phospholipid complexes) developed specifically because plain DHM absorbs/penetrates poorly. [15][16]

2. Class & mechanism (how it works at the skin/cell level)

DHM is a multi-target flavonoid. For a skin-longevity brand the three mechanistic stories that matter are:

A. RAGE antagonism / anti-glycation (the headline for this class). - RAGE (Receptor for AGEs; gene AGER) is the cell-surface receptor that AGEs bind to trigger downstream NF-κB inflammation, oxidative stress, and senescence — a core driver of "glycation aging" / "sugar sag" in skin. - DHM binds RAGE directly. In surface-plasmon-resonance (SPR), DHM bound RAGE with K_D = 28.7 µM, tighter than the well-known reference RAGE inhibitor FPS-ZM1 (K_D = 40.7 µM); molecular docking gave DHM −9.4 vs FPS-ZM1 −9.1 kcal/mol in the same ligand-binding pocket. [1] - Functionally, DHM down-regulates AGER/RAGE expression and suppresses AGE-RAGE signaling, which in turn lowers NF-κB-driven inflammation. This AGE-RAGE-suppressing action is independently corroborated in non-skin tissue (hippocampus): DHM lowered AGEs, RAGE, IL-1β, IL-6 and TNF-α. [1][3] - As a polyphenol with a pyrogallol B-ring, DHM also plausibly does the "classic" flavonoid anti-glycation tricks (scavenging reactive dicarbonyls like methylglyoxal, chelating metals, blocking cross-link formation) — strongly indicated for the myricetin/flavonoid family, with DHM specifically shown to act through the RAGE/receptor arm. [1][17]

B. Epigenetic-clock / DNA-methylation modulation (the genuinely novel, on-trend angle). - DHM was identified by high-throughput screening of ~1,800 natural compounds + 640 FDA drugs as a direct inhibitor of DNMT1 (the maintenance DNA methyltransferase) — IC50 in the same range as myricetin (~tens of µM). [2] - Aging skin accumulates age-dependent DNA hypermethylation that silences genes needed for keratinocyte vitality. By inhibiting DNMT1, DHM re-activates age-silenced genes and lowers the DNA-methylation "biological age" of skin cells/epidermis on validated skin epigenetic clocks. [2][4] - This is the mechanism behind the "reverses skin age by X years" marketing — it is real and measured, but it is a methylation-age readout, not a clinical wrinkle cure (see §4 honesty notes).

C. Antioxidant / anti-inflammatory / anti-photoaging (the broad flavonoid base layer). - Strong direct ROS scavenger; in skin-aging models DHM reduces oxidative stress and neuro/inflammation, and across the flavonoid literature this class up-regulates Nrf2 antioxidant defense and down-regulates MMP-1 (the collagen-degrading enzyme) and pro-inflammatory cytokines under UV stress. DHM specifically (oral, with ellagic acid) reduced UVB erythema, MMP-1 and inflammatory cytokines in mice via TGF-β1/Wnt. [5][6][11] - DHM also suppresses inflammation via IKKβ/NF-κB inhibition in macrophages. [17]

Skin-relevant translation (honest): the biochemistry (RAGE binding, DNMT1 inhibition, antioxidant) is well-demonstrated. The skin-aging read-through is shown mostly in rat, mouse, cell, 3D-model and ex-vivo human epidermis systems. Whether a real topical product dose meaningfully engages RAGE and DNMT1 in living human dermis at scale is suggested but not cleanly proven — and is undercut by DHM's poor penetration.

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

The single most important honesty point: DHM is one of the better-evidenced anti-glycation/epigenetic actives for the format — it actually has human topical/ex-vivo mechanistic data, which spermidine and many "longevity molecules" lack. BUT the only in-use clinical study (2026) is a 60-person, single-cohort study with NO vehicle/placebo control of a multi-ingredient serum (DHM + hyaluronic acid + saponin + enoxolone + antioxidants), so you cannot attribute the clinical result to DHM specifically, and the headline "reverses skin age 2+ years" is a DNA-methylation-age number, not a clinical efficacy endpoint.

Tier 1 — In vitro (cell, biochemical, 3D models)

  • Falckenhayn et al., 2024, Frontiers in Aging (DOI 10.3389/fragi.2023.1258184; PMID 38500495) — discovery + mechanism. Screened ~1,800 natural compounds + 640 FDA drugs; identified DHM as a DNMT1 inhibitor. In primary human keratinocytes (13 donors for methylation; 6 for expression) and 3D skin equivalents, 20 µM DHM lowered DNA-methylation age and re-activated age-silenced genes; in 3D models with aged fibroblasts DHM significantly increased epidermal thickness (p<0.05), reversing age-associated epidermal thinning. Limitation: cell/3D doses (20 µM) are direct culture exposure, not transdermal; donor n small. [2]
  • Wang et al., 2025, Nutrients 17(11):1862 (DOI 10.3390/nu17111862) — in-vitro arm. In AGE-stressed human skin fibroblasts (HFF-1; AGEs 200 µg/mL), DHM 12.5–50 µM reduced senescence (↓SA-β-Gal, ↓SAHF, ↓p16/p21), restored proliferation ~1.5× (Ki67), and transcriptomics showed ↓AGER, ↑cell-cycle/DNA-repair genes. SPR: DHM–RAGE K_D = 28.7 µM (vs FPS-ZM1 40.7 µM); docking −9.4 kcal/mol. Limitation: cell model + computational binding; n≈3–6 replicates; downstream RAGE pathway not fully mapped. [1]
  • Flavonoid/myricetin-family anti-glycation & Nrf2 work (supportive, not DHM-specific). Myricetin and derivatives inhibit non-enzymatic glycation by trapping dicarbonyls, protecting glycation sites and blocking cross-links; flavonoids up-regulate Nrf2 and suppress UV-induced MMP-1 in dermal fibroblasts/keratinocytes. Limitation: class-level / analog data, not DHM itself. [11][17]

Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

  • Wang et al., 2025, Nutrients — in-vivo arm. D-galactose-induced skin-aging model in male Sprague-Dawley rats (n=8/group, 5 groups). Oral DHM (gavage 50/100/300 mg/kg) — moderate dose (100 mg/kg) best — significantly improved skin elasticity (shortened retraction time, ↑viscoelasticity), preserved/increased collagen, and visibly improved wrinkles by dermascopy. Limitation: rat, oral route, D-gal is an accelerated/artificial aging model, wrinkles not quantified, no topical arm. [1]
  • Moon, Kang & Park (J. Photochem. Photobiol. B 2018, 178:92–100; DOI 10.1016/j.jphotobiol.2017.11.004) — photoaging. Oral DHM + ellagic acid synergistically protected mouse dorsal skin from UVB: ↓erythema, ↓pro-inflammatory cytokines, ↓MMP-1, via TGF-β1 and Wnt activation; the combination beat either alone. Limitation: mouse, oral, combination (can't fully isolate DHM), acute UVB not chronic aging. [6]
  • DHM, D-galactose brain-aging mouse model (Brain Res Bull / related). Oral DHM reduced oxidative stress, neuroinflammation and measured AGEs (ELISA) in aged-mouse brain — corroborates the systemic anti-glycation/antioxidant action, though it is brain, not skin. [11]
  • DHM + AGE-RAGE in CORT-treated mice, 2022, Cells 11(23):3730 (DOI 10.3390/cells11233730). DHM lowered hippocampal AGEs, RAGE, IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α and depression-like behavior — independent confirmation that DHM inactivates the AGE-RAGE axis in vivo (non-skin tissue). [3]

Tier 3 — Human ORAL

  • No dedicated human oral RCT for skin endpoints found. DHM's human clinical literature is dominated by anti-alcohol / hepatoprotective / metabolic uses (it is sold as a hangover/liver supplement), not skin. Treat oral skin benefit as unestablished in humans. [12][15] (Absence of evidence — important to state plainly.)

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters for a skincare brand)

  • Falckenhayn et al., 2024, Frontiers in Aging — human topical/ex-vivo arms (the strongest mechanistic human-topical data).
  • Lower-back study, n=30 women: topical 0.15% DHM (w/w) oil-in-water emulsion vs vehicle for 2 weeks, then low-dose UV; epidermis sampled by suction blister 24 h later — the arm in which DNA-methylation (Infinium array) was profiled and a ~2-year reduction in epidermal methylation age (Skin & Blood / epidermis clocks, p<0.05) was seen.
  • Forearm study, n=19 women (ages 50–65): 8-week topical treatment, suction-blister sampling, qRT-PCR of 34 age-silencing marker genes — DHM re-activated (↑mRNA) the tested marker genes vs vehicle, i.e., gene reactivation in living human skin (this is the arm the "34 genes" figure comes from, not the lower-back arm).
  • Safety: NGRA (next-generation risk assessment) concluded DHM is safe up to 0.15% topical, low acute/repeat-dose tox, not phototoxic.
  • Limitations: endpoints are molecular (gene expression / methylation), not clinical photographs/wrinkle scoring; women-only; short; the in-vivo arms validate a biomarker response, not a visible-aging outcome. [2]
  • Beiersdorf/DKFZ, 2026, Dermatology and Therapy (DOI 10.1007/s13555-026-01764-4) — the only in-use clinical study. Prospective, single-cohort (plus a 17-donor methylome pilot). n=60, ages ~40–70, Fitzpatrick I–VI (multi-ethnic Brazilian cohort), twice-daily for 8 weeks of a DHM-containing serum (code E004079A-01). Reported: epigenetic (DNA-methylation) skin age ↓ ~2.1 years, ~40% of subjects ≥5-year reduction, wrinkle visibility −13.9%, dermal density +10.4%.
  • Critical caveats: (a) NO placebo/vehicle control — the forum/peer critique flags exactly this: the same emulsion without DHM was not run, so regression-to-mean, the base formula, and the other actives are not controlled for; (b) the serum is multi-ingredient — dual-MW hyaluronic acid (52 kDa + 2000 kDa), bioactive glycine saponin, enoxolone (glycyrrhetinic acid), and unspecified antioxidants — so the clinical and even epigenetic improvements cannot be attributed to DHM alone; (c) single-cohort, single-center, 8 weeks; (d) funded by Beiersdorf (the manufacturer; the serum corresponds to a Eucerin "Epigenetics"-type launch), a clear conflict to disclose. This study supports a product, not the ingredient in isolation. [4]

Bottom line by tier: Proven for DHM the molecule → RAGE binding/anti-glycation (SPR/cell/animal), DNMT1 inhibition + epigenetic-age reduction (cell/3D/ex-vivo human), antioxidant/anti-inflammatory (broad). Demonstrated in humans topically → only molecular biomarker responses at 0.15% (gene reactivation, methylation age), plus one uncontrolled multi-ingredient in-use study with modest clinical numbers. Not yet proven → a clean, vehicle-controlled, DHM-attributable clinical anti-wrinkle/firming outcome.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • The central problem. DHM is a BCS Class IV molecule: poorly water-soluble (~0.2 mg/mL) and poorly permeable. Orally its absolute bioavailability is only ~4% (low solubility, instability, P-gp efflux, heavy gut–liver first-pass). The entire DHM delivery field (self-emulsifying systems, nanoparticles, cocrystals) exists because getting DHM into tissue is hard. [15][16]
  • For topical: MW ~320 is fine for penetration on size grounds, and moderate lipophilicity helps somewhat — but high polyphenol H-bonding + poor solubility work against partitioning through the stratum corneum, and DHM can oxidize at the surface. The Falckenhayn human-topical arms did show a molecular response in viable epidermis at 0.15%, which is encouraging evidence that enough gets in to move biomarkers — but there are no published quantitative human skin-penetration/depth data, and dermal (fibroblast-level) delivery is unproven. [2][15]
  • Practical read: penetration to the epidermis is plausible and weakly supported (biomarker data); reaching the dermis meaningfully is unproven. A serious formulation would likely need solubilization/delivery help (e.g., glycol/ethanol systems, encapsulation, or a vine-tea extract pre-solubilized matrix), which also helps stability.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: The only human-validated topical level is 0.15%, both for the mechanistic gene-reactivation data and as the NGRA safety ceiling assessed. Cosmetic databases note typical inclusion is very low (~0.01% in many current products). Use 0.15% as the evidence-anchored target; do not imply a validated dose-response above that. In-vitro actives were 12.5–50 µM (cells) and 20 µM (keratinocytes) — culture doses, not formulation guidance. [1][2][9]
  • pH: Most stable in acidic-to-neutral aqueous solution — convenient, since it sits comfortably in a typical serum pH (~5–6) and is compatible with the mildly acidic acid mantle. [13]
  • Stability (the real watch-item): A polyphenol with a pyrogallol ring → prone to oxidation and yellow/brown discoloration, accelerated by heat (degrades above ~80–100 °C), light, alkaline pH, and metal ions. Mitigate with: cool processing, chelators (e.g., EDTA/phytate), co-antioxidants, low-O₂/opaque airless packaging, and challenge/stability + color testing. Limited public finished-formula stability data — treat as a known unknown and test. [7][13]
  • Pairings (conceptual): Other anti-glycation / antioxidant actives (carnosine, niacinamide, vitamin C — though watch combined oxidation), hyaluronic acid (the 2026 serum did this), soothing agents like enoxolone/saponins (also in that serum), and barrier lipids/ceramides (brand thesis). Conceptually complements retinoids (turnover) and SPF (the one proven anti-photoaging step) for a glycation+epigenetic story.
  • Conflicts: Watch oxidative incompatibilities — co-formulating multiple oxidation-prone phenols/ascorbic acid can accelerate browning; alkaline systems and unchelated metal ions degrade it.
  • Sensory: Purified DHM at 0.15% should be low-impact; vine-tea extract forms can carry slight yellow color and herbal note. Discoloration over shelf life is the main sensory risk.

7. Safety

  • Irritation/sensitization: NGRA safety assessment supports topical use up to 0.15%, with low acute/repeat-dose toxicity and no cytotoxicity at in-use levels. No published human topical irritation/sensitization signal at cosmetic concentrations; SkinSafe/EWG-type databases list it without notable allergy flags. Caveat: no large published HRIPT/patch-test dataset — generate our own finished-formula testing. [2][9][10][14]
  • Sun sensitivity: Not phototoxic in testing; mechanistically antioxidant/photoprotective. No human topical photo-irritation data beyond the NGRA conclusion. [2][6]
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: No specific topical pregnancy data. DHM is widely consumed as vine tea/hangover supplement with a strong general safety reputation, but default to conservative copy ("consult your doctor") for a topical anti-aging product.
  • General tolerability: DHM is regarded in the pharmacology literature as highly bioactive and essentially low-toxicity / good safety (a major reason it's an attractive nutraceutical). [15][17]
  • Sourcing/allergen: Vine-tea-derived DHM is a botanical extract — disclose botanical source; standardization varies. Purified ≥98% DHM minimizes botanical-allergen ambiguity.
  • Hair-growth-inhibitor nuance (important market quirk): DHM is currently used in commercial cosmetics largely as a hair-growth inhibitor active (e.g., The Chemistry Brand "Inhibitif" line; patents on "treating hair loss and delaying aging of skin"). For a facial anti-aging serum this is not a safety problem, but it is a brand-positioning consideration — the molecule's existing consumer footprint is "slows hair regrowth," not "anti-aging hero." [10][18]

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic ingredient status: Listed as INCI Dihydromyricetin (CAS 27200-12-0), function skin conditioning (and antioxidant in practice); present in marketed EU/US cosmetics; COSMILE Europe / INCIBeauty / SpecialChem entries exist. NGRA supports up to 0.15%. Confirm legality and any restriction per target market before formulating. [9][10][14]
  • The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame — the central trap here): the most compelling DHM stories are exactly the ones that cross into structure/function (drug) territory if stated literally. "Reverses your skin's epigenetic age," "reverses aging," "inhibits DNMT1 in your skin," "blocks the RAGE receptor," "delays cellular senescence," "increases collagen by X%" are all drug-like / require substantiation or legal review — and importing the animal/oral/biomarker data into a topical visible-aging claim is the specific overreach to avoid.
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "antioxidant flavonoid from vine tea," "inspired by glycation and epigenetic skin-aging science," "helps defend skin against the look of glycation / 'sugar-stressed' dullness," "helps improve the appearance of skin firmness and even tone," "visibly smoother, more youthful-looking skin." Even these should be hedged, since the clean clinical proof for DHM alone is not there yet.
  • ⚠️ Risky / substantiation-or-legal-review: any "epigenetic age reversal in X years," "anti-glycation cure," "rejuvenates at the DNA level," "boosts collagen," or claims leaning on the 2026 study as if it isolated DHM (it didn't — multi-ingredient, no control).
  • Substantiation reality: To make even a modest DHM-specific topical performance claim, the honest path is our own vehicle-controlled finished-formula human testing; the published literature substantiates mechanism and a product, not the ingredient alone clinically. See ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Forms/suppliers: Purified DHM ≥98% (HPLC) is widely available from vine-tea-extract suppliers (e.g., Ingredients Online and numerous Chinese botanical manufacturers; analytical grade from Sigma-Aldrich/Cayman/BOC Sciences). Cosmetic supply rides the vine-tea / hangover-supplement boom, so raw material is reasonably accessible. [7][8][12]
  • Cost tier: Low-to-mid as a raw flavonoid (vine tea is a concentrated, cheap source). Delivery-enhanced forms (encapsulated, cocrystal, nano) and any proprietary "epigenetic" branded actives cost meaningfully more.
  • Supply/standardization risk: Botanical extracts vary in DHM content and color — require a COA with quantified DHM (% by HPLC) and a defined color/purity spec; specify ≥98% if avoiding botanical variability and discoloration.

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • "Reverses your skin's epigenetic age by 2+ years." This is a DNA-methylation-clock number from a 60-person, single-cohort, no-vehicle-control study of a multi-ingredient serum funded by the manufacturer. It is real data, but it is not a controlled, DHM-attributable, clinical-aging result. [4]
  • The format/route switch. The cleanest skin efficacy (elasticity, collagen, anti-UVB) is from oral DHM in rats/mice, often in combination (with ellagic acid) and in artificial aging (D-gal/UVB) models — not topical human chronological aging. [1][6]
  • "It blocks RAGE / stops glycation in your skin." The RAGE binding (K_D 28.7 µM) and ↓AGER are real in SPR/cell/rat — but engaging RAGE meaningfully in living human dermis via a topical, given DHM's poor penetration, is unproven. [1][15]
  • "Ingredient-level" vs "product-level" proof. The two human-topical data points either measure biomarkers (gene/methylation, Falckenhayn) or test a whole product (Beiersdorf 2026). Neither delivers a clean "DHM serum visibly reduces wrinkles vs vehicle" RCT.
  • The penetration elephant. ~4% oral bioavailability and BCS-IV solubility mean delivery is the hard part; "contains DHM" ≠ "delivers active DHM to where aging happens."
  • The hair-inhibitor irony. The molecule's biggest existing consumer footprint is slowing hair regrowth, not anti-aging — a reminder that its skincare identity is still being written. [10][18]

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ SUPPORTING — and an unusually strong narrative + on-trend candidate, with real but not-yet-clinically-clean topical evidence. One-line: Dihydromyricetin is a rare two-in-one anti-glycation (RAGE) + epigenetic-clock flavonoid that already has human-topical mechanistic data and a (manufacturer-funded, uncontrolled) 2026 in-use clinical study — making it a credible, differentiated supporting active and education centerpiece for a skin-longevity brand, provided copy stays mechanism-framed and avoids the "epigenetic age reversal" overreach until we have our own vehicle-controlled proof.

Why supporting, not hero (yet): the strongest efficacy data are animal/cell/biomarker; the lone in-use human trial can't isolate DHM and had no control; and DHM's penetration/stability are genuine engineering hurdles. Those are the exact gaps between "promising ingredient" and "proven topical hero."

Path to hero: (1) formulate at the evidence-anchored 0.15% with a solubilization/delivery system and oxidation control; (2) pair it into the barrier-repair/anti-glycation story (HA, niacinamide, ceramides, SPF); (3) commission our own vehicle-controlled human study measuring both a skin epigenetic clock and instrumental skin endpoints — DHM is one of the few "longevity" actives where that study is realistically powered to succeed.

Longevity-relevance note: Among anti-aging actives, DHM is exceptionally well-aligned to a skin-longevity thesis: it speaks fluently to two of the hottest longevity vocabularies at once — glycation/AGEs/RAGE and epigenetic clocks — and unlike most "longevity molecules" it has begun the topical-human translation. That makes it a premium differentiation/education asset now and a plausible hero later if we invest in delivery + our own trial.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verification pass: 2026-06-19. Method: re-read every headline claim; pulled the primary papers ([1] Wang 2025 Nutrients via PMC12156997, [2] Falckenhayn 2024 via PMC10944877, [4] Beiersdorf 2026 via Springer/abstracts, [6] photoaging via DOI/Elsevier) and checked, for each, whether the evidence is HUMAN+TOPICAL vs borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral; whether the cited study is real and says what's claimed; and who funded it.

Overall grade: C (weak / indirect for a topical efficacy claim — strong mechanism, thin human-topical proof)

DHM is genuinely better-evidenced for the format than most "longevity molecules," but it does not clear a B. The bar for B (moderate human-topical efficacy) requires a controlled human-topical study with a clinical or near-clinical endpoint attributable to the ingredient. What exists is: (i) robust mechanism (RAGE binding, DNMT1 inhibition) from SPR/cell/animal; (ii) two human-topical studies that read out only molecular biomarkers (methylation age, gene mRNA) at 0.15% — no controlled clinical wrinkle/firming endpoint; and (iii) one in-use clinical study that is uncontrolled, single-cohort, multi-ingredient, and manufacturer-funded, so it proves a product, not the ingredient. That combination is, honestly, a high C / borderline C+ — strong narrative, real human-topical mechanistic data, but no clean topical efficacy proof.

  • Citation [6] metadata was wrong. Dossier listed "Wang R. et al. (2017), DOI 10.1016/j.jphotobiol.2017.10.030, pp. 53–63" and an in-text "Yang/Wu et al." The study is real and says what the dossier claims — only the author/year/DOI/URL were faulty. Corrected in §4, §sources.
  • All other cited primary studies ([1][2][3][4]) are real, retrievable, and accurately characterized (numbers checked verbatim against source).

Main caveats (the load-bearing ones)

  1. No vehicle-controlled, DHM-attributable clinical efficacy exists. The only in-use clinical study has no control and tests a multi-ingredient serum — regression-to-mean and the other actives (HA, saponin, enoxolone, antioxidants) are uncontrolled.
  2. Route/format laundering risk. The cleanest efficacy (elasticity, collagen, anti-UVB) is oral, in animals, often in combination — it must not be read across to a topical human claim.
  3. Industry funding on the two flagship human-topical sources. Both [2] and [4] are Beiersdorf-authored/funded; the strongest non-industry evidence ([1]) is animal+cell. (Worth noting in the brand's favor: the RAGE paper [1] is academically funded, so the mechanism story is not solely industry-driven.)
  4. Penetration is unproven beyond the epidermis. Biomarkers move at 0.15% in viable epidermis, but no quantitative human skin-penetration data exist and dermal delivery is unproven (BCS-IV, ~4% oral BA).

Claims to soften / avoid in copy

  • Drop or heavily hedge "reverses skin's epigenetic age by ~2 years" — it is an uncontrolled, multi-ingredient, manufacturer-funded number and a methylation readout, not a clinical outcome.
  • Do not state or imply "blocks RAGE / stops glycation in your skin" as an in-use effect — it is SPR/cell/rat, not proven in living human dermis.
  • Do not import "increases collagen / reduces wrinkles" from the oral animal data into a topical claim.
  • Frame DHM as a mechanism-led, on-trend supporting/education active, not a clinically proven topical hero — consistent with the dossier's existing §11 verdict (which is appropriately hedged and survives verification).

Net: the dossier's own honesty framing is accurate and, if anything, slightly generous on tone; the analysis grades out to C. One citation had to be corrected and one study-arm attribution fixed; no claim was found to be fabricated.
