Ingredient: Tomatidine (INCI: Tomatidine)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ⬜ considering · ⬜ shortlisted · ☑ rejected (skip — see verdict; revisit only if real topical data appears) · ⬜ in a product Class: Autophagy & mitochondrial-targeted actives · Slug: tomatidine

One-line honesty flag up front: Tomatidine has a genuinely interesting longevity mechanism (mitophagy + Nrf2 + senomorphic SASP-suppression) and real, repeated evidence in C. elegans, mice, and human muscle myobundles — but there is essentially no published study of tomatidine applied topically to skin, and no human skin trial of any kind. The lone "skin" data point is a 2026 mouse paper where oral/systemic tomatidine reduced senescence markers in skin tissue among many organs. Every "boosts collagen / firms skin" supplier claim is marketing, not data. Treat this as a mechanistically attractive but, for a cream, unproven active.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Tomatidine (TOM). Also "tomatidine A."
  • INCI: Tomatidine. (Listed by cosmetic-raw-material suppliers; note INCI listing ≠ efficacy evidence.)
  • Chemistry: A steroidal alkaloid — specifically the aglycone of α-tomatine (the steroidal glycoalkaloid in tomato). Removing tomatine's four-sugar (β-lycotetraose) chain leaves tomatidine. [1][12][13]
  • Molecular formula: C₂₇H₄₅NO₂. MW ≈ 415.7 g/mol. CAS 77-59-8. PubChem CID 65576. [9][10][11]
  • IUPAC-style descriptor: a spirosolane-type C27 steroidal alkaloid (a nitrogen-containing spiroketal steroid). Off-white crystalline powder. [10][11]
  • Lipophilicity: No published experimental logP located, but as a non-charged C27 sterol-like molecule it is markedly lipophilic (soluble in chloroform, DCM, ethyl acetate, DMSO, acetone; poorly water-soluble). [10][11] [logP unverified — supplier TDS needed]
  • Natural origin: Tomato plant (Solanum lycopersicum, syn. Lycopersicon esculentum). α-Tomatine is concentrated in green/unripe tomatoes and tomato leaves/stems (immature fruit ~40–170 mg/kg, up to ~0.5 g/kg; ripening drops it ~99% to ~0.3–6 mg/kg). Dietary tomatidine in humans arises mainly from gut hydrolysis of ingested α-tomatine. [12][13][16] Commercial tomatidine is purified/extracted (or semisynthetic) to high purity (~95–98%). [10][11]
  • Key related forms: α-Tomatine (the glycoside; different pharmacology — forms an insoluble complex with cholesterol, poorly absorbed), solasodine, solanidine, ursolic acid (a separate muscle compound from the same research group, often co-discussed but chemically unrelated). [1][12][14][16]

2. Class & mechanism

Tomatidine is positioned as a mitophagy activator / mitochondrial-quality active in longevity science, but its mechanism is more tangled (and more interesting) than the clean "autophagy active" label implies.

  • Mitophagy + Nrf2 (the longevity story). In C. elegans and human/rodent cells, tomatidine induces a moderate ROS rise that activates the SKN-1/Nrf2–ARE antioxidant program and triggers PINK-1/DCT-1-dependent mitophagy (selective clearance of damaged mitochondria), with improved mitochondrial network organization and biogenesis. Knocking down PINK-1/DCT-1 or SKN-1 abolishes the benefit — mitophagy is causal, not incidental. [2][8]
  • mTORC1 activation (the muscle story — and a contradiction worth flagging). In skeletal muscle, tomatidine does the opposite of a rapalog: it stimulates mTORC1 (↑S6K phosphorylation, ↑protein synthesis, ↑mitochondrial biogenesis, ↑IGF1/PGC-1α), driving hypertrophy. Rapamycin abolishes this. [1] So calling tomatidine simply an "autophagy/mTOR-down active" is inaccurate: it raises anabolic mTORC1 in muscle while inducing mitophagy elsewhere — a tissue-/context-dependent, partly paradoxical profile. Honest framing matters here.
  • Senomorphic (the newest, most skin-adjacent story). A 2026 phenotypic screen identified tomatidine as a senomorphic (not senolytic) compound: in senescent human fibroblasts and brain microvascular endothelial cells it suppressed the SASP (senescence-associated secretory phenotype) without lowering p16^Ink4a/p21^Cip1 or killing cells. [4]
  • Anti-inflammatory (NF-κB/JNK). The foundational pharmacology: tomatidine blocks IκBα phosphorylation, NF-κB nuclear translocation, and JNK, cutting iNOS and COX-2 in LPS-stimulated macrophages. [3] Reproduced as MMP-9 / NF-κB suppression in arthritic joint cells and chondrocytes. [5][6]

Plain-language: Tomatidine's pitch for aging skin would be: calm cellular inflammation (NF-κB↓), clean out damaged mitochondria (mitophagy↑), switch on the cell's antioxidant defenses (Nrf2↑), and quiet the "zombie cell" inflammatory secretions (SASP↓). Mechanistically coherent for skin longevity in theory — but proven in worms, mice, and muscle, not in skin and not from a topical product.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

Read this before believing any label. I have separated what is shown for skin/topical (almost nothing) from what is borrowed from muscle, worms, joints, and oral dosing.

Tier 1 — In-vitro (cell studies) — solid mechanism, wrong tissue for our purposes

  • Dyle et al. (2014), J Biol Chem 289(21):14913–14924. Human and mouse (C2C12) myotubes. Tomatidine (EC₅₀ <300 nM; 1 µM tested) ↑mTORC1/S6K phosphorylation, ↑total protein, ↑mitochondrial DNA, myotube hypertrophy; rapamycin abolished it. DOI: 10.1074/jbc.M114.556241 (PMID 24719321). Muscle cells, not skin. [1]
  • Fang et al. (2017), Scientific Reports 7:46208 (also tested cells). Primary rat neurons (↑mitochondrial content/mitophagy), human neural cells (↑nuclear Nrf2, ↑NQO-1/HO-1), HeLa mt-mKeima reporter (mitophagy confirmed). 25 µM optimal; 50 µM toxic (biphasic/hormetic). DOI: 10.1038/srep46208. Neural/HeLa, not skin. [2]
  • Chiu & Lin (2008), FEBS Letters 582(17):2407–2412. LPS-stimulated mouse macrophages: ↓iNOS, ↓COX-2 via ↓IκBα-phosphorylation, ↓NF-κB translocation, ↓JNK/c-jun. DOI: 10.1016/j.febslet.2008.05.049 (PMID 18544347). Foundational anti-inflammatory data — macrophages, not skin. [3]
  • Yu et al. (2021), Frontiers in Pharmacology 12:670707. Arthritic fibroblast-like synoviocytes: 5–10 µM tomatidine ↓TNFα-induced proliferation/migration, ↓MMP-9 (no effect on MMP-2) via NF-κB↓. DOI: 10.3389/fphar.2021.670707 (PMC8426578). Joint/synovial, not dermal — do NOT cite as "skin collagen." [5]
  • Chondrocyte/osteoarthritis work (2020): tomatidine ↓IL-1β-induced iNOS/COX-2/MMPs and protected collagen-II/aggrecan in articular chondrocytes (NF-κB/MAPK↓). Cartilage collagen, not skin collagen. (PMC7377830) [6]
  • 2026 senomorphic screen (in-vitro arm, see Tier 3 paper [4]): senescent human fibroblasts and brain endothelial cells — tomatidine suppressed SASP without lowering p16/p21 or viability. This is the closest thing to relevant skin-cell data (fibroblasts are dermal-relevant), but it is a senescence-screen readout, not a skin-aging efficacy study, and it was paired with systemic (not topical) in-vivo work. [4]

Verdict on Tier 1: The mechanism (mitophagy, Nrf2, NF-κB↓, SASP↓, MMP-9↓) is real and reproduced across independent groups — but almost entirely in muscle, neural, macrophage, and joint cells. The single dermally-relevant readout (fibroblast SASP suppression) is promising-in-principle and nothing more. In-vitro is also the weakest tier for predicting cream performance.

Tier 2 — Animal — muscle, lifespan, metabolic; skin only as a systemic by-product

  • Dyle et al. (2014), mice: 0.05% dietary tomatidine (or 25 mg/kg IP) → +~13.7% muscle mass, ↑grip strength, ↑running distance, ↓fasting- and disuse-atrophy. Oral/systemic; muscle endpoint. [1]
  • Cholesterol/atherosclerosis: ~0.04% dietary tomatidine ×10 wk ↓plasma cholesterol and atherosclerosis in ApoE-deficient mice, no toxicity. Oral; cardiometabolic. [16]
  • Tomatidine senotherapeutic paper (2026), EMBO Molecular Medicine 18:1530–1550. Aged mice given tomatidine showed ↓frailty, ↑motor coordination, ↑cognition, with reduced senescence markers (p16, p21, telomere-associated DNA-damage foci) in liver, skin, and hippocampus, plus ↓neuroinflammation. This is the only "skin" in-vivo signal — and it is from SYSTEMIC dosing, not a topical application. Dose/route detail oral/systemic; no topical arm; senomorphic mechanism. DOI: 10.1038/s44321-026-00400-0. [4]
  • No peer-reviewed animal model of topical tomatidine for skin aging was located. Any "proven in animals for skin" claim is [unverified].

Verdict on Tier 2: Robust, repeated, oral/systemic benefits for muscle, metabolism, and (newly) brain/frailty/senescence. The "skin" line in [4] tells us tomatidine reaches and helps skin tissue when delivered systemically — it does not tell us a topical cream works.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL — thin; muscle-tissue model only, no whole-body RCT located

  • Parafati et al. (2025), Medical Research Archives 13(4), DOI 10.18103/mra.v13i4.6423. Donor-derived human skeletal-muscle myobundles (3D bioengineered, n=5 donors, young + older). 5 µM tomatidine q6h ×7 days attenuated exercise-like inflammation: ↓IL-6 (2.7-fold young / 1.6-fold older), ↓IL-8/CXCL8 (~2.5–2.9-fold), ↓MCP-1/CCL2, ↓TIMP-1. Human tissue model, not a clinical trial; muscle, not skin. (COI: an author sits on the board of a microgravity-tissue company; not a tomatidine seller.) [7]
  • No human oral RCT of tomatidine for any endpoint was located (as of 2026-06). The well-known human-tissue and human-cell work is muscle/neural; the rest is preclinical. (Contrast urolithin A, which does have oral human RCTs.)

Verdict on Tier 3: For a molecule, there is credible human-tissue signal (muscle myobundles) — but no swallowed-pill human clinical trial, and nothing on skin.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL — the tier that matters for a SKU, and it is EMPTY

  • No human topical study of tomatidine on skin was located — RCT or otherwise. No vehicle-controlled trial, no split-face study, no biopsy work, not even a sponsor preprint. Searches across PubMed, journals, and cosmetic literature returned zero topical-skin clinical data. [search-negative]
  • No published human or in-vitro study of tomatidine specifically on dermal fibroblasts for skin-aging endpoints (collagen synthesis, elastin, wrinkle-relevant ECM) was located beyond the senescence-screen SASP readout in [4].
  • Supplier/marketing claims ("improves elasticity and firmness," "boosts collagen production," "reduces wrinkles," "stimulates hair growth") are unsupported by any located primary study and trace to raw-material vendor copy. Treat as [unverified marketing]. [11]

Verdict on Tier 4 — the decisive one: For the only format you would sell (topical), tomatidine is essentially unproven. This is a lower evidence position than urolithin A (which at least has a sponsor topical preprint with biopsy transcriptomics). For tomatidine there is no human topical dataset to even argue from.

Explicit borrowed-vs-proven map: - Proven for topical human skin: nothing. - Plausible/relevant but indirect: fibroblast SASP suppression (cell screen), systemic-dose skin-tissue senescence-marker reduction in aged mice. - Borrowed from other formats (do NOT claim for a cream): muscle hypertrophy/strength, worm lifespan, joint/cartilage MMP-9↓ ("collagen protection"), macrophage anti-inflammation, cholesterol-lowering.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Tomatidine is a lipophilic C27 steroidal alkaloid, MW ~416 Da — below the ~500 Da rule-of-thumb ceiling and lipophilic enough that stratum-corneum partitioning is physicochemically plausible. That is the only favorable thing that can be said with confidence.
  • No tomatidine-specific skin-permeation/penetration study (Franz cell, tape-strip, ex-vivo) was located. Delivered dose to viable epidermis/dermis, depth, and metabolic fate in skin are entirely unknown / [unverified].
  • Its parent glycoside α-tomatine is poorly absorbed (forms an insoluble cholesterol complex in the gut) — irrelevant to topical delivery but a reminder that "from tomatoes" tells us nothing about skin uptake.
  • Net: penetration is theoretically favorable, experimentally unestablished. A formulator would be guessing at delivery.

6. Formulation

Because no topical formulation has been published, everything here is inference, not validated practice.

  • Effective concentration (topical): Unknown — no topical dose-finding exists. In-vitro active windows were ~0.3–25 µM (i.e. ~0.0001–0.001% in solution); 50 µM was toxic to cells. [2] These do not translate to a cream %, and the hormetic/biphasic dose-response in [2] is a caution against "more is better."
  • pH / stability: As a steroidal alkaloid with a basic nitrogen and spiroketal, behavior across pH is uncharacterized for cosmetics; suppliers advise prepared solutions be used same-day and stored cold/dark — hinting at limited solution stability. [10] Confirm stability window and solubilizer with supplier TDS before any base. [unverified]
  • Solubility/vehicle: Practically water-insoluble; needs an oil phase or solubilizer (DMSO/ethanol/glycols in lab; cosmetically a lipid/emulsifier system or encapsulation). [10][11] This complicates clean, low-irritant formulation.
  • Pairings (rational, unvalidated): mechanistically complementary with Nrf2/antioxidant actives (vit C/E, niacinamide), other mitophagy/longevity actives (urolithin A, spermidine), and barrier lipids — but no synergy data exist.
  • Sensory: off-white powder; at trace actives levels likely sensory-neutral if solubilized; bitterness/alkaloid character irrelevant topically.

7. Safety

  • Dietary/oral safety (good): Tomatidine and tomatine derivatives are non-toxic orally except at very high acute doses; a 200-day rat feeding study caused no reactions; dietary tomatine/tomatidine from green tomatoes is consumed without notable toxicity, and humans efficiently metabolize/excrete small amounts. [14][16] α-Tomatine is largely non-absorbed (cholesterol-complex excretion). [16]
  • Glycoalkaloid context / teratogenicity (the caution): Tomatidine belongs to the Solanaceae glycoalkaloid family. Some glycoalkaloids (notably α-solanine/α-chaconine from potato) are possible teratogens in amphibian/rodent embryo models and inhibit cholinesterases. For tomatine/tomatidine specifically, dietary human exposure appears safe and NTD links were not confirmed in animal models — but the class association means pregnancy safety for a concentrated isolated active is unestablished. [15][16] Default to caution.
  • Topical irritation/sensitization: No topical human (HRIPT/patch) or in-vitro skin-irritation data located. [unverified] — would require in-house RIPT + cytotoxicity/sensitization screening before use.
  • Sun sensitivity: No evidence of photosensitization; mechanistically (Nrf2/anti-inflammatory) more likely neutral-to-protective, but no photo-safety data. Not a sunscreen.
  • Pregnancy/lactation: No topical safety data; glycoalkaloid-class teratogenicity concern → avoid / flag for medical review. [unverified]
  • Sourcing/sustainability: Plant-derived (tomato leaf/green fruit/agricultural by-product) — a potential upcycling/clean-beauty story — but isolation to cosmetic purity and consistent specification is the real cost/quality driver.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic vs drug line: Tomatidine in a topical product would be a cosmetic ingredient. Because no topical efficacy evidence exists, any performance claim is on extremely thin ground. Claims implying it treats/prevents disease or alters skin structure/function ("boosts collagen," "induces mitophagy in your skin," "reverses aging," "regrows hair") risk drug classification (US FDA) and would be unsubstantiated — a double problem.
  • Specific substantiation problem: Unlike retinol/niacinamide (strong topical data) or even urolithin A (a sponsor topical preprint), tomatidine has no human topical data at all. Borrowing muscle / worm / oral / joint results to sell a cream would be a clear claims-discipline violation and likely misleading advertising.
  • Safer cosmetic phrasing (✅) — only if used at all: "a tomato-derived longevity-research compound," "studied for cellular and mitochondrial health (in laboratory and model-organism research)," "supports the look of healthy, resilient skin" (generic, non-specific). Even these flirt with implying benefit the cream cannot back.
  • Avoid / flag for legal (⚠️): "boosts/builds collagen," "firms," "reduces wrinkles," "anti-aging," "activates mitophagy/autophagy in skin," "senolytic/senomorphic skin renewal," "clinically proven," any muscle/hair/longevity carry-over, any "clinical" framing. [11]
  • See ../../brand/glossary.md claim cheat-sheet.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Forms to buy: High-purity (95–98%) tomatidine from specialty/phytochemical suppliers (ChemFaces, Glentham, AOBIOUS, Parchem, various China manufacturers); largely sold as a research reference standard / API-grade material, with some "cosmetic raw material" positioning. [9][10][11]
  • Commercial reality: The molecule's IP and development are dominated by Emmyon, Inc. (founder/CEO Christopher Adams, Univ. of Iowa), which holds patents on tomatidine (and ursolic acid) for muscle mass/atrophy and supplements — not skincare. The whole commercial ecosystem points at muscle/longevity supplements, not topical cosmetics. [8] This means little cosmetic-grade supply chain, little cosmetic safety dossier, and potential IP friction for non-muscle use.
  • Cost tier: High (specialty phytochemical isolate; research-grade pricing), with no economy-of-scale cosmetic supply behind it. Poor value for a cream given zero efficacy data.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • Hype: "Tomato compound proven to fight aging / build muscle / extend lifespan"; supplier copy: "boosts collagen, firms skin, regrows hair." Media (green-tomato/longevity headlines) conflate muscle and worm-lifespan findings with skin benefit. [8][11]
  • Reality #1: The flagship findings are muscle (mice + human myobundles) and C. elegans lifespan (+~7%) — not skin, not topical. [1][2][7]
  • Reality #2: The only "skin" in-vivo signal is systemic dosing in aged mice reducing senescence markers across organs (incl. skin) — a delivered-internally effect, not a cream. [4]
  • Reality #3: "Boosts collagen" almost certainly derives from joint/cartilage MMP-9↓ studies misread as dermal collagen. Different tissue, different biology. [5][6]
  • Reality #4: Zero human topical trials; zero dermal-fibroblast skin-aging efficacy studies; zero skin-penetration data. The cosmetic case is currently hypothesis, not evidence.
  • Reality #5 (mechanistic honesty): Tomatidine raises mTORC1 (anabolic) in muscle while inducing mitophagy in worms — so the tidy "autophagy active" label is partly aspirational and context-dependent. [1][2]
  • What's genuinely strong: Excellent oral/dietary safety record; a real, reproducible mitophagy/Nrf2/SASP-suppression mechanism; a credible systemic longevity story (muscle, frailty, cognition, senescence). The biology is legitimately exciting — just not demonstrated for a topical skincare product.

11. Verdict for the brand

❌ SKIP for now (fringe) — revisit only on real topical/skin data.

One line: Mechanistically on-thesis for skin-longevity (mitophagy, Nrf2, SASP-suppression) and very safe to eat — but with no human topical trial, no dermal-fibroblast efficacy study, and no skin-penetration data, it cannot support any honest cosmetic claim, and its commercial ecosystem points at muscle supplements, not skincare.

  • Why not even "conditional/supporting": Urolithin A earns a conditional grade because it has a (flawed) human topical preprint plus oral RCTs. Tomatidine has neither a topical study nor an oral RCT — its skin case rests entirely on mechanism + one systemic-mouse tissue signal. That is below the bar for a premium, claims-disciplined brand whose credibility is the product.
  • Best (limited) role if ever used: a deep-bench "science-story" trace active only after (a) the brand or a third party publishes vehicle-controlled topical human data (or at least a credible dermal-fibroblast/3D-skin study), (b) cosmetic-grade supply + topical safety (RIPT, stability) are secured, and (c) IP for non-muscle topical use is cleared with Emmyon. Until then it adds cost and claim-risk with no payoff.
  • Hard skip if: the brand wants substantiable claims (it does), a clean price point, or to avoid alkaloid-class pregnancy-safety ambiguity.
  • Longevity relevance: High in concept, unproven in skin. Tomatidine targets mitochondrial decline, senescence/SASP, and inflammaging — three core hallmarks — which is exactly why it's tempting and exactly why claims discipline must hold: the story is strong enough to oversell a product that has no skin data. Prefer better-evidenced members of the same class (urolithin A, spermidine, niacinamide as NAD precursor) for the actual formula; keep tomatidine on a watch-list.

Log under ../../ideas/not-considering.md (reason: no topical/skin efficacy data; muscle/supplement-oriented IP; claim risk) with a note to revisit if topical human data emerges.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Overall evidence grade for TOPICAL skin efficacy: D (essentially unproven for a cream)

Rationale: The tier that matters — human topical skin — is empty. There is no topical trial, no preprint, not even a dermal-fibroblast efficacy study (only a senescence-screen SASP readout in fibroblasts as part of a systemic-dosing paper). Everything robust (Dyle 2014 muscle; Fang 2017 worm lifespan; Parafati 2025 human muscle myobundles; Chiu 2008 macrophage anti-inflammation; Yu 2021 joint MMP-9) is the wrong tissue or wrong route. This sits a full grade below urolithin A's topical "C," because UA at least has a sponsor topical preprint with skin-biopsy transcriptomics; tomatidine has nothing topical. For the molecule's systemic/longevity biology the grade would be B− (good preclinical + human-tissue, but no oral human RCT located); for topical skin it is D.

Main caveats (carry into any marketing/claims work)

  1. No topical skin evidence of any kind — the central fact. Do not describe topical tomatidine as "proven," "clinical," or even "studied for skin" (it isn't, topically).
  2. The strong evidence is the wrong tissue/route — muscle, worm, joint, macrophage, oral/systemic. Borrowing it to sell a cream is the trap.
  3. "Boosts collagen" is a misread of joint/cartilage MMP-9 data — there is no dermal-collagen study.
  4. Mechanistic contradiction — mTORC1 up in muscle vs mitophagy in worms; "autophagy active" is an oversimplification.
  5. Commercial/IP and pregnancy ambiguities add risk with no efficacy upside for skincare.

Bottom line

The molecule's longevity biology is genuine and attractive (B− systemic), but for a topical skincare product the evidence is grade D — essentially unproven. The dossier does not let muscle/worm/oral data pose as skin data and explicitly quarantines supplier marketing. Verdict stands: SKIP / fringe; revisit only if real topical human (or at least 3D-skin/dermal-fibroblast) data appears.
