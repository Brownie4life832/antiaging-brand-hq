Ingredient: Phytosterols (Beta-Sitosterol, Phytosteryl Macadamiate, Lanosterol, Ergosterol)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ✅ shortlisted (SUPPORTING lipid for the barrier story; β-sitosterol & phytosteryl esters) Class: Barrier lipids & skin-identical actives · Slug: phytosterols

Exhaustive literature review. Evidence is graded by tier and format (in-vitro → animal → human ORAL → human TOPICAL). Where a benefit is borrowed from a different format (oral, animal, in-vitro, or a different tissue such as joint cartilage or the eye lens), that is stated explicitly. Citations are real and numbered in Sources; any claim that could not be primary-verified is marked [unverified]. This file is a sibling to ceramides.md — phytosterols are the plant cholesterol-analog of the same stratum-corneum lipid system, so the two should be read together.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Phytosterols ("plant sterols") — a family of triterpene sterols that are the plant kingdom's structural and functional analog of animal cholesterol. The four named here are deliberately heterogeneous; they are not interchangeable, and lumping them is the single biggest source of confusion in marketing copy:
  • β-Sitosterol (INCI: Beta-Sitosterol; CAS 83-46-5) — the most abundant dietary/plant phytosterol and the dominant sterol in soybean, rapeseed, rice-bran, perilla and most "phytosterol" cosmetic actives. Structurally closest to cholesterol (differs by one ethyl group at C-24). This is the molecule that carries almost all the topical skin evidence below.
  • Phytosteryl Macadamiate (INCI: Phytosteryl Macadamiate) — an ester: soybean-derived phytosterols esterified to the fatty acids of macadamia oil (chiefly oleic 18:1 and palmitoleic 16:1). It is a botanical mimic of human cholesteryl esters / steryl esters found in skin-surface (sebum) lipids. A rich, occlusive emollient with a melting point near body temperature; a headline component of the L22 sebum-mimetic complex. [9]
  • Lanosterol (INCI: Lanosterol; CAS 79-63-0) — a 4,4-dimethyl triterpenoid sterol; the biosynthetic precursor of both cholesterol (animals) and ergosterol (fungi). It is not a true phytosterol (it is found in animal sebum/wool wax and fungi), and its skin-relevant fame comes from an ophthalmic anti-protein-aggregation discovery, not topical skin data. Included here because it is sometimes marketed alongside the group; treated honestly as the weakest member for skin. [6]
  • Ergosterol (INCI: Ergosterol; CAS 57-87-4) — the characteristic sterol of fungi/yeast/some algae; the provitamin D2 precursor (UV converts it to ergocalciferol). Emerging photoprotection data, mostly very recent and pre-human. [4]
  • Common forms / closely related INCI you will see on labels: Phytosterols (the generic blend, typically ~40–60% β-sitosterol + ~20% campesterol + ~5–20% stigmasterol + brassicasterol), Phytosteryl/Octyldodecyl esters (Phytosteryl Macadamiate, Phytosteryl/Octyldodecyl Lauroyl Glutamate, Phytosteryl Oleate, Phytosteryl Isostearate), Soybean Sterols / Glycine Soja Sterols, Canola Sterols, and Avocado/Soybean Unsaponifiables (ASU) — an unsaponifiable fraction that is largely phytosterols and is the most clinically studied phytosterol-rich material in any tissue (joints; see §4). [13]
  • Where they come from: the unsaponifiable fraction of plant oils — soybean, rapeseed/canola, sunflower, rice bran, shea, avocado, perilla, sea buckthorn, macadamia. "Tall oil" (a pine pulping by-product) is the dominant industrial source of purified β-sitosterol for the food-supplement market.

2. Class & mechanism

Phytosterols sit in the same lipid lexicon as ceramides and cholesterol — they are barrier lipids and skin-identical(-ish) actives. There are three mechanistically distinct stories, and the honest dossier keeps them apart:

(A) Barrier / lipid-matrix mechanism (the credible core, mostly β-sitosterol & phytosteryl esters). The stratum-corneum (SC) "mortar" is built from ceramides + cholesterol + free fatty acids in ordered lamellar bilayers (see ceramides.md §2). Aged and photoaged epidermis shows a global drop in SC lipids and specifically a fall in cholesterol synthesis — which is why cholesterol-dominant physiological-lipid blends accelerate barrier repair in aged skin (Zettersten/Ghadially/Elias 1997). [8] Phytosterols are close enough to cholesterol in shape to partially substitute for it in these lamellae and to act as a cholesterol-sparing barrier lipid; the L22 sebum-mimetic deliberately uses phytosterols + phytosteryl macadamiate in place of cholesterol and cholesteryl esters. [9] Phytosteryl esters additionally behave as occlusive/emollient lipids that intermingle with SC lipids and slow water loss. This is the mechanism most aligned with the brand's barrier-repair thesis.

(B) Anti-inflammatory / PPAR & cytokine mechanism (β-sitosterol, ergosterol). β-Sitosterol and ergosterol suppress pro-inflammatory signaling in keratinocytes/macrophages — down-regulating NF-κB, MAPK (ERK/JNK/p38), COX/LOX, and cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β, TSLP), and modulating PPAR-γ (which drives epidermal lipid synthesis, hydration and anti-inflammatory tone). This underlies the atopic-dermatitis and psoriasiform animal results in §4. [1][3][4][14]

(C) Antioxidant / collagen-sparing mechanism (β-sitosterol, in phytosterol-rich unsaponifiables). Phytosterol-rich unsaponifiables reduce UVB-induced ROS, support antioxidant defense (Nrf2/catalase in non-skin tissue), restore collagen synthesis via TGF-β/Smad2-3, and suppress MMP-1/MMP-3 via MAPK/AP-1 down-regulation in UVB-stressed fibroblasts. This is the "photoaging/collagen" story — but note it is in-vitro and on a mixture (USM), not on isolated topical β-sitosterol in humans. [2][12][16]

The two outliers, mechanistically honest: - Lanosterol — famous for dissolving crystallin protein aggregates in the eye lens (a proteostasis/chaperone-like effect), shown in vitro, in rabbit lenses ex-vivo, and in dog cataracts in vivo (Zhao et al., Nature 2015). This is a genuinely interesting proteostasis/longevity mechanism — but it is ophthalmic, not cutaneous, was partly contested by a 2019 replication failure, and there is no topical-skin anti-aggregation evidence. Do not import it into skin claims. [6][7] - Ergosterol — provitamin-D2 photochemistry plus a 2025 photoprotection mechanism (dual NF-κB + MAPK inhibition; preserved tight-junction integrity, reduced collagen degradation) in keratinocytes + a mouse UVB model. Promising, but pre-human for skin. [4]

Bottom line on hallmarks: like ceramides, phytosterols are a barrier + soothing active first. The "collagen / photoaging / anti-glycation / longevity" stories are real signals but live almost entirely at the in-vitro / animal / other-tissue level. Treat barrier and anti-inflammatory support as the defensible core; treat everything else as supporting narrative requiring honesty.

4. Evidence review by tier (core of this dossier)

Tier 1 — In-vitro / biophysical / ex-vivo

  • Lee et al. (Antioxidants (Basel), 2019; 8(12):644) — in-vitro, the single best mechanistic study for the photoaging/collagen claim. Unsaponifiable matter (USM) from perilla seed meal, whose dominant component is β-sitosterol (≈23,000 mg/100 g USM) plus γ-tocopherol, octacosanol, squalene; tested at 1.0/2.5/5.0 µg/mL on Hs68 human dermal fibroblasts, UVB 30 mJ/cm². Results: UVB cut collagen synthesis 49.4%; USM restored it (+18.9% over control at 5 µg/mL); dose-dependently suppressed MMP-1 and MMP-3; cut ROS 27.1%; inhibited MAPK (p-JNK −80%, p-ERK −87%, p-p38 −85%) and AP-1; activated TGF-β/Smad2-3 (Smad7 −83%). Limitations: in-vitro only; tests a mixture (cannot isolate β-sitosterol from tocopherol/squalene); no human or topical-penetration arm. [2]
  • β-Sitosterol penetration & anti-psoriasiform SAR (Lin et al., Int Immunopharmacol, 2023) — structure–permeation study: of the common phytosterols, β-sitosterol had the highest pig-skin absorption (0.33 nmol/mg) > campesterol (0.21) > stigmasterol (0.16); in an imiquimod psoriasis-like model β-sitosterol cut psoriasiform epidermal thickness (92.4 → 63.8 µm) and IL-6/TNF-α/CXCL1. Mechanistic + ex-vivo/animal. [1]
  • β-Sitosterol as a penetration enhancer / "skin-similar" lipid — recent work positions β-sitosterol as an agent that integrates into and fluidizes SC lipids, enhancing co-delivery of other actives (and itself delivered via ethosomes/cubosomes-microneedles). Useful for the delivery angle, not a stand-alone efficacy claim. [11][17]
  • Ergosterol photoprotection (2025) — UVB-irradiated human keratinocytes: ergosterol preserved viability, cut ROS, suppressed pro-inflammatory mediators via dual NF-κB + MAPK inhibition. [4]
  • Anti-glycation screen — phytosterols inhibited AGE formation by binding lysine residues of BSA (biochemical model, not skin). [12]
  • Lanosterol anti-aggregation (in-vitro arm of Zhao 2015) — lanosterol (not cholesterol) dissolved pre-formed crystallin aggregates in vitro. Ophthalmic protein, not skin. [6]

Tier 2 — Animal

  • β-Sitosterol, atopic-dermatitis model (Han, Kim, Jeong; Exp Biol Med, 2014; 239(4):454-64) — β-sitosterol attenuated DNFB-induced AD-like skin lesions in NC/Nga mice by down-regulating TSLP and Th2 cytokines (↓ serum histamine, IgE, IL-4). Administration was systemic (this is a whole-animal AD model with serum endpoints), not a controlled topical-skin study — do not read it as topical-efficacy evidence. [3]
  • β-Sitosterol, psoriasiform model (Lin 2023, above) — anti-inflammatory, barrier-sparing vs. the barrier-thinning seen with topical steroids. [1]
  • Ergosterol, mouse UVB model (2025) — topical ergosterol attenuated epidermal hyperplasia, maintained tight-junction integrity, and inhibited collagen-matrix degradation. The most relevant topical animal photoprotection result for a sterol in the group. [4]
  • β-Sitosterol, diabetic-wound rat models (2023; comparative vs dexpanthenol) — promoted angiogenesis, M1→M2 macrophage shift, collagen deposition, re-epithelialization, reduced wound size. Supports a "repair/soothing" mechanism but in a wound (disease) context. [5][18]
  • ASU (avocado-soybean unsaponifiables) in osteoarthritis animal models — chondroprotective: ↓ MMP-2/3/13, ↑ TIMP-1, ↓ IL-1/IL-6/IL-8/TNF/PGE2, ↑ collagen & aggrecan. Wrong tissue (joint cartilage), oral/intra-articular route — relevant only as evidence the phytosterol-rich unsaponifiable fraction genuinely modulates MMP/collagen/inflammation biology; do not present as skin data. [13]
  • Lanosterol cataract (Zhao 2015) — reduced cataract severity in rabbit lenses (ex-vivo) and dogs (in-vivo). Ophthalmic; contested by a 2019 replication failure for several oxysterols including lanosterol. [6][7]

Tier 3 — Human ORAL

There is essentially no human-oral evidence that phytosterols improve skin. Oral phytosterols/stanols are one of the best-evidenced cholesterol-lowering food actives (≈2 g/day lowers LDL ~8–10%; EFSA/FDA-recognized) — a cardiometabolic claim with no demonstrated skin benefit. Oral β-sitosterol also has BPH/urinary-symptom data (5α-reductase / anti-inflammatory) again unrelated to skin. Treat the oral route as irrelevant to topical skin claims — and note that, unlike ceramides (where oral glucosylceramides at least have hydration RCTs), here even the oral-skin bridge is absent. [no qualifying human oral skin trial located]

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL

This is the decisive tier, and it is thin but real for β-sitosterol/phytosterols on barrier recovery — and absent or borrowed for everything else.

4a. Controlled / objective-endpoint human topical (strongest available):

  • Puglia & Bonina — In vivo spectrophotometric evaluation of skin barrier recovery after topical application of soybean phytosterols (Int J Cosmet Sci / Skin Res Technol, 2008) — human in-vivo: barrier was disrupted (SLS/tape-strip) and soybean phytosterols (in di-n-butyl adipate vehicle) vs vehicle were applied; phytosterols accelerated TEWL/barrier recovery vs vehicle. Limitations: small n, manufacturer/industry context, surrogate (induced-damage) model rather than aged-skin endpoints; abstract/secondary-verified. [partly unverified — primary full text not fetched] [10]
  • Puglia et al. — percutaneous absorption of niacinamide and phytosterols + in-vivo barrier-recovery evaluation (Curr Drug Deliv, 2016) — companion human/ex-vivo work confirming phytosterols penetrate the SC and support barrier recovery, and characterizing co-delivery with niacinamide. [partly unverified — abstract-level] [10]
  • L22 (botanically-derived skin-surface-lipid mimetic) — Wertz/Floratech, J Cosmet Sci, 2018 — L22 is engineered from macadamia, jojoba and olive lipids to match the SSL profile of healthy 22-year-old women; phytosterols + phytosteryl macadamiate substitute for cholesterol and steryl esters. The supporting publication characterizes the mimetic and SSL composition; manufacturer claims include improved hydration/"younger-skin lipid profile." This is a finished-complex/ingredient-marketing study, not an independent RCT of phytosterols per se. [9]

4b. Lower-tier topical (open-label, single small study, or finished-product generalization):

  • The "skin firmness/sagging" topical study widely cited (e.g. via Typology) reportedly showed a firmness/anti-sagging effect of topical phytosterols — but it involved only ~10 volunteers, which is too small to support a stand-alone firmness claim. [unverified — primary source not located; cited only as a flagged weak signal] [15]
  • Numerous finished cosmetic products use phytosterols/phytosteryl esters as supporting barrier/emollient lipids; their consumer-perception data reflect the whole formula, not isolated phytosterols.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • β-Sitosterol is lipophilic (logP ~9.6, MW 414 Da). MW sits under the ~500 Da "penetration ceiling," but extreme lipophilicity and crystallinity mean it largely deposits in and integrates with the SC lipid matrix rather than reaching viable epidermis in quantity — which is the point for a barrier lipid (cf. ceramides). It had the highest skin absorption of the common phytosterols in pig skin (0.33 nmol/mg). [1]
  • It can also act as a penetration enhancer, fluidizing SC lipids to aid co-delivery of partner actives (and is itself boosted by ethosome/cubosome/microneedle systems in drug-delivery work). [11][17]
  • Phytosteryl macadamiate and other esters are larger and even more lipophilic; they function as surface/occlusive emollients that intermingle with SC lipids — minimal deep penetration, by design. Melting point near body temperature gives even spreading and a "melts into skin" sensory. [9]
  • Lanosterol topical skin penetration/efficacy is not established (its evidence base is ophthalmic). Ergosterol topical penetration in human skin is uncharacterized (animal/in-vitro only).
  • Delivery matters: like ceramides, phytosterols are waxy, poorly water-soluble lipids best delivered in well-built emulsions/oil phases, liposomes/nanoemulsions, or physiological-lipid complexes — not thin aqueous serums.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: typically 0.1–5% for phytosterols / phytosteryl esters as supporting barrier/emollient lipids (CIR documents use up to ~8%, most products <5%). β-Sitosterol is often used at fractions of a percent within a sterol blend. There is no established "active dose" threshold for a stand-alone efficacy claim — its role is compositional (part of a lipid system), not dose-response.
  • Best practice — use as the cholesterol-analog in a physiological/sebum-mimetic lipid blend. Pair β-sitosterol/phytosterols (cholesterol slot) + ceramides + free fatty acids for SC-mortar repair, or use phytosteryl macadamiate within a sebum-mimetic (L22-style) surface-lipid system. This is where the mechanism is strongest. [8][9]
  • pH: broad compatibility (~4–7); not pH-sensitive as free sterols. Esters are stable across normal cream pH.
  • Stability: good. Sterols and especially phytosteryl macadamiate are oxidatively stable and tolerate hot-pour/high-temperature manufacturing; free phytosterols are crystalline solids requiring heated-phase incorporation and good emulsification to avoid graininess/recrystallization. Protect oil phase with antioxidant (tocopherol) as standard.
  • Pairs well with: ceramides + cholesterol + FFA (completes the physiological mortar); niacinamide (co-penetration shown by Puglia, and niacinamide independently up-regulates the skin's own lipid synthesis — see ceramides.md §6); squalane, fatty alcohols, occlusives; soothing actives (panthenol, allantoin). As a barrier/emollient base it buffers tolerance of retinoids/AHAs.
  • Conflicts: none chemically significant. Practical mismatch with thin, high-water serums and very high-surfactant cleansers that strip lipids. Free phytosterols can feel waxy/draggy if over-dosed without proper emollient balance.
  • Sensory: rich, cushioning, "nourishing." Phytosteryl macadamiate in particular gives a luxe, melting, occlusive-but-not-greasy skin feel — a genuine premium-texture asset.

7. Safety

  • Irritation / sensitization: very low. The CIR Expert Panel concluded phytosterols and phytosteryl esters are "safe as used" in cosmetics at current concentrations of use (reported maxima up to ~8%); non-irritating, non-sensitizing, not subject to a concentration restriction under EU cosmetic regulation. No notable cosmetic adverse-event signal. [19]
  • Sun sensitivity: none — phytosterols are not photosensitizers; β-sitosterol and ergosterol have photo-protective signals if anything. (Ergosterol is a provitamin D2 — UV converts it; not a skin-safety concern at cosmetic use, but worth knowing for stability/marketing accuracy.)
  • Pregnancy / nursing: considered low-risk topically (large, poorly-absorbed lipids; minimal systemic exposure). Not medical advice; defer to a clinician.
  • Suitable for sensitive, dry, compromised, post-procedure skin — a barrier/soothing profile is the selling point.
  • Sourcing / allergens:
  • β-sitosterol/phytosterols are usually soy-, rapeseed-, or tall-oil-derived → flag soy allergen relevance when soy-sourced (highly refined sterols are typically low-risk but disclose source). Tall-oil (pine) sterols avoid the soy flag.
  • Phytosteryl macadamiate carries macadamia (tree-nut) lipids → tree-nut allergen disclosure.
  • Lanosterol is classically animal-derived (wool wax/lanolin) or fungal → not reliably vegan, and lanolin-adjacent sourcing raises allergen perception; specify source. A reason to deprioritize it for a clean/vegan-leaning brand.
  • Ergosterol is fungal/yeast-derived (vegan-compatible).
  • Sustainability: mostly oil-refining co-products (soy, canola, rice bran) or pine tall-oil by-product — generally a favorable, valorized-by-product story. Macadamia and avocado sourcing carry the usual crop-water/footprint questions but are not red flags.

8. Regulatory & claims

  • Status: cosmetic ingredients, not restricted (US FDA, EU, Health Canada, South Korea); CIR-reviewed "safe as used." Phytosterols/phytosteryl esters are widely used skin-conditioning/emollient agents. [19]
  • Cosmetic vs. drug line (per ../../CLAUDE.md guardrails):
  • Barrier-support, emollient, hydration-appearance, and soothing-appearance claims are cosmetic.
  • Drug-claim landmines specific to this ingredient:
    • Oral phytosterols lower cholesterol — a recognized health/drug claim. It must never leak into topical copy ("supports healthy cholesterol," etc. = illegal for a cosmetic and irrelevant to skin).
    • β-sitosterol for hair loss / 5α-reductase / BPH — therapeutic claims; off-limits for a cosmetic.
    • Atopic dermatitis / psoriasis / wound healing — the best mechanistic data are in these disease models; using them in consumer copy = drug claims. Keep for internal credibility only.
    • Lanosterol "reverses cataracts" — emphatically not a skin or cosmetic claim.
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "plant lipids that help support the skin barrier," "replenishes skin's natural-feeling lipids," "rich, cushioning emollient that helps soften and comfort skin," "helps reduce moisture loss (appearance)," "helps calm the look of stressed skin," "a cholesterol-like plant lipid for the skin's barrier."
  • ⚠️ Risky / needs substantiation or legal review: "rebuilds collagen," "anti-wrinkle/anti-photoaging," "firms / lifts," "anti-glycation," "antioxidant protection" (would need on-product substantiation), and anything implying disease treatment or systemic/cholesterol effect.
  • Substantiation tip: the cleanest cosmetic substantiation is a barrier-recovery / TEWL + hydration + tolerability study on the finished formula (mirroring Puglia's barrier-recovery design), plus an emollient/sensory panel — not a collagen claim.
  • See ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: low–moderate. Generic phytosterols / soybean sterols are inexpensive commodity unsaponifiables. Phytosteryl esters (macadamiate, oleate, isostearate) and branded sebum-mimetic complexes (L22, Cargill/Floratech) are mid-tier specialty emollients — pricier than the raw sterol but still far below premium peptides/growth factors. Purified β-sitosterol (food/supplement grade, often tall-oil) is cheap; cosmetic-grade fractions cost more. Lanosterol (cosmetic-grade) is niche/specialty; ergosterol is specialty/relatively pricey and less commonly stocked for topical use.
  • Suppliers: broad. Phytosterols/esters from BASF, Cargill (incl. L22), Croda, AAK, DSM-Firmenich, Axiom (Phytosteryl Macadamiate / AXIMOL PSE-M), and many oleochemical houses. Low supply risk for soy/canola/tall-oil sterols.
  • Strategic note: fits the brand's "buy commodity bases, invest in the brand" logic perfectly — phytosterols are a commodity barrier lipid; differentiation comes from the complete lipid system (with ceramides/cholesterol/FFA), the sebum-mimetic narrative, sensory, and an honest barrier story, not from the sterol being exotic.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • Overreach to avoid:
  • "Phytosterols boost collagen / reverse photoaging." The collagen/MMP/TGF-β data are in-vitro on mixtures (perilla USM) or animal (ergosterol mouse); no isolated topical-human RCT. Mechanistic narrative only.
  • "Plant cholesterol that lowers cholesterol in your skin / supports heart health." Conflates the oral cardiovascular evidence with a topical cosmetic. Illegal and biologically irrelevant topically.
  • "Lanosterol anti-aging / longevity sterol." The lanosterol story is an eye-lens proteostasis finding (contested in 2019), not a skin result. Marketing that smuggles "dissolves protein aggregates → anti-aging skin" is unsupported.
  • "Firms and lifts." Rests on a single unverifiable ~10-person study. Don't.
  • "Clinically proven antioxidant/anti-glycation shield." Test-tube only.
  • "One phytosterol = the whole group." β-sitosterol carries the skin data; lanosterol/ergosterol/esters are mechanistically distinct and not interchangeable.
  • Under-appreciated truths (real, usable):
  • Phytosterols are a legitimate, well-tolerated cholesterol-analog barrier lipid — and aged skin is specifically cholesterol-synthesis-deficient, so a plant cholesterol-substitute is a mechanistically apt (and vegan-friendly) way to round out a physiological-lipid blend. [8][9]
  • Phytosteryl macadamiate is a genuinely excellent, oxidatively-stable, sebum-mimetic emollient with premium sensory — a defensible texture/barrier asset with no hype required.
  • β-sitosterol's anti-inflammatory (NF-κB/MAPK/TSLP) mechanism is real and consistent across in-vitro and animal work — a sound basis for a "soothing-feel" positioning (kept in cosmetic language).
  • It can double as a penetration-supporting lipid for partner actives. [11]

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️→✅ SUPPORTING (not hero). Use β-sitosterol / phytosterols and phytosteryl macadamiate as the plant cholesterol-analog and emollient layer inside the signature barrier blend — alongside ceramides + cholesterol/FFA — and as a vegan-friendly way to tell the "skin-identical lipids" story. Skip lanosterol for skin (animal-sourced, ophthalmic-only evidence, vegan-unfriendly). Hold ergosterol as a "watch" item (promising 2025 photoprotection mechanism, but pre-human for skin — revisit if human data land).

One-line reasoning: Phytosterols are a safe, inexpensive, mechanistically sensible barrier lipid (the plant stand-in for the cholesterol that aged skin under-makes), with a real soothing/anti-inflammatory mechanism and a premium emollient ester (macadamiate) — but the topical-human evidence is limited to small barrier-recovery studies, and the collagen/photoaging/firming/anti-glycation halo is borrowed from in-vitro, animal, or wrong-tissue (joint, eye-lens) data. So: a credible supporting player in the barrier system, not a stand-alone anti-aging hero, and copy must be disciplined to barrier/emollient/soothing-appearance language.

Longevity-relevance note: the strongest longevity tie is barrier-centric and indirect — replenishing the cholesterol fraction of the SC lipid mortar (which declines with age) helps a barrier hold water and resist inflammaging-driving irritant ingress, exactly like ceramides. The flashier "longevity" hooks — lanosterol's proteostasis/anti-protein-aggregation effect and phytosterols' anti-glycation signal — are mechanistically intriguing for the aging-biology narrative but are not demonstrated for topical skin and should stay in internal "interesting science" notes, not on a label. Net: a foundation/enabler lipid in the longevity stack, not an age-reverser.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified: 2026-06-19 (independent adversarial re-check) · Method: each headline efficacy claim and every numbered source was re-checked against primary/secondary literature via multiple web searches and direct fetch where the publisher permitted (PMC/PubMed/journal/Nature). For each claim I asked: (a) is the evidence HUMAN + TOPICAL, or quietly borrowed from in-vitro / animal / oral / a different tissue (joint, eye lens)? (b) is the cited study real and does it say what the dossier claims? (c) is it tiny, industry-adjacent, or a mixture rather than the isolated molecule? This pass independently confirmed sources [1], [2], [3], [4], [6], [7], [8], [10], [12], [19] as real and (with the fixes below) accurately described, and found two errors now corrected in-text.

Corrections made during this verification: 1. Source [3] PubMed ID was wrong — the dossier cited PubMed 24492122, which is a different paper; the Han/Kim/Jeong 2014 TSLP study is PMID 24510054. Fixed in §12. 2. The §4 Tier-2 description of [3] overstated the route — it read "topical/systemic β-sitosterol." The study is a DNFB-induced NC/Nga whole-animal AD model with serum endpoints (histamine, IgE, IL-4); there is no controlled topical-skin efficacy arm to support "topical." Softened to systemic-only, with an explicit "do not read as topical-efficacy evidence" flag.

Overall evidence grade: C+ / B− (modest human-topical) for barrier-recovery & emollient only; C–D for everything else (collagen, photoaging, firming, anti-glycation, "longevity").

Rationale: The barrier/emollient core is mechanistically strong (phytosterols are a bona-fide cholesterol analog, and aged skin is cholesterol-synthesis-deficient — Zettersten/Elias 1997 [8]) and is backed by some human in-vivo barrier-recovery work (Puglia 2008/2016 [10]) plus an established emollient function for phytosteryl macadamiate (CIR [19]). That keeps the core out of pure-C territory but well short of ceramides' grade, because the human-topical studies here are small, surrogate-damage, and industry-adjacent, with no independent aged-skin RCT and no isolated-molecule topical efficacy RCT. Everything beyond barrier/emollient leans on in-vitro (perilla USM [2], anti-glycation [12]), animal (ergosterol mouse [4], wound rat [5], ASU joints [13]), or other-tissue/ophthalmic (lanosterol [6]) data that must not be transferred to topical-skin claims.

Source check

All cited studies were located and match their descriptions, with the following honesty flags retained in-text: - [1] Lin 2023 (Int Immunopharmacol, PMID 37075671) — real; β-sitosterol had the highest skin permeation of the common phytosterols (IVPT, OECD 428) and reduced IL-6/CXCL8 in activated keratinocytes/macrophages + an imiquimod psoriasis model. Mechanistic + ex-vivo/animal, as stated. - [2] Lee 2019 (Antioxidants) — real; the collagen/MMP/TGF-β results are robust but in-vitro and on a β-sitosterol-dominant mixture (USM), so they cannot be attributed to isolated β-sitosterol or to topical use. Correctly fenced. - [3] Han 2014 (Exp Biol Med) — real, but had a wrong PubMed ID and a route overstatement in this dossier; both fixed (see corrections above). It is an animal AD model with systemic/serum endpoints, not topical-skin efficacy. - [4] Ergosterol 2025 (Mar. Drugs / "marine-derived sterol") — real; UVB keratinocytes + topical murine UVB model; dual NF-κB/MAPK inhibition; pre-human for skin, as stated. - [6] Zhao 2015 (Nature) / [7] Daszynski 2019 (Sci Rep) — both real; lanosterol's anti-aggregation effect is ophthalmic and was contradicted by a 2019 replication. The dossier explicitly excludes it from skin claims — correct. - [8] Zettersten/Ghadially/Elias 1997 (JAAD) — real and load-bearing; confirms aged skin has a global SC-lipid drop with a specific fall in cholesterol synthesis and that a cholesterol-dominant physiological-lipid blend accelerates barrier recovery in aged skin. No fabricated studies were found. One misattributed PubMed ID and one route overstatement (both on source [3]) were found and fixed; otherwise every cited study is real and accurately characterized. The main discipline this dossier must keep is the four-forms-are-not-interchangeable rule and the borrowed-evidence walls (oral/animal/in-vitro/other-tissue), both of which are enforced above.
