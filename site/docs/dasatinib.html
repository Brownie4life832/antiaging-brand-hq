Ingredient: Dasatinib (D+Q reference) (INCI: none — not a cosmetic ingredient)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ rejected (for topical/cosmetic use) — retained as a reference / category-anchor for the senolytics class

One-line bottom line: Dasatinib is a prescription anti-leukemia chemotherapy drug (an oral tyrosine-kinase inhibitor). It is the "D" in the famous D+Q (Dasatinib + Quercetin) senolytic cocktail that put senotherapeutics on the longevity map. It is not a cosmetic ingredient, has no INCI name, is not topically studied in humans, and carries serious systemic toxicity and teratogenicity. It belongs in this repo as the reference compound that defines the senolytic category — not as anything we would ever put in a jar. The brand-relevant senolytics are its plant-derived partners quercetin and fisetin (separate dossiers), not dasatinib itself.

Plain language: Dasatinib is a targeted cancer drug taken as a pill for chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) and Ph+ acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL). Around 2015 researchers discovered that, given in short pulses, it selectively kills "zombie" (senescent) cells — which is why the longevity field cares about it.

2. Class & mechanism

Class: Senotherapeutics → senolytics (drugs that selectively kill senescent cells), as opposed to senomorphics (which suppress the senescent secretome without killing the cell).

Primary pharmacology (its day job): Dasatinib inhibits BCR-ABL, the SRC family kinases (SRC, LCK, YES, FYN), c-KIT, EPHA2, and PDGFRβ at nanomolar concentrations [10]. This kinase inhibition is what makes it an effective leukemia drug.

Senolytic mechanism: Senescent cells survive by upregulating "senescent-cell anti-apoptotic pathways" (SCAPs) — pro-survival networks that protect them from their own pro-apoptotic, inflammatory internal environment. Dasatinib transiently disables some of these networks (notably ephrin/SRC-dependent and PI3K/AKT signaling), tipping primed senescent cells into apoptosis while sparing healthy cells [2][3].

Why it's paired with quercetin (the "+Q"): The two drugs hit different SCAP nodes and different cell types. In the founding work (Zhu et al., Aging Cell 2015) [2]: - Dasatinib preferentially kills senescent human preadipocytes (fat-cell precursors). - Quercetin (a flavonoid; inhibits BCL-2/BCL-xL family, HIF-1α, PI3K/AKT) preferentially kills senescent human umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVECs). - Combined (D+Q) they cover a broader range of senescent cell types than either alone — the rationale for always dosing them together.

Dosing pattern ("hit-and-run"): Senolytics are given in brief intermittent pulses (e.g., 2–3 consecutive days, repeated weekly or monthly), not continuously. Senescent cells take weeks to re-accumulate, so you don't need steady-state drug levels. This is the opposite of how a daily cosmetic actGood would behave — and it matters for why "topical daily dasatinib serum" makes little mechanistic sense.

3. Hallmarks of aging / skin-aging targeted

Dasatinib's intended target is cellular senescence. Downstream of clearing senescent cells, the SASP (senescence-associated secretory phenotype — the inflammatory/MMP-rich cocktail senescent cells secrete) is reduced, which is why several secondary hallmarks are theoretically engaged. Honest mapping below — and note almost all of this is from non-skin or non-topical contexts.

4. Evidence review — by tier

The single most important honesty point: there is zero human topical evidence for dasatinib on skin. Every human result below is oral, and almost all is in sick populations (cancer, kidney disease, lung fibrosis, Alzheimer's) — not cosmetic skin outcomes. The skin-specific data is in-vitro and animal only. Below, evidence is ranked weakest→strongest format and flagged for what it actually proves.

Tier 1 — In-vitro (cell culture)

  • Zhu, Tchkonia, Kirkland et al. (2015), Aging Cell 14:644–658 — "The Achilles' heel of senescent cells." [2] The founding paper. Transcriptomic screen → identified dasatinib and quercetin as senolytics. D killed senescent human preadipocytes; Q killed senescent HUVECs; D+Q broadest coverage. Proves: the senolytic concept and cell-type selectivity in culture. Limitation: cell lines, not skin, not topical.
  • Takaya & Kishi (2024), Biogerontology 25(4):691–704 — skin rejuvenation study (in-vitro arm). [6] D+Q "selectively eliminated senescent human dermal fibroblasts (HDFs)" across three senescence models — replicative (long-term passage), ionizing radiation, and doxorubicin-induced — vs vehicle. Markers: SA-β-gal, p16/p21 (Western/qPCR/histology). Proves: dasatinib (with Q) can clear senescent dermal fibroblasts in a dish. Limitation: in-vitro; the in-vivo arm used oral dosing, not topical.
  • Darmawan, Mun et al. (2023), Acta Derm Venereol 103:adv4475 — keloid fibroblasts. [7] Cultured keloid fibroblasts treated with dasatinib 1, 5, 10 nM for 24 h → selective clearance of senescent cells; procollagen fell dose-dependently. Proves: dasatinib clears senescent skin-derived fibroblasts AND reduces collagen output. Caution for us: the collagen-lowering effect is desirable in a keloid/scar but a red flag for healthy aged skin where we want more collagen.

Tier 2 — Animal (in-vivo)

  • Takaya & Kishi (2024) in-vivo arm. [6] Mouse-human chimera: nude mice with subcutaneously grafted aged human skin, dosed with oral-gavage D+Q for 30 days. Result: increased collagen density + SASP suppression in the grafts; no adverse events. Proves: systemic (oral) D+Q can improve some markers in human skin grafts. Critical limitation: the route was oral, not topical — this is frequently mis-cited as "topical evidence." It is not. Small n (not specified in abstract), xenograft model.
  • Numerous non-skin animal studies establish in-vivo senolytic efficacy: bleomycin lung fibrosis, diabetic kidney disease, intervertebral disc degeneration (Novais et al., Nat Commun 2021), adipose inflammation in old mice, etc. Relevance to skin: indirect — shows the drug works systemically on senescence, not that it does anything applied to skin.
  • Comparator (NOT dasatinib): Topical ABT-263 / navitoclax in 24-month-old mice reduced skin p16/p21 and SA-β-gal and sped wound healing [11]. This is the only clean "topical senolytic improves aged skin" proof-of-concept — and it uses a different senolytic. No equivalent topical-dasatinib skin study exists.

Tier 3 — Human, ORAL (the real human evidence — all systemic, none cosmetic)

  • Hickson, Kirkland et al. (2019), eBioMedicine 47:446–456 — diabetic kidney disease. [4] Open-label Phase 1 pilot, n=9 (+2 for assays). Oral D 100 mg/day + Q 1000 mg/day × 3 days. Results 11 days later: adipose p16⁺ cells −35% (p=0.001), p21⁺ −17% (p=0.009), SA-β-gal −62% (p=0.005); epidermal p16⁺ −20% (p=0.026) and p21⁺ −31% (p=0.016); lower circulating SASP (IL-1α, IL-2, IL-6, IL-9, MMP-2, MMP-9, MMP-12). Proves: oral D+Q measurably lowers senescent-cell burden in humans, including in skin (epidermis). Critical limitation: tiny, uncontrolled, sick population; measured biomarkers only — no skin appearance/function endpoint. Authors explicitly "caution against use of senolytics outside clinical trials."
  • Justice, Kirkland et al. (2019), eBioMedicine 40:554–563 — IPF (first-in-human). [12] Open-label, n=14, intermittent oral D+Q. Improved physical function (6-min walk, gait speed). Feasibility/tolerability focus.
  • Nambiar et al. (2023), eBioMedicine 90:104481 — IPF Phase 1 RCT. [13] Single-blind, single-center, randomized, placebo-controlled pilot. Intermittent D+Q feasible and generally well tolerated; underpowered for efficacy. The closest thing to an RCT in the whole D+Q literature — and it's a lung trial, not skin.
  • Gonzales, Orr, Musi et al. (2023), Nature Medicine 29:2481–2488 — SToMP-AD, Alzheimer's. [14] Open-label Phase 1, n=5, 12-week intermittent oral D+Q. Key for us: dasatinib CSF:plasma ratio only 0.42–0.92% — confirming dasatinib distributes poorly across tissue barriers; quercetin was undetectable in CSF. Well tolerated; cognition unchanged.
  • Ongoing/other: epigenetic-aging RCT (NCT04946383); D+Q in mental disorders pilot [15]. None are skin/cosmetic.

Tier 4 — Human, TOPICAL

None. Zero. No clinical trials, no case reports, no cosmetic studies of topical dasatinib on skin. The 2024 skin paper's in-vivo arm was oral [6]; reviews note the only human epidermal senolytic data (the oral DKD trial) reported "no effects on skin function or quality" beyond biomarkers [16]. Topical dasatinib for skin remains entirely hypothetical.

Honest synthesis of evidence quality

What people claim What the evidence actually supports
"Dasatinib rejuvenates skin" Oral D+Q lowers senescence biomarkers in skin; no proven change in wrinkles, firmness, or skin function in humans.
"Senolytics clear zombie cells in your skin cream" True for oral dosing / cell culture; no topical dasatinib product has ever been tested on human skin.
"Backed by clinical trials" Trials exist — for leukemia, IPF, kidney disease, Alzheimer's, all oral. None for cosmetic skin.
"Causes skin aging is settled science" A 2021 critical review judges senescence→skin-aging causation "highly suggestive but not yet conclusive." [16]

5. Topical bioavailability

Dasatinib's physicochemistry is borderline-to-poor for passive skin penetration, and its biology argues against topical use entirely.

Parameter Dasatinib value [1] Ideal for topical/transdermal Verdict
Molecular weight 488 Da < 500 Da (ideally ~400) Marginal — near the upper limit
XLogP 3.6 1–3 Slightly too lipophilic; tends to lodge in stratum corneum, poor flux into viable epidermis
H-bond donors 3 low Acceptable
H-bond acceptors 9 low High — penalizes permeation
TPSA 135 Ų < ~90 Ų for good permeation Too high — strong barrier to passive diffusion
Aqueous solubility Very low (practically insoluble; pH-dependent) moderate water + lipid Poor — formulation-hostile

Bottom line on delivery: With TPSA ~135 Ų, 9 H-bond acceptors, MW ~488, and very low water solubility, dasatinib is a poor passive-permeation candidate; advanced delivery (nanocarriers, penetration enhancers) would be needed even to attempt it, with no published evidence that it works. The CSF:plasma ratio of <1% [14] independently shows the molecule does not readily cross biological barriers. Mechanistically, a senolytic needs only brief pulsed exposure ("hit-and-run") — a daily-applied topical is a conceptual mismatch. Practically, delivering an apoptosis-inducing kinase inhibitor into living skin is exactly what you do NOT want from a leave-on cosmetic.

6. Formulation notes

For completeness only — we will not formulate with this.

  • "Effective concentration": In-vitro senolytic activity for dasatinib is at ~0.1–1 µM (250 nM commonly used); keloid work used 1–10 nM intralesionally [7]. There is no established topical concentration — none has been validated on human skin.
  • pH / stability: Solubility is highly pH-dependent (better in acidic media); the molecule is otherwise a stable crystalline solid as a tablet API, but its cosmetic stability/compatibility profile is uncharacterized.
  • Pairings: Mechanistically inseparable from quercetin (D+Q is the unit). Would conflict with the brand's barrier/collagen-building actives by being an apoptosis inducer.
  • Sensory: N/A — never formulated cosmetically.

7. Safety

This is the section that ends the conversation for a cosmetic brand.

  • It is chemotherapy. Dasatinib is a cytotoxic, apoptosis-inducing oncology drug. Its systemic adverse-event profile (oral) includes myelosuppression, fluid retention, pleural effusion (~17% in CML cohorts), pericardial effusion, pulmonary hypertension, QT/conduction effects, cardiac ischemia, hemorrhage, GI toxicity, fatigue, and skin rash [17][18].
  • Pregnancy / teratogenicity: May cause fetal harm. Animal studies showed embryo-fetal toxicity (skeletal malformations, reduced ossification, fetal death) at plasma levels below human therapeutic doses; post-marketing reports of spontaneous abortion and fetal anomalies. AU TGA pregnancy Category D. "Should not be used in women who are pregnant or contemplating pregnancy." [19] A leave-on facial product used by women of childbearing age cannot contain a Category-D teratogen.
  • Irritation / sensitization (topical): Untested on human skin; an apoptosis-inducing TKI applied to living epidermis raises clear theoretical risk of irritation, impaired wound healing, and barrier harm — the opposite of this brand's barrier-repair thesis.
  • Sun sensitivity: Not characterized topically; moot given the above.
  • Occupational/handling: As a cytotoxic API it requires controlled handling — incompatible with consumer manufacturing of a cosmetic.

8. Regulatory & claims

  • Regulatory class: Prescription drug, full stop. Putting dasatinib in a cosmetic is not "claims risk" — it is selling an unapproved new drug / an adulterated cosmetic. In the US it would render the product a drug (and an illegal one); equivalent prohibitions apply in EU/UK/etc. There is no cosmetic pathway for this molecule.
  • Claims discipline (per CLAUDE.md): Even discussing it in marketing, senolytic language ("clears senescent/zombie cells," "kills aging cells," "reverses cellular aging") is drug-territory structure/function claiming that would demand substantiation we don't have and likely cross into disease/drug claims. Per the glossary cheat-sheet, "treats / removes aging cells" is exactly the kind of language to flag for legal. Do not use dasatinib in any product or name-drop it as an active in copy.
  • Where it can appear: As educational/category context ("the senolytic field began with the D+Q discovery") in long-form science content — clearly framed as research, not as something in our formula.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Form available: Pharmaceutical API / finished oral tablets only (Sprycel + generics). No cosmetic-grade supply chain.
  • Cost: Branded Sprycel retails roughly $19,000–$22,000 per 30× 100 mg tablets (~$600–700/tablet); even discounted ~$850/month [20]. Generic dasatinib has begun entering the US market but remains a specialty oncology cost item. This is orders of magnitude outside any cosmetic raw-material budget, and irrelevant since cosmetic use is illegal anyway.
  • Supply considerations: Controlled cytotoxic handling; not sold for topical/cosmetic manufacture.

10. Hype vs evidence

The hype: "Senolytics are the future of anti-aging skincare — clear your zombie cells." Senolytic skincare is a real 2024–2026 marketing wave, and the D+Q discovery is the headline science everyone cites. The market for senolytics/anti-aging pharma was ~$4.1B in 2024 and growing [21].

The reality for dasatinib specifically: 1. Dasatinib is never the cosmetic ingredient. When brands market "senolytic skincare," they use quercetin, fisetin, or peptides — plant-derived, cosmetic-legal molecules. Dasatinib is the prescription chemo half of the famous duo and is not in any cream. 2. All human dasatinib data is oral and in sick patients — and even there, the skin readouts are biomarkers with no demonstrated change in skin appearance/quality [16]. 3. No topical human (or even topical animal) dasatinib skin study exists. The one "skin rejuvenation" paper everyone points to used oral dosing in mice [6]. 4. The collagen story is double-edged: dasatinib reduced procollagen in fibroblasts [7] — not obviously what you want in aged skin. 5. The honest senescence-skincare caveat, straight from the critical literature: causation of skin aging by senescent cells is "highly suggestive but not yet conclusive," and the only human epidermal senolytic data showed no skin-quality effect [16].

Verdict on hype: For dasatinib as a topical anti-aging active, the gap between hype and evidence is enormous — there is essentially no there there for cosmetics. The legitimate excitement is about the senolytic category and its cosmetic-legal members (quercetin, fisetin) — and even those are early.

11. Verdict for the brand

❌ SKIP — as an ingredient. Retain as a category reference.

Reasoning: - It is a prescription chemotherapy drug, not a cosmetic ingredient — no INCI, no cosmetic grade, illegal to formulate into a cosmetic, teratogenic (Cat. D), and systemically toxic. This is an immediate, non-negotiable disqualifier. - No topical human evidence whatsoever; all skin data is in-vitro or oral-in-mice. Even the best oral human data shows biomarker shifts with no proven change in how skin looks or functions [4][16]. - Poor topical permeation chemistry (TPSA 135, MW 488, very low solubility) and a mechanistic mismatch (pulsed apoptosis inducer vs daily leave-on cosmetic). - Brand-thesis conflict: our north star is barrier repair and collagen support; dasatinib is an apoptosis-inducing kinase inhibitor that lowered procollagen in fibroblasts [7] — wrong direction, wrong risk profile.

What to do instead (the constructive part): - Keep this dossier as the anchor for the "Senotherapeutics & senolytics" class. Dasatinib (D+Q) is the reference that legitimizes the category and gives us credible, science-forward education content. - Route real interest to the cosmetic-legal senolytics: quercetin (the "+Q", plant flavonoid, topical precedent) and fisetin (selective dermal-fibroblast senolytic with at least one topical animal study [8]). Those deserve their own full dossiers and are where any "senolytic" angle for the brand should live. - If we ever want a "senescence" story, lean on senomorphics (SASP-suppressing actives — niacinamide-adjacent, certain peptides, antioxidants) which are far better suited to leave-on cosmetics than cell-killing senolytics.

Longevity note: Dasatinib is genuinely important longevity science — it's the molecule that turned "remove senescent cells" from theory into a testable human intervention, and it remains the benchmark senolytic in geroscience trials. That importance is as an oral, intermittent, physician-supervised investigational therapy, not as a skincare active. For a credible skin-longevity brand, the right posture is to respect and reference the science while clearly not selling the drug — and to channel the "longevity / clear senescent cells" consumer interest toward the safe, legal, topically-plausible members of the class.

Funding / size caveats (now made explicit)

  • The senolytic human literature is dominated by the Mayo/Kirkland group (Hickson, Justice, Xu, Gonzales all share Kirkland/Tchkonia or close collaborators). This is not industry-funded hype, but it is a single-lineage evidence base — independent replication is thin.
