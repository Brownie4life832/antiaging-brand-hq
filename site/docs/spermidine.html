Ingredient: Spermidine (INCI: Spermidine)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Antioxidants (polyamine; autophagy inducer / geroprotector)

One-line honest summary: Spermidine is the marquee "longevity molecule" — its autophagy/lifespan science is genuinely strong, but almost all of that strength is oral / animal / cell-model, not human topical skin. The single human topical study that exists used Streptococcus-secreted spermidine, not a purified topical dose. Treat it as a narrative/supporting active, not an evidence-backed topical hero.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Spermidine
  • INCI: Spermidine (also appears as Spermidine HCl / spermidine trihydrochloride for salt forms; often delivered via wheat-germ extract — INCI Triticum Vulgare (Wheat) Germ Extract — as a natural source)
  • CAS: 124-20-9
  • Molecular formula / weight: C₇H₁₉N₃ · ~145.25 g/mol (a very small molecule — relevant to penetration prospects)
  • Chemical class: Aliphatic polyamine (triamine). IUPAC: N′-(3-aminopropyl)butane-1,4-diamine; structure NH₂(CH₂)₃NH(CH₂)₄NH₂.
  • Family: Putrescine (diamine) → spermidine (triamine) → spermine (tetramine). Synthesized from putrescine by spermidine synthase using decarboxylated S-adenosylmethionine. [11]
  • Physical/chemistry notes: Highly water-soluble (~145 g/L at 20 °C). The free base is strongly alkaline (pH ~12–13.5 in water) — a polycationic base — which matters a lot for formulation (see §6). [11]
  • Key forms/derivatives:
  • Purified/synthetic spermidine or spermidine trihydrochloride (salt; easier to handle, less alkaline).
  • Wheat-germ extract standardized to spermidine content (the dominant supplement source; standardization varies — verify on COA).
  • Microbially produced spermidine (e.g., Streptococcus-secreted — the basis of the one human topical skin study). [6]
  • AA-SPMD (ascorbic-acid-conjugated spermidine) — an experimental cosmetic derivative, in-vitro only. [9]

2. Class & mechanism (how it works at the skin/cell level)

Spermidine is best understood as a caloric-restriction mimetic / geroprotector whose headline action is induction of autophagy — the cell's recycling pathway that clears damaged proteins and organelles.

Mechanistic pillars (mostly established in non-skin systems): 1. Autophagy induction via histone acetyltransferase (HAT) inhibition. Spermidine inhibits HATs (notably EP300), causing epigenetic deacetylation of histone H3 and upregulation of autophagy genes (ATG7, ATG11, ATG15, etc.). This is the original Eisenberg 2009 mechanism. [3] 2. eIF5A hypusination → TFEB. In mammals, spermidine is the substrate for hypusination of eukaryotic initiation factor 5A (eIF5A), which is required to translate the master autophagy/lysosome transcription factor TFEB. [4] 3. Antioxidant / anti-necrotic. Spermidine reduces oxidative stress and necrotic cell death and is itself a free-radical scavenger / polycation that stabilizes nucleic acids and membranes. [3][11] 4. Pro-proliferative / pro-migratory in skin cells. In keratinocytes (HaCaT) and dermal fibroblasts it increases proliferation, migration, and collagen I synthesis, and (in wounds) drives the uPA/uPAR pathway involved in ECM remodeling. [1][7][8] 5. Endogenous decline with age. Tissue spermidine falls with age; the longevity hypothesis is that restoring it re-enables autophagy. [3][5]

Skin-relevant translation (honest): items 1–3 are well-proven biology in general but demonstrated for the skin mostly in cell/animal models; item 4 is real in cultured skin cells and mouse wounds; whether a topical cosmetic dose meaningfully raises intracellular spermidine in living human skin and triggers autophagy there is not demonstrated.

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

The single most important honesty point: The deep, citation-rich evidence is for autophagy and lifespan/cardiometabolic outcomes via ORAL or systemic spermidine in yeast/worms/flies/mice, plus ORAL human RCTs for hair and cognition. For human TOPICAL skin, there is essentially one clinical study, and it delivered spermidine via topical bacteria, not a purified spermidine dose. Almost everything a "spermidine serum" implies is borrowed from a different format (oral) or a different organism (model).

Tier 1 — In vitro (cell & 3D models)

  • Eisenberg et al., 2009, Nature Cell Biology (DOI 10.1038/ncb1975). Foundational: spermidine induces autophagy and extends lifespan in yeast, flies, worms, and human immune cells in culture; reduces oxidative stress; mechanism via HAT inhibition. Limitation for us: human skin not studied; cell/model organism. [3]
  • Kim et al., 2021, Communications Biology (DOI 10.1038/s42003-020-01619-4) — in-vitro arm. Spermidine 80 µg/mL added to aged human dermal fibroblasts/keratinocytes ↑COL1A1, COL3A1, ELN, FBN1 and barrier-lipid genes (ABCA12), ↓SA-β-gal senescence; 3D full-thickness model showed thicker epidermis. Limitation: cell/3D models, n = 3 technical replicates; direct medium dosing, not transdermal. [6]
  • Ramot et al., 2011, PLoS ONE (PMC3144892) — ex vivo human hair follicles + isolated epithelial stem cells. Spermidine 0.1–1 µM (optimum 0.5 µM) ↑hair-shaft elongation >20%, prolonged anagen (fewer follicles entering catagen: 47–52% vs 67% control), ↑K15 in outer root sheath, ↑progenitor colony formation. Limitation: organ culture / isolated cells, not living humans; hair, not facial skin. [12]
  • Functionalized spermidine (AA-SPMD), 2026, Molecules (DOI 10.3390/molecules31040732). Ascorbic-acid-conjugated spermidine showed antioxidant activity and protection against UVA phototoxicity in human skin cells. Limitation: in vitro only; a derivative, not plain spermidine. [9]

Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

  • Eisenberg et al., 2009 (mice arm). Spermidine reduced oxidative stress in aging mice. [3]
  • Eisenberg et al., 2016, Nature Medicine (DOI 10.1038/nm.4222). Oral spermidine extended lifespan in mice and was cardioprotective (↓cardiac hypertrophy, preserved diastolic function, ↑cardiac autophagy/mitophagy/mitochondrial respiration). Cardioprotection was abolished in autophagy-deficient (Atg5-KO) hearts, proving autophagy-dependence. Limitation: heart/lifespan, not skin; oral. [10]
  • Ito et al., 2021, Cell Communication and Signaling (DOI 10.1186/s12964-021-00717-y; PMC7986284) — wound healing in C57BL/6J mice. Topical spermidine (2 µg/wound in white petrolatum) and systemic (5 mM in drinking water) both accelerated wound closure: 50% closure at 3.7 d (topical) and 2.8 d (systemic) vs 5.3 d (control). Mechanism via uPA/uPAR, ↑PCNA proliferation. Notably ↑IL-6 and TNF-α at wound sites (pro-inflammatory in this context). Limitation: mouse, acute wound (not chronic aging skin); the only in-vivo "topical skin" data and it's not human, and inflammation findings cut against an "anti-inflammaging" story. [8]

Tier 3 — Human ORAL (this is where most human data live — and it's not about skin)

  • Hair — Noser/Pekmezci et al., 2017, Dermatology Practical & Conceptual (dp0704a05). Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled: a spermidine-based oral supplement prolonged anagen phase of hair follicles. Oral; hair, not skin. [2]
  • Hair — Hanna N. et al., 2025, MEDS Public Health & Preventive Medicine (Clausius Press). RCT, n = 100, 90 days oral: anagen V–VI follicles +12.8 (≈52% increase) vs −5.3 placebo (p<0.0001); ↑Ki-67, ↓c-Kit. Oral; hair; smaller journal — verify rigor. [1]
  • Cognition (safety/tolerability) — Schwarz et al., 2018, Aging (Albany NY) (PMC5807086). 3-month phase IIa in older adults with subjective cognitive decline; spermidine-rich wheat-germ extract well tolerated, hint of cognitive benefit. Oral; safety value for us. [13]
  • Cognition (the negative landmark) — Schwarz et al., 2022, JAMA Network Open (DOI 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2022.13875). 12-month RCT, n = 100, ~10% increase in daily spermidine supply: NO benefit on memory or biomarkers vs placebo. Important counterweight to the "spermidine reverses aging" narrative; oral. [14]
  • Circulating polyamines — 2024 RCT (Soda/related group), older men, 40 mg/day. Even 40 mg/day minimally changed circulating polyamine levels — the body tightly regulates the polyamine pool. Directly undercuts simple dose→tissue-level assumptions. [15]
  • Immune/biological age — 2024 RCT, AM3 + spermidine + hesperidin (PMC11591374). Combination improved some immune/oxidative-inflammatory markers and a "biological age" estimate. Combination product — cannot attribute to spermidine alone; oral. [5]
  • Cardiovascular epidemiology — Eisenberg 2016 + UK Biobank cohorts. Higher dietary spermidine intake associates with lower blood pressure, lower CVD incidence, and lower all-cause mortality. Observational, dietary, confounded by overall diet quality; not skin. [10][16]

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters for a skincare brand — and it's thin)

  • Kim et al., 2021, Communications Biology — human topical arm. Double-blind split-face, n = 22 women (20–59 y), 28 days. The topical emulsion contained a 30% Streptococcus solution (live S. pneumoniae / S. infantis) whose bacteria secrete spermidine — NOT a purified topical spermidine dose. Results on the treated cheek: elasticity +12.1%, TEWL −11.3%, ↑hydration, desquamation index −18.4%, ↑Streptococcus abundance. [6]
  • Critical caveats: (a) the active was a bacterial culture solution, so benefit may owe to the microbiome/other bacterial metabolites, not spermidine per se; (b) very small, women-only, single-center, 28 days; (c) this is the only human topical skin study and it does not validate a conventional "X% spermidine serum."
  • PDRN–spermidine nanoparticle, 2025, Int. J. Biol. Macromol. (S0141813025075415). A polydeoxyribonucleotide–spermidine nanoparticle complex; reported human use improving moisture, TEWL, gloss, elasticity. Combination/nanoparticle — cannot attribute to spermidine alone; limited published detail; verify independence/sample size. [17]

Bottom line by tier: Proven for spermidine specifically → autophagy & lifespan (yeast→mice), cardioprotection (mice), oral hair RCTs. NOT proven for purified topical spermidine in human skin. Any topical anti-aging claim today rests on borrowed (oral/animal/cell) or indirect (bacteria-delivered) evidence.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • In favor: Very small (~145 g/mol) and highly water-soluble → on size alone, far better penetration prospects than peptides/proteins/growth factors.
  • Against / unknown: Spermidine is a polycation (three protonatable amines) at skin pH. Highly charged, hydrophilic molecules generally penetrate the lipophilic stratum corneum poorly without a delivery system; the strong positive charge can also bind to skin-surface anions and stay superficial. There are no published human transdermal/penetration data quantifying how much topical spermidine reaches viable epidermis/dermis or raises intracellular spermidine.
  • Regulatory homeostasis problem: Cells/tissues tightly regulate the polyamine pool — even 40 mg/day oral barely moves circulating levels [15] — so even if some penetrates, the cell may export/catabolize it. This is a real mechanistic skeptic's point against assuming "applied = active inside the cell."
  • Practical read: Penetration is plausible but unproven; delivery systems (nanoparticles, conjugates like AA-SPMD) are being explored precisely because plain spermidine delivery is uncertain. [9][17]

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: No established topical efficacy concentration in humans. In-vitro actives were 80 µg/mL (Kim, cell medium) and 0.1–1 µM (Ramot, hair organ culture) — these are culture doses, not formulation guidance. Cosmetic products use it at low (often undisclosed/fractional-percent) levels, frequently via wheat-germ extract. Formulate conservatively and do not imply a validated dose-response.
  • pH: Free-base spermidine is strongly alkaline (~12–13.5 neat in water) [11]; adding it raises formula pH and can destabilize pH-sensitive partners and disrupt the acid mantle. Use the HCl salt and/or buffer back to a skin-friendly pH ~5–6. Account for its buffering capacity.
  • Stability: Polyamines are reactive amines — can participate in Maillard-type browning/discoloration with reducing sugars/aldehydes and may react with some preservatives. Watch color/odor over time; run stability and challenge testing. Limited public stability data — treat as a known unknown.
  • Pairings (conceptual): Niacinamide (barrier/B3 + NAD-renewal story), resveratrol (co-geroprotector that synergizes with spermidine on autophagy in models [4]), ceramides (barrier thesis), antioxidants. AA-SPMD pairs the molecule with vitamin C by design. [9]
  • Conflicts: Because it's a strong base/polycation: avoid co-formulating raw with low-pH actives (L-ascorbic acid, AHAs) without neutralizing; potential incompatibility with anionic systems/some preservatives. Test direct acids separately or use the buffered salt.
  • Sensory: At low use levels, no notable color/odor expected from purified salt; wheat-germ extract can carry slight color/scent.

7. Safety

  • Irritation/sensitization: No specific topical irritation/sensitization signal at cosmetic levels; it's endogenous to skin. Caveat: the free base is highly alkaline, so an unbuffered formula could irritate — neutralize. No published human topical patch-test/HRIPT data found — generate our own. [7]
  • Sun sensitivity: No photosensitivity flag; mechanistically autophagy is protective under UV. No human topical photo-data. [9]
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: For oral use, EU novel-food authorization of wheat-germ spermidine (up to 6 mg/day) excludes pregnant/breastfeeding women — a caution-by-default signal. No topical pregnancy data; advise conservative copy ("consult your doctor"). [18]
  • Proliferation/cancer nuance: Polyamines drive cell proliferation and tumors are polyamine-avid; oncologists often advise against polyamine supplementation during active cancer. Conversely, dietary spermidine is inversely associated with cancer and all-cause mortality in cohorts. Net: no alarm for topical cosmetic use, but don't market to oncology patients and keep use levels sensible. [16][19]
  • Sourcing/allergen: Wheat-germ-derived material → disclose source for wheat/gluten-aware customers (contact-allergy risk is low but relevant to labeling/trust). Synthetic/fermentation-derived avoids this.
  • General tolerability (oral, as supportive context): 3–12-month oral trials and even 40 mg/day were well tolerated with no product-related adverse events. [13][14][15]

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic ingredient status: Listed as INCI Spermidine, CAS 124-20-9; functions cited as antioxidant / skin-conditioning. Note vendor commentary that cosmetic use may be restricted in some global markets — confirm legality per target market before formulating. [7][20]
  • The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame): "Autophagy," "cellular renewal," "anti-aging," "boosts collagen," "extends cell lifespan" all flirt with structure/function (drug) claims. Importing the oral/animal longevity literature into topical claims is the central legal trap here.
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "inspired by longevity / cellular-renewal science," "a polyamine naturally found in skin," "supports the look of fresh, renewed skin," "helps improve the appearance of skin elasticity," "supports the moisture barrier" (and even the barrier/elasticity wording should be hedged given only one indirect human topical study).
  • ⚠️ Risky / substantiation-or-legal-review: "activates autophagy in your skin," "detoxifies / self-cleans cells," "anti-aging at the cellular level," "extends skin-cell lifespan," "increases collagen by X%," "reverses aging." None are supported for purified topical human use.
  • Substantiation reality: To make even modest topical performance claims we'd want our own finished-formula human testing; the literature won't substantiate a conventional spermidine serum. See ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Forms/suppliers: Synthetic spermidine and spermidine HCl from fine-chemical suppliers (Sigma-Aldrich, Santa Cruz, Glentham, and Chinese manufacturers e.g., Haihang); cosmetic-grade often via standardized wheat-germ extract suppliers riding the oral-supplement longevity boom.
  • Cost tier: Mid. Purified spermidine is inexpensive as a raw chemical; cosmetic-grade standardized extracts and any delivery-enhanced versions (nanoparticle, AA-SPMD conjugate) cost more.
  • Supply/standardization risk: With plant extracts, spermidine content varies — require a COA with quantified spermidine; specify synthetic/fermentation grade if avoiding wheat allergen messaging.

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • The format switch. Marketing leans on yeast/fly/mouse lifespan and human oral data, then sells a topical serum as if those transfer. They don't, automatically.
  • "Activates autophagy in your skin." Not demonstrated for a topical cosmetic dose in living human skin. Autophagy induction is proven in cells/animals via routes that bathe the cell in spermidine — not the same as a serum.
  • "The one human study proves topical spermidine works." That study (Kim 2021) used live Streptococcus that secrete spermidine, n = 22, 28 days — it validates a microbiome approach more than a purified spermidine serum, and benefit can't be cleanly attributed to spermidine.
  • Pool homeostasis is the inconvenient truth. 40 mg/day oral barely moves circulating polyamines [15]; assuming a topical dose floods skin cells ignores active polyamine regulation.
  • The cognition null. A 100-person, 12-month JAMA RCT found no memory benefit [14] — a useful reminder that "longevity molecule" ≠ guaranteed human outcomes.
  • Anti-inflammatory vs pro-inflammatory. Vendors say "soothing/anti-inflammatory," but the wound study showed ↑IL-6/TNF-α [8] — context-dependent, not a clean anti-inflammaging story.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ SUPPORTING — strong narrative asset, weak topical-efficacy asset (today). One-line: Spermidine is a credible, genuinely-researched longevity molecule that perfectly embodies "cellular renewal," but its strong evidence is oral/animal/cell, not human topical skin — use it as a science-forward supporting ingredient with strictly honest, mechanism-framed copy, not as a proof-backed hero.

Longevity-relevance note: Among all anti-aging actives, spermidine has arguably the best longevity pedigree (autophagy, lifespan extension across species, cardioprotection, top-tier journals) — which makes it an exceptional education/differentiation ingredient for a skin-longevity brand. The gap is purely the format/route translation to topical human skin, plus penetration and pool-homeostasis uncertainty. If we ever want to make real topical claims, the honest path is our own finished-formula human testing (and/or a delivery-enhanced form), not the existing literature.
