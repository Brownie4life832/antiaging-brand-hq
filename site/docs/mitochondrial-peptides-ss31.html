Ingredient: Mitochondrial-Targeted Peptides — SS-31 / Elamipretide; Humanin; MOTS-c (INCI: none established for cosmetic use)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ⬜ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ☑ rejected (as a near-term active) / ⬜ in a product Slug: mitochondrial-peptides-ss31 · Class: Peptides — signal, carrier, enzyme-inhibitor, neuro-inhibitor (here: mitochondrial-targeting / mitochondrial-derived peptides)

Reading note — three very different molecules under one brief. The brief bundles SS-31 (elamipretide) with the mitochondrial-derived peptides (MDPs) Humanin and MOTS-c. They share a story — "fix the aging cell's failing power plant" — but they are biochemically and developmentally distinct: - SS-31 / elamipretide is a synthetic aromatic-cationic tetrapeptide that targets cardiolipin in the inner mitochondrial membrane. As of September 2025 it is an FDA-approved drug (FORZINITY, for Barth syndrome) — that single fact dominates the regulatory section. - Humanin is a 24-amino-acid mitochondrial-DNA-encoded peptide (16S rRNA region) — anti-apoptotic, cytoprotective, neuroprotective. - MOTS-c is a 16-amino-acid mitochondrial-DNA-encoded peptide (12S rRNA region) — an AMPK-activating, exercise-mimetic metabolic regulator.

Critically: none of the three has a single published human topical (skincare) RCT. Their human data is systemic/injectable (heart failure, mitochondrial myopathy, macular degeneration, metabolic disease), their skin data is in-vitro and animal, and their "anti-aging skincare" reputation is almost entirely longevity-biotech narrative + injectable-peptide grey market, not cosmetic evidence. This dossier keeps that line hard.

0. The honest punchline (read this first)

  • Zero human topical evidence. There is no published human topical/cosmetic RCT (or even a credible vehicle-controlled human topical pilot) for SS-31, Humanin, or MOTS-c on skin aging. Every "anti-aging" human result is from injection/infusion for disease, not a cream.
  • The mitochondrial biology is real and genuinely strong — arguably the strongest mechanistic mitochondrial story in the whole portfolio. Elamipretide has randomized human data showing it raises in-vivo ATP production in aged human skeletal muscle after a single IV dose (Sweetwyne/Marcinek group, PLOS One 2021 [7]) and improved mitochondrial function in the failing human heart [8]. Humanin and MOTS-c plasma levels demonstrably decline with age in humans [11][13]. This is a legitimate "mitochondrial decline" hallmark hook — for the education layer, not the claims layer.
  • But mechanism ≠ topical efficacy, and the format gap is enormous. These are injectable peptides developed by pharma/longevity biotech. The skin-specific evidence is in-vitro fibroblast/keratinocyte work and rodent models, often requiring special delivery vehicles (e.g., milk-derived exosomes to carry SS-31 into skin [5]) precisely because the bare peptide doesn't deliver well.
  • SS-31 is now a drug — this is a hard regulatory wall. FDA accelerated approval (Sept 19, 2025) as FORZINITY (elamipretide HCl), subcutaneous injection, for Barth syndrome — narrowly indicated to improve muscle strength in patients ≥30 kg [3][4]. (Note the approval itself is qualified: the pivotal TAZPOWER crossover trial failed its primary endpoints — 6-minute walk distance and fatigue score — and accelerated approval rested on a secondary knee-extensor strength signal seen in open-label extension, with a confirmatory trial required [3][4]. So even the approval is not a clean efficacy win.) An approved-drug API in a cosmetic is a major regulatory problem in the US/EU (a cosmetic containing a recognized drug active intended to affect structure/function is an unapproved drug). This alone is close to disqualifying for a compliant cosmetic.
  • The clinical record is also mixed even systemically. Elamipretide failed its primary endpoint in the Phase 2 heart-failure trial (PROGRESS-HF [9]) and in the Phase 3 mitochondrial-myopathy trial (MMPOWER-3, n=218 [10]); benefit appears confined to subgroups. Honest framing matters: this is not a slam-dunk even in medicine.
  • Penetration is the unsolved problem. SS-31 is a small (~640 Da) but highly cationic (3+) tetrapeptide; Humanin (24 aa) and MOTS-c (16 aa) are large, charged polypeptides. None passively crosses the stratum corneum well; the skin literature leans on encapsulation/exosome carriers to get them in.
  • Grey-market reality. The consumer-facing "SS-31 / MOTS-c / Humanin anti-aging peptide" ecosystem is research-chemical vials and injectables sold online, not COSING-listed cosmetic ingredients. There is no established cosmetic INCI for any of the three.
  • Net: A scientifically fascinating, on-thesis-by-narrative family that is, for a real cosmetic launch today, a SKIP as an active — driven by (1) no human topical data, (2) SS-31's drug status, (3) penetration, (4) cost/sourcing as research chemicals, and (5) claims risk. Its value to the brand is as a "horizon science" education/credibility story about the mitochondrial-decline hallmark, not as a formulation input.

Why they're grouped. All three are "mitochondrial peptides" that converge on the mitochondrial-decline hallmark of aging: SS-31 by physically stabilizing the inner-membrane lipid cardiolipin; Humanin and MOTS-c as endogenous signaling peptides the mitochondrion itself transcribes (retrograde mito-to-nucleus signaling). Humanin/MOTS-c are genuinely endogenous to humans (they circulate in plasma and decline with age); SS-31 is wholly synthetic.

2. Class & mechanism

The marketed thesis: aging skin cells have damaged, leaky, low-output mitochondria → less ATP, more ROS, more senescence → "tired, dull, aged" skin. Mitochondrial peptides repair/refuel the power plant at its source, so applying them should re-energize skin and slow mitochondrial-decline-driven aging.

What each actually does, mechanistically (with the format the evidence comes from):

SS-31 / elamipretide 1. Cardiolipin binding & inner-membrane stabilization (core, well-characterized). SS-31 partitions into the inner mitochondrial membrane and associates with cardiolipin (the signature anionic phospholipid, ~10–20 mol% of inner-membrane lipid). This stabilizes respiratory-chain supercomplexes, improves electron-transport efficiency, and reduces ROS at the source [1][2][3]. Earlier "ROS-scavenger" framing has been superseded by this membrane-interaction model [1][2]. 2. Surface-electrostatics modulation (biophysical). SS-31 modulates membrane surface charge/electrostatics as a key part of its action — corroborated across labs [1][2]. 3. Improved ADP sensitivity / ATP output (ex-vivo + human). SS-31 increases mitochondrial ADP sensitivity (via the adenine nucleotide translocator, ANT) in aged mitochondria [6], and raised in-vivo ATPmax in aged human muscle after one dose [7].

Humanin 4. Anti-apoptotic / cytoprotective (in-vitro). Binds and antagonizes pro-apoptotic Bax/Bid/Bim, and interacts with IGFBP3, suppressing caspase activation and cell death [11][12]; activates ERK1/2, AKT, STAT3 survival signaling [12]. 5. Systemic metabolic/longevity signaling. Endogenous, plasma levels decline with age and correlate with longevity in mouse models; analog HNG improves healthspan markers in rodents [11].

MOTS-c 6. AMPK activation / metabolic regulation (in-vitro + animal). Activates AMPK, promotes glucose utilization, acts as an exercise mimetic; under stress translocates to the nucleus and engages NRF2 antioxidant-response genes [13][14]. 7. Anti-inflammatory / pro-collagen (animal skin). In a mouse skin-aging study (Li et al., cited via review [13]), injected MOTS-c increased skin collagen, attributed mainly to its anti-inflammatory action (lowering IL-6) [13][15] — the single most skin-relevant mechanistic result in this family, but it is animal, intraperitoneal. (Note: the specific "NRF2/MFN2-driven, D-galactose model" framing is not confirmed in the review and should not be asserted until the primary Li et al. paper is read directly.)

The honest mechanistic gap (applies to all three): the biology is excellent and the systemic human ATP/heart data for SS-31 is real — but the skin-aging mechanisms are demonstrated at the cell / rodent / injected level. No study shows that a topically applied mitochondrial peptide reaches living human dermis, raises dermal ATP there, restores cardiolipin in human skin, or visibly reduces wrinkles. The mechanism is far more developed than any topical efficacy proof.

Reality check: the only skin-specific hallmark data is oxidative stress / photoaging (SS-31, in-vitro + mouse, exosome-delivered [5]) and collagen/inflammaging (MOTS-c, mouse, injected [13][15]). Everything else is borrowed from systemic mitochondrial biology.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core of this dossier)

Tiers weakest→strongest format: in-vitro → animal → human ORAL/systemic → human TOPICAL (RCT vs lower). For each, I flag proven for topical human skin vs borrowed. The headline ("anti-aging skincare") sits at the weakest end: there is no human-topical tier for any of these peptides.

TIER A — In-vitro (cell / biophysics)

A1. SS-31 binds cardiolipin / modulates membrane electrostatics — the mechanistic flagship. - Mitchell W, Ng EA, Tamucci JD, et al. "The mitochondria-targeted peptide SS-31 binds lipid bilayers and modulates surface electrostatics as a key component of its mechanism of action." J Biol Chem 2020;295(21):7452–7469. PMC7247319. [1] (also biorxiv preprint [2]) - Findings: SS-31 partitions into the membrane interface; binding affinity/density track surface charge; reframes mechanism from "ROS scavenger" to membrane/cardiolipin interaction. - Limitations: biophysical/in-vitro; not skin; not efficacy.

A2. SS-31 improves ADP sensitivity in aged mitochondria via ANT. - "The mitochondrially targeted peptide elamipretide (SS-31) improves ADP sensitivity in aged mitochondria by increasing uptake through the adenine nucleotide translocator (ANT)." PMC10643647; PMID 37462785. [6] - Findings: mechanistic basis for restored bioenergetics in aged mitochondria. - Limitations: isolated mitochondria/muscle; not skin.

A3. Exo-SS31 mitigates UV photodamage in human dermal fibroblasts (the most skin-relevant SS-31 paper). - Milk-derived exosome-loaded SS31 study, Int J Biol Macromol 2025 (S1011134425000284). [5] - Design/findings: SS31 loaded into milk-derived exosomes (Exo-SS31); in UV-exposed human dermal fibroblasts it suppressed ROS and reduced UV damage, with greater efficacy than free SS31; photoprotective in a UV mouse skin model. - Critical caveat: the whole point was that free SS-31 has poor tissue bioavailability and rapid degradation, so it needed an exosome carrier — i.e., this is evidence against simple topical SS-31 and for a complex delivery system. In-vitro + mouse; no human.

A4. Humanin cytoprotection / survival signaling (in-vitro). Humanin binds Bax/IGFBP3, blocks apoptosis, activates ERK/AKT/STAT3 [11][12]. Robust biology — but neuronal/systemic cell models, not skin-aging efficacy.

A5. MOTS-c AMPK/NRF2 signaling (in-vitro). Establishes the metabolic/antioxidant mechanism [13][14]. Not skin-specific.

TIER B — Animal

B1. MOTS-c increases dermal collagen / lowers IL-6 (D-galactose aging mouse) — the key skin animal result. - Reported in the MOTS-c review (Mohtashami 2022 [13]) citing Li et al.: synthesized MOTS-c 10 mg/kg intraperitoneally, 6-week-old mice, 6 weeks → ↑skin collagen via ↑NRF2/MFN2 and ↓IL-6 [13][15]. - Limitations: animal, injected (IP), small; D-gal is an artificial aging model; not topical; effect mediated by systemic anti-inflammatory action.

B2. Exo-SS31 photoprotection in UV mouse skin [5]. Reduced UV photodamage — but exosome-delivered, not bare topical peptide; mouse.

B3. SS-31 broad preclinical organ-protection (kidney, brain, heart ischemia, etc.). Extensive rodent literature (e.g., LPS-induced neuroinflammation [16]; ischemia-reperfusion across organs [17]). Robust but non-skin; systemic injection. Establishes the molecule "works" mechanistically in vivo — not that it works on skin topically.

B4. Humanin/MOTS-c healthspan in rodents. HNG delays cognitive decline; MOTS-c improves metabolic/physical capacity and is exercise-induced [11][14]. Systemic, injected; not skin.

TIER C — Human SYSTEMIC / injectable (real, substantial — but NOT topical and NOT for skin)

This is where the family earns its scientific respect — and where the "borrowed format" trap is most seductive.

C1. Single-dose elamipretide raises in-vivo ATP in AGED human skeletal muscle (the most quotable human longevity result). - Sweetwyne MT, … Marcinek DJ, et al. "In vivo mitochondrial ATP production is improved in older adult skeletal muscle after a single dose of elamipretide in a randomized trial." PLOS One 2021;16(7):e0253849; PMC8282018 (medRxiv 2020). [7] - Design: randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled; n=39 healthy older adults (60–85 y) selected for poorly functioning mitochondria; single 2-hour IV infusion; ³¹P-MRS of first dorsal interosseous. - Finding: elamipretide rapidly and reversibly raised mitochondrial ATPmax vs placebo — first demonstration of a drug reversing mitochondrial dysfunction in vivo immediately after dosing in aging human muscle. - Limitations: IV, single dose, muscle (not skin), reversible/transient, surrogate endpoint, n=39. Compelling narrative support; says nothing about a topical cosmetic.

C2. MOTION trial — elamipretide & elderly muscle function. Phase 2 RCT, n=40, ages 60–85 with mitochondrial dysfunction; improved mitochondrial energy production [7-adjacent reporting]. Systemic; muscle; small.

C3. Elamipretide improves mitochondrial function in the FAILING HUMAN HEART. - Chatfield KC, et al. "Elamipretide Improves Mitochondrial Function in the Failing Human Heart." JACC Basic Transl Sci 2019; PMID 31061916. [8] - Ex-vivo human myocardium / mechanistic; cardiac, not skin.

C4. Elamipretide FAILED its primary endpoint in heart failure (honest counterweight). - PROGRESS-HF, Phase 2, HFrEF: elamipretide well tolerated but did NOT improve LVESV at 4 weeks vs placebo. PMID 32068002. [9]

C5. Elamipretide FAILED its primary endpoint in primary mitochondrial myopathy. - MMPOWER-3, Phase 3 RCT, n=218: did not meet primary endpoints in the overall genotypically diverse population; post hoc benefit only in mtDNA-replisome/CPEO subgroups. PMC10382259 [10]; subgroup post hoc [10b].

C6. Elamipretide in dry AMD / geographic atrophy. Phase 1 subcutaneous trial (eye disease) [4-adjacent]. Systemic/local-injection; not skin.

C7. MDP plasma levels decline with age in humans (Humanin, MOTS-c). Documented age-associated decline in circulating Humanin and MOTS-c [11][13]. Observational human biomarker data — supports the "mitochondrial decline" narrative, not a treatment claim.

There is no Tier C "oral skincare" data for these peptides — they are peptides, degraded by the gut, so they are dosed by injection, not pills. This further distances them from any "beauty-from-within" framing too.

TIER D — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters for a cream)

EMPTY. After multiple targeted searches I found no published human topical/cosmetic study — not an RCT, not even a credible vehicle-controlled pilot — for SS-31/elamipretide, Humanin, or MOTS-c applied to human skin for aging, wrinkles, barrier, or any cosmetic endpoint.

  • The closest skin work (Exo-SS31 [5]) is in-vitro fibroblasts + mouse, and it deliberately uses an exosome carrier because the bare peptide doesn't deliver — the opposite of evidence for a simple topical.
  • The consumer market for "SS-31/MOTS-c/Humanin anti-aging peptides" is injectable/research-chemical, sold by peptide vendors and wellness clinics — not cosmetic products with substantiated topical claims.

Bottom line for the brand: for topical human skin efficacy, this family is at the lowest possible evidence tier (none). Everything marketed as "topical mitochondrial peptide anti-aging" is mechanism- and injectable-data-borrowing.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • SS-31: small for a peptide (~640 Da) but highly cationic (+3) and amphipathic. Cationic cell-penetrating peptides can traverse intercellular lipid lamellae to some degree, but +3 charge fights passive stratum-corneum penetration, and the molecule is designed to be taken up by cells/mitochondria, not to cross the skin barrier. The skin literature's reliance on exosome encapsulation [5] is the clearest signal that plain topical SS-31 penetrates poorly and degrades fast.
  • Humanin (24 aa, ~2.7 kDa) and MOTS-c (16 aa, ~2.2 kDa): these are large, charged polypeptides — far above the rough ~500 Da "500-Dalton rule" heuristic for passive skin penetration. Unassisted dermal delivery of intact, bioactive peptide is highly implausible without advanced delivery (encapsulation, microneedling, etc.).
  • Stability: SS-31's D-amino acid confers protease resistance (a pharma advantage), but the skin papers still report rapid degradation / poor tissue bioavailability for free SS31 [5]. MDPs are even more labile.
  • Net: Even setting aside efficacy, getting an intact, active mitochondrial peptide into living human dermis from a leave-on cosmetic is unsolved. The whole point of the mechanism (act inside the mitochondrion of a dermal cell) requires crossing the SC → epidermis → dermis → cell membrane → mitochondrial membrane — a delivery chain no cosmetic-format study has demonstrated for these molecules.

6. Formulation

Largely hypothetical, because there is no established cosmetic-grade material, INCI, supplier spec, or use-level for these as cosmetics. Treat the below as constraints, not a recipe.

  • Concentration: No evidence-based cosmetic use level exists. Pharma/research dosing is injected (mg quantities systemically), which doesn't translate to a topical %. Any cosmetic inclusion would be an unsubstantiated trace for label-claim purposes ("peptide theater").
  • pH / charge: SS-31 (+3) and the cationic MDP regions are incompatible with anionic systems (carbomer-thickened gels, anionic surfactants/emulsifiers, many anionic actives) — cationic peptides complex and precipitate with anionics. Would need non-ionic/cationic-compatible bases.
  • Stability: Peptides are prone to oxidation, hydrolysis, and microbial degradation in water; SS-31 degrades quickly in tissue [5]. Realistically requires encapsulation (liposome/exosome/cyclodextrin) for any hope of stability and delivery — adding cost and complexity.
  • Pairings (narrative only): conceptually sits in a "cellular-energy/mitochondrial" complex with CoQ10/ubiquinone, PQQ, niacinamide (NAD⁺), acetyl-L-carnitine, creatine, succinate, urolithin A. None of these pairings is a proven topical synergy — it's storytelling.
  • Conflicts: anionic systems; strong oxidants; low/high pH extremes; anything that would hydrolyze peptide bonds.
  • Sensory: at trace levels, neutral. No sensory upside that justifies inclusion.

7. Safety

  • Topical cosmetic safety: largely UNCHARACTERIZED. There is no cosmetic safety dossier, CIR review, or SCCS opinion for these as topical ingredients, because they aren't used that way. Absence of evidence ≠ evidence of safety.
  • Systemic (injectable) safety — from drug trials: elamipretide's main reported adverse events are mild injection-site reactions [3]; it was well tolerated across HF and myopathy trials even where efficacy failed [9][10]. That is injectable tolerability, not a statement about topical sensitization potential.
  • Sensitization: unknown for topical use. Cationic peptides can carry sensitization/irritation risk depending on formulation; no patch-test/HRIPT data exists publicly for cosmetic use.
  • Sun sensitivity: not expected/known; no data.
  • Pregnancy: avoid by default. These are pharmacologically active, mitochondrially-targeted molecules (SS-31 is a drug); there is no topical pregnancy-safety data. A skin-longevity brand should not put pharma-grade mitochondrial peptides in a leave-on cosmetic used by pregnant users without data — defer to medical advice and don't make claims.
  • Sourcing safety / quality: the available supply is research-chemical / compounding-grade injectable peptide (vials, "not for human use" research labeling, variable purity, frequent TFA counter-ion). This is not cosmetic-grade raw material with COA-backed cosmetic specs. Quality, endotoxin, and identity control are real concerns.
  • Drug-status safety/regulatory overlap (SS-31): because elamipretide is an approved drug, putting it in a cosmetic isn't just a claims problem — it risks the product being deemed an unapproved new drug / adulterated cosmetic.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

The cosmetic/drug line is the headline issue here — more than for almost any other ingredient in the portfolio.

  • SS-31 / elamipretide is an FDA-APPROVED DRUG (FORZINITY, accelerated approval Sept 19 2025, subcutaneous injection, Barth syndrome — pivotal TAZPOWER trial failed its primary endpoints; approval rests on a secondary muscle-strength signal, confirmatory trial pending) [3][4]. In the US, a cosmetic that contains a recognized drug active and/or is intended to affect the structure/function of the body is an unapproved new drug and/or adulterated/misbranded cosmetic. Formulating with elamipretide as a "skincare active" is a direct regulatory red line, independent of any claim wording. The EU/UK position is similarly hostile to putting a medicinal substance into a cosmetic. Recommendation: do not formulate with SS-31/elamipretide.
  • Humanin and MOTS-c are not approved drugs, but they are pharmacologically active, injectable research/longevity compounds with no cosmetic INCI, no cosmetic safety review, and an investigational-drug-adjacent profile. Using them as cosmetic actives invites the same "is this a drug?" scrutiny, plus an unestablished-ingredient problem (no COSING/INCI standing in the EU).
  • ⚠️ Claims that would be drug-like / unsubstantiated (flag for legal — most are unusable):
  • "repairs your skin's mitochondria," "boosts cellular ATP / recharges skin cells" — structure/function drug claims; not demonstrated for a topical in human skin.
  • "reverses cellular aging / clears senescent cells" — overreach; no human topical data.
  • "clinically proven mitochondrial anti-aging peptide" — the clinical proof is injectable, for disease, in muscle/heart [7][8] — explicitly not topical skincare; quoting it for a cream is misleading.
  • any claim that name-checks elamipretide / FORZINITY or its disease indications — co-opting a drug's identity for a cosmetic.
  • ✅ The only defensible use is EDUCATIONAL (about the hallmark, not the product):
  • "Mitochondrial decline is one of the recognized hallmarks of skin aging" (cite the science generally) — true, and useful brand education.
  • "Our formulation supports skin's natural resilience" type soft language tied to proven actives, not to these peptides.
  • Key compliance nuance: the temptation is to borrow elamipretide's genuinely impressive systemic human ATP data [7] to halo a cosmetic. That is exactly the move to refuse — it is the most concrete, citable human result in the family and it is not about skin and not about topical use, and the molecule is a drug.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • "Mitochondrial anti-aging peptide — repairs your skin's power plants." — Mechanism real (systemically); topical-human skin proof absent. No study shows a topical mitochondrial peptide raises ATP or repairs mitochondria in living human skin. Overreach.
  • "Clinically proven (elamipretide is in clinical trials / FDA-approved)." — True that elamipretide is FDA-approved and trialed — for Barth syndrome, by injection [3][4]; and human ATP data is muscle, IV, single-dose [7]. None of that is topical skincare. Using a drug's clinical pedigree to sell a cream is the central hype move.
  • "Reverses the mitochondrial hallmark of aging in skin." — The hallmark is real and these peptides plausibly touch it biologically; but "reverses it in your skin from a cream" is unproven and, for SS-31, legally a drug claim.
  • "MOTS-c boosts collagen." — From one mouse study, injected (IP), systemic anti-inflammatory mechanism [13][15]. Not a topical human collagen result.
  • "Antioxidant photoprotection (Exo-SS31)." — Real in-vitro/mouse signal [5], but required an exosome carrier and is against simple topical use; mouse + cells only.
  • What's genuinely NOT hype: (1) The mitochondrial biology and the systemic human ATP/heart data for elamipretide are excellent science [1][6][7][8]. (2) Humanin & MOTS-c decline with age in humans — a legitimate biomarker of the mitochondrial-decline hallmark [11][13]. (3) These are real, FDA-touched molecules, not invented marketing peptides. (4) The honest flip side: elamipretide also failed major efficacy endpoints (PROGRESS-HF, MMPOWER-3) [9][10], so even systemically the story is mixed.

11. Verdict for the brand

  • Mitochondrial-targeted peptides (SS-31/elamipretide, Humanin, MOTS-c): ❌ SKIP as a formulation active (near-term) — ⭐ KEEP as a flagship "horizon-science" education story.
  • Reasoning: This is the single best mechanistic embodiment of the mitochondrial-decline hallmark in the whole research base, with genuinely impressive systemic human data (elamipretide raising ATP in aged human muscle [7]; improving the failing human heart [8]) and a clean longevity biomarker (Humanin/MOTS-c decline with age [11][13]). But for a real, compliant cosmetic launch, the disqualifiers stack up: 1. No human topical evidence at all (Tier D is empty). 2. SS-31/elamipretide is an approved drug — formulating with it is a regulatory red line, not just a claims issue [3][4]. 3. Penetration is unsolved — skin work needs exosome carriers because bare peptide doesn't deliver [5]; the MDPs are large charged polypeptides far past the passive-penetration limit. 4. No cosmetic supply chain / INCI — only research-grade injectable material; high cost; quality uncertain. 5. Mixed even systemically — major efficacy-trial failures [9][10] undercut a "clinically proven" halo.
  • Where it does fit: as premium brand education / thought-leadership about why mitochondria matter to skin aging — a credibility asset that signals scientific literacy without putting an unproven, drug-adjacent peptide in the jar. If the brand ever pursues advanced delivery (encapsulation/microneedle) and runs its own substantiation, the MDPs (Humanin/MOTS-c) — not the drug SS-31 — would be the side to revisit, and only with real human topical data.
  • Skip-if (i.e., basically always for now): any formulation slot; any "mitochondrial repair" efficacy claim; anything that name-checks elamipretide/FORZINITY.

One-line reasoning: Spectacular mitochondrial biology and real systemic, injectable, disease human data — but zero human topical skin evidence, an unsolved delivery problem, no cosmetic supply chain, and (for SS-31) outright drug status — so it's a skip as an active and a keep as an education story.

Main caveats a skeptic would attack

  1. Format mismatch (fatal for a cream). Every strong human result is injected and not skin. Borrowing it for a topical is the central credibility trap.
  2. Drug status (SS-31). Approved drug in a cosmetic = unapproved-drug/adulteration risk, regardless of claims.
  3. Delivery unsolved. Skin work needs exosomes [5]; MDPs are too large/charged for passive penetration. No cosmetic-format delivery shown.
  4. Mixed even systemically. PROGRESS-HF and MMPOWER-3 failed primaries [9][10] — "clinically proven" is only true in narrow, non-skin, subgroup senses.
  5. No cosmetic supply/INCI; grey-market source quality. Research/injectable peptide, not cosmetic raw material.
