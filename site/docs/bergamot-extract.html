Ingredient: Citrus bergamia (Bergamot) Extract (INCI: see §1 — multiple, route-defining)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Slug: bergamot-extract · Class: Autophagy & mitochondrial-targeted actives (citrus flavonoid polyphenol complex; geroprotector-adjacent / mitochondrial-antioxidant molecule)

One-line honest summary: Bergamot is one of the more mechanistically legitimate members of this autophagy/mitochondrial sub-class — its Bergamot Polyphenolic Fraction (BPF) is a genuinely well-characterized flavonoid complex (naringin, neohesperidin, neoeriocitrin, plus the unique statin-like brutieridin/melitidin) with real, repeatedly-demonstrated pro-autophagy (Beclin-1/LC3↑), mitochondrial-protective, anti-oxidant and anti-senescence activity — but almost all of that evidence is ORAL/systemic (cardiometabolic supplement) or in cultured non-skin cells. For skin specifically there are only a handful of in-vitro studies (anti-inflammatory keratinocytes; UVB photoaging/telomere protection in HaCaT) and zero topical cosmetic anti-aging RCTs in humans. Layered on top is a serious, brand-defining safety fork: the cold-pressed essential oil is a furocoumarin/bergapten (5-MOP) phototoxin and IARC 2A photocarcinogen, while the furocoumarin-free juice/peel flavonoid extract is the only defensible form. Treat bergamot as a science-forward supporting/narrative ingredient (use the FCF flavonoid extract, never the phototoxic EO), not a proof-backed topical hero.

0. The honest punchline (read this first)

  • There is no single "bergamot extract" — there are at least three, and conflating them is the central error. (1) Cold-pressed essential oil (INCI Citrus Aurantium Bergamia Fruit Oil) — the fragrant, phototoxic, furocoumarin-rich one (bergapten/5-MOP). (2) Bergamot Polyphenolic Fraction / juice or peel flavonoid extract (BPF) — the non-volatile flavonoid complex behind essentially all the autophagy/mitochondrial/antioxidant/anti-senescence science; this is the cosmetically-interesting, defensible material. (3) Leaf polyphenol fraction (BLPF) — an even richer polyphenol source, less studied. Any dossier or claim must specify which one.
  • The class fit (autophagy & mitochondria) is real and well-published — but mostly oral/non-skin. BPF reproducibly induces autophagy (Beclin-1, LC3-II) in liver cells, protects mitochondria from oxidative/ROS injury, lowers ROS, and activates Nrf2/SIRT1-type programs. These are exactly the "geroscience" levers this sub-class is filed under. The catch: nearly all of it is in hepatocytes, cardiomyocytes, endothelium, erythrocytes or oral human cardiometabolic trials — not skin.
  • Skin-specific evidence is thin and all in-vitro: anti-inflammatory protection of keratinocytes (Graziano 2012 [1]); UVB-photoaging + telomere/telomerase protection in HaCaT keratinocytes (Nisticò/BPF 2015 [2]). Both interesting; neither is human topical.
  • Tier 4 (human topical anti-aging) is effectively empty. The only human topical skin trial is UVB photochemotherapy of psoriasis with bergamot essential oil (a drug-like phototherapy use that deliberately exploits psoralen photoactivity — the opposite of a gentle cosmetic) [3]. No cosmetic wrinkle/elasticity/tone RCT exists for topical bergamot.
  • The safety story is the headline a skincare brand cannot ignore. Bergapten (5-MOP) + UV = blistering phytophotodermatitis and IARC Group 2A (probably carcinogenic) photocarcinogenicity [11][12][13]. EU caps furocoumarins at <1 mg/kg in sun-exposed cosmetics; IFRA caps bergamot oil at 0.4% in leave-ons for sun-exposed skin [12][13]. Only furocoumarin-free (FCF/BF) material belongs in a leave-on facial product.
  • Net: strong mechanism + strong oral/cardiometabolic pedigree + a unique flavonoid story (statin-like brutieridin/melitidin) + thin in-vitro skin data + zero topical cosmetic proof + a genuine phototoxicity/carcinogenicity fork. Supporting / conditional, never a hero on current evidence, and only as the FCF flavonoid extract.

1. Identity

Field Detail
Common name Bergamot (the citrus Citrus bergamia Risso et Poiteau; syn. Citrus aurantium subsp./var. bergamia). Grown almost exclusively in the Reggio Calabria province of southern Italy. The "Earl Grey tea" / classic cologne aroma.
INCI (route-defining — pick deliberately) • Citrus Aurantium Bergamia Fruit Oil = cold-pressed peel essential oil (volatile; phototoxic unless FCF). • Citrus Aurantium Bergamia Fruit Extract / Juice Extract / Peel Extract = the flavonoid/polyphenol material (the BPF-type extract — the cosmetically interesting one). • Citrus Aurantium Bergamia Leaf Extract = leaf polyphenol fraction (BLPF). • "FCF"/"BF" denotes furocoumarin-free / bergapten-free processed oil. Confirm the exact INCI, the part (peel/juice/leaf), the form (oil vs flavonoid extract), and the furocoumarin/bergapten spec on any raw material.
Key actives (flavonoid extract / BPF) Flavanone glycosides: naringin, neohesperidin, neoeriocitrin (the big three), plus rhoifolin, neodesmin, poncirin, rutin. Unique to bergamot: brutieridin and melitidin — flavonoid-3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl (HMG) conjugates that are structural analogues of statins (HMG-CoA-reductase inhibitors). On hydrolysis the aglycones are naringenin, hesperetin, eriodictyol, diosmetin, apigenin, luteolin (the pro-autophagic aglycone set). [4][5][6]
Key actives (essential oil) Volatile (93–96%): limonene 25–53%, linalyl acetate 15–40%, linalool 2–20%. Non-volatile (4–7%): furocoumarins incl. bergapten (5-MOP, ~0.2% of crude oil), citropten (5,7-dimethoxycoumarin), bergamottin. [10]
CAS / EC Bergamot oil (expressed) CAS 8007-75-8; bergapten (5-methoxypsoralen) CAS 484-20-8.
Chemical class A polyphenol/flavonoid complex (extract) — moderate-MW glycosylated flavanones/flavones, water/hydroalcohol-soluble — versus a terpene/furocoumarin essential oil (oil). The two could not be more different in penetration, function, or safety.
Physical notes (flavonoid extract) Typically a tan/amber powder or hydroalcoholic extract; flavanone glycosides are moderately polar and water/ethanol-soluble; bitter taste (naringin). Standardized BPF grades quote total-flavonoid % (e.g. "BPF99" ≈ 47% flavonoids).

Class context (why it's filed under "Autophagy & mitochondrial-targeted actives"): BPF's headline pharmacology is induction of autophagy (Beclin-1, LC3-II conversion, via JNK/ERK/p38 MAPK signaling) and mitochondrial protection (restoring mitochondrial membrane potential, lowering mitochondrial ROS, recovering respiration under oxidative/lipid stress). It also engages Nrf2/HO-1/NQO1 antioxidant programs and SIRT1/FOXO3 in vivo. That places it in the same conceptual neighborhood as urolithin A (mitophagy), spermidine (autophagy), and the NAD/sirtuin actives — i.e. it is a bona fide "re-engage the cell's quality-control machinery" molecule, with the usual caveat that this biology is demonstrated mostly via oral/systemic routes in non-skin tissues.

2. Class & mechanism (how it works at the cell/skin level)

Bergamot (as the flavonoid extract/BPF) is best understood as a polyphenolic mitochondrial-antioxidant + autophagy inducer with a side identity as a natural statin-like lipid modulator. Mechanistic pillars (most established in non-skin systems; a few in skin cells):

  1. Pro-autophagy (the defining mechanism). BPF and its aglycones induce autophagy dose- and time-dependently — ↑Beclin-1, ↑LC3-II, with activation of JNK, ERK, p38 MAPK — in liver cells, and the effect is amplified under lipid (palmitic-acid) stress. The natural polyphenol mixture outperforms the individual pure flavones (additive/competitive, not merely additive-of-equals). [6][7]
  2. Mitochondrial protection / mitohormesis. Under oxidative or salt/lipid load, BPF lowers mitochondrial ROS, restores mitochondrial membrane potential and respiratory function, and preserves mitochondrial integrity (shown in cerebral endothelium, cardiomyocytes, erythrocytes). [8][9][14]
  3. Antioxidant / Nrf2 axis. Strong radical-scavenging (high ORAC; DPPH/ABTS), plus induction of Nrf2 → HO-1, NQO1 and antioxidant enzymes (SOD, catalase), reducing lipid peroxidation. [4][8][15]
  4. Anti-senescence. BPF/bergamot juice counteracts chemically-induced senescence (↓SA-β-gal) in stressed cells, and in vivo upregulates SIRT1, NRF2, FOXO3, HO-1, NQO1 — a classic geroprotective signature. [8] (Note: the headline antisenescence paper used cardiomyocytes, not skin cells — see §4.)
  5. Statin-like HMG-CoA-reductase inhibition (unique). Brutieridin and melitidin carry an HMG moiety and competitively inhibit HMG-CoA reductase, the basis of bergamot's oral cholesterol-lowering reputation. [5] (Systemic/metabolic; not a skin mechanism, but part of the molecule's identity and narrative.)
  6. Anti-inflammatory (skin-relevant). In keratinocytes, the flavonoid extract dose-dependently suppresses ICAM-1, iNOS, NO, ROS and GAG induced by IFN-γ + histamine, without inhibiting proliferation. [1] In other models it blunts NF-κB and pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, IL-8). [2][15]
  7. Photoaging / telomere protection (skin-relevant, in-vitro). In UVB-stressed HaCaT keratinocytes, 38% BPF inhibited the UVB-driven drop in viability, the IL-1β over-expression, telomere shortening, and the fall in telomerase activity, dose-dependently. [2]

Skin-relevant translation (honest): Items 1–5 are well-established general/oral biology, mostly in non-skin cells; they are rationale, not skin proof. Items 6–7 are the genuine skin-cell datapoints — anti-inflammatory keratinocyte protection and UVB-photoaging/telomere protection — and they are real and on-class, but they are in-vitro on immortalized cell lines, not living human skin. Whether a topical cosmetic dose of the flavonoid extract reaches viable epidermis/dermis and reproduces these effects in vivo is not demonstrated.

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

The single most important honesty point: Bergamot's deep, citation-rich evidence is for the flavonoid fraction's autophagy/mitochondrial/antioxidant/lipid biology via ORAL/systemic dosing, plus oral human cardiometabolic RCTs (cholesterol/lipids). For skin, the evidence is two solid in-vitro studies (keratinocyte anti-inflammatory; HaCaT UVB-photoaging/telomere) and a mouse combination topical study. For human TOPICAL anti-aging skin, there is no cosmetic RCT — the only human topical skin trial is UVB photochemotherapy of psoriasis using bergamot essential oil's psoralen activity, which is a drug-like phototherapy, not gentle cosmetic anti-aging. A "bergamot anti-aging serum" today sells mechanism + the oral-supplement halo, not topical clinical proof.

Tier 1 — In vitro (cell & biochemical) — bergamot's strongest skin-relevant tier

  • Graziano A.C., Cardile V., Crascì L., Caggia S., Dugo P., Bonina F., Panico A., 2012, Life Sciences 90(25–26):968–974 (DOI 10.1016/j.lfs.2012.04.043). The key skin-cell anti-inflammatory paper. A well-characterized C. bergamia flavonoid extract (neoeriocitrin, naringin, neohesperidin) on human NCTC 2544 keratinocytes stressed with IFN-γ + histamine dose-dependently reduced ICAM-1, iNOS, NO, ROS and GAG without inhibiting proliferation. Limitation: single immortalized keratinocyte line; inflammatory not aging endpoints; no in-vivo/human; extract not fully dose-standardized to a product. [1]
  • Nisticò S., Ehrlich J., Gliozzi M., Maiuolo J., Del Duca E., Muscoli C., Mollace V., 2015, PharmaNutrition 3(2):pp. (DOI 10.1016/j.phanu.2015.04.002; PMID 26403416). ("Bergamot polyphenolic fraction counteracts photoageing in human keratinocytes" / telomere–telomerase modulation.) In HaCaT keratinocytes under UVB, 38% BPF dose-dependently inhibited the UVB-induced viability loss, IL-1β over-expression, telomere shortening, and telomerase decline. Limitation: immortalized line, in-vitro, UVB-acute model; "could be a useful supplement in skin care products" is the authors' own speculative framing, not proof; some co-authors are affiliated with the bergamot/nutraceutical commercial sphere — check funding. [2]
  • Janda E. et al., 2016/2018 (pro-autophagy of BPF flavonoids). Original analysis: Biochimie 2016 (S0955-2863-series methods) + companion Data in Brief 2018 (PMC6140830). In GR-LC3-HepG2 hepatocytes (DsRed-LC3-GFP autophagy reporter), the BPF aglycone mixture (naringenin, hesperetin, eriodictyol, diosmetin, apigenin, luteolin) induced autophagy dose/time-dependently (tested ~30–60 µg/mL; 6 h; effect amplified by palmitic-acid loading), and the natural mixture outperformed pure flavones. Limitation: liver cells, not skin; mechanism (Beclin-1/LC3) is the defining class evidence but borrowed-tissue for skin claims. [6][7]
  • Da Pozzo E. et al., 2018, Oxidative Medicine and Cellular Longevity 2018:9395804 (DOI 10.1155/2018/9395804; PMID 30116497). "Antioxidant and Antisenescence Effects of Bergamot Juice." Important correction to common mis-citation: the cell model was H9c2 cardiomyocytes (rat heart-derived), NOT skin cells. "Fantastico" bergamot juice (0.01/0.1/1 mg/mL) prevented doxorubicin- and H₂O₂-induced senescence (↓SA-β-gal), with an inversely-proportional dose trend (lowest 0.01 mg/mL most protective); in-vivo arm (mice, 1 mg/kg/day ×3 mo) ↑SIRT1/NRF2/FOXO3/HO-1/NQO1. Limitation: cardiomyocytes + mice, not skin; biphasic/inverse dose-response; do not present as a skin-fibroblast study. [8]
  • Remigante A. et al., 2023, Frontiers in Physiology 14 (PMC10348362; PMID 37457030). "Mechanisms underlying the anti-aging activity of bergamot extract in human red blood cells." In human erythrocytes aged with D-galactose, 5 µg/mL peel or juice extract prevented ROS, restored band-3 anion-transport, blunted glycated-Hb (A1c) formation, normalized SOD/catalase/G6PDH and GSH/GSSG. Critical safety datapoint: ≥10 µg/mL caused hemolysis and pro-oxidant effects (a biphasic, narrow useful window). Limitation: erythrocyte model, no skin relevance beyond shared oxidative biology; the pro-oxidant flip at higher dose is a real caution. [16]
  • Composition/biochemistry: Detailed UPLC-DAD-MS / LC-NMR characterization of BPF (naringin/neohesperidin/neoeriocitrin + brutieridin/melitidin) [4]; HMG-CoA-reductase inhibition by brutieridin/melitidin in HepG2/Caco-2 and enzyme/docking studies [5]; fruit-vs-leaf polyphenol profiling (Baron 2021, Antioxidants 10(2):141 — 108 compounds; leaf richer; NF-κB inhibition in reporter cells) [15]. All in-vitro/analytical.

Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo) — mostly ORAL/systemic, with one (combination) topical skin study

  • Topical skin/hair (combination — interpret cautiously). A mouse study applying bergamot + boxthorn (Lycium) extract topically ×42 days reported ↑SOD, ↑skin collagen content, ↓MDA, and significant hair growth vs control [3]. Limitations: it is a bergamot + boxthorn combination (bergamot's contribution not isolable), mouse skin, single study; cannot anchor a bergamot-specific topical collagen claim.
  • Oral/systemic anti-inflammatory & organ-protective. BPF/bergamot juice extract (BJe) is anti-inflammatory in rodent intestinal ischemia-reperfusion and other models (↓pro-inflammatory cytokines), and cardioprotective against doxorubicin cardiotoxicity via autophagy restoration. [8][14][17] Oral/systemic, not skin, not topical.
  • Oral cardiometabolic / NAFLD. Bergamot polyphenols improve dyslipidemia and liver pathology in mouse NAFLD models. [18] Systemic; classic "borrowed-format" data — do not import to topical skin.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL (substantial — but cardiometabolic/mood, not skin aging)

  • Oral cardiometabolic RCTs/meta-analyses. A large oral literature supports bergamot-derived polyphenols (BPF) for lowering total/LDL cholesterol and triglycerides and improving HDL/lipid ratios (the statin-like brutieridin/melitidin story); systematic reviews count ~20 human studies / ~1,700 subjects across cardiovascular, metabolic, skin and mood endpoints. [3][5][19] Systemic endpoints; the "natural statin" claim is oral, dose-dependent, and not a skin claim.
  • Note on a negative trial: a 60-day open-label study found low-dose BPF did not improve metabolic parameters in antipsychotic-treated patients — a useful reminder that even the oral story is dose- and context-dependent. [20]
  • No oral human bergamot trial measured a validated skin-aging endpoint (wrinkles/elasticity/tone) as a primary outcome. The skin mentions in reviews are predominantly the in-vitro/psoriasis data.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters for a skincare brand — effectively empty for cosmetic anti-aging)

  • No published, peer-reviewed human topical cosmetic RCT shows that a topical bergamot extract visibly reduces wrinkles, improves elasticity, evens tone, or changes any chronological skin-aging parameter in living humans.
  • The only human topical skin trial is photochemotherapy, not cosmetics: an RCT-style study in n=193 psoriasis patients applied bergamot essential oil to plaques 30 min before UVB, 3×/week, achieving significant PASI reduction and fewer sessions vs UVB alone [3]. This deliberately uses bergapten's psoralen photoactivity as a drug (PUVA-like) — it is evidence of bioactivity but the opposite of a safe cosmetic, and it must never be repackaged as cosmetic anti-aging proof.
  • Vitiligo/khellin/psoralen heritage: bergamot/psoralen has historic use in repigmentation phototherapy — again a drug use exploiting phototoxicity.
  • Most topical bergamot R&D is fragrance, aromatherapy, and FCF-safety engineering — confirming that the dominant topical reality is "fragrance ingredient with a phototoxicity problem," not "clinically-proven anti-aging active."

Bottom line by tier: Proven for bergamot specifically → autophagy/mitochondrial/antioxidant/anti-senescence biology (in-vitro, mostly non-skin) and oral human lipid-lowering. Skin-specific in-vitro wins (keratinocyte anti-inflammatory [1]; UVB photoaging/telomere [2]) are genuine and on-class. NOT proven: topical bergamot doing anything measurable for cosmetic aging in human skin. Any topical anti-aging claim rests on in-vitro + borrowed oral evidence.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Two molecules, two penetration stories — specify which.
  • Essential oil (terpenes/furocoumarins): small, lipophilic, penetrates readily — which is precisely why it is phototoxic (bergapten reaches viable epidermis and intercalates DNA under UVA). Good penetration here is a liability, not an asset.
  • Flavonoid extract / BPF (glycosylated flavanones): moderately polar, glycosylated, higher-MW flavonoid glycosides (naringin ~580 Da, neohesperidin ~610 Da) — penetration is modest at best. Glycosylation and polarity work against stratum-corneum passage; the aglycones (naringenin, hesperetin) penetrate better than their glycosides but are a minority unless the extract is hydrolyzed.
  • No published human-skin penetration data quantify how much topical bergamot flavonoid extract reaches viable epidermis/dermis or engages autophagy/mitochondrial pathways in living human skin. The in-vitro skin wins [1][2] bathed cultured cells in extract — not the same as a cream on intact barrier.
  • Practical read: For the desirable (flavonoid) form, penetration is the likely bottleneck for any dermal/anti-senescence intent; a surface-acting antioxidant/anti-inflammatory role is the more defensible topical use. For the undesirable (EO) form, penetration is fine but the payload is a phototoxin. Either way, the deliverable cosmetic active is the FCF flavonoid extract used for surface antioxidant/soothing support, with no validated dermal-remodeling claim.

6. Formulation

  • Form choice (the most important decision): use the furocoumarin-free (FCF/BF) flavonoid extract or FCF oil. Never use crude expressed bergamot oil in a leave-on facial product.
  • Effective concentration: No established topical efficacy concentration in humans. In-vitro actives were ~5–60 µg/mL (cell-bathing) [6][16]; the RBC model showed a pro-oxidant/hemolytic flip ≥10 µg/mL [16], underscoring that "more is not better." Cosmetic flavonoid-extract use is typically low (often fractional-percent of a standardized extract). For any fragrance use of FCF oil, respect IFRA (≤0.4% in sun-exposed leave-ons) and EU furocoumarin limits regardless of phototoxicity claims (see §8).
  • pH: flavanone glycosides are reasonably stable across mildly acidic–neutral cosmetic pH (~4.5–6.5). No special pH dependence for the extract; avoid extremes that could hydrolyze glycosides unpredictably.
  • Stability/sensory: polyphenols can discolor/oxidize over time (browning) and may interact with metals — chelate (EDTA) and consider antioxidants/inert packaging. The flavonoid extract is bitter but largely odorless; the EO brings the signature bergamot scent (and the phototoxicity baggage). Run color/oxidation stability and challenge testing.
  • Pairings (conceptual): other antioxidants (vitamin C/E, ferulic) and Nrf2 actives; niacinamide (barrier/anti-inflammatory synergy); the autophagy/mitophagy narrative cluster (urolithin A, spermidine) for a coherent "cellular housekeeping" story. Soothing partners (centella, panthenol) complement the anti-inflammatory data.
  • Conflicts: the EO conflicts with sun exposure (phototoxic) — disqualifying for daytime leave-ons unless FCF. Polyphenol discoloration can clash with white/clear aesthetics and light-sensitive actives. Broad antimicrobial activity (EO) can perturb preservative challenge tests and skin flora.

7. Safety

  • Phototoxicity / photocarcinogenicity — THE flag (essential-oil form). Bergapten (5-MOP) and other furocoumarins are phototoxic: documented bullous phytophotodermatitis ("berloque dermatitis") within 24–72 h of EO application + UV [11][13]. 5-MOP + UV is IARC Group 2A — probably carcinogenic to humans [12]. This is not a sensitivity quirk; it is DNA-adduct photochemistry. Mitigation: use FCF/bergapten-free material; obey limits (below). [11][12][13]
  • Regulatory limits (must be met regardless of marketing): EU (Reg. 1223/2009, per SCCP opinion): furocoumarins <1 mg/kg in sun-protection/bronzing products and, by extension, sun-exposed cosmetics; many references cite a 15 ppm (0.0015%) 5-MOP finished-product ceiling for leave-ons. IFRA: bergamot oil ≤ 0.4% in leave-ons applied to sun-exposed skin. [12][13]
  • Pro-oxidant/hemolytic at higher dose (extract form). The erythrocyte study showed bergamot extract flips pro-oxidant and hemolytic at ≥10 µg/mL [16]. While erythrocyte hemolysis ≠ skin toxicity, it is a clean demonstration that the antioxidant benefit is dose-windowed — formulate conservatively.
  • Irritation/sensitization (extract): no strong irritation/sensitization signal for the flavonoid extract at cosmetic levels in the limited data; citrus extracts are generally well tolerated. But there is no published cosmetic HRIPT/patch-test for our specific material — generate our own. Limonene (in the EO) is a labeled fragrance allergen in the EU and can oxidize to sensitizers.
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: No specific reproductive-toxicity data for topical bergamot were identified (CIR/CIR-adjacent assessments noted no relevant published reproductive studies for several citrus leaf/flower ingredients) [21]. Given the phototoxicity/5-MOP carcinogenicity flag and absent data, the prudent label position is avoid the phototoxic EO; FCF flavonoid extract has no specific pregnancy contraindication but defaults to "consult your doctor" pending our own assessment. (Oral high-dose BPF supplements are a separate question outside cosmetics.)
  • Sun behavior: any non-FCF bergamot in a leave-on = photosensitizing; even FCF should be paired with the brand's standard "use SPF" guidance for any antioxidant/anti-aging positioning.
  • Sourcing/allergen: specify part (peel/juice/leaf), form (oil vs flavonoid extract), quantified furocoumarin/bergapten content, standardized flavonoid %, heavy metals, pesticide residues (citrus peel concentrates contaminants), and authenticity (bergamot oil is an adulteration target). Demand a COA.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic ingredient status: the various Citrus Aurantium Bergamia ingredients are established cosmetic materials (fragrance, skin-conditioning, antioxidant). But furocoumarin content is regulated (EU <1 mg/kg sun-exposed; 5-MOP 15 ppm ceiling; IFRA 0.4% EO leave-on). Confirm legality and limits per target market and verify the exact INCI + furocoumarin spec of the supplied material before formulating. [12][13]
  • The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame): bergamot's whole appeal — "autophagy," "mitochondrial," "anti-senescence," "telomere protection," "statin-like," "cellular longevity" — is structure/function (drug-adjacent) language, and the strongest data are oral/non-skin. Importing them into topical claims is the central legal trap. The psoriasis/PUVA and vitiligo uses are explicitly drug/phototherapy territory — do not reference them as cosmetic benefits.
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "a bergamot (furocoumarin-free) flavonoid extract studied for its antioxidant activity," "helps defend the look of skin against everyday environmental/oxidative stress," "soothing botanical polyphenols," "inspired by autophagy/cellular-housekeeping science" (clearly framed as inspiration/heritage, not a skin effect).
  • ⚠️ Risky / substantiation-or-legal-review: "induces autophagy / mitophagy in your skin," "protects your telomeres," "reverses cellular senescence," "natural statin for your face," "boosts collagen by X%," "anti-aging clinically proven." None hold for purified topical human use. Also avoid any wording implying tanning/pigment benefit (the 5-MOP melanogenic effect is a hazard, historically abused in tanning accelerators).
  • Substantiation reality: to make even modest topical performance claims we'd want our own finished-formula human testing on the FCF flavonoid extract; the literature won't substantiate a conventional bergamot anti-aging serum. The furocoumarin limits and allergen labeling (limonene/linalool if EO used) need label/legal handling. See ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Origin: ~80–90% of genuine bergamot is grown in Reggio Calabria, Italy (PDO-protected oil). Tight geography → price/supply sensitivity and adulteration pressure.
  • Forms/suppliers: (a) cold-pressed EO and FCF/BF EO (fragrance houses, EO suppliers); (b) standardized flavonoid extract / BPF (nutraceutical-grade, e.g. "BPF99" ~47% flavonoids; supplement-driven supply chain) — this is the cosmetically-interesting material; (c) leaf polyphenol fraction (niche). Standardize to total-flavonoid % and quantified brutieridin/melitidin if telling that story.
  • Cost tier: mid for FCF oil; mid–high for standardized flavonoid/BPF extract (riding the cardiometabolic supplement boom); crude EO cheaper but unusable in a safe leave-on.
  • Supply/sourcing risk: (a) adulteration of bergamot oil is common — demand authenticity/GC profiling; (b) furocoumarin variability — require quantified bergapten/5-MOP COA; (c) pesticide/heavy-metal carryover in peel concentrates; (d) PDO/seasonal supply and price volatility; (e) ensure the flavonoid extract is genuinely from C. bergamia (cheaper citrus substitution).

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • The format/route switch (the big one). Marketing leans on oral cardiometabolic results and in-vitro autophagy/mitochondrial biology, then sells a topical serum as if those transfer. They don't automatically — and the desirable (flavonoid) form penetrates poorly.
  • The "which bergamot?" bait-and-switch. Brands cite BPF autophagy/anti-senescence science (the juice/flavonoid extract) while putting fragrant essential oil in the bottle — a different, phototoxic molecule with none of those data and a carcinogenicity flag.
  • "Protects your telomeres / reverses senescence." Telomere/telomerase protection is real in UVB-stressed HaCaT cells [2]; the antisenescence data are in cardiomyocytes [8] — there is no human topical proof, and effects are dose-windowed/biphasic (Da Pozzo inverse dose [8]; RBC pro-oxidant flip ≥10 µg/mL [16]).
  • "Natural statin for your skin." Brutieridin/melitidin HMG-CoA inhibition is an oral lipid mechanism [5] — irrelevant and unsubstantiated as a topical skin claim.
  • "Clinically proven for skin." The human topical skin trial is UVB photochemotherapy of psoriasis [3] — a drug use exploiting phototoxicity, not cosmetic anti-aging.
  • The safety asterisk vendors omit. Bergapten/5-MOP phototoxicity + IARC 2A photocarcinogenicity rarely appears in upbeat bergamot skincare copy — but it's the defining, citable risk [11][12].
  • What's NOT hype: the autophagy/mitochondrial/antioxidant biology is genuinely strong and well-published, BPF is unusually well-characterized chemically, and the keratinocyte anti-inflammatory and UVB-telomere in-vitro data are legitimately interesting, on-class rationale. Bergamot is one of the better-pedigreed molecules in this sub-class — the gap is entirely topical skin proof + the phototoxicity fork.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ SUPPORTING / CONDITIONAL — strong autophagy/mitochondrial pedigree and a great longevity story, real in-vitro skin-cell data, but zero topical cosmetic proof and a serious phototoxicity/carcinogenicity fork that mandates the FCF flavonoid form.

One-line: Bergamot (as the furocoumarin-free flavonoid extract / BPF, never the phototoxic essential oil) is a well-pedigreed autophagy/mitochondrial-antioxidant molecule with genuine in-vitro keratinocyte anti-inflammatory and UVB-photoaging/telomere data, but it has no human topical anti-aging RCT, penetrates modestly in its desirable form, is dose-windowed (pro-oxidant at higher doses), and carries an IARC-2A phototoxic essential-oil hazard — so use it (if at all) as a science-forward supporting/narrative antioxidant-soothing ingredient with strictly honest, mechanism-framed copy, never as a proof-backed hero.

Conditions that would upgrade it: 1. A standardized FCF flavonoid extract (quantified flavonoids; certified low furocoumarin) — never crude EO in a leave-on; and 2. Our own finished-formula human testing for any performance claim; and 3. A penetration/delivery rationale if dermal (anti-senescence) intent is claimed; and 4. Clean handling of furocoumarin limits + allergen labeling + the dose-window in formula and label/legal review. Absent those, it is a supporting/parking-lot candidate, not a lead active.

Longevity-relevance note: For a skin-longevity brand, bergamot is an attractive education/differentiation ingredient — it sits squarely in the autophagy/mitochondrial/anti-senescence geroscience story and pairs naturally with urolithin A (mitophagy) and spermidine (autophagy) to tell a coherent "cellular housekeeping & energy" narrative, with the bonus of a vivid, recognizable botanical (Calabrian bergamot, the Earl Grey/cologne note). The gaps are the familiar ones for this sub-class — format/route (topical), penetration (glycosylated flavonoids), and absent human topical proof — plus a sub-class-unusual photo-safety hazard that makes ingredient/form selection a hard gate. If we want it in a product, the honest path is FCF flavonoid extract + our own testing, letting proven actives (niacinamide, retinoids, barrier lipids) carry the efficacy claims while bergamot carries antioxidant support and the longevity story.
