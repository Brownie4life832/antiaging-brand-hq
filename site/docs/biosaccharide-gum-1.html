Ingredient: Biosaccharide Gum-1 / Fucogel (INCI: Biosaccharide Gum-1)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Humectants & hydration actives (anionic film-forming exopolysaccharide; "fucose-rich" microbial biopolymer)

One-line honest summary: Biosaccharide Gum-1 (trade name Fucogel, also Fucocert) is a genuinely useful, very-well-tolerated, sensory-and-hydration polysaccharide — think "a smarter, soothing, film-forming humectant cousin of hyaluronic acid." Its hydration, soft-focus sensory, and skin-soothing roles are real and supported by a CIR safety review and decades of cosmetic use. Its louder anti-aging claims (sirtuin-1, collagen-1, hyaluronic-acid synthesis up, "reverses skin aging") rest almost entirely on manufacturer/patent data plus a small cluster of old, uncontrolled, inventor-authored studies — not on independent, controlled human RCTs. Treat it as a strong supporting hydration/texture/soothing active, not an evidence-backed anti-aging hero.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Biosaccharide Gum-1; best known by the trade name Fucogel® (Solabia) — also sold as Fucocert®, and by other suppliers as e.g. BioYoung FGL-G, "fucose-rich polysaccharide / FROP."
  • INCI: Biosaccharide Gum-1 (sometimes paired with a carrier, e.g. Maltodextrin in powder grades).
  • CAS: 194237-89-3 (a second CAS, 178463-23-5, also appears in supplier listings).
  • Chemical class: High-molecular-weight anionic bacterial exopolysaccharide. The backbone is a linear polymer of a repeating trisaccharide unit: α-L-fucose → α-D-galactose → α-D-galacturonic acid (roughly equimolar fucose : galactose : galacturonic acid). The galacturonic acid carboxyls give the polymer its anionic (negative) charge. [3][7][11]
  • Molecular weight: High — on the order of ~1,000 kDa (≈1 × 10⁶ Da, i.e. >1 MDa). ⚠️ Manufacturer datasheets list "1.106 Da," which is a European decimal-comma artifact for 1,106 kDa; it is NOT a 1-dalton molecule. Some purified/lower-MW grades (e.g. Fucogel 1.5P) are offered for different sensory/penetration profiles. [7][11]
  • Physical form / supply: Most commonly a 1% (w/w) aqueous solution (clear, viscous, gel-like) or a powder on a maltodextrin carrier. Naturally derived content >95% (ISO 16128); ECOCERT/COSMOS- and often HALAL-certified grades exist. [4][7]
  • Manufacture (important sourcing nuance):
  • Modern marketing says it is "obtained by bacterial fermentation of non-GMO vegetable/plant substrates (e.g. corn-/sorbitol-derived sugars)." [3][4]
  • The original Solabia process and patent describe production by a strain of Klebsiella pneumoniae subsp. *pneumoniae (strain BEC 1000 / I-1507) — i.e. it is a Gram-negative-bacterium exopolysaccharide that is then purified to remove the producing organism and endotoxin*. [12] This matters for transparent "where it comes from" copy (see §7, §9).

2. Class & mechanism (how it works at the skin/cell level)

Biosaccharide Gum-1 is best understood as a multifunctional anionic polysaccharide that works mostly at the skin surface / superficial epidermis, plus a biological "fucose-signal" hypothesis for its claimed deeper effects.

Mechanistic pillars (in rough order of how well-supported they are):

  1. Surface film + humectancy (well-supported, physicochemical). As a large hygroscopic anionic polymer, it binds water and lays down a thin, non-occlusive, breathable film on skin (and hair). The film holds water at the surface, reduces transepidermal water loss (TEWL), smooths micro-texture, and delivers the characteristic "soft, velvety, non-sticky" slip — i.e. it behaves like a film-forming humectant/sensory polymer (same broad mechanism class as HA, but with its own touch). [1][2][3][7]
  2. Skin-soothing / anti-irritant (supported by use + some data). Marketed as soothing; the proposed mechanism is the film physically buffering the surface plus an L-fucose "receptor" interaction said to modulate keratinocyte inflammatory signalling, and (per newer Solabia materials) modulation of neuronal sensitivity via the ASIC (acid-sensing ion channel) pathway to reduce stinging. [3][7]
  3. "Fucose signal" / glyco-biology hypothesis (mechanistically plausible, skin-proof weak). L-fucose is a real, biologically important sugar: it caps many cell-surface glycans (e.g. selectin ligands, Notch, blood-group antigens) and fucosylation governs cell adhesion, inflammation, and signalling. The hypothesis is that supplying fucose-rich material feeds/triggers fucose-dependent pathways that favour fibroblast activity and matrix renewal. This is the conceptual basis for the anti-aging claims. [5][13]
  4. Matrix-supportive cell effects (in-vitro / inventor data). In cultured human skin fibroblasts, the fucose-rich material (FROP) reportedly stimulates fibroblast proliferation, protects against ascorbate/ROS-induced cytotoxicity, stimulates glycosaminoglycan (GAG) biosynthesis, and inhibits activation of pro-MMP-9 → MMP-9 (i.e. less matrix breakdown). [6][14][15]
  5. "Longevity protein" claim (manufacturer/patent). Solabia claims ex-vivo stimulation of sirtuin-1 and collagen I, and an increase in epidermal/dermal hyaluronic-acid synthesis with a decrease in hyaluronidase-2 — framed as cellular-longevity / self-hydration support. These are patented manufacturer claims, not independently published. [3][9]

Skin-relevant translation (honest): Pillars 1–2 (surface film, hydration, soothing) are real and account for most of what users feel. Pillars 3–5 (the "anti-aging / longevity" story) are plausible biology resting on thin, mostly non-independent evidence for this material on human facial skin.

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

The single most important honesty point: Biosaccharide Gum-1's evidence base is lopsided. (a) Safety is well-established by a formal expert review (CIR). (b) Hydration / sensory / soothing are supported by manufacturer testing, very long cosmetic use, and read-across to similar humectant polymers — credible for surface-level claims. (c) The anti-aging biology (fibroblast, GAG, MMP, wrinkle, "rejuvenation") traces almost entirely to a single, closely-related cluster of early-2000s papers from the inventors' group (L. Robert / G. Péterszegi, Paris) — small, mostly in-vitro or uncontrolled human studies — plus manufacturer/patent data. There is little or no independent, randomized, placebo-controlled human facial-skin trial isolating this ingredient. Some often-cited human data are from a vaginal (not facial) study where it was combined with hyaluronic acid, so the effect can't be attributed to Biosaccharide Gum-1 alone.

Tier 1 — In vitro (cell & biochemical models)

  • Péterszegi G, Isnard N, Robert AM, Robert L (2003), Biomed Pharmacother 57(5–6):187–194 (PMID 12888253; DOI 10.1016/S0753-3322(03)00031-3). "Studies on skin aging. Preparation and properties of fucose-rich oligo- and polysaccharides…fibroblast proliferation and survival." On cultured human skin fibroblasts, FROP (the fucose/galactose/galacturonic-acid trisaccharide polymer = the Fucogel material) stimulated fibroblast proliferation and protected cells from ascorbate-induced ROS cytotoxicity. Limitations: in-vitro; inventor group; concentrations not clearly generalizable to a topical dose; this is the conceptual cornerstone the marketing leans on. [6]
  • Companion paper, same group/issue (2003), Biomed Pharmacother 57(5–6): "…penetration, skin tissue production and fibrillogenesis." Reports skin penetration of fucose/FROP and effects on matrix production/fibrillogenesis in skin models. Limitations: in-vitro/explant; inventor group; not isolated-ingredient human proof. [14]
  • Robert L, et al. (2004), "Stimulation of glycosaminoglycan (GAG) biosynthesis by L-fucose and fucose-rich polysaccharides." In human skin fibroblast culture, L-fucose ↑heparan-sulfate incorporation ~20%; FROP-3 ↑keratan-sulfate ~45%; fucose/FROP also inhibited activation of pro-MMP-9 → active MMP-9 (matrix-protective). Limitations: in-vitro; small/mechanistic; inventor group. [15]
  • "Protection by L-fucose and fucose-rich polysaccharides against ROS-produced cell death in presence of ascorbate." Antioxidant/cytoprotection in cell models. Limitations: in-vitro; inventor group. [14]
  • Generic L-fucose anti-aging mechanism literature (e.g. fucose/FROP downregulating elastase-type and MMP-2/MMP-9 activity in skin explant cultures; reported >70% increases in dermal thickness/collagen-bundle density with 1% L-fucose cream in explant work). ⚠️ Much of this tests L-fucose itself or generic FROP, not necessarily the exact commercial Biosaccharide Gum-1 polymer — read-across, not identity. [5]

Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

  • No substantive, independent topical-skin animal efficacy literature specific to Biosaccharide Gum-1 was found. Animal data in the relevant CIR safety dossier are toxicology/irritation, not anti-aging efficacy (see §7). [10] Adjacent fucose work exists in non-skin animal models (gut, immunity) but is not skin-topical and not this polymer. [5][13]

Tier 3 — Human ORAL

  • Not applicable / none relevant. Biosaccharide Gum-1 is a topical cosmetic film-former, not an oral supplement; there is no oral human efficacy literature to borrow from (unlike, say, spermidine or collagen peptides). Treat any oral-fucose immunology as off-topic for topical claims. [5][13]

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters — small, old, mostly uncontrolled)

  • Robert C, Robert AM, Robert L (2005), "Effect of a preparation containing a fucose-rich polysaccharide on periorbital wrinkles of human voluntaries," Skin Res Technol 11(1):47–52 (PMID 15691259). n = 20 women, 29 periorbital (crow's-feet) wrinkles, FROP-3 cream twice daily for 4 weeks; wrinkles assessed by negative-replica + semi-automated morphometry ("wrinkle factor"). ~65% of wrinkles improved (a few ~75–100%); 6 unchanged, 4 worsened; high inter-individual and left/right variability. Limitations: small; no placebo/vehicle control described; inventor group; short; single-center; subjective-to-morphometric endpoint. [16]
  • Péterszegi G, et al. (2003), "Effect of a fucose-rich polysaccharide preparation on the age-dependent evolution of the skin surface micro-relief," Pathol Biol (Paris) (ScienceDirect S0369811403001925). n = 20 women, ages 39–71, 4 weeks; 17/20 showed skin-surface micro-relief shifting toward a "younger" pattern; 15/20 improved >15% (avg ~37%); authors framed it as "slowing/reversing" age-related relief change. Limitations: small; uncontrolled (no placebo arm reported); inventor group; micro-relief morphometry only; strong language not matched by design rigor. [17]
  • Caruso S, Bruno MT, Boemi S, et al. (2023), "Vaginal health and quality of sexual life of postmenopausal women on hyaluronic acid and Biosaccharide Gum-1 vaginal gel," Taiwan J Obstet Gynecol 62(5):702–708 (PMID 37678998). Non-randomized cohort, n = 104 (50 on HA+BG-1 gel vs 54 on-demand lubricants); 12 weeks; the gel group improved Vaginal Health Index, female sexual function, and somatic QoL. ⚠️ Two big caveats: (a) vaginal mucosa, not facial skin; (b) combined with hyaluronic acid, so the benefit cannot be attributed to Biosaccharide Gum-1 alone. Useful only as weak corroboration of "hydrating/soothing on epithelium." [8]
  • Manufacturer (Solabia) in-use / clinical claims (no peer-reviewed publication located): hydration "measured up to 8 h," stronger hydration than hyaluronic acid at ~3 h (HA faster at first; similar by 8 h); anti-sting — 3% Fucogel reduced tingling ~24% after one application and cut lactic-acid (10%) sting by ~47%; ↑collagen-1 and sirtuin-1 (ex-vivo, patented); ↑HA synthesis / ↓hyaluronidase-2. ⚠️ Manufacturer data — directional, not independently verified; treat as marketing-grade. [1][3][9]

Bottom line by tier: Proven → safety (CIR), and surface hydration/sensory/soothing as a category-typical film-forming humectant. Suggestive but weak → fibroblast/GAG/MMP/anti-wrinkle anti-aging, supported only by small, old, uncontrolled, inventor-authored or manufacturer/patent work. Not proven → any quantified "boosts collagen / activates sirtuins / reverses aging" claim on human facial skin via an independent RCT.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • By design it is largely a surface/superficial actor. At ~1 MDa, the intact polymer is far too large to penetrate the stratum corneum — like HA, its main action (film, water-binding, slip, surface soothing) happens on and just within the top layers, which is exactly what a humectant film-former is supposed to do.
  • The "fucose signal" depends on penetration that is largely unproven. The anti-aging hypothesis requires fucose-bearing fragments (or released L-fucose) reaching viable epidermis/dermis to influence fibroblasts. The inventor papers report some penetration of fucose/FROP in skin models [14], and lower-MW / oligosaccharide grades (e.g. Fucogel 1.5P, "FROP" oligomers) are offered partly to improve this — but independent human transdermal quantification is lacking.
  • Practical read: Excellent at what surface humectant films do (no penetration needed). Uncertain for the deeper biological claims, which hinge on penetration that hasn't been robustly demonstrated for the commercial polymer in living human skin. Don't imply dermal "collagen-building" delivery.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: Commonly 0.5–8% of the supplied gel (i.e. as the ~1% solution), with soothing/sensory benefits often cited around 1–5% and the manufacturer sting study run at 3%; powder/concentrated grades are used at ~0.1–1%. CIR reports leave-on use up to 6% (gel). Choose level for feel and hydration target, not for an implied dose-response anti-aging effect. [9][10][7]
  • pH: Broad compatibility (roughly pH ~3–8+). As an anionic carboxylated polysaccharide it is robust across typical skincare pH; very low pH or high ionic strength/strong cations can affect viscosity/film. Generally easy. [7]
  • Stability: Stable, water-soluble; add to the water phase. Standard, reliable. Like most natural polysaccharides it needs a competent preservative system (microbial substrate). Watch viscosity interactions with high electrolyte or strongly cationic ingredients (anionic polymer).
  • Pairings (synergy / story):
  • Hyaluronic acid — classic multi-weight humectant stack; complementary kinetics (HA fast, Fucogel more sustained per manufacturer); the vaginal study even combined them. [1][8]
  • Glycerin / panthenol / betaine — layered humectancy + soothing.
  • Niacinamide, ceramides — barrier/soothing narrative (fits the brand's barrier-repair thesis).
  • Exfoliating acids (lactic/AHA), retinoids, vitamin C — Fucogel as the anti-sting / comfort layer to improve tolerability of irritating actives (its best-supported "supporting" role). [9]
  • Conflicts: Few. Mainly rheology/clouding risks with strong cations or very high salt; confirm in finished-formula stability.
  • Sensory: A standout asset — smooth, cushiony, "soft-focus," non-tacky afterfeel; reduces stickiness of glycerin/HA-heavy systems; useful texturizer in serums, essences, eye creams, mists, masks, and lightweight creams.

7. Safety

  • Formal expert review (strong): CIR (Cosmetic Ingredient Review) Expert Panel, "Safety Assessment of Microbial Polysaccharide Gums as Used in Cosmetics," Int J Toxicol 2016;35(Suppl 1):5S–49S (PMID 27383198) assessed Biosaccharide Gum-1 among 34 microbial polysaccharide gums and concluded they are safe in cosmetics in the present practices of use and concentration. Biosaccharide Gum-1 was reported in ~346 formulations, used up to ~6% in leave-on products. Functions: film former, skin-conditioning, viscosity/sensory. [10]
  • Irritation / sensitization: Very low-risk; broadly recognized as gentle, and it is itself marketed as an anti-irritant/soothing agent (sting-reduction data). No notable sensitization signal in cosmetic use. Still, finished-formula patch/HRIPT testing is best practice. [9][10]
  • Sun sensitivity: No photosensitivity concern; not a photosensitizer.
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: No specific contraindication for a topical, surface-acting, well-tolerated polysaccharide; standard "consult your doctor" hedging in copy is sufficient. (No data suggesting concern.)
  • Sourcing / microbial-origin nuance: Because the original/likely production organism is Klebsiella pneumoniae (a Gram-negative bacterium), the purification must remove the organism and bacterial endotoxin (LPS) — reputable suppliers (Solabia) do this and the material is widely cleared and certified. For transparency and "clean"/sensitive-skin positioning, request COA evidence of low endotoxin/heavy-metal/microbial counts and confirm the current substrate (non-GMO plant-derived) claim. [3][4][12]
  • Allergen / vegan / cert: Typically vegan, gluten-free, ECOCERT/COSMOS-compatible; verify per grade. [4][7]

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic ingredient status: Established cosmetic ingredient (INCI Biosaccharide Gum-1; CAS 194237-89-3), CIR-reviewed safe, broadly accepted (EU, US, Japan, China, Australia, Canada per supplier). Functions: humectant / film former / skin-conditioning / soothing. [4][7][10]
  • The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame): "Hydrates," "soothes," "improves softness/comfort," "smooths the look of skin," "supports the moisture barrier" are safe cosmetic claims. The trouble starts when the anti-aging biology is stated as fact: "boosts collagen," "increases hyaluronic acid synthesis," "activates sirtuins / cellular longevity," "reverses skin aging" → these are structure/function (drug-leaning) claims and, worse, are backed only by manufacturer/patent + small uncontrolled studies, so they're a substantiation risk as well as a regulatory one.
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "a fucose-rich, water-loving sugar that hydrates and helps lock in moisture," "helps soothe and reduce the feeling of tingling/sensitivity," "leaves skin feeling soft and smooth (silky, non-sticky finish)," "helps improve the appearance of skin's surface texture," "a gentle comfort layer that can improve the wear of active ingredients."
  • ⚠️ Risky / needs substantiation or legal review: "boosts collagen by X%," "increases your skin's hyaluronic acid," "activates longevity proteins / sirtuins," "reverses wrinkles," "rejuvenates / makes skin 10–15 years younger" (the inventor papers' framing — do not repeat as a brand claim).
  • Substantiation reality: For anything beyond hydration/soothing/sensory and appearance wording, we'd want our own controlled, finished-formula human testing — the public literature won't carry a hard anti-aging claim. See ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Suppliers / grades: Solabia (Fucogel® / Fucocert®) is the originator/benchmark, in grades like Fucogel 1.5P, 1000, 1000PP, 100PP, Powder (differing MW/concentration/sensory). Other suppliers offer Biosaccharide Gum-1 equivalents (e.g. Sinolion "BioYoung FGL-G"). [4][7][11]
  • Cost tier: Mid. More expensive per kg than commodity humectants (glycerin, propanediol) but it is used at low percentages and supplied mostly as a 1% solution, so finished-formula cost impact is modest. Branded Fucogel carries a premium vs generic Biosaccharide Gum-1.
  • Supply / spec considerations: Confirm MW grade (sensory vs penetration intent), substrate origin (non-GMO plant), endotoxin/microbial/heavy-metal COA (microbial-origin polymer), and cert (ECOCERT/COSMOS/HALAL/vegan) for the brand's positioning. Naturally derived >95% supports "clean/biotech" storytelling. [4][7]

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • "Smart sugar that does five things" (S.M.A.R.T.: Soothing, Moisturizing, Anti-aging, Restructuring, Touch). Two of the five — Moisturizing and Touch — are genuinely strong. Soothing is reasonable. Anti-aging and "Restructuring" are the overclaimed parts. [3]
  • "Increases collagen / hyaluronic acid synthesis / activates sirtuin-1." These are manufacturer/patent (ex-vivo) claims — not independently published, not RCT-backed on human facial skin. Mechanistically plausible, evidentially thin. [9]
  • "Clinically proven to reverse skin aging / make skin look 10–15 years younger." Traces to small (n≈20), uncontrolled, inventor-authored micro-relief and crow's-feet studies from 2003–2005 with no placebo arm and high variability. Real papers, weak design — do not repeat the dramatic phrasing. [16][17]
  • "Backed by clinical studies (PubMed)." True literally, but the most-cited recent human study is vaginal, non-randomized, and combined with hyaluronic acid — it cannot prove a facial anti-aging effect of Biosaccharide Gum-1 alone. [8]
  • "Better than hyaluronic acid." Manufacturer testing suggests different kinetics (HA faster, Fucogel more sustained, similar by ~8 h) — that's "complementary," not "superior." [1]
  • The genuine, under-marketed win: it is one of the nicest-feeling, gentlest hydrating film-formers available, with real anti-sting utility to make harsher actives tolerable. That's a defensible, honest story.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ SUPPORTING — excellent hydration/sensory/soothing workhorse; not an evidence-backed anti-aging hero. One-line: Biosaccharide Gum-1 (Fucogel) earns a place as a supporting ingredient — a gentle, premium-feeling, water-binding, soft-focus, anti-sting polysaccharide that improves a formula's texture, comfort, and tolerability — but its anti-aging/"longevity protein" claims are manufacturer/patent + small uncontrolled studies, so we should sell the feel, hydration, and soothing, not collagen/sirtuin biology.

Where it fits the brand: A natural ally to the barrier-repair / lean-routine thesis. Best used as (1) a comfort + sensory upgrade in a barrier cream or hydrating serum, paired with HA, glycerin, ceramides, niacinamide; and (2) a tolerability layer alongside retinoids/acids/vitamin C, where its sting-reduction is the most useful, honest benefit. It is not a SKU-justifying hero on its own.

Longevity-relevance note: The "fucose / fucosylation" angle is scientifically interesting and on-narrative for a skin-longevity brand (glycobiology is a real frontier), and the molecule carries a tidy biotech/natural story (>95% natural, microbial-fermentation, ECOCERT). But for now that's a narrative/education asset, not a substantiated topical-longevity mechanism. If we ever want to make a real anti-aging claim with it, the honest path is our own controlled finished-formula human study — not the existing literature. If sourcing, disclose the microbial-fermentation origin transparently and require low-endotoxin COAs.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19. Citations independently re-checked against PubMed/journal abstracts, the CIR open report, and supplier/registry listings. This dossier was already written with unusual honesty; verification largely confirms it. Findings below.

Overall grade: C (split: A for safety, B for surface hydration/sensory/soothing, D for anti-aging biology)

Biosaccharide Gum-1 does not have a single grade because its evidence base is genuinely lopsided, and the dossier is right to say so:

  • Safety — A. The CIR review is real and verified ([10], PMID 27383198): human-relevant expert safety assessment, Biosaccharide Gum-1 in ~346 formulations, up to 6% leave-on, judged safe among 34 microbial polysaccharide gums. Independent, authoritative, not industry-funded.
  • Hydration / sensory / soothing — B. Human + in-use, and topical, but the strongest comparative numbers (hydration vs HA kinetics, sting-reduction %s) are manufacturer-measured and unpublished ([1][3][9]). Read-across from the broad humectant/film-former class is fair; the specific percentages are not independently confirmed. "B" reflects real human-topical signal carried partly by category logic and marketing-grade data.
  • Anti-aging biology (collagen, GAG, MMP, sirtuin-1, "reverses aging") — D topically. This is the honest verdict and the dossier reaches it correctly.

Main caveats (the load-bearing ones)

  1. Independence / funding. Verified: the anti-aging studies ([6][14][15][16][17]) are from the L. Robert / G. Péterszegi group (Université Pierre et Marie Curie, Paris), who worked explicitly on "the original Fucogel from Solabia." This is the inventor/originator lineage — effectively industry-aligned, not arm's-length. The dossier flags this; verification confirms it.
  2. No independent facial RCT. A 2020–2024 search found no independent, randomized, placebo-controlled facial-skin trial isolating Biosaccharide Gum-1. Nothing has emerged to upgrade the anti-aging tier.
  3. Tiny, uncontrolled, old human work. The two human topical studies are n=20, 4 weeks, no placebo arm, single-group, 2003–2005. Real papers, weak design — directional at best.
  4. Wrong-tissue / combination corroboration. The only recent human study is vaginal, non-randomized, and HA-combined — it cannot support a facial anti-aging claim for BG-1 alone.
  5. Manufacturer percentages. All the crisp numbers used in marketing (sting −47%, hydration-vs-HA kinetics, collagen/sirtuin/HA-synthesis) are Solabia/patent data with no located peer-reviewed publication — treat as marketing-grade.

Claims to soften (for any consumer-facing copy)

  • Drop or never repeat: "boosts collagen," "increases hyaluronic acid synthesis," "activates sirtuins / longevity proteins," "reverses skin aging," "10–15 years younger." (Structure/function + unsubstantiated.)
  • "Cuts sting by 47%" and "more hydrating than hyaluronic acid" → reframe as "helps soothe / reduce the feeling of tingling" and "long-lasting, comfortable hydration" unless we run our own finished-formula test.
  • Keep (defensible): hydrates / helps lock in moisture, soothes / reduces the feeling of sensitivity, soft non-sticky finish, improves appearance of surface texture, comfort layer that improves tolerability of harsher actives.

Net: The dossier's existing grading, hedging, and verdict are sound and well-evidenced. No overstated claim required reversal; the only edits made were to mark the hydration-vs-HA comparison as manufacturer-measured (§3) and to complete/correct the [17] citation (pages 586–590; 15/20 improved >15%). Sell feel, hydration, soothing; do not sell collagen/sirtuin biology.
