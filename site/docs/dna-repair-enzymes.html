Ingredient: DNA-Repair Enzymes (Photolyase, T4 Endonuclease V / Micrococcus Lysate, OGG1 / Arabidopsis, UV Endonuclease)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Sunscreen / photoprotection filters & adjuncts (active photoprotection "repair" layer — NOT a UV filter itself)

1. Identity

"DNA-repair enzymes" is an umbrella for several distinct, biologically unrelated enzymes that each fix a different type of UV-induced DNA lesion. In skincare they are almost always encapsulated in liposomes (delivery is the whole ballgame — see §5) and sold as plant/microbe-derived extracts. The marketing name rarely matches the INCI name.

Marketing / functional name What it actually is Lesion repaired Typical INCI on label
Photolyase (a.k.a. "CPD-photolyase") Flavoprotein from cyanobacterium Anacystis nidulans (= Synechococcus) or microalgae Cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers (CPDs) — needs visible/UVA light to work ("photoreactivation") Plankton Extract; sometimes Anacystis Nidulans Extract
T4 endonuclease V ("T4N5") Bacteriophage-T4 enzyme; the Micrococcus luteus version is the cosmetic source CPDs — light-INDEPENDENT (base/nucleotide excision) Micrococcus Lysate
OGG1 (8-oxoguanine DNA glycosylase) Sourced from mustard plant Arabidopsis thaliana 8-oxo-guanine (oxidative base damage, mainly UVA/ROS) Arabidopsis Thaliana Extract
UV endonuclease Endonuclease from Anacystis nidulans / cyanobacteria Pyrimidine dimers (excision-type) often grouped under Plankton Extract
  • Common name: "DNA repair enzymes," "photoreactivating enzymes," "active photoprotection."
  • Key forms/derivatives: liposome-encapsulated lotion/cream/sunscreen; "film-forming" SPF medical device (e.g. ISDIN Eryfotona, photolyase + SPF100+); cosmetic serums (e.g. NEOVA DNA Total Repair = Micrococcus Lysate + Plankton Extract + Arabidopsis Thaliana Extract).
  • Important nuance: Because the three big ones repair three different lesions, products often blend all three to cover CPDs and oxidative damage. They are frequently co-formulated WITH sunscreen filters as an "after-the-photons-land" repair layer.

2. Class & mechanism

UV light damages keratinocyte DNA in two main ways: (1) UVB fuses adjacent pyrimidines into CPDs and 6-4 photoproducts (CPDs are the predominant dimeric photoproduct — ~75% of UV dimers — and account for ≥80% of UVB-induced mutations in mammalian cells); (2) UVA/ROS oxidise guanine to 8-oxo-guanine. Sunscreen filters block photons but do nothing for damage that already occurred. DNA-repair enzymes are pitched as the "repair" half of "protect + repair."

  • Photolyase: binds a CPD and, on absorbing a photon of visible/UVA light (~350–450 nm), uses photoinduced electron transfer to split the dimer back into normal bases ("photoreactivation"). Mammals lost this enzyme in evolution, so it is a genuinely additive repair pathway. It only works in light.
  • T4 endonuclease V (Micrococcus Lysate): initiates the cell's own excision repair of CPDs; works in the dark.
  • OGG1 (Arabidopsis): glycosylase that excises 8-oxo-guanine, the first step of base-excision repair of oxidative damage.

Downstream, faster removal of these lesions is shown to reduce sunburn-cell formation, erythema, p53 induction, and UV-induced local immunosuppression in human skin — the same cascade that drives photocarcinogenesis and, plausibly, photoaging.

3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

Hallmark Relevance Evidence basis
Photoaging Primary target — the entire mechanism is UV-damage repair Strong mechanistically; weak on clinical photoaging endpoints (§4)
Oxidative stress OGG1 directly reverses oxidative DNA base damage; CPD repair reduces ROS-linked apoptosis Moderate (in-vitro / ex-vivo biopsy)
Inflammaging / immune CPD repair prevents UV-induced immunosuppression & sunburn-cell formation Moderate (human ex-vivo)
Cellular senescence UV DNA damage is a senescence trigger; faster repair should reduce it Indirect / theoretical only
Pigmentation / tone UV damage drives uneven tone; one small cosmetic study showed minor tone gains Weak
Collagen/ECM, barrier, glycation, mitochondrial, microbiome, hydration Not a meaningful direct target Not claimed

Bottom line: this is a photoaging-prevention / DNA-integrity ingredient, not a barrier, hydration, or firming actor. Its longevity logic is upstream (protect the genome) rather than cosmetic-surface.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

Headline honesty: The strongest, cleanest human evidence is for reducing UV-induced DNA lesions and for preventing actinic keratoses / skin cancers in high-risk skin (xeroderma pigmentosum, field cancerization). Evidence that these enzymes visibly reverse cosmetic photoaging (wrinkles, elasticity, tone) in healthy skin is thin to absent — the 2020 systematic review found zero RCTs on photoaging endpoints. Much of the supportive clinical work is industry-linked.

Tier 1 — In vitro / ex vivo (mechanism: solid)

  • Stege et al., PNAS 2000 — 1% liposomal photolyase (A. nidulans) on UVB-irradiated human skin + photoreactivating light removed 40–45% of CPDs, and prevented erythema, sunburn-cell formation, and UV-induced immunosuppression vs. controls. The cornerstone mechanistic proof in humans. [Source 1]
  • Emanuele et al., J Drugs Dermatol 2014 — double-blind, biopsy-based irradiation study: the combination product (SPF50 + antioxidants + DNA-repair-enzyme complex, "TPF 50") gave the largest reductions in UV-induced CPDs and protein carbonylation vs. comparator products; the synergy held for CPD and PC but not for 8-OHdG. Mechanistic, not a clinical-appearance endpoint. Note: industry-authored (study of the authors' own TPF 50 product) — conflict of interest, treat like the other manufacturer-linked work. [Source 6]
  • Multiple base/glycosylase studies confirm OGG1 (Arabidopsis) excises 8-oxo-guanine and T4N5 (Micrococcus) accelerates CPD excision. [Sources 5, 8]

Tier 2 — Animal

  • Mouse work (UVB models, NER-deficient mice) shows photolyase-mediated CPD removal attenuates UV skin effects and tumorigenesis; OGG1 delivery reduces tumor burden. Supports mechanism; limited translation. [Sources 5, 8]

Tier 3 — Human ORAL

  • None. These are topical enzymes; there is no meaningful oral-supplement literature. (Do not borrow oral antioxidant/Polypodium data — different ingredient class.)

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL — the decisive tier

(a) RCTs — skin-cancer / actinic-keratosis prevention (the strong evidence): - Yarosh et al., Lancet 2001 — the landmark RCT. T4N5 (T4 endonuclease V) liposome lotion vs. placebo, daily for 1 year, n=30 xeroderma pigmentosum patients. New actinic keratoses annualized 8.2 vs 25.9 (≈68% lower; difference 17.7 [95% CI 11.8–26.5]; p=0.004); new basal-cell carcinoma 3.8 vs 5.4 (≈30% lower; difference 1.6 [95% CI 0.38–2.82] — does not cross zero but the trial was not powered for BCC). No significant adverse effects. Limitation: tiny n, XP (genetically repair-deficient) population — not generalizable to healthy cosmetic users. [Source 2] - Stoddard / Moy et al., J Drugs Dermatol 2017 — RCT, double-blind, n=15 (13 completed), topical DNA-repair enzyme lotion vs. Eucerin placebo, 8 wks on AKs. Enzyme arm −46.6% AKs vs −32.7% placebo at 8 wks; by wk 12 enzyme −additional 29.2% vs placebo +31.4% (p=0.0026). Limitation: very small, short, subjective lesion counts. [Source 3] - Negative RCT — Alvares et al., An Bras Dermatol 2022 — randomized, double-blind, factorial, n=40 (80 forearms): sunscreen + photolyase was NOT superior to plain sunscreen for photodamage/field-cancerization outcomes ("no significant differences between SC and SC+P"). Notably the antioxidant arm beat placebo (partial clearance 47.4% vs 23.7%, p=0.018), the enzyme arm did not. A genuinely independent, well-designed counterweight to industry studies. [Source 4]

(b) Lower-tier human clinical — field cancerization (mostly industry-linked): - Puig et al., Dermatol Ther 2019 — 10-year review of ISDIN Eryfotona (photolyase + SPF100+): 11 studies / 228 participants. Includes Moscarella 2017 (RCT n≈36; fewer new lesions vs SPF50+ in mild AK, 14% vs 54%), Eibenschutz 2016 (post-PDT, 0% vs 66% needing retreatment), Giustini 2014 (XP, n=8, 65% fewer AK), Navarrete-Dechent 2017 (case series, 76.6% AK reduction). Heavy conflict of interest: ISDIN funded the article-processing charges, employs three co-authors, and the lead author (Puig) has sat on the ISDIN advisory board since 2016. Treat as supportive but biased. [Source 7] - A 6-month study (UV endonuclease + photolyase sunscreen) reduced CPDs 61% vs 35% for sunscreen alone; an 8-day study (UV endonuclease + photolyase + OGG1) cut 8-oxo-guanine 17% vs sunscreen alone — biomarker endpoints, not appearance. [Source 8 (Luze review) — primary studies not independently retrieved; treat numbers as secondary-sourced.]

(c) Human TOPICAL — cosmetic PHOTOAGING (the weak spot): - Luze et al., Photodermatol Photoimmunol Photomed 2020 — systematic review, 52 studies (31 in-vivo, 14 in-vitro, 7 animal). Verdict: "There is a lack of randomized controlled trials demonstrating the efficacy of DNA repair enzymes on photoageing," and "controlled studies confirming … the superiority of sunscreens with DNA repair enzymes over conventional sunscreens are still lacking." Most studies conflate cancer prevention with photoaging. [Source 8] - One small cosmetic study (tinted photolyase sunscreen, 30 women 45–65, 28 days) reportedly showed only 6–12% improvement per photoaging parameter vs control — modest, short, single study. [Unverified: the primary study was not located; this is NOT in Source 10 (Yarosh 1999), which is a 1999 T4N5 paper in XP/skin-cancer patients and predates it. Likely drawn from a secondary review — do not cite with confidence until the primary is found.] - Krutmann et al. ("PINGing Sunshine"), Photodermatol Photoimmunol Photomed 2025 — expert evidence-grading review rates photolyase "Strong" and T4 endonuclease "Moderate" for DNA-damage repair specifically; notes photolyase raised CPD protection of an SPF50 from 62%→93%. But flags that <2% of candidate add-in ingredients are clinically validated and calls for more trials before broad adoption. The strong rating is for DNA-repair, not for wrinkle reversal. [Source 9]

Skeptical counterweight

  • "Six critical questions for DNA repair enzymes" (Yarosh, Rosenthal & Moy, Clin Cosmet Investig Dermatol 2019, dialog review) — frames the open questions: do enzymes actually penetrate and stay active in vivo, is benefit beyond the sunscreen proven, and how much is industry-driven. Caveat: authored by Daniel Yarosh (who pioneered/commercialized T4N5/Dimericine) and Ronald Moy — these are industry-aligned figures, not independent skeptics, so this is a "questions the field acknowledges" piece rather than a truly external critique. [Source 11, full text not retrieved — abstract/secondary only]

Net: Proven for TOPICAL human use → fewer measurable DNA lesions; fewer AKs/skin cancers in high-risk skin. NOT proven for TOPICAL human use → visibly reversing wrinkles/elasticity/tone in healthy skin (borrowed from biomarker + cancer-prevention data). At least one independent RCT (Alvares 2022) found no benefit over plain sunscreen.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

This is the make-or-break issue. Enzymes are large proteins (photolyase ~50–60 kDa) that cannot cross intact stratum corneum on their own. Efficacy is entirely dependent on liposomal encapsulation (~150 nm pH-sensitive vesicles) that localizes to the epidermis within ~1 hr, is taken up by keratinocytes/Langerhans cells, releases the enzyme intracellularly, which then diffuses to the nucleus. Key caveats: - The exact path through stratum corneum is not fully characterized (acknowledged in the literature). - Photolyase additionally needs visible/UVA light exposure after application to function at all — a real-world adherence requirement most consumers won't know. - A non-encapsulated "DNA repair enzyme" on an INCI list is likely a marketing token, not a functional delivery system. Delivery technology > the enzyme name.

6. Formulation

  • Typical use level: photolyase ~0.5–1% of a liposomal preparation; the others as standardized liposomal extracts. Exact actives are supplier-proprietary.
  • pH: keep near physiological / mild (≈5–7) to preserve protein folding.
  • Stability: heat-labile — add in the cool-down phase (<40 °C); use gentle emulsifiers to avoid denaturation; protect from oxidation; protein actives have limited shelf stability and may need preservative/packaging care (airless, opaque). Liposome integrity is essential.
  • Pairings: synergistic with UV filters (repair layer on top of protection) and with antioxidants (the industry-authored Emanuele 2014 found the combination product gave the biggest CPD/PC reductions — though not for 8-OHdG; the independent Alvares 2022 found the antioxidants, not the enzyme, were the part that worked). Compatible with niacinamide.
  • Conflicts: avoid high heat, strong surfactants, low-pH acid systems (AHAs/BHAs, high-% vitamin C) and high alcohol that can disrupt liposomes/denature protein — formulate in a separate vehicle.
  • Sensory: delivered in lotions/creams/fluids; generally light, non-greasy; no inherent color/odor issues at use levels.

7. Safety

  • Irritation/sensitization: Excellent real-world record — across the XP RCT and field-cancerization studies, no significant adverse events reported. Low irritation potential.
  • Sun sensitivity: None added; the opposite — used to mitigate UV damage. (Photolyase needs light to work, but does not photosensitize.)
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: No specific human safety data. Topical enzymes act locally with negligible expected systemic absorption, so likely low concern, but no affirmative safety studies exist — flag as "data not established; consult clinician."
  • Sourcing/sustainability: microbial/algal fermentation (photolyase, endonuclease) and plant extract (Arabidopsis) are biotech-fermentation friendly — generally favorable sustainability story; verify supplier process.

8. Regulatory & claims

  • Cosmetic vs. drug/device line: In the US, a DNA-repair enzyme in a moisturizer/serum is a cosmetic; the moment you pair it with SPF and make sun claims you enter OTC sunscreen drug territory (and DNA-repair enzymes are NOT FDA-approved UV filters — they cannot contribute to the SPF claim). In the EU, AK-focused photolyase products (e.g. Eryfotona) are marketed as Class I medical devices, not cosmetics — a regulatory pathway a cosmetic brand should not imply.
  • What we can say (cosmetic-safe): "helps support the skin's natural repair processes," "active photoprotection that works alongside sun protection," "helps defend against the visible effects of UV exposure," "supports skin DNA integrity" — kept appearance-/support-framed.
  • What we MUST NOT say: "repairs DNA," "prevents skin cancer," "reverses sun damage," "treats actinic keratoses," "reduces basal-cell carcinoma." These are drug/disease claims (the cancer-prevention data is real but legally off-limits for a cosmetic) and would trigger substantiation/legal review. Per CLAUDE.md guardrails and ../../brand/glossary.md: use "supports," "helps," "visibly"; never "treats/cures/prevents."
  • Substantiation: Any efficacy claim should ride on the brand's own finished-formula data plus cited mechanism — do not borrow the XP/AK clinical numbers into consumer copy.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: High / premium. Encapsulated enzyme actives (photolyase liposomes, Micrococcus Lysate, Arabidopsis OGG1) are specialty biotech ingredients, far pricier per kg than commodity humectants/niacinamide; they add cost in both raw material and cold-chain/stability formulation.
  • Suppliers: specialty actives houses (e.g. liposomal photolyase from cyanobacterial fermentation; Micrococcus Lysate and Arabidopsis Thaliana Extract are commercially listed and appear in finished products such as NEOVA DNA Total Repair). Buy the delivery-validated grade, not a raw extract.
  • Supply consideration: proprietary, few suppliers, MOQ and stability constraints — not a "buy commodity base" ingredient; this is an invest-in-the-active line item.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • Overreach to avoid: "Repairs your DNA," "reverses sun damage," "anti-cancer skincare." The cancer/AK data is in genetically repair-deficient (XP) or already-sun-damaged field-cancerization patients, not healthy users seeking glow — and it's a drug claim regardless.
  • The quiet caveat brands omit: photolyase only works in light and delivery (liposome) is everything — an enzyme on the INCI list proves nothing about activity in skin.
  • Conflict of interest: a large share of the favorable clinical literature is funded by/authored by the product makers (ISDIN). The most independent, well-designed RCT (Alvares 2022) found no benefit over ordinary sunscreen, and the best systematic review (Luze 2020) found no RCT evidence for photoaging at all.
  • Fair framing: genuinely interesting, real mechanism, excellent safety — but a complement to sunscreen, not a hero anti-wrinkle active. The honest pitch is "active photoprotection," not "DNA repair miracle."

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ CONDITIONAL — supporting actor, not a hero. Strong, premium "science-forward" story and superb safety, and it reinforces the brand's #1 evidence-based anti-aging lever (sun protection) — but the clinical evidence for cosmetic photoaging is weak (no photoaging RCTs), an independent RCT found no benefit over plain sunscreen, the real benefits (cancer/AK prevention) are unclaimable drug territory, it's costly, fragile to formulate, and photolyase needs light. Use only if: (1) it's positioned as an "active photoprotection / repair-support" layer alongside a real SPF or in an after-sun, (2) we buy a delivery-validated liposomal grade, and (3) copy stays strictly in cosmetic "supports/helps" language. Not a fit for the lead barrier-repair cream; a credible premium add-on for a future SPF / after-sun / "daytime defense" SKU.

Longevity relevance: Conceptually on-thesis — protecting genomic integrity from UV damage is upstream "skin longevity" (photoaging is the dominant driver of visible aging). But translation to visible longevity benefit in healthy skin is unproven; value is preventive and additive to sunscreen, not corrective.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19 by independent re-checking of the cited primary sources against the claims made above. Method: each headline efficacy claim was tested for (a) whether the evidence is HUMAN + TOPICAL or quietly borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral data, (b) whether the cited study is real and says what is claimed, (c) whether key studies are industry-funded or very small.

Overall evidence grade: C+ (split verdict)

There is no single grade because the dossier makes two very different claims:

  • For reducing UV-induced DNA lesions (CPDs, 8-oxo-G) and for preventing actinic keratoses / skin cancers in HIGH-RISK skin (xeroderma pigmentosum, field cancerization): grade B. Real, human, topical, includes one landmark RCT (Yarosh 2001) and an independent-leaning grading review (Krutmann 2025 rates photolyase "Strong" for DNA-damage repair). Caveat: the strongest RCT is tiny (n=30) and in a genetically repair-deficient population; much of the field-cancerization literature is manufacturer-funded.
  • For visibly reversing COSMETIC PHOTOAGING (wrinkles, elasticity, tone) in healthy skin — the brand-relevant claim: grade D. Essentially unproven topically. The best systematic review (Luze 2020) found zero RCTs on photoaging endpoints; the one independent RCT in sun-damaged skin (Alvares 2022) found no benefit of photolyase over plain sunscreen; the lone cosmetic-appearance study cited could not be located/verified.

The dossier is, to its credit, mostly honest about this split. The verification did not find fabricated studies — all 13 sources are real and the headline numbers are largely accurate. The problems are smaller: a few overstatements, two miscitations, and some industry conflicts that were under-labeled. These have been corrected inline above.

What was verified as accurate (claims that hold)

  • Stege 2000 (PNAS, Source 1): Real. 40–45% CPD reduction, prevented erythema/sunburn cells/immunosuppression — accurate. (In-vitro/ex-vivo mechanistic; correctly tiered.)
  • Yarosh 2001 (Lancet, Source 2): Real RCT. n=30 XP, AK 8.2 vs 25.9 (≈68%, p=0.004), BCC 3.8 vs 5.4 (≈30%) — accurate. (BCC wording tightened: 95% CI 0.38–2.82 does not cross zero, but trial under-powered for BCC.)
  • Stoddard/Moy 2017 (JDD, Source 3): Real RCT. n=15, Eucerin placebo, −46.6% vs −32.7% at 8 wks, +29.2% vs +31.4% later, p=0.0026 — accurate.
  • Alvares 2022 (An Bras Dermatol, Source 4): Real RCT. n=40 (80 forearms), factorial, verbatim "no significant differences between SC and SC+P"; antioxidant partial clearance 47.4% vs 23.7% (p=0.018) — accurate. This is the genuine independent counterweight the dossier claims it is.
  • Luze 2020 (Source 8): Real. 52 studies; verbatim "lack of randomized controlled trials demonstrating the efficacy of DNA repair enzymes on photoageing" — accurate.
  • Krutmann 2025 "PINGing Sunshine" (Source 9): Real. photolyase "Strong" / T4 endonuclease "Moderate" for DNA-repair; SPF50 CPD protection 62%→93% — accurate. (Rating is for DNA-repair, NOT wrinkle reversal — correctly noted.)
  • Puig 2019 (Source 7): Real. 11 studies / 228 subjects; substudy numbers (Moscarella 14% vs 54%, Eibenschutz post-PDT, Giustini XP n=8 65%, Navarrete-Dechent 76.6%) all check out; ISDIN COI confirmed.
  • "Six critical questions" (Source 11): Real (CCID 2019;12:617-624).
  • CPD biology (§2): CPDs are the predominant dimeric photoproduct and account for ≥80% of UVB mutations — accurate after wording fix.

Main caveats (what a skeptic should hold onto)

  1. The brand-relevant claim is the weak one. Everything strong here is about DNA lesions and cancer/AK prevention in high-risk skin. The cosmetic-photoaging case for healthy users is grade-D: no RCTs, and the one independent RCT in damaged skin was negative for the enzyme.
  2. The cancer/AK data is in special populations (XP = genetically repair-deficient; field-cancerization = already heavily sun-damaged). It does not transfer to healthy cosmetic users, and it is unclaimable drug territory regardless (§8 handles this well).
  3. Industry conflict is broader than the dossier originally flagged. It correctly flagged Puig/ISDIN, but Source 6 (Emanuele/TPF 50) is also industry-authored, and Source 11 (the "skeptical" piece) is authored by Yarosh + Moy — both commercially aligned with these enzymes. Now labeled inline.
  4. The independent evidence is thin and skews negative or neutral. Strip out manufacturer-linked work and you are left mainly with Luze 2020 ("no RCTs for photoaging") and Alvares 2022 ("no benefit over sunscreen").
  5. Delivery and the light requirement remain the make-or-break unknowns (§5) — and even the pro-industry "Six questions" piece concedes in-vivo penetration/activity is an open question.

Claims that were softened or corrected in the dossier

  • Emanuele 2014 (§4 Tier 1 & §6): Originally implied the combo reduced "CPDs, 8-OHdG, and protein carbonylation." The study found synergy for CPD and PC but NOT 8-OHdG. Corrected, and the study is now labeled industry-authored (it studies the authors' own TPF 50 product).
  • Puig 2019 (§4b): "ISDIN funded the review" → narrowed to "funded the article-processing charges" (the accurate, slightly less damning phrasing) plus the confirmed employment/advisory-board COIs.
  • Yarosh 2001 BCC (§4a): "≈30% lower, CIs overlapping" was imprecise → replaced with the actual difference 1.6 [95% CI 0.38–2.82] and the note that the trial was under-powered for BCC.
  • CPD share (§2): "~80% of UVB photoproducts" conflated photoproducts with mutations → corrected to ~75% of dimers / ≥80% of UVB mutations.
  • Source 10 miscitations (§4b and §4c): Yarosh 1999 (Source 10) is a T4N5 paper in XP/skin-cancer patients and contains neither the 6-month/8-day biomarker studies nor the 30-women cosmetic study attributed to it. Both citations corrected: the biomarker numbers reattributed to the Luze review as secondary-sourced; the cosmetic-appearance study marked unverified with the primary not located.
  • Source 11 framing (§4): Re-labeled from independent "skeptical counterweight" to an industry-aligned (Yarosh/Moy) "questions the field acknowledges" piece.

Bottom line for the brand

The dossier's §10–11 verdict ("real mechanism, excellent safety, complement to sunscreen, NOT a hero anti-wrinkle active; cosmetic-photoaging evidence is weak") is well-supported and survives adversarial checking. Keep that framing. The corrections above tighten honesty at the margins (industry labeling, two miscitations, one overstated biomarker) but do not change the strategic conclusion: usable as a premium "active photoprotection / repair-support" layer alongside real SPF, in strict cosmetic "supports/helps" language — never as a DNA-repair or anti-wrinkle hero claim.
