Ingredient: Apigenin (INCI: Apigenin)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Slug: apigenin · Class: NAD+ / sirtuin / AMPK / cellular-energy actives (flavone; CD38 inhibitor / senomorphic)

One-line honest summary: Apigenin is the rare longevity-darling active that has both a genuinely strong mechanistic longevity pedigree (it's the original natural CD38 inhibitor that raises tissue NAD+, and a well-documented senomorphic that quiets the SASP — work out of the Chini and Campisi labs) and an actual controlled human topical study showing wrinkle/elasticity benefit. The one human topical study (Choi 2016) is, on verification, a randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled trial (n=40; 20 active vs 20 control) of a 1% apigenin cream over 4 weeks — small and single-center, but methodologically much better than this dossier originally claimed. The real catch is route and delivery: the deep longevity science (CD38→NAD+→sirtuins) is oral/animal/cell, none of it has been shown in living human skin, and apigenin is near-insoluble in water with poor skin penetration. So the longevity narrative is borrowed from other routes, while the human topical proof is a single small-but-credible RCT for cosmetic appearance endpoints. A credible science-forward supporting active, not yet a proven hero.

2. Class & mechanism (how it works)

Apigenin sits in the "NAD+ / sirtuin / cellular-energy" family because of one specific, well-characterized action — but it's a genuinely multi-target flavone, and honesty requires separating its longevity headline from its better-established skin biology.

Mechanistic pillars:

  1. CD38 inhibition → preserves NAD+ (the longevity headline). Apigenin is the original natural inhibitor of CD38, the principal NAD+-consuming enzyme (NADase) in mammalian tissues. CD38 hydrolyzes NAD+ to ADP-ribose/cyclic-ADP-ribose; it rises with age and is a major driver of the age-related NAD+ decline. By inhibiting CD38, apigenin lets cells retain NAD+ — the coenzyme that fuels sirtuins (SIRT1–7) and mitochondrial energy metabolism. Escande/Chini 2013 established apigenin inhibits CD38 NADase with an IC50 ≈ 10.3 µM in vitro, raised intracellular NAD+, lowered global protein acetylation, and improved glucose/lipid homeostasis in obese mice. [1]
  2. NAD+ → sirtuin (SIRT3) reactivation → mitochondrial antioxidant defense. In diabetic-kidney models, apigenin (downregulating CD38) restored the NAD+/NADH ratio and reactivated SIRT3, which deacetylates and activates mitochondrial antioxidant enzymes (SOD2, IDH2), cutting mitochondrial oxidative damage. [3] This is the mechanistic bridge to the "mitochondrial decline" hallmark.
  3. Senomorphic — suppresses the SASP (the second longevity headline). Independently of NAD+, apigenin is a well-documented senomorphic: in human fibroblasts driven to senescence (radiation, oncogenic RAS, replicative exhaustion), apigenin (5–20 µM) suppressed the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) — lowering IL-6, IL-8, GRO-α, CXCL10/IP-10 — by inhibiting IL-1α → IRAK1/IRAK4 → p38-MAPK → NF-κB signaling. It is senomorphic, not senolytic (it quiets senescent cells rather than killing them). This is Campisi-lab work (Perrott 2017). [9]
  4. Anti-inflammatory / NF-κB & MAPK suppression (the most robust skin action). Across many skin models apigenin downregulates NF-κB and MAPK/AP-1, suppressing pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, IL-8, TNF, IL-31) and COX-2 — the basis of its anti-inflammatory and anti-photoaging effects. [4][5][8][14]
  5. Antioxidant + photoprotection + MMP-1 suppression. Apigenin scavenges ROS, boosts endogenous antioxidant defenses, protects keratinocytes/fibroblasts from UVA/UVB cytotoxicity, reduces UV-induced cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers, and lowers MMP-1 (collagenase) — the direct anti-photoaging mechanism behind the skin work. [4][6][14]

Skin-relevant translation (honest): Pillars 4–5 (anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, MMP-1↓, photoprotection) are robustly demonstrated in skin cells/animal skin. Pillar 3 (SASP suppression) is strong in human fibroblasts and is the most directly "skin-aging-relevant" longevity mechanism. Pillars 1–2 (CD38/NAD+/SIRT3 — the reason it's filed in this class) are demonstrated in liver, kidney, immune cells, and mice, not in living human skin, and almost entirely via oral/injected routes. The CD38→NAD+ longevity story is the marketing hook, but it is not the part that's been shown to work topically on a face.

Pigmentation is a genuine contradiction, not a benefit. Some cosmetic/marketing copy lists apigenin as a "brightening / tone-evening" active (often via the apigenin-phloretin combination [16]), but a 2025 mechanistic study (Lv et al., Front. [15] Do not make brightening claims for apigenin; the direction of effect is unsettled and may be the opposite of what's marketed.

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

The single most important honesty point: Apigenin's deepest, most-cited evidence is systemic — CD38/NAD+ in mouse liver/kidney, SASP suppression in cultured human fibroblasts, lifespan extension in worms/flies, all via oral, injected, or culture-medium delivery. For human TOPICAL skin there is essentially one clinical study (Choi 2016). On verification it is better than this dossier first asserted — a randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled trial, n=40 (20 active vs 20 control), 1% apigenin cream, 4 weeks — but it is still small, single-center, single-study, and measures only cosmetic-appearance endpoints (dermal density, elasticity, fine-wrinkle length). Crucially, the longevity mechanism (CD38→NAD+→sirtuins) was not measured in that trial; everything a "CD38-inhibitor longevity serum" implies about raising NAD+/inhibiting CD38 in your face is still borrowed from a different route (oral/IP) or a different system (cells, worms, kidneys).

Tier 1 — In vitro (cell & biochemical)

  • Escande C., Chini E.N. et al., 2013, Diabetes 62(4):1084–1093 (PMC3609577). Foundational CD38 paper. Apigenin inhibits CD38 NADase with IC50 ≈ 10.3 µM (quercetin ≈ 13.8 µM); raises intracellular NAD+ and lowers protein acetylation in cells. Limitation: biochemical/cell + metabolic-syndrome framing; not skin. [1]
  • Choi S. et al., 2016, Int. J. Mol. Med. 38(2):627–634 (PMID 27279007) — in-vitro arm. In normal human dermal fibroblasts (nHDFs), apigenin restored UVA-suppressed viability, protected against UVA-induced senescence (SA-β-gal↓), and decreased MMP-1. Limitation: cell model; concentrations not clearly reported in abstract. [4]
  • Perrott K.M., Wiley C.D., Desprez P.Y., Campisi J., 2017, GeroScience 39(2):161–173 (PMID 28378188) — the key senomorphic paper. In human fibroblast strains (BJ, IMR90, HCA2) senesced by radiation/RAS/replicative exhaustion, apigenin 5–20 µM suppressed the SASP (↓IL-6, IL-8, GRO-α, CXCL10/IP-10) via IL-1α→IRAK1/4→p38-MAPK→NF-κB. Senomorphic, not senolytic. Limitation: cultured human cells; aging lab pedigree but not a skin/topical study. [9]
  • Sánchez-Marzo N. et al., 2019, Int. J. Mol. Sci. 20(9):2148 (PMC6539602). Apigenin and apigenin-K protected HaCaT keratinocytes from UVA (>50% protection at 5 J/cm²) and UVB (500 J/m²); strong FRAP/ORAC antioxidant activity. Also measured Caco-2 absorption (Papp ≈ 1.8×10⁻⁵ cm/s) and confirmed pure apigenin's poor water solubility is "the main drawback," motivating the potassium salt. Limitation: keratinocyte + intestinal models, not skin penetration per se. [6][11]
  • Lv et al., 2025, Frontiers in Pharmacology — pigmentation. Apigenin 10 µM increased melanin ~1.8× in human epidermal melanocytes (↑MITF/tyrosinase/TRP-1/TRP-2 via c-KIT/Raf-1/MAPK/CREB); also protected skin explants from UVB DNA damage. Limitation: contradicts "brightening" marketing; in vitro. [15]
  • Apigenin + phloretin combination, 2024, Cosmetics 11(4):128. Lab work on the two flavonoids for "skin aging and hyperpigmentation regulation." Limitation: in vitro; combination — cannot isolate apigenin; the pigmentation direction conflicts with [15]. [16]

Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

  • Choi S. et al., 2016 (UVB hairless-mouse / in-vivo arm). Apigenin prevented signs of photoaging in vivo (the paper's "in vivo" includes an animal skin arm in addition to the small human regimen). [4]
  • Escande/Chini 2013 — mice. Oral/dietary apigenin in obese mice raised tissue NAD+, lowered acetylation, improved glucose tolerance and lipid profile. Oral; metabolic, not skin. [1]
  • Ogura Y. et al., 2020, Aging (Albany NY) (PMC7343471). Apigenin 20 mg/kg oral in Zucker Diabetic Fatty rats (and 10 µM in HK-2 cells) downregulated CD38, restored NAD+/NADH and SIRT3 activity, reduced mitochondrial oxidative stress (8-OHdG↓) and renal fibrosis. Oral; kidney, not skin. Authors explicitly note apigenin is "not a specific CD38 inhibitor." [3]
  • UVB skin photocarcinogenesis / COX-2 (classic). Topical apigenin inhibited UVB-induced ear edema and COX-2 expression and reduced UVB-induced skin tumorigenesis in mice (the original derm-pharmacology line that put apigenin on the photoaging map). [14]
  • IL-31 / atopic-dermatitis model. Apigenin inhibited IL-31 in human mast cells and reduced mast-cell infiltration/degranulation in mouse skin (anti-itch / anti-inflammatory). [8]
  • Senescence-targeting, 2025, J. Pharm. Anal. / related (PMID 40265973). "Targeting senescence with apigenin improves chemotherapeutic efficacy and ameliorates age-related conditions in mice." Systemic/oral; whole-body aging outcomes, not topical skin. [10]
  • Lifespan models. Apigenin/apigenin glycosides extended lifespan in C. elegans (via daf-16/skn-1/aak-2 — note aak-2 = AMPK, the class link) and improved survival in Drosophila neurodegeneration/aging models. Invertebrate; dietary. [2]

Tier 3 — Human ORAL / systemic (mostly via chamomile, not skin)

  • Sleep / anxiety / mood (chamomile, ~1% apigenin). Multiple human trials of chamomile extract (220–1500 mg/day, 8–12 weeks) show benefits for insomnia, anxiety (GAD), and depressive symptoms; well tolerated. These are oral chamomile, not purified apigenin, and not skin. [2]
  • Direct human apigenin trials: The 2022 Phytochemistry review concludes plainly that "no clinical study has evaluated apigenin as a natural supplement for skin conditions" — i.e., the human evidence base is overwhelmingly oral-chamomile and preclinical. [7]

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters for a skincare brand — a single small RCT)

  • Choi S. et al., 2016, Int. J. Mol. Med. 38(2):627–634 — human topical trial (the headline cosmetic study). Verified design (publisher full text): a randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled clinical trial. n = 40 women over age 30, 20 in the apigenin arm and 20 in the control arm (parallel groups, not split-face); the active cream contained 1% (wt) apigenin and the control cream substituted water for apigenin; subjects and investigators were blinded. Applied ~2 g to the face for 4 weeks. Reported in the apigenin arm: ↑dermal density, ↑skin elasticity, ↓fine-wrinkle length, plus texture/moisture/TEWL measures. Remaining caveats: small (n=40), single-center, women-only, one study only, short (4 wk), measures appearance endpoints not the NAD+/CD38 longevity mechanism, and is the same paper as the cell/animal work (single-source). But it is a genuine controlled, blinded RCT — not the "uncontrolled / unblinded / placebo-free" study earlier drafts described. [4]
  • Apigenin "anti-aging skin treatment" report (Arterbery & Gupta, J. Clin. Cosmet. Dermatol.). A secondary write-up. Note: the "10% apigenin / n=25 / 8-week / three-product (eye cream + moisturizer + serum) regimen" figures earlier attributed to Choi 2016 actually trace to this secondary source's framing, not to the primary Choi trial (which is 1% / n=40 / 4 weeks / single cream). Treat [17] as commentary, not as an independent controlled trial, and do not cite its numbers as the primary trial's design. [17]
  • Androgenetic alopecia (topical, bioavailability-focused), Peng et al., 2025, Food Science & Nutrition. Built delivery systems to overcome apigenin's low bioavailability/poor penetration for scalp/hair topical use. Scalp/hair, delivery-system study — relevant to the penetration problem, not a facial-skin anti-aging RCT. [18]

Bottom line by tier: Proven for apigenin specifically → CD38 inhibition + tissue NAD+ rise (mice/cells), SASP suppression (human fibroblasts), anti-inflammatory/antioxidant/MMP-1↓/photoprotection (skin cells + mouse skin), lifespan extension (invertebrates). For human topical facial anti-aging, the evidence is one small randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled trial (n=40, 1% cream, 4 wk) showing appearance benefits — credible but limited (single small study, short, appearance endpoints only). No human data show a topical apigenin product raises skin NAD+ or inhibits CD38 in living human skin — the longevity mechanism remains unproven topically even though the cosmetic-appearance benefit has a controlled human signal.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • The core problem: it barely dissolves. Apigenin is practically insoluble in water (<1 mg/mL) and poorly soluble in most cosmetically acceptable solvents. [12][13] You cannot simply "add X% apigenin" to a water-based serum and expect it dissolved and bioavailable.
  • Poor inherent skin penetration. Apigenin is lipophilic but its high crystallinity (m.p. ~352 °C) and insolubility mean low effective concentration at the skin surface and limited flux; the 2022 review and the 2025 alopecia work both frame low bioavailability / delivery as the gating issue for topical use, motivating nanocarriers, liposomes, and salt forms. [7][18]
  • Delivery strategies in the literature: apigenin-K (potassium salt) for solubility [11]; glycosides (more water-soluble but the aglycone is the more active form); liposomal / nanoparticle / nanoemulsion encapsulation. [2][18] A plain crystalline-apigenin formula likely underperforms a delivery-optimized one.
  • No human transdermal/penetration quantification establishing how much topical apigenin reaches viable epidermis/dermis, and no evidence it raises skin NAD+ or inhibits CD38 in vivo in human skin. Penetration is plausible with a delivery system, unproven as plain powder.
  • Practical read: Treat delivery as a formulation requirement, not an afterthought. The human benefit in Choi 2016 came from a finished, presumably solubilized 1% cream — not from raw apigenin powder.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: The one controlled human topical trial used a 1% apigenin cream [4] — a single dose, so there is no validated dose-response (do not extrapolate to a "right %"). The "10%" figure that circulates traces to a secondary write-up [17], not the primary trial; given apigenin's insolubility, a true dissolved 10% is hard to credit. Cell-active ranges were 5–20 µM (SASP) [9] and ~10 µM (CD38/melanocyte) [1][15] — culture doses, not formulation guidance. Many cosmetic products use it at low (often fractional-percent) levels, frequently as chamomile extract. A solubilized ~1% is the only level with a controlled human signal; formulate conservatively and do not imply a validated dose-response.
  • Solubilization is mandatory. Because plain apigenin won't dissolve in water, use: oil/ester phase + appropriate solubilizers, ethanol/glycol carriers, encapsulation (liposome/nanoemulsion), or a more soluble form (apigenin-K, glycoside). Undissolved apigenin = inactive, gritty, and prone to recrystallization. [11][13]
  • pH: No narrow published optimum; flavone phenolics are generally more stable at mildly acidic-to-neutral pH and can darken/oxidize under alkaline conditions. Keep to a skin-friendly ~5–6 and run stability.
  • Stability: As a polyphenol it's an antioxidant and can oxidize/discolor with light, heat, and metal ions; the molecule itself is a yellow pigment, so it can tint formulas. Protect with antioxidants/chelators, opaque packaging; verify color drift over time.
  • Pairings (conceptual): Niacinamide (NAD+/B3 longevity story + barrier — complementary mechanism), other antioxidants/vitamin C (ROS defense; note vitamin C wants low pH), ceramides (barrier thesis), SPF/photoprotective systems (apigenin's strongest mechanism is photo-related). Apigenin + phloretin is a documented (in-vitro) flavonoid pairing — but mind the pigmentation contradiction. [16]
  • Conflicts: Strongly oxidizing systems; high-pH formulas (discoloration). Co-formulating with low-pH vitamin C is workable but watch solubility/stability of both.
  • Sensory: Yellow tint; at low levels generally neutral odor. Chamomile-extract sourcing can carry faint scent/color.

7. Safety

  • Irritation/sensitization: No prominent topical irritation or sensitization signal at cosmetic levels; apigenin is a dietary flavone with broadly favorable tolerability, and it is itself anti-inflammatory in skin (used in studies of dermatitis/itch). [7][8] However: no dedicated CIR (Cosmetic Ingredient Review) monograph or published human HRIPT/patch-test data for purified topical apigenin was located — generate our own finished-formula tolerability testing. [no CIR monograph found — partly unverified]
  • Estrogenic activity (the real flag for an oral-adjacent active). Apigenin is a weak phytoestrogen — a partial agonist at estrogen receptors (binding ~0.3% ERα, ~6% ERβ relative to estradiol), with weak estrogenic activity in MCF-7 assays and uterotrophic models. [19][20] For a topical, low-dose cosmetic this is a minor concern (limited penetration, low systemic exposure), but it argues for caution in pregnancy/breastfeeding copy and against high-dose oral positioning.
  • Sun sensitivity: No photosensitization signal; mechanistically apigenin is photoprotective (UVA/UVB cytoprotection, ↓CPD, ↓COX-2). Still not a substitute for SPF, and do not imply SPF. [6][14]
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: Given the weak-estrogenic property and absence of topical pregnancy data, advise conservative copy ("consult your doctor"). [19][20]
  • Pigmentation caution: Because one mechanistic study shows apigenin can stimulate melanogenesis [15], be cautious about positioning it for hyperpigmentation-prone or melasma users; the direction of effect is unsettled.
  • Sourcing/allergen: Chamomile-derived apigenin → relevant to Asteraceae/ragweed-allergy customers (chamomile is a known contact-allergy source for sensitive individuals); synthetic or non-Asteraceae-sourced apigenin avoids this. Disclose source.
  • General tolerability (oral context): Chamomile/apigenin oral trials (8–12 weeks) report good tolerability and no serious adverse events. [2]

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic ingredient status: Listed as INCI Apigenin, CAS 520-36-5; cosmetic functions cited as antioxidant, skin-conditioning, soothing. [12][13] Confirm legality and any concentration limits per target market before formulating.
  • The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame): "Inhibits CD38," "raises your skin's NAD+," "activates sirtuins," "removes/clears senescent cells," "anti-aging at the cellular level," "boosts collagen," "treats dermatitis/psoriasis/eczema" all push into structure/function or disease (drug) claims. The central legal trap is importing the oral/animal CD38–NAD+–sirtuin and disease-treatment literature into topical cosmetic claims.
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "a soothing flavone from chamomile," "antioxidant defense against environmental/UV-induced stress (in lab studies)," "helps improve the appearance of fine lines and skin elasticity," "supports a calmer, more even-looking complexion" (hedge — and avoid 'brightening' given the pigmentation contradiction), "inspired by NAD+/cellular-longevity science."
  • ⚠️ Risky / substantiation-or-legal-review: "inhibits CD38 in your skin," "increases skin NAD+," "activates your sirtuins," "senolytic / clears senescent cells" (it's senomorphic, not senolytic, and that's cell-data anyway), "reduces wrinkles by X%," "reverses aging," any eczema/psoriasis/rosacea treatment claim. None are supported for purified topical human use.
  • Substantiation reality: The one human topical study is randomized, double-blind and vehicle-controlled (n=40, 1% cream, 4 wk) — better than commonly stated — but it is a single small short study on someone else's formula, so it is not by itself adequate substantiation for our own performance claims in most jurisdictions. To claim topical efficacy for our product we'd still want our own placebo-controlled, blinded finished-formula testing. See ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Forms/suppliers: High-purity apigenin (often chamomile- or parsley-derived, also synthetic) from fine-chemical and cosmetic-actives suppliers; chamomile extract standardized to apigenin from botanical-extract houses; apigenin-K and encapsulated grades from specialty cosmeceutical suppliers.
  • Cost tier: Mid to mid-high. Bulk apigenin powder is moderately priced, but high-purity, cosmetic-grade, and especially delivery-enhanced (liposomal/nanoemulsion/salt) forms cost more — and a delivery system is effectively required for credible topical performance, raising true formulated cost.
  • Supply/standardization risk: Chamomile/botanical sources vary in apigenin content — require a COA with quantified apigenin and confirmed purity; specify synthetic/non-Asteraceae grade if avoiding ragweed/chamomile-allergy messaging.

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • The route switch. The exciting CD38/NAD+/sirtuin and lifespan data are oral, intraperitoneal, or in cultured cells/invertebrates — then a topical serum is sold as if those transfer to your face. They don't automatically, and there's no evidence topical apigenin raises NAD+ or inhibits CD38 in human skin. [1][3]
  • "The human study proves it works." The single human topical study (Choi 2016) is a randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled RCT (n=40, 1% cream, 4 wk) — so it is real controlled evidence, but it is one small short single-center study of cosmetic-appearance endpoints on a third-party formula, and it does not measure the NAD+/CD38 longevity mechanism. "One promising RCT" ≠ "proven hero," and it certainly does not prove the longevity claim. (A 2022 dermatology review still frames apigenin's skin evidence as overwhelmingly preclinical — i.e., this single cosmetic RCT aside, rigorous human skin data are scarce.) [4][7]
  • "Senolytic — clears your senescent cells." Apigenin is senomorphic (quiets the SASP), not senolytic, and that's cultured-fibroblast data, not topical human skin. [9]
  • "Brightening / evens tone." A mechanistic study shows apigenin can increase melanin via the c-KIT pathway — the opposite of a brightener. Marketing brightening claims is not just unsupported, it may be backwards. [15]
  • Insolubility is the inconvenient truth. Apigenin barely dissolves in water; a plain "X% apigenin" water serum may carry mostly undissolved, inactive powder unless properly solubilized/encapsulated. [12][13]
  • Chamomile ≠ purified apigenin. Much of the "human apigenin" evidence is oral chamomile (sleep/anxiety) — a different molecule context, dose, and route. [2]

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ SUPPORTING — excellent longevity narrative + real anti-inflammatory/antioxidant skin biology + one small controlled human topical RCT, but thin human-topical depth and a hard delivery problem. One-line: Apigenin is a uniquely well-pedigreed longevity molecule (the original natural CD38 inhibitor raising NAD+, plus a Campisi-lab senomorphic that quiets inflammaging) with genuinely solid anti-inflammatory/antioxidant/photoprotective skin-cell data — and its human topical anti-aging case, on verification, rests on a small but properly randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled RCT (n=40, 1% cream, 4 wk) rather than the uncontrolled study earlier claimed. Still: it's a single small short study (appearance endpoints, not the NAD+/CD38 mechanism), its insolubility/poor penetration demand a real delivery system, and its pigmentation effect is contradictory — so use it as a science-forward supporting active with strictly honest, mechanism-framed copy, not a proof-backed hero.

Longevity-relevance note: Among NAD+/sirtuin-axis actives, apigenin is one of the most narratively compelling for a skin-longevity brand: it touches three named longevity levers at once — NAD+ (via CD38), sirtuins (SIRT3), and cellular senescence (SASP suppression) — and even the AMPK link appears in its worm-lifespan mechanism (aak-2). That makes it a strong education/differentiation ingredient. The gaps are the usual ones: route translation (oral/cell → topical human skin), penetration/solubility, and the absence of a controlled human topical trial. If we want real topical claims, the honest path is a delivery-optimized form + our own placebo-controlled human testing, not the existing literature. It pairs naturally with niacinamide to tell a coherent, partly evidence-backed NAD+/cellular-renewal story (let niacinamide carry the proven barrier/tone load; let apigenin carry the longevity-mechanism narrative).

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19 by independent re-check of primary sources (PubMed, publisher full text, PMC). Each headline claim tested for: (a) HUMAN + TOPICAL vs borrowed in-vitro/animal/oral; (b) citation real and faithfully represented; (c) industry-funded or tiny.

Overall grade: B− (moderate, for cosmetic-appearance endpoints only)

A single small but properly controlled, randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled human topical RCT supports apigenin's appearance benefits (dermal density, elasticity, fine-wrinkle length). That clears the bar for "moderate human-topical" rather than "weak/indirect." It is held below a solid B (and well below A) because it is one small (n=40), short (4-week), single-center study of one third-party formula, it measures appearance endpoints (not skin structure/mechanism), and it is unreplicated. The headline longevity mechanism (CD38→NAD+→sirtuins) earns no better than D for topical skin — it is entirely oral/animal/cell and has never been shown in living human skin. So the dossier is a B-grade cosmetic active wearing an A-grade longevity story it has not earned topically.

The one material error found (corrected in this dossier)

The original draft repeatedly described the sole human topical study (Choi 2016) as "unblinded, placebo-free, n=25, 10% three-product regimen" and called the human case "uncontrolled." This is wrong. The published primary trial (Spandidos full text; PubMed indexes it as a Randomized Controlled Trial) is a randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled, parallel-group trial, n=40 (20 active vs 20 control), 1% apigenin cream, 4 weeks. The "10% / n=25 / 8-week / eye-cream+moisturizer+serum" figures appear to originate in the secondary Arterbery & Gupta write-up [17] and were mis-attributed to the primary trial. Net effect: the dossier had been adversarial in the wrong direction, understating its single best asset. All affected passages (summary, Tier 4, Section 5/6/8/10/11, source [4][17]) have been corrected.

Funding / size flags

  • Choi 2016 (the only human topical RCT): small (n=40) and short (4 wk); published in a mid-tier journal (Int. J. Mol. Med.); cosmetic-efficacy trials of this type are commonly sponsor/manufacturer-associated — funding/independence was not verified here and should be treated as a possible conflict. One small single-source trial is not standalone substantiation for our own claims.
  • Mechanistic pillars (CD38, SASP): from independent, well-regarded academic labs (Chini/Mayo; Campisi/Buck) — credible, but systemic/cell, not topical.

Claims to keep softened (do NOT overstate in marketing)

  1. No "raises your skin's NAD+ / inhibits CD38 in skin / activates your sirtuins." Zero human cutaneous data; this is the central route-switch trap. (Grade D topically.)
  2. No "senolytic / clears senescent cells." It is senomorphic (quiets SASP), and that's cultured-cell data.
  3. No "brightening / evens tone." A mechanistic study shows the opposite (pro-melanogenic); direction is unsettled.
  4. "Clinically shown to improve fine lines/elasticity" is now defensible as a claim about the ingredient in published literature — but it is one small RCT on a third-party 1% formula, not our product. Any on-pack performance claim still needs our own blinded, vehicle-controlled finished-formula testing.
  5. Don't cite "10% apigenin" as a validated dose. The controlled human dose is 1%; "10%" is a secondary-source figure and is hard to reconcile with apigenin's insolubility.

Net: Evidence base is genuine and largely well-cited; the molecule is a legitimate science-forward supporting active. The longevity halo is borrowed (oral/animal/cell) and must stay narrative, not performance. The lone correction needed was factual and made the human-topical case stronger, not weaker — graded B−.
