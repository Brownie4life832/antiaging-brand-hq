Ingredient: Nrf2 / Antioxidant Peptides — Acetyl sh-Heptapeptide-1 (P7) & Acetyl Tripeptide-30 Citrulline (peptide pair)

Slug: nrf2-antioxidant-peptides · Class: Peptides (antioxidant / cytoprotective "Nrf2-axis & anti-inflammatory" subgroup) Researched: 2026-06-19 (exhaustive) · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product

How to read this dossier. This is an evidence-graded literature review of two marketed peptides that are routinely co-listed under an "antioxidant / Nrf2 / cytoprotective" banner. They are not the same kind of molecule and the brief's framing slightly over-unifies them: - Acetyl sh-Heptapeptide-1 (PerfectionPeptide P7, Mibelle) is a genuine Nrf2-pathway peptide — it mimics the ETGE motif of Nrf2 and competes with Keap1. This is the real "Nrf2 antioxidant peptide." - Acetyl Tripeptide-30 Citrulline (Peptide AC29 / part of Vanistryl, Lipotec/Lubrizol) is primarily an MMP-inhibiting ECM-protective / anti-inflammatory peptide with secondary antioxidant (ROS-lowering) activity reported in vitro. It is not a documented Nrf2/Keap1 activator. Calling it an "Nrf2 peptide" is a mild category error that marketing copy makes — flagged here so we don't repeat it.

The single most important honest finding up front: the target biology (Nrf2/Keap1 as a master antioxidant switch that declines with age; MMPs as ECM destroyers; ROS/inflammaging as real hallmarks) is A-grade, independent science. But the topical human efficacy of these two specific cosmetic peptides is weak and almost entirely supplier-owned: P7's "clinical" data is unpublished Mibelle marketing data on skin explants + in-vitro models (no independent peer-reviewed human RCT located); AC29's only in-vivo human data is a single n=12 supplier split-area stretchmark study using a 5% complex (Vanistryl), not the isolated peptide. The one genuine peer-reviewed paper in this pair (Liu et al. 2024, Int. J. Pharm.) is in-vitro only (HaCaT + 3T3 cells) and carries a published 2025 corrigendum. Treat every "%" you see online as a dish or supplier number, not a clinical outcome.

2. Class & Mechanism

These are signal / cytoprotective peptides, not structural building blocks and not neuro-relaxants. They act by engaging intracellular pathways — but by different ones:

2a. Acetyl sh-Heptapeptide-1 (P7) — a direct Keap1–Nrf2 protein–protein-interaction (PPI) peptide

  • Nrf2 (NFE2L2) is the master transcription factor for the cell's endogenous antioxidant/detox program. Under basal conditions Nrf2 is bound by its repressor Keap1, ubiquitinated, and degraded. Nrf2 docks on Keap1 via two motifs — a high-affinity ETGE motif and a lower-affinity DLG motif [5][6].
  • P7's sequence (Ac-DEETGEF) reproduces the ETGE motif, so it acts as a competitive inhibitor of Keap1: it occupies Keap1's Kelch pocket, frees endogenous Nrf2, which then translocates to the nucleus, dimerizes with small Maf proteins, binds antioxidant response elements (ARE), and switches on cytoprotective genes — HO-1, NQO1, glutathione S-transferase, glutathione peroxidase, SOD, etc. [1][3][5][6]. This is a legitimate, well-characterized drug-discovery strategy (Keap1–Nrf2 PPI inhibition); P7 is a cosmetic application of it.
  • Why it's longevity-relevant: Nrf2 activity declines with age — nuclear Nrf2 accumulation is blunted in older adults even under oxidative stress [6]. A peptide that re-sensitizes the switch targets a genuine aging mechanism (see §3).

2b. Acetyl Tripeptide-30 Citrulline (AC29) — an MMP-inhibiting / ECM-protective + anti-inflammatory peptide

  • Designed (by combinatorial chemistry) to inhibit matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) — the enzymes that degrade dermal collagen/elastin in photoaging and striae. Supplier in-vitro data: complete inhibition of MMP-2, MMP-9 −63.1%, MMP-1 −34.7%, MMP-3 −39.1% [9][10]. Mechanistically this is ECM-preservation, not antioxidant signaling.
  • The 2024 peer-reviewed paper (Liu et al.) then reported, in skin-cell models, anti-inflammatory activity (↓ COX-2, TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6) and antioxidant activity (ROS reduction), plus ↑ Collagen IV, ↓ MMP-9, ↑ barrier proteins (TGM1/transglutaminase-1, filaggrin), and ↑ aquaporin-3 (hydration) [7]. The antioxidant effect is a downstream ROS-lowering readout — the paper does not demonstrate Keap1/Nrf2 engagement. So AC29 is "antioxidant-adjacent," not an Nrf2 mechanism peptide.

Honest mechanistic read. P7 has a clean, specific, biochemically credible mechanism (ETGE→Keap1) that is squarely on the cutting edge of antioxidant pharmacology — its weakness is delivery and human proof, not concept. AC29's mechanism is plausible and multi-pronged (MMP-inhibition is real ECM logic; the anti-inflammatory/antioxidant data are in-vitro). The leap in both cases is the same as for all peptides: from "moves the target in a dish/explant" to "applied topically, reaches living cells and produces a visible benefit." That leap is largely unproven (§4–§5).

Cleanest framing: P7 occupies the "endogenous antioxidant-defense / cytoprotection (Nrf2 axis)" lane — conceptually a systems-level "make the cell defend itself" play, complementary to direct antioxidants (vitamin C/E, ergothioneine) rather than redundant with them. AC29 sits in the "ECM-protection + anti-inflammatory" lane, overlapping more with matrikine/firming and soothing peptides than with true antioxidants. Co-marketing them as one "antioxidant peptide duo" is a marketing convenience, not a mechanistic unity.

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (core of this dossier)

Reading guide: Benefits proven for topical human use must live in the human-topical tier. For this pair, that tier is thin, small, and supplier-owned. The strongest peer-reviewed and independent evidence is about the targets (Keap1/Nrf2 biology; MMPs) — not about the cosmetic peptides themselves.

Tier 0 — Foundational target biology — STRONG, independent, peer-reviewed

(Validates the rationale, not the cosmetic peptides.) - Keap1–Nrf2 as the master oxidative-stress/aging axis. Yu C, Xiao J-H. The Keap1-Nrf2 System: A Mediator between Oxidative Stress and Aging. Oxid Med Cell Longev 2021. Establishes that Nrf2 expression/activity decline with age, blunting antioxidant defense [6]. The Keap1/ETGE structural biology that P7 exploits is well characterized in the broader literature [5]. - Nrf2 as a dermatology therapeutic target. Multiple 2022–2026 reviews (e.g., NRF2 as a Therapeutic Target in Dermatological Disorders, Ph 2026; Emerging Therapeutic Strategies for Nrf2-Associated Skin Disorders, Antioxidants 2026; Frontiers in Pharmacology 2022 on phytochemical Nrf2 photoprotection) confirm Nrf2 is a credible, actively researched axis in photoaging/skin defense [5][14][15]. - MMPs degrade dermal ECM in photoaging/striae — foundational, independent dermatology (the rationale AC29 is built on).

Takeaway: The premises are solid science. Marketing routinely borrows this A-grade target credibility for the C/D-grade peptide-efficacy claims — watch for that (§10).

Tier 1 — In-vitro / ex-vivo on the cosmetic peptides — present, mostly SUPPLIER-origin (one peer-reviewed paper)

Acetyl sh-Heptapeptide-1 (P7) — supplier in-vitro + ex-vivo: - Nrf2/ARE activation: Mibelle reports P7 stimulates Nrf2-dependent antioxidant enzymes in cell models [1][2]. No independent peer-reviewed in-vitro paper located for the cosmetic peptide. - Skin-explant (ex-vivo) data: protective effect against UV-induced sunburn-cell formation and against Langerhans-cell depletion in human skin explants; protection of the epidermis against oxidative-stress-induced DNA damage [1][2]. Origin: Mibelle technical literature — not independently published; no methodology/sample sizes available.

Acetyl Tripeptide-30 Citrulline (AC29) — one genuine peer-reviewed in-vitro paper + supplier MMP data: - Liu Z, Zhao N, Liang L, et al. 2024. Evaluation of the anti-aging potential of acetyl tripeptide-30 citrulline in cosmetics. Int J Pharm 663:124557. PMID 39103061, DOI 10.1016/j.ijpharm.2024.124557 [7]. Design: in-vitro only — human immortalized keratinocytes (HaCaT) + mouse embryonic fibroblasts (3T3). Results: anti-inflammatory (↓ COX-2, TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6); antioxidant (ROS inhibition reported across a wide, oddly-expressed "95–340%" range); ↑ Collagen IV; ↓ MMP-9; ↑ TGM1 and filaggrin; ↑ aquaporin-3. Limitations: no human or animal data; no topical-delivery model; cell-line (not primary/3D-skin) models; industry-authored (HBN Research Institute / Shenzhen Hujia Technology); concentrations not clearly stated in the abstract; the ROS metric phrasing ("95% to 340%") is ambiguous. A published 2025 corrigendum exists (Int J Pharm 684, 2025; DOI 10.1016/j.ijpharm.2025.[corrigendum]) [16] — verify what was corrected before quoting any specific figure from the original. - Supplier MMP-inhibition data: complete MMP-2 inhibition; MMP-9 −63.1%; MMP-1 −34.7%; MMP-3 −39.1% [9][10]. In-vitro enzyme assays in Lipotec/Lubrizol technical literature.

Limitations (whole tier): Supplier ELISA/gene/enzyme data at µM/ppm in dishes or explants say nothing about delivered dose in living human skin; the one peer-reviewed paper is in-vitro, industry-authored, and corrigendum-flagged; "%" figures are dish numbers.

Tier 2 — Animal data

Essentially none located for skin efficacy of either cosmetic peptide. (The 3T3 line in Liu 2024 is mouse-derived cells in vitro, not a live-animal study.) Do not borrow unrelated Nrf2-knockout-mouse biology as efficacy proof for P7.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL data

Not applicable. Both are topical cosmetic actives; there is no oral-supplement evidence and none should be implied. (Systemic Nrf2 activators — sulforaphane, etc. — are a different category and must not be conflated with a topical heptapeptide.)

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL data (the tier that should carry claims)

4a. Acetyl sh-Heptapeptide-1 (P7): - No independent, peer-reviewed human topical RCT located. Mibelle states "clinical studies have confirmed its claims" — but the specific, attributable P7 human data are unpublished supplier studies, and the most concrete results widely repeated online (reduced DNA damage, fewer sunburn cells, less Langerhans-cell depletion) trace to ex-vivo skin explants, not living-subject in-vivo trials [1][2]. INCIDecoder's entry carries no peer-reviewed studies or evidence rating for the ingredient [17]. Bottom line: P7 has NO verifiable published human topical efficacy trial.

4b. Acetyl Tripeptide-30 Citrulline (AC29): - The only in-vivo human data is a supplier study, and it's for stretchmarks, not facial anti-aging. Lipotec study: n=12 female volunteers with recent striae; split-area, intra-individual placebo-controlled (active vs placebo cream on different striae on the same person); twice daily, 60 days; the cream contained 5% Vanistryl (the complex, not 5% isolated peptide). Dermatologist + instrumental assessment reported improvement in ~all parameters for ~100% of volunteers (e.g., firmness +70.8%, softness +133%, brightness +75%, elasticity +44%), p<0.02 [10][11]. Limitations: very small (n=12); supplier-owned/unpublished; tests a multi-ingredient complex (Vanistryl = AC29 + Pseudoalteromonas Ferment Extract + Pentapeptide-18), so the effect cannot be attributed to AC29 alone; the actual peptide concentration is far below 5%; stretchmark endpoint ≠ facial wrinkle/anti-aging endpoint; intra-individual split-area design is reasonable but the effect-size figures are supplier-reported.

4c. Honest summary of Tier 4: Neither peptide has an independent, adequately powered, peer-reviewed human topical RCT. P7 = explant + unpublished supplier "clinical." AC29 = one small (n=12) supplier in-vivo study on a complex for stretchmarks. The 2024 Liu paper — the only proper journal article in the pair — does not include any human use. Even the broader literature acknowledges this: Liu et al. note that AC29's efficacy "is often proclaimed in various commercial advertisements, yet there is a lack of comprehensive evaluations regarding [its] effectiveness" [7].

Tier 5 — Adjacent / analogous evidence (context only, do NOT transfer as proof)

  • Novel Keap1–Nrf2 PPI peptides for UVA skin aging (2025). A peer-reviewed computational-discovery + experimental-validation paper identified new bioactive peptides targeting the Keap1–Nrf2 interaction against UVA-induced skin aging [18]. This validates the concept P7 is built on — but tests different peptides, not Acetyl sh-Heptapeptide-1.
  • Other antioxidant/anti-wrinkle peptides (SNAP-8/Acetyl Glutamyl Heptapeptide-1; Acetyl Hexapeptide-8; cyclized Hexapeptide-9) have their own small human trials — none are these two molecules and their results must not be borrowed.

5. Topical Bioavailability / Penetration

  • The central, under-acknowledged problem for the whole class. Peptides are hydrophilic and (other than the very smallest) sit at or above the ~500 Dalton practical threshold for passive stratum-corneum penetration [19]. Neither of these is small: P7 ≈ 868 Da and AC29 ≈ 547 Da — both above ~500 Da, so unaided passive penetration to living cells is questionable for both, and more so for P7. Marketing's "low MW → good penetration" line for AC29 is only marginally true (it's small for a peptide, but still over the rule of thumb).
  • Where the peptide needs to reach matters. P7's target (Nrf2/Keap1) is intracellular — the peptide must not only cross the stratum corneum but enter the cytoplasm of viable keratinocytes/fibroblasts to compete with Keap1. That is a demanding delivery requirement, and the strongest honest caveat against P7. AC29's MMP targets are extracellular (dermal ECM) but still require reaching the dermis.
  • What helps (P7 specifically): P7 is supplied liposomally encapsulated (hydrogenated lecithin) [1][2] — a deliberate, rational delivery strategy that improves partitioning and cellular uptake versus a bare peptide. This is the strongest practical argument in P7's favor, but no published Franz-cell / tape-strip / in-vivo permeation data confirming it reaches viable-cell cytoplasm were located.
  • What helps (general): N-acetylation (both peptides) increases lipophilicity and exopeptidase resistance — modestly favorable for partitioning and stability.
  • Systemic-absorption framing (safety side, see §7): the same barrier that limits efficacy also limits systemic exposure — peptide cosmetics show minimal transcutaneous penetration and rapid in-skin proteolysis [20].
  • What's missing: No published permeation data for either peptide demonstrating delivery to the relevant living compartment. Penetration is assumed (P7: assumed + encapsulation-assisted), not demonstrated.

6. Formulation Notes

  • The dosing trap (read first). Both are sold as dilute trade materials. Use levels refer to the trade solution, not peptide content. Always specify trade-% vs. peptide-%.
  • P7 (PerfectionPeptide P7): recommended 0.5–3% of the trade material [1][2]. Water-soluble liposomal dispersion; add in the cool-down phase (heat can disrupt liposomes). Preservative/alcohol-free base.
  • AC29 / Vanistryl: supplier use levels for the Vanistryl complex are typically in the ~3–5% range (the n=12 study used 5% Vanistryl) [10][11]; isolated AC29 use levels per supplier datasheet. Water-soluble; cool-down phase.
  • pH: Peptides of this type are generally stable mildly acidic to near-neutral (~pH 5–6.5). No published optimal-pH/stability curves were located for either — treat exact tolerances as unverified and follow supplier specs.
  • Stability: Liposomal encapsulation (P7) is sensitive to high shear/heat and to some surfactants — follow Mibelle handling guidance. N-acetylation aids enzymatic stability. Independent stability data not located; supplier-spec dependent.
  • Pairings (rational, not proven):
  • P7 + direct antioxidants (vitamin C/E, ferulic, ergothioneine) is the most conceptually coherent stack — P7 boosts endogenous defense while the others provide exogenous scavenging (complementary, not redundant). Pairing P7 with niacinamide and barrier lipids is also sensible.
  • AC29 pairs logically with humectants (HA, glycerin), niacinamide, and other ECM/soothing actives; it's the kind of peptide designed to sit in a multi-active firming/soothing blend.
  • Conflicts: As with most peptides, avoid co-formulating in a strongly acidic vehicle (high-dose direct AHA/BHA, pure L-ascorbic acid) in the same product — protects peptide integrity and (for P7) liposome stability. Layering in a routine (separate products) is fine. Avoid high concentrations of incompatible charged surfactants with the liposomal P7.
  • Sensory: Both are light, water-thin, low-odor at use level; P7's shea-butter-containing base may add slight richness.

7. Safety

  • Irritation / sensitization: This class is among the most benign in skincare. At ppm–low-% levels, irritation and sensitization potential is low; marketed products report only rare mild reactions. A 2026 peptide-safety framework concludes contact (Type IV) sensitization is unlikely because short cosmetic peptides lack the electrophilicity for hapten formation, and Type I hypersensitivity risk is minimal for sequences under ~12 amino acids [20] — both peptides here are 3–7 residues.
  • Sun sensitivity: No evidence of photosensitization for either (P7 is, if anything, photoprotective in mechanism — though that must not be sold as a substitute for SPF). Daily SPF still applies to any anti-aging routine.
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: No specific safety studies for either peptide. General peptide reviews note no known contraindication to topical peptides in pregnancy and minimal systemic absorption [20][21], but absence of harm data is not proof of safety — do not market as "pregnancy-safe." Default: "insufficient ingredient-specific data; consult a clinician."
  • Systemic exposure: Minimal — large/hydrophilic peptides show negligible transcutaneous penetration and undergo rapid in-skin proteolysis [20] (the same barrier that limits efficacy limits systemic risk).
  • Sourcing/ethics: Both fully synthetic (solid-phase / recombinant synthesis); vegan / cruelty-free compatible; no animal-derived inputs. P7's base contains shea butter (plant) and lecithin (typically soy/sunflower — check for allergen labeling). No PFAS/fluorination concern in either.

8. Regulatory & Claims

  • Cosmetic vs. drug line. Both are cosmetic ingredients with established INCI names and CosIng-type listings (AC29 in EU CosIng; P7 INCI per CosIng) [4][12]. Their mechanisms — "activates Nrf2," "protects DNA," "inhibits MMPs," "boosts antioxidant enzymes" — sit dangerously close to (and in places over) the structure/function "drug" line under US FDA logic. "Protects DNA," "prevents aging," "activates the cell's defense system" are exactly the kind of claims that draw regulatory scrutiny. Keep consumer copy in appearance / support language.
  • Safe (cosmetic) phrasings: "helps support the skin's natural antioxidant defenses," "helps visibly protect against the look of environmental stress," "helps skin look more resilient/even," "supports a healthy-looking barrier," "helps improve the appearance of firmness." Per ../../brand/glossary.md: "supports / visibly / helps improve the appearance of," not "boosts X%."
  • Risky / unsupported — DO NOT USE without substantiation/legal review:
  • "Activates Nrf2," "switches on your DNA-defense genes," "protects DNA from UV damage." These are mechanism/structure-function claims built on in-vitro/explant supplier data — high regulatory risk; treat as drug-adjacent.
  • "Clinically proven to [firm / reduce wrinkles / protect against aging] by X%." The "%" figures (MMP −63%, ROS −95–340%, firmness +70%, etc.) are in-vitro or supplier in-vivo (and for a complex, n=12) — quoting them invites a substantiation challenge.
  • Borrowing the 2025 Keap1–Nrf2 UVA peptide paper [18], the Liu 2024 in-vitro paper, or the AC29 stretchmark study as proof of a facial anti-aging benefit — each result belongs only to its specific molecule/model/endpoint. The stretchmark data in particular is not evidence for facial wrinkles.
  • "Antioxidant peptide" applied to AC29 as if it were an Nrf2 activator — it isn't; that's a mechanism overclaim.
  • EU/other markets: Standard cosmetic-claims substantiation rules (EU Reg. 655/2013 common criteria) apply — keep an evidence file for any efficacy claim, and note that "DNA protection" / "antioxidant" claims face heightened scrutiny in the EU.

9. Sourcing & Cost

  • Suppliers: Branded — PerfectionPeptide P7 (Mibelle Biochemistry) and Peptide AC29 / Vanistryl® (Lipotec / Lubrizol). Both have many Chinese/Korean INCI-equivalent sources (MedChemExpress, BOC Sciences, Spec-Chem "SpecPed CIT AT30P," ExperChem, Mobel Biochem, etc.) at lower cost.
  • Cost tier: Mid-to-premium for the branded materials — P7 in particular carries an encapsulation/IP premium and is a specialty Swiss active; AC29/Vanistryl is a mid-tier branded peptide. Both are potent at low-% trade levels, so cost-in-use is moderate, not extreme. Generic copies are cheaper but carry identity/purity and "is the encapsulation/dilution real?" risk — for a premium brand, branded or fully-spec'd material is safer (especially P7, where the liposomal delivery is the whole point).
  • Supply: Synthetic, scalable, no botanical/seasonal constraints.

10. Hype vs. Evidence

  • Biggest overreach: target-credibility laundering. Marketing cites the solid, independent Keap1/Nrf2-and-aging science [5][6] (and the genuinely exciting Nrf2-as-skin-target reviews [14][15]) and then silently transfers that A-grade credibility to the C/D-grade P7-efficacy claims. Nrf2 really is a master antioxidant switch that declines with age — that does not prove that topical, encapsulated Acetyl sh-Heptapeptide-1 reaches keratinocyte cytoplasm and meaningfully activates it in living human skin.
  • "Clinical studies have confirmed" (P7). No independent peer-reviewed human RCT is locatable; the attributable data are ex-vivo skin explants + unpublished supplier "clinical." "Clinically tested" ≠ "independently proven."
  • Dish/explant/supplier numbers paraded as proof. "MMP-2 fully inhibited," "MMP-9 −63%," "ROS −95–340%," "firmness +70.8%," "+133% softness" are in-vitro or supplier in-vivo figures (the firmness numbers are from a n=12 study of a 5% complex for stretchmarks), repeated everywhere as if from facial anti-aging trials.
  • The "antioxidant peptide duo" framing is a marketing construct. P7 is an Nrf2/Keap1 peptide; AC29 is an MMP-inhibitor with secondary antioxidant readouts and is not a documented Nrf2 activator. Bundling them as one antioxidant mechanism is convenient, not accurate.
  • Penetration is assumed. Both are at/over ~500 Da; P7 needs to reach the cytoplasm. Encapsulation is a credible help but is presented as if delivery is solved — it isn't demonstrated.
  • Corrigendum caution. The one real journal paper (Liu 2024) has a published 2025 corrigendum [16] — quoting its figures without checking what was corrected is risky.
  • Fair steelman: This is not snake oil. P7's mechanism is genuinely sophisticated and on-thesis — directly engaging the Keap1–Nrf2 axis is exactly where antioxidant science is heading [18], it's encapsulated (rare and thoughtful for a cosmetic peptide), and it's exceptionally safe/clean-label. AC29 has a coherent ECM-protective rationale, real (if in-vitro) anti-inflammatory data, and a (small, complex-based) human signal. The pair is mechanistically credible and very benign — its honest gap is independent human topical proof and demonstrated penetration, not plausibility.

11. Verdict for the Brand

⚠️ CONDITIONAL — interesting supporting/"story" actor, not a hero. P7 is the more compelling of the two (real Nrf2 mechanism + encapsulation); AC29 is a secondary ECM/anti-inflammatory peptide better classified elsewhere. Use only with strictly appearance-based, non-mechanistic claims; do not anchor a flagship benefit on either.

One-line reasoning: A mechanistically sophisticated, longevity-on-thesis Nrf2 peptide (P7) and a separate ECM/anti-inflammatory peptide (AC29) that are exceptionally safe but lack any independent, peer-reviewed human topical efficacy trial (P7 = explant + unpublished supplier data; AC29 = one n=12 supplier study of a complex for stretchmarks; the only journal paper is in-vitro and corrigendum-flagged) — credible enough to support an "endogenous antioxidant defense" narrative, not strong enough to anchor or to carry quantified claims.

  • Why not hero: The brand's credibility rests on evidence over hype (CLAUDE.md). Neither peptide has independent, adequately powered, peer-reviewed human proof; the headline "%" figures are dish/supplier/complex numbers we cannot honestly substantiate; and the most defensible mechanistic claims ("activates Nrf2," "protects DNA") are exactly the drug-adjacent claims we must avoid.
  • Where they fit (recommended):
  • P7 as a differentiated supporting antioxidant active layered onto better-evidenced antioxidants (vitamin C/E + ferulic, ergothioneine, niacinamide) — a genuinely interesting "helps support the skin's own defense system" story peptide, valuable for narrative distinctiveness if copy stays appearance-based. Its encapsulation and clean mechanism make it the preferred pick of the pair.
  • AC29 as a minor ECM/soothing supporting peptide — but it belongs conceptually with the firming/matrikine and soothing-peptide lanes (firming-matrikine-peptides.md, soothing-peptides.md), not in the antioxidant story. Lower priority than P7 for this brand's purposes.
  • Exclude / reclassify: Don't sell AC29 as an "Nrf2 / antioxidant peptide" — that's a mechanism overclaim.
  • Longevity-relevance note: P7 engages a bona-fide longevity hallmark — the age-related decline of the Nrf2/Keap1 antioxidant master switch [6] — which is strongly on-thesis for a skin-longevity brand and conceptually complementary to direct antioxidants and to the senescence/mitochondrial peptides. That makes P7 attractive narratively. But narrative fit must not outrun evidence: the target is longevity-relevant; the proof that this specific topical, encapsulated peptide activates Nrf2 in living human skin and yields a visible benefit is not yet there.

Recommendation: Keep in ../../ideas/considering.md as a conditional supporting "endogenous antioxidant defense" peptide, led by P7 (AC29 reclassified to the ECM/soothing lanes). Do not shortlist as a hero or use mechanistic/quantified claims without our own substantiated testing. Revisit if an independent, adequately powered, peer-reviewed human topical RCT appears for Acetyl sh-Heptapeptide-1.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Independent adversarial fact-check performed 2026-06-19. Method: each headline claim re-checked against primary sources for (a) human-vs-topical provenance, (b) whether cited studies are real and say what is claimed, (c) molecule/mechanism identity (is AC29 really an Nrf2 peptide? does the named INCI match the tested molecule?), and (d) funding / sample-size / endpoint red flags. Sources re-verified live: PMID 39103061 (Liu 2024) + its 2025 corrigendum, PMC8106771 (Yu & Xiao 2021), the Keap1–Nrf2 UVA peptide paper (S2095177925002631), supplier P7/AC29/Vanistryl literature, INCIDecoder/CosIng identity records, peptide-penetration (500 Da) and peptide-safety-framework sources.

Overall evidence grade for topical human efficacy of THIS pair: P7 = D+ (mechanism-led, no published human trial), AC29 = C–/D+ (one in-vitro journal paper + one tiny supplier complex study). Target biology grades A but validates the rationale, not the products.

What held up under scrutiny (real, accurately described): - Liu et al. 2024 (PMID 39103061, Int J Pharm 663:124557) — REAL. In-vitro only (HaCaT + 3T3). Anti-inflammatory + antioxidant + ECM/barrier readouts. Correctly flagged: industry-authored, no human/animal data, and a 2025 corrigendum exists [16] (verify corrected content before quoting figures). - Yu & Xiao 2021 (PMC8106771) — REAL. Supports the central, independent point that Nrf2 declines with age. - Keap1–Nrf2 UVA peptide paper 2025 (S2095177925002631) — REAL; correctly flagged as validating the concept on new peptides, NOT Acetyl sh-Heptapeptide-1. - P7 identity — sequence Ac-Asp-Glu-Glu-Thr-Gly-Glu-Phe (ETGE motif), CAS 1395088-14-8 (INCI) / 1356845-72-1 (synthesized), MW ~868, liposomal base — consistent across ci.guide, MedChemExpress/Ambeed, Mibelle. - AC29 identity & MMP/stretchmark data — Lys-α-Asp-Ile-Citrulline, CAS 2098345-00-5, MW ~547; MMP-2 fully inhibited / MMP-9 −63.1%; the n=12, 60-day, split-area stretchmark study used 5% Vanistryl (a complex) — all correctly attributed to supplier (Lipotec/Lubrizol) literature.

Corrections / cautions applied during this pass: 1. Mechanism-category correction (important). The brief bundles AC29 as an "Nrf2 / antioxidant peptide." AC29 is an MMP-inhibitor / ECM-protective + anti-inflammatory peptide with secondary in-vitro ROS-lowering — NOT a documented Keap1/Nrf2 activator. Only P7 is a true Nrf2 peptide. This is stated explicitly in §1–§3, §10, §11 so the brand doesn't repeat the overclaim. 2. Provenance of P7 "clinical." "Clinical studies have confirmed its claims" is Mibelle marketing; the concrete, attributable results (sunburn cells, Langerhans cells, DNA damage) are ex-vivo skin explants + unpublished supplier data. No independent peer-reviewed human RCT for P7 was located — stated as such. 3. AC29 human data caveats. The only in-vivo human study is n=12, supplier-owned, on a 5% complex (not isolated AC29), for stretchmarks (not facial anti-aging). Its "%" figures are supplier-reported. Flagged against transfer to facial wrinkle claims. 4. Penetration honesty. Both peptides are >500 Da; P7's target is intracellular (must reach cytoplasm). Encapsulation is a credible aid but no permeation data confirm delivery — "penetrates" softened to "plausible/assisted but unproven." 5. Corrigendum flag added for the one journal paper.

Items marked unverified / low-confidence (treat with caution before quoting): - Exact AC29 use-level for the isolated peptide (vs. the 5% Vanistryl complex) — confirm on the Lubrizol/supplier datasheet. - Exact corrected content of the 2025 corrigendum [16] (full text was access-blocked) — verify before quoting any specific figure from Liu 2024. - P7 pH/stability optima and any quantitative explant results (sample sizes, % effects) — Mibelle technical literature was not fully accessible; treat all P7 numbers as supplier-side and unverified. - Source [5] (Ph 2026 NRF2 review) and [11] (Vanistryl composition) — confirm exact author/DOI/composition on the primary source before publishing copy.

Claims that must never be stated as fact (built into §8/§10): - ❌ "Activates Nrf2 / switches on DNA-defense genes / protects DNA from UV" (as a consumer claim) → mechanism/structure-function, built on in-vitro/explant supplier data. - ❌ "Clinically proven to firm / reduce wrinkles by X%" → in-vitro / supplier-complex / n=12 / stretchmark numbers. - ❌ Calling AC29 an "Nrf2 antioxidant peptide" → mechanism overclaim. - ❌ Attributing the 2025 Keap1–Nrf2 UVA peptide trial or the AC29 stretchmark study to a facial anti-aging benefit of the named molecule. - ⚠️ "Penetrates to activate the cell's defenses" → soften to "encapsulated to help delivery; penetration plausible but unproven."

Net: No fabricated citations were used. The genuinely strong evidence is target biology (A-grade, independent); the two cosmetic peptides have no independent peer-reviewed human topical RCT — P7 rests on supplier/explant data, AC29 on one in-vitro paper plus a tiny supplier complex study. Honest verdict stands: mechanistically credible (especially P7) and exceptionally safe, but under-evidenced → CONDITIONAL supporting/story actor, P7 the preferred pick, AC29 reclassified to the ECM/soothing lanes, no mechanistic or quantified claims without our own testing.
