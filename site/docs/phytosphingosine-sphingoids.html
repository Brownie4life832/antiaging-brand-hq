Ingredient: Phytosphingosine & sphingoid bases (INCI: Phytosphingosine; Sphingosine; Sphinganine; Salicyloyl Phytosphingosine; Sphingosine-1-Phosphate / "S1P")

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Barrier lipids & skin-identical actives · Slug: phytosphingosine-sphingoids

Scope note: this dossier covers the sphingoid base family — the long-chain amino-alcohol backbones of ceramides, plus their derivatives. These are chemically and functionally distinct from intact ceramides (covered in ceramides.md). Where the brand's barrier story leans on whole ceramides, this family is the precursor + bioactive-signal layer underneath it. The most relevant members for a topical anti-aging brand are phytosphingosine (PS) and its salicyloyl ester (salicyloyl phytosphingosine, SP/SLA); sphingosine/sphinganine matter mostly for antimicrobial defense; S1P is a powerful endogenous signaling lipid but is essentially a biology story, not a topical-cosmetic actives story.

1. Identity

  • Common name(s): Phytosphingosine (PS); sphingoid bases / sphingosine bases; "skin-identical" amino-alcohol lipids.
  • Key forms / derivatives (all real, all distinct):
  • Phytosphingosine (INCI: Phytosphingosine; also sold as Phytosphingosine HCl for water-handling). A C18 4-hydroxysphinganine — the sphingoid backbone of the phyto-ceramide sub-family (Ceramide AP, EOP, NP partly). The dominant cosmetic member. CAS 388-01-2.
  • Sphingosine (4-sphingenine) and Sphinganine (dihydrosphingosine) — the backbones of the "sphingosine" and "dihydro" ceramides; in skin they exist as free sphingoid bases with a recognized antimicrobial role.
  • Salicyloyl Phytosphingosine (INCI: Salicyloyl Phytosphingosine; trade "Phytosphingosine SLC/SLA/SA"; CAS 212908-67-3) — phytosphingosine esterified to salicylic acid. Marketed as a barrier-friendly, lipid-soluble exfoliant / "novel photoaging-repair" agent.
  • Acyl-phytosphingosines / N-acyl derivatives (e.g. Caprooyl Phytosphingosine, N-acetyl/N-acyl phytosphingosine) — phytosphingosine acylated toward true phyto-ceramides; bridge into the ceramide family.
  • Sphingosine-1-phosphate ("S1P") — the phosphorylated, signaling sphingoid; a potent endogenous lipid mediator. Almost never used as a literal cosmetic ingredient (instability, potency, regulatory); appears in the brand context mainly via sphingosine-kinase-activator actives (e.g. K6PC-5) that raise endogenous S1P.
  • Where it comes from in skin: Free sphingoid bases (sphingosine, sphinganine, phytosphingosine) are released by ceramidase hydrolysis of stratum-corneum ceramides; phytosphingosine is also a direct biosynthetic intermediate. They sit at the center of the ceramide ⇄ sphingosine ⇄ S1P ("sphingolipid rheostat").

2. Class & mechanism

Sphingoid bases are not one mechanism but a small metabolic network, and that is the key to reading the evidence honestly:

  1. Ceramide precursor / barrier substrate (phytosphingosine). Phytosphingosine is the biosynthetic backbone of phyto-ceramides, and topically applied PS is plausibly acylated into endogenous phyto-ceramides and integrated into the stratum-corneum lamellae, supporting the lipid "mortar" and lowering transepidermal water loss (TEWL). This is the classic "feed the barrier its building blocks" rationale shared with ceramides, but at the backbone level — by analogy to how exogenous ceramide can serve as a precursor to endogenous ceramide synthesis (8). Note: the precursor citation [8] is a ceramide study, not a PS study; direct human evidence that topical free PS is converted to endogenous phyto-ceramide is lacking — treat this step as well-reasoned mechanism, not proven fact.
  2. Antimicrobial defense (sphingosine, sphinganine, phytosphingosine). Free sphingoid bases are cationic at skin pH and are part of the skin's innate chemical barrier, directly killing Gram-positive bacteria and fungi — including Cutibacterium (Propionibacterium) acnes and Staphylococcus aureus (4). Deficiency of sphingosine is linked to S. aureus over-colonization in atopic skin (5).
  3. Anti-inflammatory signaling (phytosphingosine). PS inhibits protein kinase C (PKC), is a PPAR ligand, and suppresses pro-inflammatory cytokines/chemokines (IL-1α, IL-8, CXCL2, endothelin-1); derivatives suppress NF-κB and JAK/STAT signaling (1, 9).
  4. ECM / "retinoid-like" dermal remodeling (salicyloyl phytosphingosine). SP increases procollagen-I and fibrillin-1 and reduces MMP-1 in fibroblasts and ex-vivo skin, plus delivers a salicylate-driven surface smoothing — a genuinely retinoid-adjacent profile, with one supporting human study (2).
  5. S1P receptor signaling (S1P / sphingosine-kinase activators). S1P acts via G-protein-coupled receptors S1PR1–5 to drive keratinocyte differentiation (not proliferation), barrier homeostasis, antimicrobial-peptide and ceramide production, mast-cell/immune modulation, and (in dermis) fibroblast migration/proliferation and collagen expression (3, 6). This is the "longevity-signaling" layer — but almost all of it is biology, not a finished topical product.

One-line model: Phytosphingosine = barrier substrate + calmer; sphingosine/sphinganine = built-in antimicrobials; salicyloyl-PS = barrier-friendly mild retinoid-mimic; S1P = the master signal you mostly influence indirectly.

4. Evidence review by tier (core of this dossier)

Read this first — the honest summary: The barrier/antimicrobial/anti-inflammatory biology of sphingoid bases is deep and well-established, but most of it is in-vitro and animal, or describes endogenous skin physiology rather than a tested topical product. The single best piece of human topical anti-aging evidence in this whole family is one ~2007 set of studies on salicyloyl-phytosphingosine (Farwick et al.), which includes a small human half-face arm. There are no large, independent, peer-reviewed RCTs proving wrinkle/firmness benefit for plain phytosphingosine as a standalone topical. S1P has essentially no consumer-topical efficacy evidence — it is a research/pharma molecule.

Tier A — In-vitro (cell & biochemical)

  • Antimicrobial activity of sphingoid bases. Bibel DJ, Aly R, Shinefield HR. J Invest Dermatol 1992;98(3):269-73. Sphingosines gave a 4-log kill of S. aureus at 6.25 µg/mL (20 µM) and 2-log at 0.78 µg/mL, with similar activity vs Streptococcus pyogenes, Micrococcus luteus*, Propionibacterium (Cutibacterium) acnes, and Candida albicans; weak vs Pseudomonas; E. coli resistant. Calcium-dependent, optimal ~pH 6.5; confirmed by topical in-vivo microbial challenge. (4) Limitation: model-organism/contrived challenge, not a clinical infection endpoint.*
  • PKC inhibition by sphingoid bases. Sphingosine inhibits PKC, an effect tied to its positive charge (Hannun-era biochemistry; basis for PS's anti-inflammatory claim). Reflected in trade/review summaries reporting ~90% PKC inhibition by phytosphingosine at 0.01–0.2% (7). Limitation: enzyme-/cell-level, supplier-aggregated.
  • Anti-inflammatory cytokine suppression (keratinocytes). PS reduces IL-1α, IL-8, CXCL2, and endothelin-1 in keratinocyte culture and UVB models; trade data cite 0.2% PS inhibiting IL-1α release ~78% vs untreated UVB-exposed skin and SDS-irritation reduction ~40–50% (1, 7).
  • Procollagen-I induction (fibroblasts) — salicyloyl-PS. SP produced a ~2-fold increase in procollagen-I in adult human fibroblasts (P<0.01) in the Farwick study's cell arm (2).
  • Exogenous ceramide as precursor (analogy, not direct PS data). Exogenous ceramide (NDS/NP) serves as a precursor to endogenous ceramide synthesis and modulates keratinocyte differentiation. Shin K-O et al., Cells 2022;11(11):1742 (8). Limitation: this study tested ceramides, not phytosphingosine — it supports the "applied lipid feeds the barrier pathway" idea only by analogy; there is no direct study showing topically applied free PS is acylated into endogenous phyto-ceramides in human skin.
  • S1P / sphingolipid rheostat biology. S1P inhibits keratinocyte proliferation and induces differentiation via intracellular Ca²⁺; ceramide-derived S1P is a messenger between the SC barrier and the basal layer (Masuda-Kuroki & Di Nardo review, and S1P-phosphatase/keratinocyte-differentiation work). (3, 6)

Tier B — Animal / ex-vivo human skin

  • Phytosphingosine derivatives in mouse inflammation models. Kim B-H et al. J Invest Dermatol 2014;134(4):1023-32. Two synthetic PS derivatives (mYG-II-6, fYG-II-6) suppressed TPA-induced skin inflammation and IL-23-induced psoriasiform dermatitis in mice via NF-κB, JAK/STAT and MAPK inhibition; reduced toxicity vs parent PS. (9) Limitation: derivatives (not plain PS), disease models, animal.
  • Salicyloyl-PS ex-vivo human skin (occluded patch). In the Farwick study, an 8-day occluded patch on photoaged volunteers (n=5) with biopsy showed increased fibrillin-1 and procollagen-I and reduced MMP-1 (P<0.05) at SP 0.05% and 0.2% (vs retinoic acid 0.025% comparator). (2) Limitation: tiny n, occlusion, biomarker not clinical wrinkle endpoint.
  • S1P-receptor / sphingosine-kinase modulation in mouse skin. Topical S1P/S1PR modulation alters Langerhans-cell migration and psoriasiform inflammation in mice (mixed results across agents). S1PR2 is central to epidermal barrier homeostasis (mouse). (10, 3)
  • Sphingosine-kinase activator anti-aging (K6PC-5). Youm JK et al. J Dermatol Sci 2008;51(2):89-102 — K6PC-5 (a SphK activator that raises endogenous S1P) showed anti-aging effects in intrinsically aged hairless-mouse skin (↑fibroblasts, collagen, dermal thickness) via intracellular Ca²⁺ signaling. (11) Limitation: a SphK-activator tool molecule, animal/in-vitro model; not phytosphingosine and not a marketed S1P topical.

Tier C — Human ORAL

  • None relevant. Sphingoid bases / S1P are not used as ingestible skin actives; oral sphingolipid (e.g. wheat/konjac glucosylceramide) data belong to the ceramide dossier, not here. No oral phytosphingosine skin RCTs identified. (Mark any future oral claim "[unverified]".)

Tier D — Human TOPICAL

D1 — Controlled human study (the strongest human evidence in this family — but a single supplier-led one):

  • Salicyloyl-phytosphingosine, half-face study. Farwick M, Watson REB, Rawlings AV, Wollenweber U, Lersch P, Bowden JJ, Bastrilles JY, Griffiths CEM. "Salicyloyl-phytosphingosine: a novel agent for the repair of photoaged skin." Int J Cosmet Sci 2007;29(4):319-29. The in-vivo arm was a 4-week half-face (split-face), placebo-controlled study in n=30 moderately photoaged women (ages 41–69): SP cream vs placebo showed significant reductions in wrinkle depth, Rz (smoothness) and Ra (roughness) (P<0.05). Combined with the fibroblast (procollagen-I ↑2×) and ex-vivo (fibrillin-1 ↑, procollagen-I ↑, MMP-1 ↓) arms, this is a genuinely retinoid-adjacent dataset. (2) Limitations: single study (2007), supplier-affiliated authorship (Degussa/Evonik — Wollenweber, Lersch, Bowden, Farwick), modest n=30, instrument profilometry endpoints (abstract does not confirm randomization or blinded clinical photo-grading), not independently replicated. Verified via PubMed 18489360 — title, design, n, endpoints, and concentrations all match the dossier.

D2 — Lower-tier human topical (formulation studies, acne, barrier — mostly supplier/applied):

  • Phytosphingosine in acne (in-vitro + in-vivo). Pavicic T, Wollenweber U, Farwick M, Korting HC. "Anti-microbial and -inflammatory activity and efficacy of phytosphingosine: an in vitro and in vivo study addressing acne vulgaris." Int J Cosmet Sci 2007;29(3):181-90. Concludes PS gives "excellent clinical results in skin care in acne" via anti-inflammatory + antimicrobial action. (1) Limitations: abstract gives no clear n or concentration; supplier-affiliated; cosmetic "skin care" framing, not a drug acne trial.
  • N-acyl-phytosphingosine (marula-derived) barrier cream — human. Liu et al. "Efficacy Evaluation of N-Acyl-Phytosphingosine Prepared from Marula Oil Derived Fatty Acids in Skin Barrier Repair." Clin Cosmet Investig Dermatol 2026 (in press/recent). Double-blind, single-center, pre-post controlled trial, n=32 women (18–60, sensitive skin), 0.05% MO-CER nanoparticles, twice daily ×28 days: TEWL ↓3.72%, hydration ↑10.19%, erythema (a) ↓6.92% by day 28; outperformed conventional C18-ceramide NP. (12) Limitations: pre-post (no placebo arm on the active), small effect sizes, an acyl-PS/ceramide hybrid (not free PS), recent and supplier-adjacent.*
  • Phytosphingosine-based ceramide hydration (supplier). Trade/formulation report: test creams with phytosphingosine-based ceramides raised hydration ~26% vs Ceramide NP alone in ~20 volunteers (7). Limitation: supplier marketing data, small n, not peer-reviewed.
  • Free sphingosine / antimicrobial barrier in humans. Arikawa J et al. J Invest Dermatol 2002;119(2):433-9 — human stratum corneum study: sphingosine is significantly reduced in atopic-dermatitis skin (both lesional and non-lesional), correlating with acid-ceramidase activity (r=0.65) and with S. aureus over-colonization. (5) This is observational human physiology supporting the "low sphingosine → infection-prone" rationale — it argues for replenishment but does not by itself prove a topical product works.

Bottom line on tiers: Proven for topical human use = (a) barrier/hydration support from PS-based formulations (moderate), (b) a single supporting human anti-aging study for salicyloyl-PS specifically, (c) calming/anti-irritant and anti-acne support. Borrowed / mechanism-only = antimicrobial kill data (mostly in-vitro), most anti-inflammatory pathway data (cell/animal), all S1P "longevity signaling," and any deep-wrinkle/collagen claim for plain phytosphingosine.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Phytosphingosine is a relatively small, amphiphilic C18 amino-alcohol — better penetration prospects than intact ceramides or peptides. It localizes in and integrates with stratum-corneum lipids and is expected to act as a precursor acylated to endogenous phyto-ceramides in the stratum granulosum (by analogy to exogenous-ceramide precursor behavior, 8 — which studied ceramides, not PS; not directly demonstrated for topical free PS in human skin).
  • Delivery matters: poorly water-soluble in free-base form; liposomal / lipid-vesicle encapsulation improves penetration to deeper layers and is commonly used (7). The HCl salt aids aqueous handling.
  • Salicyloyl-PS is lipophilic (salicylate ester) — penetrates the lipid SC and pore lining well, which underpins its dual barrier-friendly + exfoliating profile.
  • S1P is highly polar, charged, rapidly metabolized, and a potent signaling lipid — poor and uncontrolled topical delivery and a regulatory liability; this is why the field uses SphK activators or S1PR modulators rather than S1P itself.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration (leave-on): typically 0.05–0.5% phytosphingosine, with 0.2% the most-cited functional level for calming/barrier; safe-use range cited up to ~2%. Salicyloyl-PS studied at 0.05–0.2%. (7, 13, 2)
  • pH: PS is an amine — most stable/active around pH 5.5–7 (optimum ~6.0–6.8); degrades/can irritate outside ~5–8. (Important: PS is cationic at low pH, which is exactly what drives its antimicrobial activity, but it is awkward to pair with strongly acidic actives.)
  • Processing/stability: must be pre-dissolved/solubilized in the hot oil phase (~70–90 °C) or in a solvent (low-MW alcohol, glycols, octyldodecanol) to avoid recrystallization; otherwise gritty/unstable. Robust once properly incorporated in a well-made emulsion. (13)
  • Pairings (synergy):
  • Ceramides + cholesterol + fatty acids — PS completes/feeds the physiological-lipid story; a natural co-star to the ceramides.md hero.
  • Niacinamide — niacinamide upregulates sphingolipid/ceramide synthesis; PS supplies backbone → coherent "build the barrier" pairing.
  • Hyaluronic acid / humectants, panthenol, allantoin — hydration + calming stack.
  • Conflicts / cautions: strongly low-pH systems (high-% direct acids, L-ascorbic acid) fight PS's preferred pH and can compromise stability — formulate in separate steps or buffer. Cationic nature can interact with strongly anionic systems.
  • Sensory: at functional levels, contributes to a smooth, comforting emulsion feel; not a sensory liability. Salicyloyl-PS adds a mild "smoothing" exfoliant character without classic acid sting.

7. Safety

  • Irritation/tolerance: Skin-identical lipid; very well tolerated, low irritation, suitable for sensitive/compromised skin at cosmetic levels. Rare mild irritation in highly sensitive users; PS outside its stable pH window can form irritating byproducts (a formulation issue, not an intrinsic one).
  • Sensitization: True allergy is rare; no notable sensitization signal at cosmetic use levels.
  • Sun sensitivity: None — PS does not increase photosensitivity (contrast with retinoids/AHAs). Salicyloyl-PS, despite a salicylate moiety, is not a classic photosensitizer, but general SPF advice with any exfoliating/anti-aging routine applies.
  • Pregnancy: No reproductive/developmental red flags noted for topical PS as a skin-identical lipid; widely regarded as pregnancy-compatible. Keep copy conservative ("generally considered suitable; consult your clinician"). Note: salicyloyl-PS contains a bound salicylate — although topical exposure is low and it is an ester (not free salicylic acid), pregnancy-cautious consumers may flag "salicyl-"; address proactively. ([CIR / supplier safety data])
  • Regulatory safety status: Phytosphingosine and related sphingolipids are reviewed by the Cosmetic Ingredient Review (CIR) within/alongside the Safety Assessment of Ceramides (IJT 2020) family; the panel's conclusion for assessed sphingolipid ingredients is safe as used in cosmetics. PS is classed as a skin-conditioning/hair-conditioning agent with high frequency of use. (14)
  • Sourcing / sustainability: Modern PS is made by green fermentation of the yeast Wickerhamomyces (Pichia) ciferrii, which secretes tetraacetyl-phytosphingosine (TAPS); engineered strains reach ~20 g/L. This is a vegan, animal-free, biotech route (Evonik is the dominant producer) — a clean, defensible sourcing story vs. legacy animal-derived lipids. (15, 16)

8. Regulatory & claims

Per ../../CLAUDE.md and ../../brand/glossary.md:

  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "supports the skin's natural barrier lipids," "a skin-identical lipid that helps replenish what skin already makes," "helps reduce the look of moisture loss," "comforts and calms the look of stressed skin," "helps maintain a healthy-looking skin surface." For salicyloyl-PS specifically: "helps smooth and refine the look of skin texture; supports the appearance of firmer, more even skin."
  • ⚠️ Risky / drug-like (flag for substantiation/legal):
  • "Antibacterial / kills acne bacteria / antimicrobial" — the kill data are real but largely in-vitro; an antimicrobial/anti-acne efficacy claim drifts toward OTC drug territory (acne = drug claim in the US). Keep it as "helps support skin's natural defenses / clarity," not "treats acne / kills bacteria."
  • "Treats eczema/atopic dermatitis / psoriasis" — disease claims; the supporting science (sphingosine deficiency in AD; PS-derivative psoriasis models) is internal-credibility material only.
  • "Boosts collagen / erases wrinkles" — only salicyloyl-PS has any human anti-aging data, and it is a single modest study; do not generalize a collagen/wrinkle claim to plain phytosphingosine. Avoid "%-boost" language.
  • Anything invoking "S1P," "activates skin-cell signaling," "cellular longevity," or "regenerates skin" — S1P biology is not a proven topical benefit; keep strictly as education/mechanism, never efficacy.
  • Cosmetic vs. drug line: PS sits comfortably as a cosmetic when framed around barrier/hydration/appearance/comfort. It crosses toward drug the moment copy claims to treat acne/eczema/infection or to alter structure/function (collagen) beyond "appearance."

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: Low-to-moderate for phytosphingosine (mature fermentation supply; commodity-ish at cosmetic use levels). Salicyloyl-phytosphingosine is moderate (specialty derivative, fewer suppliers, often the more interesting anti-aging buy). Both are far cheaper per finished-formula impact than peptides, growth factors, or exosomes.
  • Suppliers: Evonik (the originator/dominant fermentation producer of phytosphingosine and Phytosphingosine SLC/SA), plus distributors (Glenn, etc.). Low supply risk.
  • Format: powders (free base or HCl), and pre-solubilized/liposomal trade forms; salicyloyl-PS sold as Phytosphingosine SLC/SA.
  • Sustainability angle: biotech/fermentation, vegan, animal-free — consistent with a premium "clean science" sourcing narrative.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • Overreach to avoid: (1) Treating phytosphingosine as an anti-aging hero — the wrinkle/collagen evidence belongs to the salicyloyl derivative, not plain PS. (2) "Natural antibiotic / antimicrobial skincare" language — the kill data are striking but mostly in-vitro and edge into drug claims. (3) Importing S1P "cell-signaling/longevity" biology into topical efficacy promises — there is essentially no consumer-topical S1P efficacy evidence; it's a pharma/research molecule. (4) "Skin-identical = clinically proven" — skin-identity is a strong rationale, not an outcome.
  • Genuinely defensible: PS is a real, skin-native barrier lipid and ceramide precursor with credible barrier/hydration/calming support; sphingoid bases are a bona fide part of innate skin defense; salicyloyl-PS has a real (if singular, supplier-led) human anti-aging dataset; sourcing is clean and the safety record is excellent. As a supporting actor inside a ceramide-led barrier system, the value is real and the story is honest.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ SUPPORTING (with a conditional hero angle for salicyloyl-PS).

  • Phytosphingosine = a strong supporting barrier/calming lipid that completes the ceramide hero (ceramides.md) and pairs cleanly with niacinamide — credible, cheap, gentle, vegan, excellent claims hygiene. One-liner: "the skin-identical backbone that feeds and calms the barrier."
  • Salicyloyl-phytosphingosine = the only family member with a conditional hero case: a barrier-friendly, retinoid-adjacent texture/photoaging active with one supporting human study — worth shortlisting for a "gentle resurfacing / smoothing" SKU if we treat its single dataset honestly and don't overclaim.
  • Sphingosine/sphinganine & S1P = skip as standalone actives — antimicrobial/signaling biology is fascinating and good for education, but it's drug-adjacent (antimicrobial) or unproven topically (S1P).
  • Longevity-relevance note: the durable longevity tie is barrier integrity and inflammaging (a calm, intact barrier ages better and tolerates the real anti-aging heroes — retinoids/acids/vitamin C). The flashier "S1P / sphingolipid-rheostat / cellular-renewal" framing is narrative, not proven topical efficacy — keep it as mechanism storytelling only.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified: 2026-06-19. Method: each headline claim was checked against the cited primary source (PubMed/PMC abstracts and search verification). The question asked of every claim: is the evidence human + topical, or is it quietly borrowed from in-vitro / animal / endogenous-physiology / oral data?

Overall evidence grade: C+ (family-wide), with one B-island

  • Family-wide grade: C — "weak / indirect for the anti-aging headline." The barrier, antimicrobial, and anti-inflammatory biology of sphingoid bases is genuinely well-established, but for topical anti-aging efficacy the human-topical evidence is thin. Most of the impressive data is in-vitro, animal, or endogenous skin physiology — not a tested topical product producing a clinical outcome.
  • Salicyloyl-phytosphingosine, in isolation: B-minus — there is exactly one controlled human study (Farwick 2007, n=30, split-face, placebo-controlled, 4 weeks) showing reduced wrinkle depth / roughness, backed by matching fibroblast and ex-vivo biomarker arms. That lifts SP above the rest of the family. But it is a single, ~20-year-old, supplier-authored (Degussa/Evonik), unreplicated study with instrument-profilometry endpoints — not enough for an A or a clean B.
  • Plain phytosphingosine as an anti-aging/collagen active: D — essentially unproven topically for wrinkles/collagen. Its defensible human role is barrier/hydration/calming support (grade C–B, mostly supplier or small studies), not anti-aging.
  • Sphingosine / sphinganine antimicrobial benefit: C — striking in-vitro kill data (Bibel 1992) + observational human AD physiology (Arikawa 2002), but no human topical trial proving a cosmetic antimicrobial outcome, and the claim is drug-adjacent.
  • S1P "longevity signaling": D — real, deep biology; zero consumer-topical efficacy evidence. A research/pharma molecule, not a proven topical actives story. The dossier already says this; verification confirms it.

Main caveats (read before using any of this in copy)

  1. The whole family rides on ONE human anti-aging study, and it's supplier-authored. Farwick 2007 (Degussa/Evonik) is real and accurately reported — but it is singular, old, and unreplicated. Treat "salicyloyl-PS works on photoaging" as promising and supplier-led, not established.
  2. Plain phytosphingosine has no standalone human wrinkle/collagen evidence. Its honest claim space is barrier/hydration/comfort. Do not let SP's data halo over to plain PS.
  3. The "topical PS → endogenous phyto-ceramide" conversion is mechanism, not measured fact. The only precursor citation [8] tested ceramides, not PS, and in cell models. Defensible as rationale; not as proof.
  4. Antimicrobial data is real but in-vitro and drug-adjacent. Bibel's kill numbers are striking; there is no human topical trial showing a cosmetic antimicrobial outcome, and "kills acne bacteria" is an OTC-drug claim in the US.
  5. S1P is biology, not a product. No consumer-topical efficacy evidence exists. Keep strictly to education.
  6. Supplier concentration in the evidence base. The strongest human-leaning sources (2, 1, 7, 13, and arguably 12) trace back to ingredient suppliers (Degussa/Evonik, or supplier-adjacent). Independent replication is largely absent across the family.
