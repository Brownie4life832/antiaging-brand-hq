Ingredient: Resveratrol Analogs (Pterostilbene, Oxyresveratrol, Acetyl/Hydroxy-resveratrol, Resveratryl Triacetate, Piceatannol, Rhapontigenin)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Slug: resveratrol-analogs · Class: NAD+ / sirtuin / AMPK / cellular-energy actives (stilbene polyphenols)

Reading note. The brief targets a family of stilbene polyphenols, all structural relatives of resveratrol (3,5,4′-trihydroxystilbene). They are marketed under one umbrella story — "sirtuin-activating, longevity-grade antioxidants" — but they are chemically distinct, have very different potencies, stabilities and (critically) very different amounts of human topical evidence. This dossier keeps a hard line between (a) what the parent molecule resveratrol has shown, (b) what each analog has shown, and (c) which format the evidence comes from (cell → animal → human oral → human topical). The marketing collapses all of this into "clinically proven resveratrol antioxidant." We will not.

0. The honest punchline (read this first)

  • Resveratrol itself is the most-studied stilbene and has some genuine human topical data (a 2025 RCT; a UV-tanning study; swine), but it is unstable (oxidizes, browns, photodegrades), poorly soluble, and mostly stays in the stratum corneum. Its solo topical wrinkle data is weak; its solo human depigmentation data is modest.
  • The "analogs" exist precisely to fix resveratrol's flaws — mostly stability and solubility, not to deliver dramatically more proven efficacy:
  • Resveratryl Triacetate (RTA) is the best-evidenced analog for cosmetics: a stable acetylated pro-drug with four small Korean human topical RCTs (whitening + one anti-aging) and a clean safety/irritation profile. This is the one with a real, defensible cosmetic dossier.
  • Pterostilbene (dimethyl-resveratrol) is the most pharmacologically interesting — far more lipophilic/bioavailable, strong in-vitro senescence/mitochondrial data, plus one 28-day clinical emulsion study at 0.1%. But the best human data is for oral use, and it carries a melanocyte-toxicity nuance (see §7).
  • Piceatannol and Oxyresveratrol are potent tyrosinase inhibitors in vitro (piceatannol especially) but have essentially no stand-alone human topical RCTs — their human story is borrowed (oral piceatannol; in-vitro/3D-skin for both).
  • Rhapontigenin and Acetyl-/Hydroxy-resveratrol are the thinnest — mostly in-vitro / extract-level / chemistry-rationale, no meaningful human topical proof as isolated cosmetic actives.
  • The class label is aspirational. "NAD+/sirtuin/AMPK activators" describes resveratrol's proposed longevity biology (largely from oral/systemic and cell work). No human topical RCT shows a stilbene cosmetic activates sirtuins, raises NAD+, or "switches on longevity genes" in living human skin. That is mechanism and story, not topical efficacy.
  • Net: This family is a legitimate antioxidant / brightening / anti-glycation supporting story with one analog (RTA) carrying real cosmetic RCTs. It is not a proven "topical sirtuin/NAD longevity" hero. Build on RTA (or stabilized resveratrol) for substance; treat the sirtuin/longevity framing as education, not a claim.

INCI caution. "Acetyl resveratrol" and "hydroxy-resveratrol" are marketing terms, not always clean INCI. In practice "acetyl resveratrol" usually = Resveratryl Triacetate (RTA); "hydroxyresveratrol" usually points to oxyresveratrol or piceatannol. Always verify the exact INCI/CAS on any supplied raw material before making a claim — these names are used inconsistently across suppliers.

Why they're grouped: all are stilbenes (two phenyl rings + ethylene bridge), all are antioxidant polyphenols, all share resveratrol's proposed targets (SIRT1, Nrf2, NF-κB, tyrosinase, MMPs). The substitutions are deliberate chemistry: methoxylation (pterostilbene, rhapontigenin) raises lipophilicity/bioavailability; acetylation/esterification (RTA, RTG) raises stability; extra hydroxylation (oxyresveratrol, piceatannol) raises tyrosinase-inhibitory and antioxidant potency.

2. Class & mechanism

Class: Stilbene polyphenols, filed under NAD+/sirtuin/AMPK/cellular-energy actives in the taxonomy. The "cellular-energy/longevity" label derives from resveratrol's reputation as a sirtuin (SIRT1) activator (the original "calorie-restriction mimetic" / red-wine longevity story).

The proposed mechanistic stack (what the family is claimed to do):

  1. Antioxidant / Nrf2 activation. Direct ROS/RNS scavenging + activation of the Nrf2–ARE pathway → upregulates HO-1, NQO1, glutathione-S-transferase, lowering oxidative load and lipid peroxidation (resveratrol review, Ratz-Łyko/Brinke-type reviews [9]; Dermatological-therapy critical review [10]).
  2. Anti-inflammatory / NF-κB suppression. Inhibits NF-κB and downstream cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α, COX-2) — relevant to inflammaging and UV inflammation [10]. Rhapontigenin specifically downregulates COX-2 and 5-LOX in endothelial cells [12].
  3. Sirtuin / "longevity gene" activation. Resveratrol is the archetypal SIRT1 activator; SIRT1 deacetylates targets (FOXO, p53, PGC-1α) linked to stress resistance and mitochondrial biogenesis. This is the core of the longevity narrative — and the weakest part of the human-skin evidence.
  4. Mitochondrial quality / mitophagy. Pterostilbene restores mitochondrial membrane potential, respiration and ATP, and promotes mitophagy (TOM20/LC3 colocalization) in senescent dermal fibroblasts (Zhou et al. 2025 [3]).
  5. MMP suppression / collagen protection. Suppress UVB-induced MMP-1/MMP-3 via MAPK (ERK/JNK/p38) and Akt/mTOR inhibition → less collagen breakdown; some pro-collagen signaling [2][10].
  6. Anti-melanogenesis (tyrosinase). Inhibit tyrosinase catalytic activity and MITF/TYR/TYRP1 gene expression, disrupt tyrosinase ER→Golgi trafficking, reduce melanosome maturation (Boo 2019 [1]).
  7. Anti-glycation. Block sugar–protein adduct formation (Amadori products, AGEs) — shown for resveratrol, oxyresveratrol, piceatannol and triacetyl-resveratrol in 3D skin (Yokozawa-type / Wahedi-type study [11]).

The honest caveat (mirror of the NAD dossier). Almost the entire mechanistic stack above is in-vitro or systemic. SIRT1 "activation" by resveratrol is itself contested even outside skin (the original fluorophore-assay SIRT1 data was confounded; whether resveratrol directly activates SIRT1 or acts via AMPK/PDE inhibition is still debated). There is no human in-vivo skin demonstration that a topical stilbene activates sirtuins, raises NAD+, or activates AMPK in living skin. The mechanism is a plausible scaffold for an education story; it is not topical efficacy.

Where this family is genuinely strongest: antioxidant / environmental-defense, anti-glycation, and pigmentation/tone (especially RTA, piceatannol, oxyresveratrol). Where it is weakest: standalone topical wrinkle/firmness in humans, and the entire NAD/sirtuin/"cellular energy" claim set.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core of this dossier)

Order: in-vitro → animal → human ORAL → human TOPICAL (RCT vs lower). For each I flag whether a benefit is proven for topical human use or borrowed from another format/species.

TIER A — In-vitro (cell / biochemistry)

A1. Tyrosinase / melanogenesis (the most-developed in-vitro area). Boo 2019 review [1] (Yong Chool Boo, Antioxidants 2019;8(9):332): - Resveratrol: human tyrosinase IC50 ≈ 0.39 µg/mL (potent on human enzyme; weak on mushroom enzyme, >100 µM). Inhibits MITF/TYR/TYRP1/DCT, disrupts tyrosinase ER→Golgi trafficking, induces autophagy, activates Nrf2 and SIRT1. - Piceatannol: mushroom tyrosinase IC50 ≈ 1.53 µM — far more potent than resveratrol (~63 µM) and kojic acid (~50 µM). Most potent mushroom-tyrosinase inhibitor in the family. - Oxyresveratrol: more potent than resveratrol on mushroom tyrosinase — Kim et al. 2002 [5] report IC50 ≈ 1.2 µM vs ≈ 54.6 µM for resveratrol (~45× more potent) on mushroom tyrosinase. (The "~150×" figure sometimes quoted comes from a different study, NOT Kim 2002 — do not attribute "150×" to [5]. Boo's review [1] lists a separate 52.7 µM value reported on murine tyrosinase, so cite the enzyme source whenever quoting a number.) Notably, oxyresveratrol is less effective than resveratrol at suppressing cellular melanogenesis — a classic enzyme-vs-cell disconnect. - RTA / RTG: inhibit human tyrosinase more potently than arbutin or glycolic acid; act as pro-drugs converted to resveratrol by esterases; similar anti-melanin effect to resveratrol with lower cytotoxicity and higher stability [1].

A2. Pterostilbene + dermal-fibroblast senescence (flagship "longevity" cell paper). Zhou et al. 2025, Frontiers in Pharmacology 2025; DOI 10.3389/fphar.2025.1732154; PMID 41438513 [3]. - Models: human dermal fibroblasts (HDFs) under (i) UVB acute oxidative stress and (ii) replicative senescence (P35–40 vs young P5–10). Pterostilbene 13 µM and 40 µM. - Findings: ↓SA-β-gal, ↓p16 mRNA, ↓p21 protein (dose-dependent); ↑collagen I/III; restored mitochondrial morphology, membrane potential, basal/maximal respiration, ATP; ↓mitochondrial ROS; promoted mitophagy (↑TOM20/LC3 colocalization, restored LC3-II). - Limitation: in-vitro fibroblasts; single lab; mechanistic, not efficacy. (One mouse arm — see B2.)

A3. Pterostilbene UVA/UVB fibroblast protection. Scoping review of pterostilbene & resveratrol vs photoaging (2025, [journal per S2405580825000986]) [2]: pterostilbene ↓MMP-1/-3, ↑collagen I, ↑lipid synthesis, ↓oxidative stress; broadly more potent than resveratrol per unit due to better cellular uptake. (Author/journal byline not fully captured — see verification notes.)

A4. Anti-glycation in 3D human-skin models. Markiewicz, Jerome, Mammone, Idowu, Clin Cosmet Investig Dermatol 2022;15:[CCID.S364538]; PMC9126233 [11] (author byline now verified — earlier "Wahedi/Kim-type" placeholder corrected). - Methylglyoxal (MGO) induced senescence/AGEs in fibroblasts and 3D reconstructed skin; resveratrol, oxyresveratrol, piceatannol and triacetyl-resveratrol (RTA) each prevented MGO-induced senescence, inhibited AGE formation, and (topically applied to the 3D model) increased fibroblast/collagen density and improved epidermal stratification. - Limitation: in-vitro/3D reconstructed skin, not living humans — but it is a relatively strong, multi-analog model and a good substantiation seed for an "anti-glycation" appearance story.

A5. Pterostilbene tyrosinase-activated cytotoxicity (a safety nuance, not a benefit). Tanaka et al. 2024, Int J Mol Sci [PMC11432345] [6]. Tyrosinase oxidizes pterostilbene to an ortho-quinone that oligomerizes, is pro-oxidant, and binds cellular thiols (cysteine/glutathione). At 3–30 µM it suppressed melanin ~50% (depigmenting); at 100 µM it was tyrosinase-dependently cytotoxic to melanocytes, more so than resveratrol; pterostilbene uptake was 8.9× resveratrol. Implication: a real depigmenting mechanism and a melanocyte-toxicity flag at high local concentrations (see §7).

A6. Resveratrol antioxidant/anti-photoaging mechanism (foundational). Critical review, Clin Cosmet Investig Dermatol 2025 [PMC12433249] [10] and Ratz-Łyko & Arct-type review [9]: Nrf2/HO-1 activation, NF-κB suppression, MMP-1 suppression via MAPK & Akt/mTOR, fibroblast proliferation, estrogen-receptor affinity contributing to collagen signaling.

TIER B — Animal (mostly topical mouse, some oral)

B1. Topical resveratrol & pterostilbene in UVB mice. Across the photoaging scoping review [2]: topical pterostilbene/resveratrol on UVB-exposed mouse skin → restored collagen deposition and dermal thickness, ↓wrinkles/roughness, ↓MMP, ↓p21.

B2. Pterostilbene mouse arm (within [3]). Topical 0.01% pterostilbene cream on UVB-exposed C57BL/6 mice restored collagen and dermal thickness, ↑LC3 (autophagy), ↓p21. Useful because it is a topical route in a whole animal — but still mouse, single study.

B3. Resveratrol Yucatan swine depigmentation. Lin et al. 2002 (in [1]): 1% topical resveratrol twice daily ×8 weeks on dark pig skin → visible lightening, no irritation. (Animal; supports the pigment story.)

B4. Piceatannol / passion-fruit-seed extract (animal/ex-vivo). Passion-fruit seed extract rich in piceatannol: suppressed melanogenesis, enhanced collagen, anti-MMP, wound-healing effects (Maruki-Uchida-type work; Kunsorn et al. 2024 [4b]). (Extract, not isolated piceatannol — attribution caveat.)

TIER C — Human ORAL (real RCTs, but systemic / borrowed format)

Oral is where the strongest human data sits for parts of this family — but it is not topical, and the dose route is different. Classic borrowed-format trap.

C1. Oral piceatannol skin RCT. Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial, Frontiers in Nutrition 2026 [10.3389/fnut.2026.1765478] [4] (Seto et al.): n=82 (41/group) Japanese women 30–59, 10 mg/day passion-fruit-seed piceatannol drink ×8 wk. Oral piceatannol improved stratum-corneum hydration (p=0.001) and reduced crow's-feet wrinkle grade (p=0.009) vs placebo. Conflict: funded by Morinaga & Co., with multiple authors (including the lead) employed by Morinaga, which makes the supplement. (Real human skin endpoints — but ORAL and industry-funded; says nothing about a topical piceatannol cosmetic. Classic borrowed-format + sponsorship caveat.)

C2. Oral trans-resveratrol arm (Rao et al. 2025). In the 4-arm RCT below [8], the oral 75 mg BID arm contributed to the only statistically significant wrinkle improvement (in combination with topical). Oral resveratrol broadly has the systemic antioxidant literature; dermatologic oral endpoints remain modest.

C3. Oral pterostilbene. Best human pterostilbene data is cardiometabolic (BP, lipids, oral safety to ~250 mg/day), not dermatologic. Pterostilbene's headline advantage is pharmacokinetic: ~80% oral bioavailability vs ~20% for resveratrol in animal models (methoxylation reduces phase-II glucuronidation), and far higher cellular uptake [7]. (Bioavailability figures vary widely across studies — 12–95% reported — so treat "80%" as directional, not precise.)

TIER D — Human TOPICAL (the format that actually matters for us)

D1. Resveratryl Triacetate (RTA) — the best cosmetic-format dossier in the family. All from Boo's group / Korean cosmetic science, summarized in Boo 2019 [1]:

Study Conc. n Duration Outcome
Ryu et al. 2015 [1a] — artificial UV-tanning model 0.4% RTA 22 women (Fitz III–IV) 8 wk ITA° ↑17.60% (test) vs 13.81% (control), p<0.05
Ryu et al. 2015 [1a] — natural pigmentation 0.4% RTA 21 women 8 wk Pigmented area ↓2.67% vs 1.46%, p<0.05
Boo 2016 [1b] — higher dose 0.8% RTA 23 women 8 wk ITA° ↑20.38% vs 16.31%; pigmentation ↓35.79% vs 30.93%, p<0.05
Ryu et al. 2018 [1c] — anti-aging 0.8% RTA (8000 ppm) 20 women 8 wk Wrinkle area ↓, elasticity ↑, dermal density ↑, moisture ↑, brightness ↑ (all p<0.05)
Patch tests 0.1–0.5% RTA 33 women — No adverse skin reactions (vs weak irritation from 0.5% resveratrol)
  • Bottom line: RTA has multiple small but real human topical RCTs for whitening, plus one for anti-aging appearance, with a clean irritation profile — a genuinely defensible cosmetic-format evidence base. Caveat: all small (n=20–23), mostly single-group/vehicle-controlled, and from one research lineage with commercial interest (Boo's group; the ingredient is commercialized). Independent replication is thin.

D1b. Resveratryl Triglycolate (RTG). Jo et al. 2018 (in [1]): 0.4% RTG, 22 women, 8 wk, UV-tanning model → melanin index ↓ (205→163 vs 172 control), ITA° ↑28.96% vs 22.06%, p<0.05; efficacy "slightly superior to RTA"; better water solubility; no adverse reactions at 0.4%. (Same lab/limitations.)

D2. Resveratrol topical — the 2025 RCT (most rigorous standalone human topical data). Rao, Briskey, Roche, Tremblay, Da Silva Pinto, Tompkins, Frontiers in Aging 2025;DOI 10.3389/fragi.2025.1727244; PMC12757695 [8]. - Design: double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled, 4 arms (placebo/placebo; oral active/topical placebo; placebo oral/topical active; oral+topical active). 134 enrolled, 122 completed, healthy women ≥40, 1.5% trans-resveratrol cream + 75 mg oral capsule BID ×8 wk. - Topical-ALONE arm (P/A) vs placebo: - Wrinkles: NOT significant. ❗ - Hydration: NOT significant. Elasticity: NOT significant. - Pigmentation: significantly reduced (p<0.001). ✅ - U-zone sebum: significantly increased (p<0.05). - Only the combined oral+topical arm achieved significant wrinkle improvement. - Limitations / conflicts: funded by Evolva AG; three authors employed by Lallemand Bio-Ingredients (which makes the actives, Veri-te™/Juneo™); no collagen/senescence biomarkers measured. - Honest read: the best-designed standalone topical resveratrol trial shows NO significant solo wrinkle/hydration/elasticity benefit — only pigmentation. This is the single most important data point for sober expectations.

D3. Resveratrol topical UV-tanning (Wu et al. 2013, in [1]). 15 volunteers, 1% resveratrol, 4 UV exposures → less darkening. Per Boo's review [1]: control L* fell 63.89→55.91; 1% resveratrol fell 64.20→59.3 (i.e. less darkening on the treated side), plus less erythema. Small, supports photoprotection/pigment story.

D4. Pterostilbene topical — the one clinical emulsion study. "Anti-aging efficacy of a 0.1% pterostilbene skincare emulsion: a 28-day clinical trial," J Dermatol Sci Cosmetic Tech 2025 [14]. Design (verified): double-blind, split-face controlled, n=31 healthy Chinese volunteers aged 32–53; pterostilbene emulsion vs control emulsion. Findings: significant improvement in elasticity/firmness and reduction in forehead/under-eye/crow's-feet wrinkles vs baseline at D14 and D28, with statistically significant gains vs the control half-face. Limitations: short (28 d), small (n=31), single study, and published in a manufacturer-adjacent context with heavily promotional press coverage ("remarkable results in 28 days") — treat effect sizes with caution; confirm exact numbers/funding from the primary before any copy. Promising but not high-tier human proof.

D5. Oxyresveratrol, Piceatannol (topical), Rhapontigenin, Acetyl-/Hydroxy-resveratrol — the gap. - Oxyresveratrol topical: strong in-vitro tyrosinase data; no published standalone human topical RCT identified. Used as a brightening active largely on in-vitro strength. - Piceatannol topical: potent tyrosinase inhibitor in vitro and in oral RCT for skin [4], but no standalone human topical RCT identified; topical human data is borrowed from oral + passion-fruit-extract work. - Rhapontigenin: antioxidant/anti-inflammatory in cells (COX-2/5-LOX ↓ [12]); rhubarb-extract photoprotective/tyrosinase data exists [12b]; no human topical RCT as an isolated cosmetic active. - Acetyl-/Hydroxy-resveratrol: as noted, these terms usually map to RTA / oxyresveratrol / piceatannol; no independent human topical evidence beyond those.

TIER-D verdict: The only family members with real human topical RCT support are RTA (and RTG) — for pigmentation/tone (strong-ish) and one anti-aging study (suggestive). Resveratrol topical alone is proven for pigment, NOT for solo wrinkle/hydration/elasticity. Everything else is in-vitro/animal/oral — borrowed format.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Resveratrol: lipophilic but poorly water-soluble; accumulates mainly in the stratum corneum, with much lower amounts reaching viable epidermis (skin-permeation study, Hung-type / Arch Dermatol Res 2017 [13]). Retains antiradical activity in skin after application, but transdermal delivery is limited by solubility and SC barrier. Encapsulation (nanoliposomes ~2.6× retention; lipid-core nanocapsules) markedly improves penetration and photostability [13b].
  • Pterostilbene: methoxylation → much higher lipophilicity and cellular uptake (~8.9× resveratrol) [6] → the most promising penetration profile of the family; the trade-off is the melanocyte-quinone toxicity at high local levels (§7).
  • RTA / RTG: acetylation/esterification improves stability and (for RTG) water solubility; act as pro-drugs cleaved by skin esterases to release resveratrol in situ — a sensible delivery solution, and the reason their human topical data exists at all.
  • Oxyresveratrol / piceatannol: extra hydroxyls raise polarity (and antioxidant/tyrosinase potency) but can worsen stability (more oxidizable) — penetration not well characterized in human skin.
  • General rule: these are unstable, oxidation-prone actives. Real-world topical efficacy depends heavily on stabilization and delivery system, not just % on the label.

6. Formulation

Resveratrol (parent): - Concentration: cosmetic use typically 0.1–1% (research topical used 1–1.5%). Higher % fights solubility and color. - pH / stability: most stable in acidic–neutral (pH ~5–7), least stable in basic media; degrades with UV, heat, oxygen → browning/discoloration and loss of trans (active) isomer. The headline formulation problem. - Stabilization: opaque/airless packaging, antioxidants (ferulic, vit E), chelators, encapsulation (liposomes, cyclodextrin, lipid nanocapsules), low water activity / anhydrous bases. - Sensory: can impart yellow/tan tint and faint odor at higher %; manage in formula.

Pterostilbene: more lipophilic, better-behaved in oil phases; clinical emulsion used 0.1% [14]; in-vitro effective at low µM. Keep below melanocyte-toxic local concentrations.

RTA / RTG (the formulator's friends): far more oxidation-stable than resveratrol; RTA used at 0.4–0.8% in human studies; RTG more water-soluble; both low-irritation. If we want the resveratrol story with fewer stability/color headaches, RTA is the pragmatic pick.

Oxyresveratrol / piceatannol: potent but oxidation-prone; need robust antioxidant/stability systems; piceatannol notably colored.

Pairings: synergistic antioxidant networks — ferulic acid, vitamin C, vitamin E (classic photoprotection stack); niacinamide (tone, barrier — pairs cleanly); the family also pairs conceptually with the NAD/sirtuin story. Watch-outs: high pH systems (degrade resveratrol), and stacking multiple oxidation-prone polyphenols without adequate stabilization.

7. Safety

  • Resveratrol: generally well tolerated topically; weak irritation reported at 0.5% in patch testing [1]; broadly regarded as low-risk at cosmetic levels. Estrogenic (phytoestrogen) activity: resveratrol binds ERα/ERβ — relevant to its collagen-signaling claim and a reason for caution in pregnancy/breastfeeding and hormone-sensitive conditions (no established topical harm, but the phytoestrogen flag is real and worth a conservative line). No strong photosensitization signal; it is itself photo-degradable (a stability issue, not a phototoxicity one).
  • RTA / RTG: best-tolerated — patch tests at 0.1–0.5% (RTA) and 0.4% (RTG) showed no adverse reactions [1]; lower cytotoxicity than resveratrol in vitro.
  • Pterostilbene: the melanocyte nuance — tyrosinase oxidizes it to a thiol-binding ortho-quinone; cytotoxic to melanin-producing cells at high concentrations (100 µM), harmless below ~3 µM [6]. At cosmetic-relevant low levels this is likely a depigmenting mechanism rather than a hazard, but it argues for conservative concentrations and against pushing pterostilbene high "for more effect." Oral pterostilbene is well tolerated to ~250 mg/day.
  • Oxyresveratrol / piceatannol: limited dedicated topical safety data; as food-derived polyphenols, low intrinsic concern expected, but oxidation products of polyphenols can be sensitizers in principle — patch-test logic applies.
  • Rhapontigenin: thin safety data as an isolated topical; rhubarb-derived; treat conservatively pending data.
  • Sun / daytime use: the family is antioxidant/photoprotective in concept (good in AM under SPF), but resveratrol's photodegradation means opaque packaging + SPF are practical musts.
  • Sourcing / sustainability: resveratrol mostly from Japanese knotweed (high yield, invasive plant — generally sustainable) or grape/fermentation; pterostilbene from blueberry/synthesis; RTA/RTG semi-synthetic. Demand CoA: trans-isomer %, purity, oxidation/color spec, and (per §1) confirm the actual INCI/CAS vs the marketing name.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

The cosmetic/drug line. Cosmetics may improve appearance; they may not claim to treat/prevent disease or alter the body's structure/function without becoming a drug (US FDA; analogous EU rules).

  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "antioxidant support / helps defend against environmental stress," "helps visibly even skin tone and reduce the look of dark spots," "helps improve the appearance of dullness and uneven texture," "formulated with stabilized resveratrol (resveratryl triacetate)," "supports skin's natural resilience."
  • ⚠️ Risky / drug-like / unsubstantiated — flag for legal:
  • "activates sirtuins / longevity genes," "boosts NAD+ / cellular energy / ATP in your skin" — mechanism only; no human topical proof; structure/function overreach.
  • "boosts collagen by X%," "reverses aging / wrinkles" — quantified/structure-function claims; note our best topical RCT showed no solo wrinkle benefit for resveratrol [8].
  • "anti-glycation proven to firm skin" — the anti-glycation data is in-vitro/3D, not clinical.
  • implying oral piceatannol's hydration/wrinkle RCT [4] applies to a topical product — different format; do not transfer.
  • "clinically proven" for oxyresveratrol/piceatannol/rhapontigenin topically — no standalone human topical RCTs.
  • Regulatory status nuance: Resveratryl Triacetate is registered/used as a functional whitening cosmetic ingredient in South Korea (where "whitening" is a government-recognized functional-cosmetic category) — meaningful as substantiation context, but "whitening"/"depigmenting" framing is drug-adjacent in the US; use "even tone / reduce the look of dark spots" for US/EU copy. Confirm the current Korea functional-ingredient listing before citing it as a regulatory fact.
  • Key compliance line: the only human topical efficacy we can lean on is tone/pigment (RTA RCTs + resveratrol pigment data). The wrinkle/firmness and NAD/sirtuin/longevity stories must stay educational, not efficacy claims.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • "Sirtuin-activating, NAD+-boosting longevity antioxidant." — Mechanism/story; no human topical proof. SIRT1 "activation" by resveratrol is debated even systemically; nothing shows topical sirtuin/NAD activation in living human skin. Overreach.
  • "Resveratrol clinically reduces wrinkles." — The best standalone topical RCT (n=122) found NO significant solo wrinkle, hydration, or elasticity benefit — only pigmentation [8]. Wrinkle improvement appeared only when combined with oral. Sober this claim hard.
  • "Pterostilbene = super-resveratrol for skin." — True it's more bioavailable/potent in vitro and has a 28-day emulsion study, but human topical evidence is thin and it carries a melanocyte-quinone toxicity nuance at high levels [6]. Promising ≠ proven.
  • "Powerful tyrosinase inhibitor — fades dark spots." — Piceatannol/oxyresveratrol are potent on the enzyme in vitro, but enzyme potency ≠ cellular/human efficacy (oxyresveratrol underperforms in cells), and they lack standalone human topical RCTs. RTA is the one with actual human tone data.
  • "Anti-glycation, fights AGEs to firm skin." — Real and interesting, but in-vitro/3D human-skin only [11] — an appearance/education story, not clinical firming.
  • What's NOT hype: the family is a genuine antioxidant/Nrf2 stack; RTA has real (small) human topical RCTs for tone and one for anti-aging appearance with a clean safety profile; anti-glycation in 3D skin is a credible substantiation seed; pterostilbene's mitochondrial/senescence cell data is legitimately interesting longevity mechanism.

11. Verdict for the brand

  • Resveratryl Triacetate (RTA): ✅ SUPPORTING (the substance pick). Stable, low-irritation, and the only family member with real human topical RCTs (tone + one anti-aging). If we commit to the resveratrol/stilbene story, anchor it on RTA, claim even-tone/antioxidant appearance, and keep wrinkle/longevity as education.
  • Resveratrol (parent): ⚠️ CONDITIONAL. Iconic name with marketing pull and a real pigment result, but unstable, color/odor-prone, and NOT proven for solo wrinkles/firmness [8]. Use only encapsulated/stabilized, ideally alongside ferulic/C/E, and never as a standalone "proven anti-wrinkle."
  • Pterostilbene: ⚠️ CONDITIONAL (premium garnish / future hero candidate). Best mechanism and bioavailability, decent cell + one clinical study, but thin human topical proof and a melanocyte-toxicity nuance — use low %, watch claims.
  • Piceatannol / Oxyresveratrol: ⚠️ CONDITIONAL (brightening garnish). Potent in vitro, but borrowed human data; fine as part of an antioxidant/tone blend, not as a proven hero.
  • Rhapontigenin / Acetyl-/Hydroxy-resveratrol: ❌ / ⚠️ SKIP for now. Thin evidence and/or ambiguous INCI; revisit only if a substantiated, clearly-specified raw material emerges.

One-line reasoning: Stilbenes are a credible antioxidant + tone + anti-glycation supporting story; RTA carries the only real cosmetic-format proof, so build on RTA (or stabilized resveratrol) and keep the NAD/sirtuin/longevity framing strictly educational.

Longevity-relevance note: This family is the narrative twin of the NAD/niacinamide axis — same "cellular-energy/sirtuin/longevity" story, same trap. The biology (senescence, mitophagy, glycation, oxidative stress) maps beautifully onto the hallmarks-of-aging education we want to tell; the danger is identical — over-claiming topical "longevity activation" that no human topical RCT supports. The defensible move is to let tone/antioxidant appearance carry the claims and let the sirtuin/NAD/anti-glycation science carry the story. Credibility is the brand's core asset; this is exactly where it would be spent unwisely.
