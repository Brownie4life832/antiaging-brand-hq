Ingredient: Placenta Extracts (human & animal — porcine, equine, bovine, ovine/deer, goat)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ⬜ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ☑ rejected (lean) / ⬜ in a product Class: Growth factors, cytokines, exosomes, conditioned media & regenerative Slug: placenta-extracts

One-line bottom line: A century-old "biogenic stimulator" with a genuinely rich, biologically interesting payload (growth factors, peptides, PDRN-type nucleic acids, amino acids) — but for the question that matters to us, does a leave-on placenta cosmetic measurably rejuvenate intact human skin?, the human-topical evidence is essentially one small (n=24), 2-week, single-blind, half-face, manufacturer-run study on an eye cream. Everything stronger is either oral (a different format), in-vitro/animal (a different question), or wound/microneedle (the barrier is breached). Layered on top is a serious endocrine/hormone-contamination flag, a prion/viral sourcing problem for human-derived material, and a regulatory reality where the FDA does not recognize "placental extract" as a cosmetic ingredient, the EU bans human-origin tissue in cosmetics outright, and Japan prohibits human placenta in cosmetics. For a brand whose entire moat is evidence-and-honesty, this is a hard, slightly distasteful pass. Lean SKIP.

1. Identity

Common names / marketing terms: Placenta extract; placental extract; "placenta therapy"; "placentine" (vintage); JBP/Laennec/Melsmon (Japanese injectable lineage); "placental protein," "hydrolyzed placental protein," "placental peptides." In K-beauty/J-beauty often "horse placenta," "sheep placenta," "deer placenta."

What it physically is: The placenta is a transient, highly vascular organ that, during gestation, performs nutrient/gas exchange, endocrine signaling, and immune modulation. Harvested post-partum and processed (aqueous extraction, acid/enzymatic hydrolysis, sometimes fermentation or γ-irradiation sterilization), it yields a complex, undefined biological mixture — not a single molecule. It is conceptually a naturally occurring conditioned-media/secretome analogue: a cocktail of signaling proteins and matrix fragments harvested from a real tissue rather than from cultured cells (cross-reference conditioned-media-secretomes.md and growth-factors.md).

Reported constituents (composition varies enormously by species, tissue fraction, and processing): - Growth factors: EGF, bFGF/FGF, IGF-1, IGF-2, TGF-β1, VEGF, HGF, KGF, PDGF (Hong et al. 2010 [10]; Tansathien et al. 2022 [9]; Sriram et al. 2025 [11]). - Peptides & amino acids: free amino acids (aspartic acid often predominant), oligopeptides; enzymatically hydrolyzed horse-placenta peptides reported <3,000 Da [16]. - Nucleic acids: polydeoxyribonucleotide (PDRN)-type fragments, nucleosides/nucleobases (cross-ref polynucleotides-pdrn.md). - Enzymes (incl. placental alkaline phosphatase), vitamins, minerals/trace elements, lipids, hyaluronic acid precursors. - Steroid hormones as endogenous/contaminant content: the placenta is a major endocrine organ — it synthesizes progesterone and estrogens (estrone, estradiol, estriol). Residual hormones are a documented contamination concern in placenta-containing personal-care products (BCPP [13]; FDA [1]; CIR [2]). This is a feature of the source tissue, not an avoidable impurity — it is central to both the mechanism story and the safety problem.

2. Class & mechanism

Placenta extract is, mechanistically, a paracrine signaling cocktail + nutrient/antioxidant matrix. Its proposed actions overlap almost entirely with the growth-factor / conditioned-media class:

  • Fibroblast stimulation → ECM synthesis. In cultured human dermal fibroblasts, human placental extract upregulated ECM/structure genes — COL1A1 (~1.4×), ELN/elastin (2.9×), HAS2 (2.1×), COL5A3 (1.9×), VCAN (1.3×), ITGA11 (4×), TGFBI (1.2×) (Chang et al. 2022 [7]). This is the cleanest mechanistic signal in the literature — but it is in-vitro, on proliferating (not senescent) cells, and industry-funded.
  • Antioxidant / anti-MMP. Animal data: cow placenta (oral) raised SOD/CAT/GPx and TIMP-1, lowered MDA and MMPs, increased collagen in D-galactose-aged mice (Shen et al. 2022 [12]); porcine placenta (topical) blocked ROS and preserved hyaluronic acid by inhibiting hyaluronidase (Heo et al. 2018 [5]).
  • Anti-inflammatory / immunomodulatory. Porcine and human placenta suppressed Th2/IgE-driven inflammation in contact-dermatitis and atopic-dermatitis mouse/keratinocyte models (Heo et al. 2018 [5]; HPH study [6]).
  • Wound healing / angiogenesis. Human placenta extract accelerated full-thickness wound closure in mice with raised TGF-β (day 6) and VEGF (day 14) (Hong et al. 2010 [10]); placental ECM scaffolds carry endogenous growth factors that drive re-epithelialization (Choi et al. 2013 [17]).
  • Possible anti-pigment. Cited mechanism: modulation of tyrosinase/TRP-1/TRP-2 in melanoma cells; an oral equine-placenta RCT showed the placebo group's melanin index rose while the placenta group's held steady (Nagae et al. 2022 [4]) — i.e. prevention of a seasonal rise, not active lightening.

The aging logic is the same as for growth factors/secretomes: aged skin has fewer/less-active fibroblasts and blunted repair signaling; supplying a "young tissue" signaling cocktail is meant to re-instruct cells. This is coherent in a dish and in a wound. The leap to intact, un-wounded human skin via a leave-on cosmetic is exactly where placenta inherits the same ~500-Da penetration ceiling that throttles the whole protein/growth-factor class (§5).

Honest read: Placenta is an "ECM + antioxidant + anti-inflammatory + repair" story, like a wild-harvested secretome. Its most credible signals (collagen-gene upregulation, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, wound healing) are all in-vitro/animal or oral. For the decision-relevant endpoint — visible anti-aging from a leave-on placenta cream on intact skin — the cupboard is nearly bare.

4. Evidence review by tier — the core of this dossier

Reading guide. Placenta evidence splits four ways, and the marketing systematically blurs them: (1) in-vitro (strong mechanism, no skin barrier), (2) animal (mostly wound/dermatitis/oral, rodent skin far more permeable), (3) human ORAL (a supplement, not a topical — several real RCTs here), and (4) human TOPICAL (the question that matters — almost empty, and what exists is small/single-blind/industry-run or wound-context). Treat any "clinically proven placenta cream" claim as borrowing from tiers 1–3 until proven otherwise.

Tier 1 — In-vitro (cell culture) — mechanistically supportive, industry-tilted

  • Chang P-Y, Chin L-C, Kimura K, Nakahata Y (2022) — "Human placental extract activates a wide array of gene expressions related to skin functions," Scientific Reports 12:11031, doi:10.1038/s41598-022-15270-y (PMC9246867). Normal human dermal fibroblasts (from a 66-yo donor), RNA-seq, 3 biological replicates. HPE upregulated ECM genes: COL1A1 ~1.4×, ELN 2.9×, HAS2 2.1×, COL5A3 1.9×, VCAN 1.3×, ITGA11 4×. Limitation: cells were proliferative, not senescent (authors' own caveat); 3 of 4 authors employed by Melsmon Pharmaceutical (the placenta-drug maker). Mechanism, not topical proof. [7]
  • Tansathien K, et al. (2022) — Pharmaceutics 14(6):1221 (PMID 35745793). Compared deer, goat, porcine placenta; quantified GF content (EGF, FGF, IGF-1, TGF-β1) and showed fibroblast proliferation/migration/regeneration in vitro; goat reported as highest-protein source (~705 mg/g, abstract-level figure). Delivered via dissolving microneedles (HA/PDMA) that bypass the stratum corneum — see Tier 4b. [9]
  • Horse-placental peptides (<3,000 Da) added to 3-D epidermal cultures promoted cell growth (OA Text review [16]) — relevant because enzymatic hydrolysis is the field's main attempt to shrink the molecules toward the penetration ceiling.

Limitation: cultured cells have no stratum corneum; in-vitro potency proves bioactivity-on-arrival, never arrival.

Tier 2 — Animal — supportive for wounds/dermatitis/oral, thin for cosmetic photoaging

  • Heo Y, et al. (2018) — "Topical anti-inflammatory and anti-oxidative effects of porcine placenta extracts on DNCB-induced contact dermatitis," BMC Complement Altern Med 18:331 (PMC6291973, PMID 30541534). BALB/c mice; topical PPE cream (10 mg/g). Reduced lesion area, spleen index −60%, serum IgE −60%, blocked ROS, inhibited hyaluronidase (~30%); anti-oxidative effect reportedly > dexamethasone. No declared COI. But: this is a dermatitis (disease) model in mouse skin, not cosmetic photoaging — and mouse skin is far more permeable than human. [5]
  • Shen X, et al. (2022) — "Antioxidant Capacity and Protective Effect of Cow Placenta Extract on D-Galactose-Induced Skin Aging in Mice," Nutrients 14(21):4659 (PMID 36364921). Oral gavage (2000 mg/kg/day, 8 wk); ↑SOD/CAT/GPx/TIMP-1/TGF-β, ↓MDA/MMP, ↑collagen. Oral, rodent. Authors note CPE was weaker than purified peptides from other species. [12]
  • Hong JW, et al. (2010) — "The effect of human placental extract in a wound healing model," Ann Plast Surg 65(1):96–100 (PMID 20548228). Full-thickness mouse wounds; direct application to wound margins accelerated closure (days 3–9), ↑TGF-β (d6), ↑VEGF (d14). Wound model — barrier already breached. [10]

Limitation: rodent (often depilated/hairless) skin overstates human topical delivery; wound/dermatitis ≠ intact aging skin.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL — real RCTs, but the wrong format

Placenta is widely sold as an ingestible supplement; several legitimate small RCTs exist. None of this substantiates a topical claim — proteins/peptides are digested, the route and pharmacology differ entirely. Included for completeness and to keep us from importing oral data into topical copy.

  • Nagae M, et al. (2020) — "Effect of Porcine Placenta Extract Supplement on Skin Condition in Healthy Adult Women," Nutrients 12(6):1671 (PMID 32512710). Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled; n=19 completers (9 PPE / 10 placebo), 200 mg/day, 4 weeks, ages 40–59. Arm skin: ↓TEWL (p=0.006), ↑elasticity R2 (p=0.017), R7 (p=0.003); cheek: no significant difference. Funded by Kasyu Industries; 3 authors employees. Tiny n. [3]
  • Nagae M, et al. (2022) — "Effects of oral equine placental extract on facial skin," Health Sci Rep 5(2):e537 (PMC8855633, PMID 35224222). RCT, double-blind, placebo-controlled; n=29, 220 mg/day, 4 weeks. Primary outcome melanin index: placebo rose (p=.006), placenta unchanged (p=.329), group×time interaction p=.009; hydration/TEWL n.s. Industry-funded (Dr. PROLABO JAPAN), with at least one author tied to the funder (verify exact role before citing). [4]
  • Jung EY, Hong YH (2023) — "Oral intake of porcine placental extract improves skin hydration and wrinkles," Progress in Nutrition 25(1):e2023012, doi:10.23751/pn.v25i1.12028. Double-blind, placebo-controlled; n=78 (38 PPE / 40 placebo), 200 mg/day, 12 weeks. Skin hydration differed vs placebo (p<0.05); TEWL at wk 4 −1.93 vs −0.52 g/h·m² (p<0.01). Funding/COI not disclosed in abstract. [14]
  • Porcine placental peptides, 12-week RCT — Prog Nutr / J Funct Foods-lineage (ScienceDirect S0965229925001475, 2025): n=90, ages 35–60, twice daily, 12 wk — skin-rejuvenation endpoints. (Abstract-level; verify primary + COI before quoting figures.) [15]
  • Injectable human placenta (Laennec/Melsmon) is approved in Japan as a drug for liver dysfunction / menopausal disorder, with off-label "skin whitening/rejuvenation" use and RCTs in fatigue (PMC3182342) — a prescription biologic, not a cosmetic; do not borrow its halo.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL — the decision-relevant tier (and it is nearly empty)

4a. Plain-topical cosmetic (barrier intact) — essentially ONE study: - Watanabe T, Tahara K, Hirano E (2023) — "Evaluating the Impact of a Cream Containing Horse Placental Extract on Eye Corner Wrinkles in Healthy Women: Single-Blind Comparative Study," JMIR Dermatology 6:e51070 (PMC10687675, PMID 37966883). n=24 Japanese women (37–54), 2 weeks, single-blind, half-face (treated vs untreated side). LNC eye cream (horse placental extract as primary component; other actives unspecified). Replica analysis: significant ↓ in maximum wrinkle width and number of wrinkles (p=.05); wrinkle grade improved on treated side. All three authors are employees of Japan Bio Products Co. The authors themselves state the lack of a placebo control limits interpretation and explicitly call for an RCT. This is the single best plain-topical anti-aging datapoint for the entire ingredient — and it is 2 weeks, n=24, single-blind, no vehicle control, fully industry-run, on an undefined multi-ingredient cream. [8]

4b. Wound / device-delivered topical (barrier breached — NOT a leave-on cosmetic): - Sriram VS, Chittoria RK, Amrutha JS (2025) — placental-extract gel (Placentrex 0.1g/1g, a marketed human-placenta wound product) on a skin-graft donor site, Arch Med Case Rep 7(035). Single 12-yo burn patient, case report. Wound (not cosmetic). [11] - Tansathien 2022 [9] placental dissolving-microneedle patches (15 human volunteers, tolerability) — delivery deliberately bypasses the stratum corneum; evidences a device, not a cream. - Placental-ECM scaffolds for full-thickness wounds (Choi 2013 [17]) — tissue-engineering/wound, not cosmetic.

4c. Systematic / review-level reads: - Tampucci S, et al. (2024) — "Placental Extracts, Proteins, and Hydrolyzed Proteins as Active Ingredients in Cosmetic Preparations for Hair Loss: A Systematic Review," Applied Sciences 14(22):10301, doi:10.3390/app142210301. Closest thing to a systematic review of cosmetic placenta; concludes the clinical evidence base (even for hair, the better-studied claim) is limited and of modest quality. No comparable systematic review exists for skin anti-aging topical — itself telling. [18] - CIR (Nair & Elmore 2002 [2]) functionally reviewed the safety literature and found it wanting (§7–8).

Tier-4 honesty summary

  • Proven for human TOPICAL anti-aging: essentially nothing robust — a single n=24, 2-week, single-blind, vehicle-uncontrolled, manufacturer eye-cream study [8].
  • Borrowed, NOT proven for cosmetic topical: collagen-gene upregulation (in-vitro [7]), antioxidant/anti-MMP (oral/animal [5][12]), wound healing/angiogenesis (wound models [10][11][17]), and all oral-supplement skin benefits ([3][4][14][15]).
  • Confounded throughout: undefined multi-component extracts, no species/processing standardization, near-universal industry funding, and the same protein penetration problem as growth factors.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration — the inherited weakness

  • The ~500-Da rule. Passive penetration of intact stratum corneum falls off sharply above ~500 Da / ~5–7 nm. Placenta's putative actives are large proteins (EGF ~6 kDa; FGF/TGF-β/VEGF 17–45 kDa; HGF ~80 kDa) and HA/matrix fragments far above the ceiling [16]. Whole growth factors from a placenta cream face the same delivery wall as recombinant GFs (cross-ref growth-factors.md §5).
  • The field's workaround — hydrolysis. Enzymatically hydrolyzed placental peptides <3,000 Da [16] are smaller but still mostly above ~500 Da; "<3,000 Da" is a marketing-friendlier number, not a penetrating one. The amino-acid/nucleoside/mineral fraction can penetrate, but those are commodity humectant/antioxidant constituents, not a unique "placenta" effect.
  • The honest delivery routes are the breached-barrier ones. Every clearly positive human placenta result that isn't oral is wound-bed ([10][11][17]) or microneedle ([9]) — i.e. the barrier is gone or bypassed. That validates a procedure/wound product, not a leave-on.
  • Brutal read: placenta is a wild-harvested secretome with the secretome's exact Achilles' heel. Any visible benefit from a cream is plausibly (a) surface/upper-epidermal signaling, (b) the humectant/antioxidant small-molecule fraction, (c) the rest of the formula, or (d) expectation — not demonstrated dermal delivery of intact placental growth factors.

6. Formulation

  • "Concentration": no meaningful standard — potency depends on species, fraction, and processing, not a label %. Vendor "% placenta extract" describes content, not activity. Demand quantified GF/peptide content if pursued.
  • pH: near-physiologic (~5–7.5) to preserve protein/peptide integrity; not an acid-active.
  • Stability — hard. Proteins/peptides and any GF cargo are heat/light/oxidation-sensitive with real lot-to-lot variability; biologic-derived material needs cold-chain, preservation, and ideally a potency (not just presence) assay. Hydrolyzed/lyophilized forms are more stable but less "intact-GF" (the marketing premise).
  • Pairings: behaves like a gentle restorative layer — fine with hyaluronic acid, niacinamide, panthenol, ceramides (barrier-friendly companions consistent with our core).
  • Conflicts: keep away from low-pH actives (L-ascorbic acid, AHAs/BHAs), strong oxidants, and harsh surfactants that denature protein.
  • Sensory: crude extracts can carry a "biological"/protein odor often masked with fragrance (an allergen-loading risk on top of an already fraught ingredient).

7. Safety — this is where placenta is genuinely different (and worse) than recombinant GFs

  • Endocrine / hormone contamination — the headline safety problem. The placenta is an estrogen- and progesterone-producing organ; placental extracts can carry residual estrone/estradiol/progesterone (FDA [1]; CIR [2]; BCPP [13]). This is not theoretical: four girls aged 1–8 developed breast budding/pubic hair 2–24 months after use of estrogen- or placenta-containing hair products, regressing on discontinuation (documented in the hormones-in-personal-care literature [13]). The CIR panel's explicit condition was that such ingredients "should deliver any [no] metabolic/endocrine activity." An anti-aging brand cannot responsibly ship an ingredient whose active premise (hormonal/biologic stimulation) is also its primary hazard.
  • Disease transmission (human-derived). Human placenta/tissue raises prion (Creutzfeldt-Jakob / spongiform encephalopathy) and viral (HIV, hepatitis) transmission risk — the stated basis for the EU ban and CIR's caution [1][2]. Bovine sources carry the BSE/prion concern that gutted Western placenta cosmetics post-1990s [19].
  • Theoretical proliferation concern. Shared with the GF class: chronically supplying mitogenic GFs (and estrogens) raises long-debated questions about stimulating dormant pre-malignant cells. No cosmetic-specific carcinogenicity data exists; CIR flagged the absence of genotox/carcinogenicity testing [2].
  • Irritation/sensitization: generally reported as well tolerated in the small trials; added fragrance is a separate allergen concern.
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: avoid — undefined biologic with hormone content; injectable forms are explicitly contraindicated in pregnancy/lactation.
  • Sourcing/ethics: human and animal placenta carry consent, slaughterhouse-provenance, religious (porcine/bovine), and "ick-factor" considerations; species is often undisclosed on labels.

Net safety read: placenta uniquely couples a delivery problem (large proteins) with an endocrine hazard (hormones that can be small enough to act) and a biosafety/provenance problem (prion/viral) — a worse risk profile than recombinant single GFs or plant-derived alternatives.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • FDA (US) — does not recognize it as a cosmetic ingredient. FDA's position: "Different materials called Human Placental Extracts and Placental Extracts, assumed to contain estrogenic hormones or other biologically active substances, are not recognized as cosmetic ingredients," and FDA recommends the ingredient be identified by a name other than "placental extract" because consumers associate the name with therapeutic/biological activity [1]. A placenta product making structure/function claims (firms, regenerates, boosts collagen) is a drug — FDA literally seized placenta "rebirth/anti-wrinkle" cosmetics in 1958 under the FD&C Act for false/misleading claims [20]. Under MoCRA (2022), cosmetic oversight is tightening, not loosening.
  • EU — banned. Cells, tissues or products of human origin are prohibited in cosmetics (Annex II, Reg. (EC) 1223/2009, entry ~416) over CJD/spongiform/viral risk. Human placenta is out entirely; animal-derived placental proteins are technically permissible but carry TSE/BSE documentation burdens and reputational risk [19].
  • Japan — bans human placenta in cosmetics. Japan's Standards for Biologically Derived Materials prohibit human-placenta extract in cosmetics (it survives only as the drug Laennec/Melsmon). Animal placenta is used cosmetically.
  • CIR (US industry panel): "the available data are insufficient to support safety of these ingredients in cosmetics," with required-but-missing sensitization/toxicity/genotox/carcinogenicity data and the condition of no endocrine activity and freedom from pathogens (Nair & Elmore 2002 [2]).
  • Claims we could/can't make (if we ever used an animal-derived, hormone-stripped form, which we won't recommend):
  • ✅ Cosmetic-safe: "helps improve the look of skin," "visibly smooths," "supports a hydrated, comfortable feel."
  • ⚠️ Drug-territory / off-limits: "regenerates," "repairs/heals," "boosts collagen," "rejuvenates at the cellular level," "anti-aging treatment," any "wound/scar" framing, any hormonal/biological-activity implication.
  • See ../../brand/glossary.md claim cheat-sheet.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: moderate→high, but the real cost is non-monetary: provenance documentation, viral/prion testing, hormone screening, cold-chain, potency assays, and brand/reputational risk.
  • Supplier landscape: mostly Asian (Korea/Japan/China) animal-placenta suppliers (porcine, equine, deer, goat, ovine) and the Japanese human-placenta pharma lineage (Melsmon/JBP). Quality, species disclosure, and hormone-stripping vary widely and are often opaque.
  • Diligence if ever pursued: species + slaughterhouse provenance; residual-hormone assay (estrogens/progesterone below detection); TSE/BSE & viral-inactivation certificates; endotoxin/sterility; quantified bioactive content; finished-formula stability. The bar is high and the upside is low.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • "Ancient/proven regenerative secret" (Filatov 1933, glamorous mid-century brands). History is real; the supporting science was "seriously flawed, some being mere conjecture," and FDA seized the flagship products [20]. Heritage ≠ evidence.
  • "Rich in growth factors → regenerates skin." Composition is real; topical delivery and clinical regeneration of intact skin are not — same ~500-Da wall as the GF class.
  • "Clinically proven." For topical anti-aging this rests on one n=24, 2-week, single-blind, vehicle-uncontrolled, manufacturer eye-cream study [8]; stronger data are oral, in-vitro, animal, or wound/microneedle.
  • "Natural/biomimetic." It is also hormonally active and biohazard-screened — the "natural" framing hides the exact reasons regulators restrict it.
  • Injectable-halo borrowing. Laennec/Melsmon are drugs in Japan; their (limited) clinical data cannot substantiate a Western cosmetic.
  • Where any reality lives: the small-molecule fraction (amino acids, minerals, antioxidants, hydrolyzed peptides) can give a mild humectant/antioxidant cosmetic effect — i.e. placenta can act like a generic conditioning extract, just with far more baggage than alternatives that do the same job cleanly.

11. Verdict for the brand

❌ / ⚠️ — Lean SKIP.

One-line reasoning: Placenta extract is a biologically interesting wild-harvested secretome whose best evidence is in-vitro/animal/oral, whose human-topical anti-aging case is a single tiny manufacturer study, and whose distinctive properties — hormone content, prion/viral sourcing risk, and "is-it-even-recognized-as-a-cosmetic-ingredient" regulatory status across the US/EU/Japan — make it actively dangerous to a brand selling evidence and honesty. It offers nothing our cleaner actives don't, at far higher reputational and compliance cost.

Why not even "supporting": every plausible benefit (ECM signaling, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, hydration) is delivered better, more cleanly, and with stronger independent evidence by ingredients already in our research base — peptides, copper peptides (GHK-Cu), niacinamide, the barrier-lipid triad/ceramides, PDRN/polynucleotides, beta-glucan, panthenol, and (for the "growth-factor renewal" narrative, if ever wanted) a vetted, hormone-free recombinant or conditioned-media option. Placenta is a strictly dominated choice.

Longevity-relevance note: Conceptually on-thesis (a "young-tissue signaling cocktail" is a tidy longevity story, and the ECM-gene/antioxidant mechanisms map to real hallmarks). But the longevity framing would require structure/function claims that are drug territory, and the ingredient's hormonal mechanism is precisely the part that's unsafe. The narrative is seductive; the substance and its regulatory/safety baggage are not worth it.

If ever revisited (high bar): only an animal-derived, fully hormone-stripped (assayed below detection), TSE/BSE- and virus-certified, hydrolyzed fraction, positioned strictly as a supporting conditioning extract with cosmetic-appearance claims only — never human-derived, never a hero, never a regenerative/wound/hormonal claim, and never in markets (EU) where it's banned.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19 by independent re-check of PubMed abstracts, PMC full texts where reachable, FDA/CIR records, and EU regulatory references. Goal: catch (a) oral/in-vitro/animal/wound data quietly laundered into a leave-on cosmetic claim, (b) the hormone/biosafety/regulatory issues being underplayed, and (c) any citation that doesn't exist or is mischaracterized.

Overall evidence grade for the decision-relevant question (leave-on topical anti-aging on intact human skin): D

There is a real but thin and indirect signal, which keeps this out of "pure hype/F," but the human-topical anti-aging base is one n=24, 2-week, single-blind, vehicle-uncontrolled, fully manufacturer-run eye-cream study [8] whose own authors say it can't support conclusions and call for an RCT. The mechanistically strong tiers (in-vitro ECM-gene upregulation [7]; antioxidant/anti-inflammatory/wound [5][10][12]) answer the wrong question (a dish, a mouse, an oral supplement, or a wound — not a cream on intact skin). Unlike the growth-factor dossier (graded C, on the strength of multiple vehicle-controlled topical RCTs from the TNS lineage), placenta has no comparable controlled plain-topical evidence, so it grades a tier lower: D.

What verified cleanly (citations real and accurately characterized)

  • [7] Chang 2022 — real (Sci Rep 12:11031); NHDF, RNA-seq, COL1A1/ELN/HAS2 etc. upregulated; Melsmon-employed authors; non-senescent cells (their caveat). In-vitro only. Accurately reported.
  • [8] Watanabe 2023 — real (JMIR Dermatol, PMID 37966883); n=24, 2 wk, single-blind half-face, no vehicle control, horse placental eye cream, Japan Bio Products employees, authors call for RCT. This is correctly framed as the lone (and weak) plain-topical datapoint.
  • [3] Nagae 2020 / [4] Nagae 2022 / [14] Jung & Hong 2023 — real oral RCTs (n=19 / n=29 / n=78); small, short, industry-funded; correctly fenced as oral, not topical.
  • [5] Heo 2018 (porcine, topical, DNCB mouse) and [10] Hong 2010 (HPE, mouse wound) and [12] Shen 2022 (cow, oral, D-gal mouse) — all real; correctly placed in animal/wound/oral tiers with permeability caveats.
  • [9] Tansathien 2022 — real (Pharmaceutics 14(6):1221); microneedle delivery (barrier bypassed) — correctly excluded from "leave-on" support.
  • [2] CIR (Nair & Elmore 2002) — real (PMID 12042062); "insufficient data to support safety," no endocrine activity, pathogen-free conditions. Accurately quoted.
  • [1] FDA position — confirmed: FDA states placental/human placental extracts (assumed estrogenic/biologically active) are not recognized as cosmetic ingredients and recommends a different name. Accurately represented.
  • EU/Japan bans — confirmed in principle: human-origin tissue prohibited in EU cosmetics (Annex II); Japan prohibits human placenta in cosmetics. (Exact Annex II entry number cited as "~416" — directionally correct; verify the precise current entry number before any legal/public use.)
  • Hormone/premature-puberty signal [13] — confirmed in the hormones-in-personal-care literature; correctly flagged as the central safety concern.
  • Penetration premise — confirmed: placental GFs are large proteins; "<3,000 Da" hydrolyzed peptides are still mostly above the ~500-Da ceiling. The inherited-Achilles'-heel framing is sound.

Main caveats / where to stay honest

  1. The topical anti-aging case is essentially a single weak study. Do not let [8] read as "clinically proven." Any topical efficacy copy would need our own vehicle-controlled testing.
  2. Oral ≠ topical. [3][4][14][15] are legitimately positive but are supplements; they must never substantiate a cream.
  3. Industry funding is pervasive ([3][4][7][8] all manufacturer-run/-employed). No independent plain-topical replication exists.
  4. Hormone content is the decisive differentiator vs. recombinant GFs — it is simultaneously the mechanism and the hazard, and it underlies the regulatory non-recognition. This is appropriately weighted as a skip-driver.
  5. [15], [16], [21] are abstract-/secondary-level — do not quote specific figures without retrieving primaries; [15] flagged [partially unverified]. The exact EU Annex II entry number should be re-verified against the consolidated regulation before legal use.
  6. No fabricated sources detected. Every study cited is real and says what is attributed to it; the species/route/format of each is labeled to prevent tier-laundering.

Net

The dossier's skepticism and SKIP conclusion are well-supported. Placenta is mechanistically the most "interesting" member of the regenerative class and the worst fit for this brand: weakest plain-topical evidence, plus a unique stack of endocrine, biosafety, and regulatory liabilities that cleaner alternatives entirely avoid. Confidence in the D grade and Lean SKIP verdict: high.
