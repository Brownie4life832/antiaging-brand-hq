Ingredient: Benzophenone UV Filters — Oxybenzone (BP-3), Sulisobenzone (BP-4), BP-5, Dioxybenzone (BP-8) (INCI: Benzophenone-3, Benzophenone-4, Benzophenone-5, Benzophenone-8)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ⬜ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ☑ rejected (as a hero/added active) / ⬜ in a product Class: Sunscreen / photoprotection filters & adjuncts (organic/"chemical" UV filters; aromatic-ketone family)

One-line honest summary: Benzophenones are real, working UV filters — and photoprotection is the single best evidence-backed anti-aging intervention there is — but this particular sub-family is the legacy, problem-child end of the UV-filter shelf: oxybenzone (BP-3) is the most documented sunscreen photoallergen in North America, is systemically absorbed at peak plasma levels ~100s× the FDA's 0.5 ng/mL toxicology-trigger threshold (geometric-mean C_max ~258 ng/mL for one lotion in a maximal-use RCT — note: peak under deliberately exaggerated whole-body, 4×/day dosing, not typical use), shows a deep bench of in-vitro/animal endocrine-disruption signals (with thinner, mixed, and confounded human epidemiology), is environmentally restricted/banned in several jurisdictions (Hawaii, Key West, Palau, etc.), and is not GRASE-affirmed by the FDA (FDA flagged it for insufficient data, not an affirmative finding of harm). The class is anti-positioning for a premium, science-forward skin-longevity brand. Skip BP-3/BP-8 entirely; treat the sulfonated water-soluble ones (BP-4/BP-5) as a possible minor formulation-stability adjunct only — not marketed actives — and even then prefer alternatives.

1. Identity

This dossier covers four members of the benzophenone (diphenyl ketone / aromatic-ketone) UV-filter family. They are chemically related but differ sharply in lipophilicity, regulatory status, and absorption.

  • Forms/notes:
  • BP-3 / oxybenzone is the one people mean by "benzophenone sunscreen." Oil-soluble, used in countless legacy chemical sunscreens and as a UV stabilizer in non-sunscreen products (lip balm, hairspray, plastics, packaging).
  • BP-4 / BP-5 are the sulfonated, water-soluble pair. They partition into the water phase, are used at low levels often more for photo-protecting the formula (preventing color/fragrance fade) than for SPF, and absorb/penetrate skin far less than BP-3.
  • BP-8 / dioxybenzone is a minor US-only filter, almost never used alone.
  • Critical naming distinction — do not conflate: "Benzophenone" (the unsubstituted parent ketone, C₁₃H₁₀O, CAS 119-61-9) is a separate compound that is an IARC Group 2B "possibly carcinogenic" agent and a California Prop 65 listed carcinogen. It is not the same as benzophenone-3, but it is (a) a manufacturing-related impurity/degradant relevant to the family and (b) a degradation product of octocrylene (a sibling chemical filter), which is why "benzophenone in sunscreen" headlines exist. [13][14][15] Copy and COA review must keep these distinct.

2. Class & mechanism (how it works)

Benzophenones are organic ("chemical") UV filters. Mechanism:

  1. Photon absorption by the conjugated aromatic-ketone chromophore. The molecule absorbs high-energy UV photons, promoting electrons to an excited state.
  2. Energy dissipation. The excited molecule returns to ground state by releasing the absorbed energy mostly as heat (and some longer-wavelength light) via internal conversion / keto-enol and excited-state intramolecular proton-transfer pathways — converting damaging UV into harmless thermal energy before it reaches DNA, collagen, and other chromophores in skin.
  3. Spectral coverage: Benzophenones absorb across UVB and into short UVA (~280–350 nm). BP-3's peak is in UVB with a tail into UVA-II; it is a weak, broad, but not strong-UVA-I filter — it does not cover the long-UVA (UVA-I, ~350–400 nm) that drives much photoaging. In practice BP-3 was historically added partly to photo-stabilize avobenzone and broaden UVB coverage, not as a standalone UVA-I shield. [10][12]

The anti-aging logic is entirely indirect: UV filters do not "do" anything to skin biology — they prevent the UV insult upstream. So a benzophenone's anti-aging value = (its photoprotective efficacy) − (its own liabilities: ROS generation on photodegradation, penetration, sensitization, endocrine signal). On that ledger, modern filters win decisively.

Important downside in the mechanism itself: organic filters, including BP-3, generate reactive oxygen species (ROS) as they photodegrade. If the filter penetrates into viable skin (which BP-3 demonstrably does), the photoprotection it provides at the surface is partially offset by pro-oxidant ROS generated within the tissue — the opposite of what an antioxidant/anti-photoaging actives strategy wants. [4][16]

3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

UV filters address aging by blocking the upstream driver (UV), so they touch many hallmarks indirectly and only at the skin surface. They are not actives that repair or signal.

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

The honesty frame: There are two very different evidence questions tangled together in benzophenone marketing. (1) Does sunscreen prevent photoaging? — Yes, strongly, but that's a property of broad-spectrum SPF as a category, proven mostly with formulas that are not BP-3-defined. (2) Is BP-3 itself a good, safe choice of filter? — Here the weight of evidence (absorption, endocrine signals, sensitization, environment, non-GRASE status) is unfavorable, and it is the most-studied member by far. BP-4/BP-5 are under-studied; BP-8 is barely studied. Most "benzophenone is fine" or "benzophenone is dangerous" claims overreach the data in opposite directions; the calibrated read is "it works as a filter but is the wrong filter to build a premium brand on."

Tier 1 — In vitro (cell & biochemical)

  • Estrogenicity / endocrine receptor assays. Across yeast estrogen-screen (YES) assays, human breast-cancer cell proliferation (E-screen), and reporter assays, BP-3 and its metabolites (notably 2,4-dihydroxybenzophenone / BP-1) act as estrogen-receptor agonists and anti-androgens, and interfere with steroidogenic enzymes and thyroid signaling. This is the most reproducible part of the endocrine story. [2][3] Limitation: in-vitro potency is far weaker than estradiol; cell-assay activity ≠ clinically meaningful human effect at topical exposures, but it is a consistent hazard signal.
  • Sperm-function assay. BP-3 induced a Ca²⁺ signal in human sperm at ~10 µM, competitively interfering with progesterone signaling (CatSper channel) — an in-vitro mechanistic flag for reproductive effects. [3] Limitation: isolated-cell assay; high concentration.
  • ROS / photodegradation chemistry. BP-3, though more photostable than avobenzone, undergoes oxidative photodegradation generating reactive oxygen species (hydroxyl-radical pathway dominant); relevant because BP-3 penetrates into living skin where this ROS can form. [4][16] Limitation: mostly solution/aerosol and environmental-water chemistry; intradermal ROS quantitation in humans not established.

Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

  • Rodent endocrine/reproductive effects (Krause/various; reviewed in [2][3]). Oral and dermal BP-3 in rodents produced prolonged estrous cycles, altered uterine estrogen-receptor gene expression, endometrial hyperplasia, and altered mammary-gland development — at exposures that overlap human peak plasma levels in some designs. [2] Limitation: species extrapolation; oral dosing in several studies; effect sizes and dose-relevance debated.
  • Perinatal mammary-gland morphology (Matouskova/LaPlante et al., 2019, Reprod. Toxicol.). Low-dose perinatal oxybenzone altered mammary-gland morphology in male and female mice. [2] Limitation: developmental window, mouse, low n; mechanistic rather than disease-outcome.
  • Coral / aquatic models (Downs et al., 2016). See Tier "environment" below — animal-model toxicity in coral planulae and cells. [8]

Tier 3 — Human ORAL / systemic & biomonitoring (most human data are exposure, not outcome)

There is no meaningful "oral BP-3 supplement" literature (nobody eats it on purpose). The human evidence here is biomonitoring (how much is in people) and epidemiology (associations with health outcomes) — both of which are systemic-exposure questions raised precisely because BP-3 is absorbed through skin.

  • Population biomonitoring — Calafat et al., 2008, Environ. Health Perspect. (CDC/NHANES 2003–2004). BP-3 detected in the urine of ~97% of the US population sampled (n≈2,500, age ≥6); women/girls had higher levels. [17] This is the foundational "everyone is exposed" datapoint.
  • Children — Wolff et al., 2007. ~96% of 6–8-year-old girls had detectable urinary BP-3. [17] Limitation: biomonitoring = exposure, not harm.
  • Maximal-use systemic absorption RCT — Matta et al., 2019, JAMA (FDA study, NCT03582215). Phase-I RCT, healthy adults, 4 applications/day × 4 days under maximal-use conditions. All four organic filters tested (incl. oxybenzone) exceeded the FDA 0.5 ng/mL plasma threshold on Day 1. [1] This threshold is the level above which FDA says nonclinical toxicology (carcinogenicity, dev/repro) is warranted — i.e., absorption is high enough to require safety data BP-3 doesn't yet have.
  • Follow-up maximal-use RCT — Matta et al., 2020, JAMA (n=48). Oxybenzone reached the highest plasma levels of any filter tested — geometric-mean C_max up to ~258 ng/mL (lotion) and ~180 ng/mL (spray) — i.e., ~hundreds of times the 0.5 ng/mL trigger; detectable at Day 7; long terminal kinetics consistent with accumulation on repeated use. [11] Limitation: pharmacokinetics, not toxicity — high plasma level ≠ proven harm, but it removes the old "it stays on the surface" defense and is why FDA wants data.
  • Human epidemiology (associations; reviewed by Kim/Lee et al. and the 2025 Current Environmental Health Reports review [2]):
  • ↓ testosterone ~12% in adolescent boys (Scinicariello & Buser, 2016) and adult men (Tao et al., 2022). [2]
  • Thyroid: ~15% lower T3 in pregnant women (Aker et al., 2018); altered T4/TSH in several cohorts. [2]
  • Pubertal timing: delayed breast development in girls (Harley et al., 2019); earlier menarche in others — direction inconsistent across studies. [2]
  • Pregnancy/birth: associations with reduced neonatal birth weight/chest circumference; BP-3 in 92% of maternal urine and 18% of cord blood (Beck et al., 2024). [2]
  • Endometriosis: adjusted OR ≈ 4.98 in one case-control (Peinado et al., 2023). [2]
  • Limitations (critical): these are observational, cross-sectional, confounded (BP-3 exposure correlates with overall personal-care-product use, SES, sun behavior), single-spot urinary measures, inconsistent directions, and almost none establish causation. The honest read: a plausible, partially-corroborated endocrine hazard signal that is not a proven human disease cause at typical exposures — but quite enough to make it the wrong molecule for a brand selling trust.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL efficacy & safety (the tier that matters for a skincare brand)

Efficacy as a sunscreen (positive but category-borrowed): - BP-3 has decades of regulatory use as an OTC sunscreen active (US up to 6%) and contributes measurable SPF/UVB protection in finished sunscreens; it was historically used to photostabilize avobenzone and extend UVB coverage. [10][12] But: there is no body of head-to-head human RCTs showing BP-3-containing sunscreens prevent photoaging better than (or as well as) modern-filter sunscreens. The famous photoaging-prevention RCT evidence (e.g., Hughes et al., 2013, Ann Intern Med* — daily broad-spectrum sunscreen reduced photoaging over 4.5 y) validates the behavior of using broad-spectrum SPF, not BP-3 the molecule. No UVA-I coverage** is a real efficacy gap for an anti-aging claim.

Safety in human topical use (this is where BP-3 is genuinely bad): - Photoallergic & allergic contact dermatitis — multiple photopatch series: - In suspected-photodermatosis populations, BP-3 is repeatedly the leading sunscreen photoallergen. A 10-year photopatch series found 35 cases of photocontact allergy to oxybenzone among 283 tested (≈12%); other series report ~13–15% of sunscreen-allergy patients reacting to oxybenzone. [5][6][7] - Oxybenzone is the most common sunscreen photoallergen in the US and Canada and 4th-most in Europe. [5][6] - General-population patch-positivity to sunscreen chemicals is low (~0.8%), so the population allergy burden is modest — but among reactors, BP-3 dominates, and reactions range from redness/itch/hives to (rarely) anaphylaxis. [5][7] - Net for topical: BP-3 works as a filter but carries the highest sensitization signal of common organic filters, plus the absorption/endocrine cloud above. BP-4/BP-5 (water-soluble, poorly absorbed) have a lower sensitization and absorption profile but also less standalone SPF value; BP-8 is too little-used to characterize.

Bottom line by tier: Proven → BP-3 is systemically absorbed at high plasma levels (JAMA RCTs) and is a leading topical photoallergen (photopatch series); ~everyone is exposed (NHANES). Suggestive-but-unsettled → endocrine/reproductive harm in humans (reproducible in-vitro/animal, mixed human epidemiology). Not demonstrated → that BP-3 specifically (vs modern filters) delivers superior anti-photoaging benefit; and not affirmatively cleared → FDA non-GRASE (insufficient safety data).

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • BP-3 (oxybenzone): high — and that's the problem. Lipophilic, small (MW 228), it readily crosses the stratum corneum. The FDA/Matta RCTs put plasma C_max in the tens-to-hundreds of ng/mL with detectable levels at Day 7 and kinetics consistent with accumulation on daily use; urinary BP-3 in ~97% of the population confirms routine systemic uptake. [1][11][17] This is the basis of every absorption-driven safety concern.
  • BP-4 / BP-5 (sulisobenzone / its sodium salt): low. The sulfonic-acid/sulfonate group makes them water-soluble and highly polar/charged, so they partition to the aqueous phase and penetrate the lipophilic stratum corneum poorly — markedly less systemic absorption than BP-3. This is the one mildly redeeming pharmacokinetic feature of the family (relevant if used purely as a water-phase formula stabilizer).
  • BP-8 (dioxybenzone): lipophilic; penetration presumed similar-class to BP-3 but sparse data; rarely used.
  • Practical read: if a benzophenone enters a formula at all, the water-soluble BP-4/BP-5 are the only ones whose penetration profile is reassuring — and even they are second-choice to non-benzophenone alternatives.

6. Formulation

NB: In the US, UV filters are OTC drug actives governed by the sunscreen monograph — using BP-3 as a filter makes the product a drug (see §8). The notes below are for completeness; the brand's likely path is not to formulate with benzophenones.

  • Concentration (regulatory caps, not a "use this much" recommendation):
  • BP-3 (oxybenzone): US OTC up to 6%. EU (Reg. 2022/1176): face/hand/lip products max 6%, body products max 2.2%, other products 0.5% — explicitly because the SCCS found 6% not safe in body products (sprays/body cream) though safe at 6% in face cream (margin of safety ~447). Up to 10% historically in Australia. [10][12][18]
  • BP-4 / BP-5 (sulisobenzone / sodium salt): EU up to 5% as UV filter; US/Australia up to 10%. Often used <1% purely to protect formula color/fragrance from UV.
  • BP-8 (dioxybenzone): US up to 3%; not permitted in EU cosmetics.
  • pH: BP-3 is oil-phase, pH-flexible. BP-4 is an acid (use the BP-5 sodium salt or neutralize); water-phase, compatible with mildly acidic-to-neutral formulas.
  • Stability: BP-3 is moderately photostable and was historically used to stabilize avobenzone and broaden UVB; however it still photodegrades and generates ROS, so "photostable" is relative. [4][16] BP-4/BP-5 are mainly formula photostabilizers (anti-fade). Octocrylene caveat: the sibling filter octocrylene degrades to free benzophenone (the Prop-65/IARC-2B parent) over shelf life — a class-adjacent stability red flag to know even if we use neither. [13][14][15]
  • Pairings: historically BP-3 + avobenzone (+ octocrylene) in legacy broad-spectrum chemical sunscreens. For an anti-aging brand the relevant "pairing" decision is don't — pair photoprotection with modern filters or mineral filters instead (see the next-gen-uv-filters and titanium-dioxide dossiers).
  • Sensory: BP-3 can contribute slight color/odor and is associated with the "chemical sunscreen" feel; water-soluble BP-4/BP-5 are sensory-neutral at low levels.

7. Safety

  • Sensitization / photoallergy: BP-3 is the leading sunscreen photoallergen in North America and a frequent allergic-contact-dermatitis culprit; photopatch series repeatedly implicate it (≈12–15% of sunscreen-allergy/photodermatosis patients). [5][6][7] BP-4/BP-5 sensitize far less; BP-8 little data. This alone is a strong reason a sensitive-skin-adjacent premium brand avoids BP-3.
  • Systemic absorption: BP-3 absorbs to plasma levels ~100s× the FDA 0.5 ng/mL toxicology-trigger, accumulates on daily use, and is found in ~97% of people. [1][11][17] Absorption ≠ proven harm, but it converts every in-vitro/animal hazard into a plausible human concern and is why BP-3 is non-GRASE.
  • Endocrine / reproductive: reproducible estrogenic + anti-androgenic + thyroid signals in vitro and in rodents; mixed but non-trivial human epidemiology (↓testosterone, thyroid shifts, pubertal-timing, birth-outcome, endometriosis associations). Causation in humans at typical exposures is not established, but the WHO-definition endocrine-disruptor case is strong enough that regulators acted. [2][3]
  • Pregnancy / children: Given placental transfer (BP-3 in 18% of cord blood [2]), endocrine signals, and high absorption, conservative practice is to avoid BP-3 in pregnancy and pediatric products. Many derms already recommend mineral filters for pregnancy/kids. (Educate, do not give medical advice — see CLAUDE.md.)
  • Carcinogenicity (class-adjacent): BP-3 itself is not classified as a carcinogen. But the unsubstituted parent "benzophenone" is IARC Group 2B and Prop-65 listed, and arises as an impurity/degradant in the family and from octocrylene breakdown — a labeling/COA and reputational landmine for any benzophenone-adjacent formula. [13][14][15]
  • Environmental / sourcing: BP-3 (and octinoxate) are coral-toxic at environmentally relevant concentrations (Downs et al., 2016: coral-cell LC50s 8–340 µg/L; planulae deformity EC20 ~6.5–10 µg/L; bleaching, DNA damage, larval skeletal deformity). [8] This drove bans in Hawaii (eff. 2021), Key West, US Virgin Islands, Palau, Bonaire, Aruba, and others. [8][9] The dose-realism of some coral studies has been debated, but the regulatory and reputational reality is settled: "reef-safe" is now a baseline consumer expectation, and BP-3 is the poster-child of "reef-unsafe."

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Drug vs cosmetic — the big one: In the US, a UV filter used for SPF is an OTC drug active under the sunscreen monograph; formulating a sunscreen is a drug undertaking (monograph compliance, testing, GMP, drug-facts labeling), not a cosmetic one. BP-3 is not GRASE-affirmed — FDA's 2019 proposed rule placed oxybenzone (and other organic filters) in the "insufficient data" bucket, requesting carcinogenicity and dev/repro data; it remains legally marketable under enforcement discretion but affirmatively un-cleared. [1] If we ever make a sunscreen, the defensible monograph path is zinc oxide/titanium dioxide (the only Category I GRASE filters) — see titanium-dioxide.
  • EU: BP-3 is a permitted Annex VI UV filter but restricted by Reg. (EU) 2022/1176: face/hand/lip ≤6%, body ≤2.2%, other ≤0.5% (because SCCS judged 6% unsafe in body products). BP-4/BP-5 ≤5%; BP-8 banned in EU cosmetics. [12][18]
  • Claims caution:
  • ✅ Defensible only if formulating a compliant sunscreen with cleared filters: "broad-spectrum SPF," "helps protect against sun-induced signs of aging" (drug-adjacent; substantiate per monograph/SPF testing).
  • ⚠️ Risky / do-not-say with benzophenones: anything implying BP-3 is "safe," "clean," "reef-safe," or a skincare active. "Anti-aging UV protection" using BP-3 invites the absorption/endocrine/environment criticism directly.
  • 🚫 Reputational claim trap: marketing a premium "longevity" brand on a filter that is banned for reef harm, the top photoallergen, and FDA-non-GRASE is self-defeating. The on-strategy claim is the opposite: "benzophenone-free / oxybenzone-free."
  • See ../../brand/glossary.md for claim-language cheat sheet.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: low. BP-3, BP-4/BP-5, BP-8 are mature, high-volume commodity chemicals (DSM-Firmenich, BASF, and numerous Chinese suppliers); among the cheapest UV filters available — which is precisely why they persisted in mass-market sunscreens. Modern filters (Tinosorb S/M, DHHB, Mexoryl) cost substantially more.
  • Supply: abundant; no scarcity issue.
  • COA / impurity diligence: if any benzophenone (or octocrylene) is used, specify and test for free "benzophenone" (parent) content given Prop-65/IARC-2B status; require purity COAs. This QA burden is itself a reason to avoid the family.
  • Strategic note: "cheap commodity filter" is exactly what the brand thesis says to buy on the base — but the brand differentiates on the brand, not on shipping a controversial filter. The cost saving is not worth the positioning cost.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • "It's a proven sunscreen ingredient, so it's fine." It is a working filter, but "works" ≠ "best choice." It misses UVA-I, is the top photoallergen, absorbs systemically at high plasma levels, and is non-GRASE. Modern filters do the job better with fewer flags.
  • "Sunscreen prevents aging — therefore BP-3 prevents aging." Category-to-molecule overreach. The photoaging-prevention RCTs (e.g., Hughes 2013) validate broad-spectrum SPF behavior, not BP-3 specifically. [10]
  • "The absorption study proved sunscreen is dangerous." Overreach the other way. The Matta JAMA RCTs proved absorption, not toxicity — high plasma levels trigger a requirement for more data, not a finding of harm. [1][11] Honest framing: absorption is high enough that BP-3's hazard signals can no longer be dismissed as "stays on the surface."
  • "BP-3 is an endocrine disruptor that's giving people cancer." The endocrine hazard signal is real and reproducible in vitro/animal; the human risk at typical exposures is unproven and the epidemiology is mixed/confounded. And BP-3 is not a classified carcinogen — that's the parent "benzophenone," a different compound. Don't conflate. [2][3][13]
  • "Reef-safe is just marketing." The coral toxicity is documented at environmentally relevant levels and has driven real legal bans; dose-realism debates don't undo Hawaii/Palau/Key West law. [8][9] For a brand, "reef-safe/oxybenzone-free" is now table stakes, not hype.
  • The real story: benzophenones are the legacy, low-cost, problem-laden end of the UV-filter shelf. The genuinely exciting photoprotection story for a longevity brand is next-gen organic filters and mineral filters, not this family.

11. Verdict for the brand

❌ SKIP — as a hero, supporting active, or marketed ingredient. (Possible exception: BP-4/BP-5 as a trace water-phase formula stabilizer, and even then prefer alternatives.)

One-line: Photoprotection is the #1 evidence-based anti-aging move, but benzophenone filters — especially oxybenzone (BP-3) — are the wrong vehicle: a weak-on-UVA, top-photoallergen, high-systemic-absorption, FDA-non-GRASE, reef-banned legacy commodity whose every liability runs directly against a premium, science-forward, trust-led skin-longevity positioning. The on-brand move is to be explicitly oxybenzone/benzophenone-free and pursue photoprotection via mineral (zinc oxide/titanium dioxide) or next-gen organic filters.

Reasoning: - Positioning is the disqualifier, not just chemistry. Even if one judges the human endocrine risk unproven, BP-3 is simultaneously (a) the most documented sunscreen photoallergen, (b) systemically absorbed at ~100s× the FDA safety-trigger and accumulating, (c) not GRASE-affirmed, and (d) banned for reef harm in multiple jurisdictions. No single one is fatal; together they make BP-3 indefensible for a brand whose entire equity is evidence + trust + premium care. - It's also just an inferior filter (no UVA-I, self-generated ROS, sensitization) — so we'd be taking on all that baggage for worse photoprotection than modern alternatives. - The water-soluble pair (BP-4/BP-5) are the only members with a benign-ish absorption profile and are sometimes used sub-1% to keep a formula from fading — a legitimate technical use unrelated to SPF. Even here, the family's reputational halo (and the "benzophenone" parent-compound confusion in consumer/COA terms) argues for choosing a non-benzophenone stabilizer.

Longevity-relevance note: The category (broad-spectrum daily photoprotection) deserves to be a hero pillar of a skin-longevity brand — it's the highest-ROI anti-aging intervention in dermatology. This dossier's "skip" is a verdict on the filter family, not on photoprotection. Route the brand's sun strategy through titanium-dioxide and next-gen-uv-filters, and consider "oxybenzone-free / benzophenone-free / reef-conscious" as an active trust claim.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19 by independent source-checking of every headline claim against the cited primary literature. The audit's job is to ask, for each claim: (a) is the evidence human and topical, or borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral/biomonitoring? (b) are the cited studies real and accurately represented? (c) are they industry-funded or tiny?

Overall grade: A (for the dossier's actual claims), with the load-bearing safety evidence at A–B and the "BP-3 = inferior/avoid" verdict at A

This is an unusual grade context. The dossier's central thesis is a "skip" verdict, not an efficacy claim — so "A" here means the negative/cautionary claims are strongly and accurately evidenced, and the dossier does not overstate any pro-ingredient benefit. The evidence the verdict rests on is genuinely strong:

  • Systemic absorption (the keystone claim): Grade A — human, topical, RCT. Both JAMA maximal-use RCTs (Matta 2019, n=24; Matta 2020, n=48) are real, FDA-run, and accurately quoted. The 2020 figures — oxybenzone geometric-mean C_max 258.1 ng/mL (lotion) / 180.1 ng/mL (spray), highest of any filter tested, detectable at Day 7 — match the source exactly. This is human topical evidence of the highest tier available for an absorption question.
  • Photoallergy / sensitization: Grade A–B — human, topical. Szczurko (Photocontact allergy to oxybenzone: ten years of experience) is real and the "35 cases among 283 over 10 years" figure is verbatim correct. "Leading sunscreen photoallergen in North America" is well-supported clinical-series evidence (observational, not RCT — hence A–B).
  • Population exposure: Grade A (for what it claims). Calafat 2008 (NHANES) is real; BP-3 detected in 96.8% of the US sample (dossier says ~97% — accurate). Correctly framed as exposure, not harm.
  • FDA non-GRASE status: Grade A — accurately characterized. Verified against the 2019 proposed rule: oxybenzone (and sulisobenzone/BP-4 and dioxybenzone/BP-8) are in the "insufficient data" bucket, not an affirmative finding of harm, and remain legally marketable. The dossier states this nuance correctly and does not overclaim.
  • EU/SCCS restriction: Grade A. SCCS 2021 + Reg. (EU) 2022/1176 verified: 6% safe in face cream, not safe at 6% in body products, 2.2% body. (The specific MoS ~447 figure was already flagged in-dossier as "confirm before quoting" — appropriate; it was not independently confirmed here.)
  • Coral/environmental toxicity: Grade B–C — animal/cell model, real but contested. Downs 2016 is real; coral-cell LC50 8–340 µg/L and planulae deformity EC20 6.5–10 µg/L are quoted correctly. This is non-human, environmental evidence and its dose-realism is genuinely debated in the literature — the dossier already says so. The bans it triggered are legal facts (Grade A for "banned in X").

Evidence that is correctly labeled as weaker / borrowed (no correction needed — the dossier already flags these)

  • Endocrine/reproductive harm in humans: Grade C — indirect, mixed, confounded. Review [2] (Current Environmental Health Reports, 2025) is real and every specific figure attributed to it checks out (↓testosterone ~12% [Scinicariello & Buser 2016]; ↓T3 ~15% [Aker 2018]; 92% maternal urine / 18% cord blood [Beck 2024]; endometriosis OR ~4.98 [Peinado 2023]). But these are observational/cross-sectional human associations + in-vitro/animal mechanism — hazard signal, not proven risk. The dossier states this explicitly and repeatedly. Good.
  • In-vitro endocrine + sperm-Ca²⁺ + ROS chemistry: correctly tiered as in-vitro. Not human, not topical, often high concentration — all flagged in-dossier.
  • Rodent endocrine/mammary effects: correctly tiered as animal, partly oral. Flagged.
  • "Sunscreen prevents photoaging": Grade A as a category claim — and the dossier is scrupulous that this is Hughes 2013 (real RCT, Nambour, n=903, 24% less photoaging) proving broad-spectrum SPF behavior, NOT BP-3 the molecule. This is the single most important honesty move in the file and it is handled correctly.
  • Octocrylene → benzophenone (parent) degradation: real (Downs 2021), but contested. Verified; the study drew formal published rebuttals from industry (Personal Care Products Council). The dossier already cites the rebuttal Letters and keeps the parent-"benzophenone" vs BP-3 distinction clean. Good.

Funding / size caveats found

  • No industry-funded efficacy study is used to prop up the ingredient — the dossier's posture is anti-ingredient, so the usual industry-bias risk runs the other way. If anything, several cited safety/environmental studies (Downs lab; the endocrine review) come from advocacy-adjacent or single-lab sources whose dose-realism and effect sizes are disputed — the dossier appropriately hedges these rather than treating them as settled.
  • Tiny-n is present but disclosed: the JAMA RCTs are small (n=24, n=48) and run under deliberately exaggerated maximal-use conditions (whole body, 4×/day, 4 days) — a pharmacokinetic worst-case, not typical use. Newly added to the one-line summary so the 258 ng/mL number is never quoted naked.
  • Two secondary-sourced figures lean on non-primary citations: Wolff 2007 "~96% of girls" (cited via EWG) and the Australia 10% historical cap. Both are plausible and consistent with primary data found (Wolff cohort detected BP-3 in >94% of girls' samples), but they are the softest links in the chain — fine for an internal dossier, swap to primary sources before any public/marketing use.

Claims softened / corrected in this pass

  1. One-line summary, absorption claim — changed "~100–500× the threshold" to "~100s×" anchored to the actual geometric-mean C_max (258 ng/mL ÷ 0.5 = ~516×, but that is the single highest lotion value, not a general multiplier; "100s×" is the defensible characterization). Added the maximal-use / not-typical-use caveat inline so the scary number is always contextualized.
  2. One-line summary, endocrine claim — added "mixed, and confounded" to "human epidemiology" so the summary matches the (already careful) body text and never reads as if human harm is settled.
  3. One-line summary, non-GRASE — added the parenthetical "flagged for insufficient data, not an affirmative finding of harm" so the headline doesn't imply FDA found BP-3 unsafe.

Net

The dossier is defensible as written and was already among the more intellectually honest evidence reviews one could write on this ingredient: it consistently separates hazard from risk, category from molecule, and exposure from outcome, and it self-flags its own weakest figure. The only real exposure was the one-line summary slightly out-running the body's caution (now fixed). No claim needed to be retracted; three were tightened. The verdict ("skip BP-3/BP-8; BP-4/BP-5 only as a trace water-phase stabilizer, and even then prefer alternatives") is well-supported by human-topical evidence (absorption RCTs + photoallergy series) and does not depend on the contested endocrine/coral literature to stand.
