Ingredient: Bioactive Peptide SA1-III (KP1) (INCI: Acetyl SH-Decapeptide-4 SP Amide Trifluoroacetate)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering (lean: CONDITIONAL / likely SKIP — see Verdict) Slug: peptide-sa1-iii-kp1 · Class: Peptides — primarily an enzyme-inhibitor / anti-protease peptide (marketed as a "collagen modulator"; sometimes labelled a "signal" peptide, but its proven action is degradation-inhibition, not classic ECM signalling)

Evidence-quality headline (read this first). SA1-III/KP1 is a real, peer-reviewed, university-developed peptide with a coherent mechanism and genuine in-vitro data. But it is proprietary to a single company (RELIFE / Menarini Group, "Definisse" line), the human data are all manufacturer-funded, open-label, uncontrolled, n≈20 single-arm studies with no randomisation, no vehicle control, and no blinding, and the active is not (publicly) sold as a raw material to third-party formulators. The peptide's own developers have published a 2024 paper redesigning it because the native molecule is unstable against skin enzymes. Treat the marketing as far ahead of the evidence.

One-line description: A lab-made 10-amino-acid peptide copied from the tail end of the body's own elastase-inhibitor protein (alpha-1-antitrypsin), designed to slow the breakdown of collagen rather than stimulate new collagen.

2. Class & mechanism

SA1-III is best understood as an anti-protease / enzyme-inhibitor peptide, despite occasional "signal peptide" labelling in marketing and one review's taxonomy [5].

Parent biology. Serpin A1 (alpha-1-antitrypsin) is the body's main endogenous inhibitor of neutrophil elastase, a serine protease that degrades elastin and contributes to extracellular-matrix (ECM) breakdown. Researchers at the University of Florence synthesised overlapping fragments of Serpin A1's C-terminus (residues 393–418) and found that the 409–418 decapeptide (SA1-III) reproduced a collagen-protective effect in cell culture [1][2].

Proven cellular mechanism (in vitro). In primary human dermal fibroblasts:

  • SA1-III raises extracellular type I collagen levels by reducing its degradation, not by increasing biosynthesis [2][3]. This is the single most important and most consistently reported mechanistic claim.
  • It lowers secreted/active MMP-2 and MMP-9 (gelatinases) in conditioned media (gelatin zymography) [2][3].
  • It does not alter collagen gene expression, does not stimulate fibroblast proliferation, and did not change inflammatory-mediator gene expression [2][3].
  • The effect persisted in fibroblasts from donors across a wide age range [2][3].

So mechanistically it is a "collagen-sparing" peptide: it tilts ECM turnover toward retention by damping proteolysis. It is not a classic signal peptide (like Matrixyl/palmitoyl pentapeptide), not a carrier peptide (like copper tripeptide GHK-Cu), and despite the "neuro-inhibitor" descriptor in the task brief, there is no evidence SA1-III is a neurotransmitter-inhibitor / "Botox-like" peptide. (The Definisse eye cream contains separate neuro-inhibitor peptides — Acetyl Hexapeptide-8 / Argireline and Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5 / Eyeseryl — but those are different molecules, not SA1-III.) [3]

Known liability. The native peptide is proteolytically unstable against the enzymes present in human skin. The same research group published a 2024 J Med Chem paper designing a shorter, D-amino-acid retro-inverso analog (AAT11RI) specifically because it "hampers recognition by most proteases" and showed higher in-vitro activity than SA1-III — implicitly conceding the parent molecule's stability/potency limitations [4].

Honest summary: SA1-III has one credible target — MMP-driven collagen degradation — and it hits that target in a dish. Every other hallmark is either not its mechanism or is claimed only through whole-formula product studies where SA1-III's contribution cannot be isolated.

4. Evidence review by tier

Reading guide: strength descends from controlled human topical RCTs (best for our use case) down to in-vitro. SA1-III's evidence is strongest at the in-vitro tier and weakest exactly where we need it most (controlled human topical).

Tier 1 — In-vitro (strongest evidence for this ingredient)

  1. Pascarella et al., 2016 — ChemMedChem 11(16):1850–1855. DOI: 10.1002/cmdc.201500472. PMID 26615979. [1] - Design: Synthesised overlapping C-terminal Serpin A1 peptides; screened on cultured human dermal fibroblasts. - Result: Identified SA1-III as the lead — "induce[d] a significant increase in type I collagen levels in the culture medium of treated cells at micromolar concentrations." - Limitations: Discovery/screening paper; cell culture only; no mechanism nailed down here; small in-vitro scope.

  2. Cipriani et al., 2018 — Cell Biology International 42(10):1340–1348. DOI: 10.1002/cbin.11018. PMID 29908000. Authors: Cipriani, Pascarella, Errante, Menicacci, Magnelli, Mocali, Rovero, Giovannelli. [2] - Design: Mechanistic study in primary human dermal fibroblasts (donors of different ages); Western blot (cell lysate + conditioned media) for collagen forms; gelatin zymography for degrading enzymes. - Result: SA1-III increased extracellular collagen by reducing degradation, with no effect on biosynthesis or proliferation; reduced MMP-2 and MMP-9. - Limitations: In-vitro only; no animal/skin-model bridge; manufacturer-adjacent authorship (University of Florence group that developed the peptide). This is the mechanistic backbone of the entire SA1-III story.

  3. Errante et al., 2024 — J Med Chem 67(6):5053–5063. DOI: 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.4c00137. PMID 38470817. [4] - Design: Second-generation peptide design; created a tetrapeptide and a retro-inverso D-amino-acid analog (AAT11RI); tested activity + stability in human skin homogenates. - Result: AAT11RI showed higher in-vitro activity than its precursors and resists dermal proteases. - Why it matters for SA1-III: It is, in effect, a published admission that the original SA1-III is comparatively unstable / sub-optimal in skin. Important caveat against assuming the marketed peptide is robust on skin.

  4. (Contextual review) Apostolopoulos, Ferro et al., 2020 — Frontiers in Chemistry 8:572923. DOI: 10.3389/fchem.2020.572923. [5] Classifies SA1-III among cosmeceutical peptides and reproduces the fibroblast/collagen-turnover figure. Useful framing; not new primary data.

Tier 2 — Animal studies

None found. No in-vivo animal efficacy or pharmacokinetic study of SA1-III was identified in the literature. This is a genuine gap.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL

Not applicable / none. SA1-III is a topical cosmetic active; there are no oral-ingestion studies. (Note: oral collagen peptides — a different class — have far more human RCT support [7], but that evidence does not transfer to topical SA1-III.)

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier we actually need — and it is weak)

All human SA1-III data come from the same manufacturer-funded review and its underlying instrumental/self-assessment studies. None is a randomised controlled trial. None has a vehicle/placebo arm. None is blinded. All are single-arm, ~4-week, n≈20.

  1. Rovero, Malgapo, Sparavigna, Beilin, Wong, Lao, 2022 — "The Clinical Evidence-Based Paradigm of Topical Anti-Aging Skincare Formulations Enriched with Bio-Active Peptide SA1-III (KP1)…" Clinical, Cosmetic and Investigational Dermatology 15:2693–2703. DOI: 10.2147/CCID.S374295. PMID 36540724. PMCID PMC9760069. [3] Bundles three single-arm, open-label instrumental studies (twice-daily, 4 weeks):
  • KP1 Face Cream — n=20 women, 40–70 y (mean 60): skin hydration +43.1% (p<0.0001), extensibility −14.3%, gross elasticity +5.8%, density (p<0.001), roughness −13.0% (p<0.01); self-assessed fine wrinkles +11%, tone/elasticity +12%, luminosity +42% (p<0.05).
  • KP1 Serum — n=20 women, 40–65 y: hydration +32.0%, extensibility −28.4%, gross elasticity +8.7%, roughness −13.4%, density (p<0.0001); self-assessed fine wrinkles +19%, compactness +14%, brightness/luminosity +33%, microrelief +17% (p<0.05).
  • KP1 Eye-Contour Cream — n=20 women, 45–60 y: dark circles −10.7% (colorimetry), crow's-feet roughness −19.2% (profilometry) (p<0.05); high self-reported satisfaction; no adverse effects.

Critical limitations (these gut the human evidence): - No control / no placebo / no randomisation / no blinding — open-label single-arm. Regression to the mean, seasonal/hydration effects, and placebo/expectation effects are uncontrolled. - Confounded actives. Each formula contains high- and low-MW hyaluronic acid, glycerin, panthenol, niacinamide, creatine, tocopheryl acetate (and the eye cream adds Argireline + Eyeseryl). Any of these alone explains the hydration, smoothness, and "luminosity" results. The studies cannot attribute anything to SA1-III specifically. - SA1-III concentration is never disclosed in any publication or product page. - Funding/COI: RELIFE Srl unrestricted educational grant; one author employed-by/granted-by RELIFE; the lead author leads the academic group that invented the peptide. Strong commercial alignment. - n≈20, 4 weeks, all-female, single ethnicity-skewed cohorts. - The article carries a published correction (2023).

  1. Santorelli, Mentone, D'Alessio, Uderzo, Manni, Wong, 2026 — "The Combined Benefits of Definisse™ Revitalise Threads and Definisse [KP1]® Redensifying Cream: A 19-Patient Case Series." Life (Basel) 16(3):465. DOI: 10.3390/life16030465. PMID 41900983. [6] - Design: Case series, 19 patients (17F/2M, 38–72 y), 6 months, 3D imaging + satisfaction questionnaires. 10 patients got threads + KP1 cream; 9 got threads alone. - Result: Improvements in satisfaction, cheek fullness, perceived aging across all; the combination group reported "greater" skin satisfaction. - Critical limitation: The KP1 cream was only an add-on to PDO/thread procedures, never tested standalone. The dominant intervention is the thread lift. This provides essentially no independent evidence for the topical peptide, and the comparison is unblinded/subjective. - COI: one author is a RELIFE employee; another received Menarini consultancy fees.

Where SA1-III sits in the broader peptide evidence landscape

  1. Nukaly, Halawani, Irtaza et al., 2026 — "Oral and topical peptides for skin aging: systematic review and meta-analysis of RCTs." Front Med 13:1618306. DOI: 10.3389/fmed.2026.1618306. [8] - 19 RCTs / 1,341 participants, but the interventions were ~92% oral collagen peptides and only ~8% topical (i.e. roughly 1–2 topical RCTs); SA1-III/KP1 was not included (it has no RCT to include). - The meta-analysis reports significant pooled improvements in hydration, elasticity and wrinkles (e.g. crow's-feet, p<0.01) — but those pooled effects are overwhelmingly driven by the oral-collagen arm; the topical evidence is too thin (1–2 trials) to support a robust standalone topical conclusion. [verify direction of the topical subgroup before quoting it] - Takeaway: the human RCT evidence for topical peptides as a category is sparse, and SA1-III specifically has zero RCTs in this or any review.

Bottom line on evidence: Mechanistically credible and in-vitro reproducible; human topical efficacy specific to SA1-III is unproven (no RCT, no controlled study, confounded formulas, undisclosed concentration, manufacturer-funded).

5. Topical bioavailability

  • Size: ~1.1 kDa decapeptide — above the often-cited ~500 Da rule of thumb for easy stratum-corneum passage. Hydrophilic, charged (two Lys residues, free at physiological pH). Penetration of intact peptide is the central unsolved question for nearly all topical peptides, and SA1-III is no exception. [8]
  • Acetylation + C-amidation improve metabolic stability vs. a free peptide and slightly raise lipophilicity, but do not solve barrier penetration.
  • Stability red flag: The developers' own 2024 work shows native SA1-III is degraded by dermal proteases, motivating the retro-inverso redesign [4]. On-skin half-life of the marketed molecule is therefore questionable.
  • No published human penetration, dermal-delivery, or PK data for SA1-III were found. Claims of dermal collagen modulation in vivo rest entirely on the uncontrolled clinical proxies above, not on demonstrated delivery.

6. Formulation notes

  • Concentration: Not disclosed anywhere (publications or product pages). In-vitro activity is at "micromolar" levels [1]; the use level in commercial creams is proprietary and unverifiable.
  • pH: No published guidance for SA1-III specifically. As a Lys-containing amidated peptide it is generally compatible with mildly acidic–neutral aqueous systems; avoid strongly acidic or oxidising environments. [unverified for this specific peptide]
  • Salt form: Supplied as the trifluoroacetate (TFA) salt [11]. TFA is a common SPPS counter-ion; residual TFA is a quality/spec consideration (and a talking point for "clean" positioning) but is present at trace levels in finished cosmetics.
  • Stability: Likely sensitive to proteolysis and possibly to heat/oxidation; the molecule benefits from a well-buffered, preservative-adequate, low-water-activity or encapsulated system. The retro-inverso successor exists precisely because stability is a concern [4].
  • Pairings (as used in Definisse): High- + low-MW hyaluronic acid, glycerin, panthenol, niacinamide, creatine, tocopheryl acetate, salicyloyl phytosphingosine; eye version adds Argireline + Eyeseryl [3]. No reported incompatibilities, but note these co-actives carry the proven hydration/smoothing load.
  • Sensory: As a small water-soluble peptide it is sensory-neutral; the finished-product feel comes from the base.

7. Safety

  • Local tolerance: No adverse effects reported across the Definisse studies (n≈60 across the three single-arm studies) [3]. Generic cosmetic-peptide safety reviews note peptides "tend to be well tolerated" in skin/eye irritation testing [10].
  • Sensitization: Low theoretical risk. Per the 2026 peptide-safety framework, cosmetic peptides are "typically not electrophilic," so Type IV (hapten) sensitisation is unlikely; peptides <2.5 kDa / <~20–30 aa pose minimal Type I (IgE) risk — SA1-III (10 aa, ~1.1 kDa) sits comfortably below those thresholds [10].
  • Sun sensitivity: None expected (not a photosensitiser; no retinoid-like mechanism).
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: No specific data. As a topical cosmetic peptide with negligible expected systemic absorption, no specific contraindication is documented — but absence of data is not proof of safety; standard "consult your clinician" caution applies. [unverified — no pregnancy-specific study]
  • Sourcing/quality: Synthetic; carries the usual peptide-manufacture considerations (purity, residual solvents/TFA, endotoxin). [11]
  • Caveat: All tolerability data are short (4 weeks) and from the manufacturer; no independent or long-term safety surveillance was found.

8. Regulatory & claims (cosmetic vs. drug — see CLAUDE.md)

  • Regulatory status: A cosmetic ingredient (EU INCI "Acetyl SH-Decapeptide-4 SP Amide Trifluoroacetate"; declared function skin conditioning) [11]. It is not an approved drug.
  • Drug-line risk — high if mis-worded. The whole pitch ("collagen modulator," "inhibits collagen degradation," "increases collagen") drifts toward structure/function (drug) claims in markets like the US (FDA). Saying our product "modulates collagen turnover," "inhibits MMPs," "increases collagen," or "blocks neutrophil elastase" would almost certainly be read as a drug claim and is not substantiated for our finished product by controlled human data.
  • Substantiation gap: Even cosmetic-safe efficacy phrasing is hard to support, because no SA1-III study isolates the peptide and there is no RCT. We could not honestly claim peptide-attributable benefits.
  • Safe cosmetic phrasing only (per glossary): "helps improve the appearance of fine lines," "supports the look of firmer skin," "visibly smooths," "hydrates" — and the hydration/smoothing we'd actually be describing would come from the HA/humectant/niacinamide base, not the peptide. Any "collagen," "MMP," or "elastase" language → flag for legal/regulatory review.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Availability — the dealbreaker. SA1-III/KP1 appears proprietary to RELIFE Srl (Menarini Group) and embedded only in finished Definisse products. We found no evidence it is sold as a raw material (INCI "Acetyl SH-Decapeptide-4 SP Amide Trifluoroacetate") to independent formulators. A small "[unverified]" research-grade listing exists via a chemical reseller (BenchChem) [12], but that is not a cosmetic-grade, regulatory-documented supply line.
  • Implication: A from-scratch brand likely cannot buy this active at cosmetic grade with proper documentation; it would mean either private-labelling Menarini's product or finding an undocumented grey-market source — both poor fits for a credibility-first brand.
  • Cost tier: Custom synthetic decapeptides are expensive (high tier); with single-source proprietary control, pricing/MOQ leverage would be poor even if obtainable. [unverified — no public price]
  • Provenance upside (if it were available): University-of-Florence origin + Menarini pharma backing is a strong credibility story — but only usable if we could actually source and substantiate it.

10. Hype vs. evidence

Marketing message Reality check
"Clinically proven collagen modulator" "Clinical" = open-label, uncontrolled, n≈20, manufacturer-funded; no RCT, no placebo, no blinding. [3]
"Decreases wrinkles, increases hydration in 4 weeks" Whole-formula results; hydration is almost certainly the HA/glycerin/niacinamide base, not the peptide. Peptide contribution never isolated. [3]
"Inhibits collagen degradation / blocks elastase" True in a dish [2]; no in-vivo human proof the peptide reaches the dermis intact and does this on real skin.
"Bench to bedside / evidence-based paradigm" The title oversells; it's a narrative review of in-vitro work + uncontrolled product studies by the inventors. [3]
"Stable, robust topical actigen" The developers published a 2024 redesign because native SA1-III is degraded by skin proteases. [4]
"A new era — collagen modulation beats classic anti-aging" Category-level meta-analysis is dominated by oral collagen (~92% of trials); the topical-peptide RCT base is tiny (1–2 trials), and SA1-III has zero RCTs in it. [8]

Net: A legitimate scientific idea wrapped in marketing that runs well ahead of the data. The in-vitro mechanism is real; the human topical efficacy claims are unsubstantiated for the peptide itself.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ CONDITIONAL, trending to ❌ SKIP — as a hero; not usable as a sourced active today.

Reasoning: 1. We probably can't even buy it. It is single-source proprietary to Menarini/RELIFE with no documented cosmetic-grade raw-material supply to third parties [9][11]. For a brand whose thesis is buy commodity bases, invest in brand, building a hero around an unobtainable, expensive, single-supplier peptide is the opposite of the strategy. 2. The human evidence is weak exactly where we'd need it. No RCT, no controlled/blinded study, undisclosed concentration, and every "clinical" result is confounded by a strong HA/niacinamide base [3][6]. We could not make peptide-specific claims without crossing into unsubstantiated (and drug-adjacent) territory — a direct violation of our claims discipline. 3. Stability question. The inventors' own 2024 paper implies the native molecule underperforms on skin and needs redesigning [4]. 4. The mechanism it sells (anti-MMP/anti-elastase collagen sparing) is real but not unique — and the barrier-repair cream that sits in our actual competitive gap is far better served by ceramides, niacinamide, HA, panthenol, and a retinoid, all of which have stronger, independent, controlled human evidence and clean commodity supply.

If we ever revisit it (the "conditional" path): only if (a) a documented, cosmetic-grade, multi-source supply with disclosed use level becomes available, AND (b) we treat it as a supporting "collagen-support / firmness-support" peptide layered onto a proven base — never as a standalone hero with "modulates collagen" copy. The retro-inverso successor (AAT11RI) [4] is worth watching if it ever reaches the market with better stability data.

Longevity / skin-longevity note: Conceptually on-thesis — preserving existing ECM by damping protease-driven degradation is a "maintain what you have" (longevity) framing rather than a "force turnover" one, which is attractive. But conceptual fit doesn't substitute for evidence and supply. The longevity angle is better delivered by proven anti-protease/antioxidant staples (niacinamide, vitamin C, sunscreen, retinoids) than by a proprietary peptide we can't source or substantiate.

Recommended action: Log in ../../ideas/not-considering.md as "parked — unobtainable single-source proprietary active; revisit only if cosmetic-grade supply + controlled human data emerge." Do not design a SKU around it.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19. Independent adversarial check of every headline claim and citation against primary sources.

Overall grade: D (with a C-grade mechanistic core). Topically, on humans, SA1-III is unproven: there is no RCT, no controlled, blinded or vehicle-comparison study, the peptide's use concentration is undisclosed, and every "clinical" result comes from manufacturer-funded, open-label, single-arm (n≈20, 4-week) studies on multi-active formulas that cannot isolate the peptide. The only well-replicated evidence (anti-MMP-2/-9 collagen-sparing) is in-vitro, from the inventors' own group, and the developers published a 2024 redesign because the native molecule is degraded by skin enzymes. The marketing ("clinically proven collagen modulator," "a new era in anti-aging") runs far ahead of the data. The dossier as written already reaches this conclusion honestly — this section confirms it.

Citation check (real? say what's claimed?): All six core citations are genuine and accurately characterized: - [1] Pascarella 2016, ChemMedChem (PMID 26615979) — REAL. Identifies SA1-III (Ac-MGKVVNPTQK-NH₂) raising type-I collagen at micromolar levels in fibroblasts. (Note: the original paper frames it as a candidate wound-healing lead, not an anti-aging cosmetic — accurately in-vitro.) - [2] Cipriani 2018, Cell Biol Int (PMID 29908000) — REAL. Human vs. borrowed evidence: The dossier correctly keeps the tiers separate — the load-bearing mechanism is in-vitro (borrowed from a dish), human data is weak/indirect, and it explicitly notes that oral-collagen RCT evidence does not transfer to topical SA1-III.
