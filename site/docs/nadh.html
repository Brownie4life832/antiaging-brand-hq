Ingredient: NADH — Reduced Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide (INCI: Disodium NADH)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ⬜ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ☑ conditional / ⬜ in a product Slug: nadh · Class: NAD+ / sirtuin / AMPK / cellular-energy actives

Reading note. This dossier covers NADH — the reduced form of NAD — as a distinct ingredient, not as a synonym for the broader "NAD+ longevity" story. That distinction is the single most important thing in this file, and the marketing world routinely blurs it. NADH and NAD+ are the two halves of the same redox couple, but they are chemically different molecules with opposite oxidation states, and the entire aging-biology literature is built around raising NAD+ (the oxidized form). NADH is the form that accumulates in dysfunctional, aged, or hypoxic cells. So a "topical NADH" product is, mechanistically, swimming against the current of the very longevity narrative it borrows from. We keep that line hard and explicit throughout. For the NAD+/NMN/niacinamide side of the family, see nad-niacinamide-longevity.md; this file is the NADH-specific companion.

0. The honest punchline (read this first)

  • NADH is the reduced, electron-carrying form of NAD. In a healthy cell most of the pool sits as NAD+ (oxidized); NADH is the minority partner that ferries electrons into the mitochondrial electron transport chain to make ATP. The NAD+/NADH ratio — high NAD+, low NADH — is a hallmark of healthy, energetic, "young" cellular metabolism.
  • The longevity field is almost entirely about raising NAD+, not NADH. Sirtuins and PARPs — the "longevity enzymes" — consume NAD+. NAD+ (not NADH) declines with age; CD38 (an NAD+-consuming enzyme) rises with age (Covarrubias 2021 [1]; Chini 2024 [2]). The validated intervention is to push the pool toward NAD+ (via niacinamide / NMN / NR), which is the opposite direction from supplying NADH.
  • Elevating the NADH/NAD+ ratio has a name in the literature, and it is not flattering: "reductive stress." An elevated NADH/NAD+ ratio is associated with mitochondrial dysfunction and disease, and can itself generate reactive oxygen species (Xiao 2025 reductive-stress review [3]; Leigh-syndrome reductive-stress study [4]). One topical NAD+ cosmetics paper even flagged that NAD+ applied to keratinocytes was "quickly converted to NADH," producing an elevated NADH/NAD+ ratio the authors explicitly called "reductive stress" (Ministrini 2025 [5]). That is a cautionary signal for a deliberate NADH product.
  • Human topical evidence for NADH is essentially absent. There is no peer-reviewed human topical RCT showing an NADH cosmetic visibly reduces wrinkles, evens tone, or raises skin NAD(H). The marketed topical (Prof. George Birkmayer NADH Skin Serum) cites no clinical study for the topical and ships with a refrigerate-and-use-within-3-months instruction — a tell that the molecule is unstable [6].
  • NADH's real human data is ORAL, modest, and non-dermatological (chronic fatigue syndrome pilots, Parkinson's open-label series, jet-lag/cognition) — borrowed format and borrowed indication.
  • Net: NADH is a legitimate biochemical, and as a low-molecular-weight reductant it has some antioxidant cofactor activity, but as a skincare hero it is the wrong member of the family: unstable, large/charged, unproven topically, and pointed in the reductive direction while the science rewards the oxidized (NAD+) direction. Treat it as skip-to-conditional. If a "cellular energy / NAD" story is wanted, build it on niacinamide (proven, cheap, an actual NAD+ precursor), not NADH.

Biochemical relationship (why this matters). NAD exists as an interconverting redox couple: NAD+ ⇌ NADH. NAD+ accepts two electrons + one proton (a hydride) to become NADH; NADH donates them back (e.g. to Complex I of the electron transport chain) to regenerate NAD+. Both forms are built on the same nicotinamide–adenine dinucleotide scaffold. Crucially, NADH is not a precursor that "tops up" the NAD+ pool the way niacinamide/NMN/NR do — those feed the salvage/biosynthesis pathways. NADH is the already-reduced end of the redox seesaw. Supplying NADH does not obviously raise total NAD or NAD+; if anything it biases the ratio toward the reduced side.

2. Class & mechanism

Class: NAD+ / sirtuin / AMPK / cellular-energy & redox actives (the "cellular bioenergetics" family). Filed here, but note the mechanistic mismatch described below.

The mechanistic thesis (as marketed): - NADH is the electron donor to the mitochondrial electron transport chain: NADH → Complex I (NADH dehydrogenase) → proton pumping → ATP synthase → ATP. Oxidising 1 NADH yields roughly 2.5–3 ATP. So NADH is genuinely "the molecule that powers ATP." - The cosmetic pitch: skin cells need ATP for collagen synthesis, proliferation, differentiation and barrier repair; deliver NADH → more reducing equivalents → more ATP → "energized," younger-acting skin. NADH is also a reductant, so it is pitched as an antioxidant that quenches oxidative damage.

Why the thesis is shaky at the mechanism level (the honest version): 1. Direction problem. Healthy, youthful metabolism is defined by a high NAD+/NADH ratio. Sirtuins and PARPs — the enzymes the whole "longevity" story rests on — require NAD+ as substrate, not NADH; their activity tracks NAD+ availability. Adding NADH does not feed sirtuins and pushes the ratio the wrong way (Covarrubias 2021 [1]; Chini 2024 [2]; Verdin 2015 [10]). 2. Reductive stress. An elevated NADH/NAD+ ratio (reductive stress) is associated with mitochondrial dysfunction and can itself generate ROS (Xiao 2025 [3]; Leigh-syndrome study [4]). The intracellular machinery doesn't simply "burn extra NADH for free ATP" — it is feedback-regulated, and overload backs up the electron transport chain and increases superoxide leak. 3. The double-edged antioxidant. NADH is a substrate for NADH/NADPH oxidases (NOX) and for microsomal/iron-dependent ROS generation — i.e. NADH can drive reactive-oxygen production, not only quench it (Dicker & Cederbaum 1991 [11]). Its net redox effect is context-dependent, not unambiguously "antioxidant." 4. Delivery problem. None of this matters topically unless intact NADH reaches living cells, which the molecule's size/charge/instability make doubtful (see §5).

What is genuinely true: NADH is a real, central metabolic coenzyme; cellular ATP and the NAD pool do decline with age; raising cellular bioenergetics is a legitimate anti-aging target. The flaw is the choice of molecule and direction — the evidence points to raising NAD+, and NADH is the reduced counterpart, not a clean route to that goal.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core of this dossier)

Tiers, strongest format first within the longevity story: in-vitro → animal → human ORAL → human TOPICAL (RCT vs lower). For NADH I flag whether each item is about NADH itself or NAD+/precursors (borrowed), and whether it is topical or another format.

TIER A — In-vitro (cell / biochemistry)

A1. NAD+ converts to NADH in keratinocytes → reductive stress (the most NADH-relevant cosmetic finding). - Ministrini S, et al. "A Liposomal Formulation Enhances the Anti-Senescence Properties of Nicotinamide Adenine-Dinucleotide (NAD+) in Endothelial Cells and Keratinocytes." Curr Issues Mol Biol 2025;47(9):722. DOI 10.3390/cimb47090722; PMC12468054. [5] - Design: primary human aortic endothelial cells (HAECs) and primary human epidermal keratinocytes (HEKas); NAD+ (oxidized) at 5% w/w, free vs liposomal (LF-NAD+); ex-vivo skin penetration on a single donor. - Findings: LF-NAD+ raised intracellular NAD+, improved HAEC survival (+19.3%) and reduced senescent cells (−28.7% vs NAD+ alone), lowered p16/p21; in keratinocytes senescent-cell reduction −15.4%, neutral on survival. Liposomal delivery improved skin penetration ~30% vs free NAD+. Most relevant here: in keratinocytes the applied NAD+ was "quickly converted to NADH," yielding an elevated NADH/NAD+ ratio the authors describe as reductive stress. - Why it matters for NADH: this is the field telling us that, in skin cells, accumulating NADH is the undesirable state — and that even when you supply the "good" NAD+, drifting toward NADH is a problem. Supplying NADH directly starts you in that unwanted state. - Limitations: in-vitro 2D; cells not in advanced senescence; penetration n=1 donor; studies NAD+, not NADH, as the active.

A2. Exogenous NAD+ (not NADH) protects skin fibroblasts; boosted by CD38 inhibition. - Kang et al. "Novel Approach to Skin Anti-Aging: Boosting Pharmacological Effects of Exogenous Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide (NAD+) by Synergistic Inhibition of CD38 Expression." (2024); PMC11544843. [13] - Design: in-vitro, human fibroblasts; exogenous NAD+ ± a phytochemical complex (quercetin + enoxolone/glycyrrhetinic acid) to inhibit CD38 (the NAD+-consuming enzyme that rises with age). - Findings: NAD+ protected against UV-induced (extrinsic) and intrinsic aging; effects amplified by CD38 inhibition; mechanism via ↑sirtuin activation, autophagy, mitochondrial function. - Relevance to NADH: reinforces that the validated lever is NAD+ availability (raise NAD+, slow its degradation) — not supplying NADH. Borrowed (NAD+, in-vitro).

A3. NADH as a redox-active small molecule — double-edged. - NADH provides reducing equivalents and shows moderate radical-scavenging activity in antioxidant assays (general redox literature). But NADH is also the substrate for NADH-dependent oxidases / microsomal systems that generate ROS in the presence of iron and redox-cycling agents (Dicker E, Cederbaum AI. Arch Biochem Biophys 1991; PMID 1650215 [11]). So NADH's antioxidant credentials are conditional, not categorical. - Limitation: biochemical/assay context; not skin-efficacy data.

A4. The reductive-stress literature (mechanistic caution). - Reductive stress (elevated NADH/NAD+) is linked to mitochondrial dysfunction and disease and can produce ROS (Xiao W, et al. reductive-stress reviews; e.g. Redox Biol / Free Radic Biol Med 2025 [3]; Leigh-syndrome patient-fibroblast + mouse pilot showing NADH reductive stress correlating with severity, PMC11764390 [4]). - Relevance: directly undercuts the naive "more NADH = more energy = younger skin" pitch.

TIER B — Animal

  • No published animal study was found testing topical NADH on skin photoaging or barrier. (Contrast: oral/IP NMN has multiple mouse photoaging studies — see nad-niacinamide-longevity.md. Those are NMN, not NADH.)
  • Birkmayer's foundational NADH animal/biochemical work centered on dopamine biosynthesis / neuro applications, not dermatology. [partly unverified for dermatology — no skin animal model located]

TIER C — Human ORAL (NADH's only real human data — and it isn't about skin)

Oral NADH has a small, old, mostly Birkmayer-associated human literature in fatigue/neurology, not dermatology. Classic borrowed-format and borrowed-indication trap.

C1. Oral NADH in chronic fatigue syndrome (the lead human RCT). - Forsyth LM, Preuss HG, MacDowell AL, Chiazze L Jr, Birkmayer GD, Bellanti JA. "Therapeutic effects of oral NADH on the symptoms of patients with chronic fatigue syndrome." Ann Allergy Asthma Immunol 1999;82(2):185–191. PMID 10071523. [14] - Design: randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover; n=26 (CDC-criteria CFS); NADH 10 mg/day; two 4-week periods, 4-week washout. - Results: favorable response in 8/26 (31%) on NADH vs 2/26 (8%) on placebo; no serious AEs. - Limitations: small pilot; modest absolute benefit; Birkmayer (NADH patent holder) is a co-author — conflict of interest; subjective endpoints.

C2. Oral NADH vs conventional therapy in CFS (open comparison). - Santaella ML, Font I, Disdier OM. "Comparison of oral nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NADH) versus conventional therapy for chronic fatigue syndrome." P R Health Sci J 2004;23(2):89–93. [15] - Findings: symptom improvement reported but no durable superiority over conventional management; small, unblinded. Weak.

C3. CoQ10 + NADH combination in ME/CFS (the best-powered RCT — but combination). - Castro-Marrero J, Segundo MJ, Lacasa M, et al. "Effect of Dietary Coenzyme Q10 Plus NADH Supplementation on Fatigue Perception and Health-Related Quality of Life in Individuals with ME/CFS: A Prospective, Randomized, Double-Blind, Placebo-Controlled Trial." Nutrients 2021;13(8):2658. PMC8399248. [16] - Design: RCT; 207 screened, 144 completed (72/group); 200 mg CoQ10 + 20 mg NADH/day; 8 wk + 4-wk follow-up. - Results: significant reduction in cognitive fatigue (p<0.001 at 4 & 8 wk) and improved physical functioning/quality of life; sleep improvements. NAD+ levels were NOT measured. - Limitations: combination only — NADH effect not isolable from CoQ10; subjective endpoints; single-center, mostly Caucasian women.

C4. Oral NADH for Parkinson's disease (historic, open-label). - Birkmayer GD et al. open-label series — e.g. 34 patients IV NADH (62% "very good," 38% "moderate" improvement); larger 885-patient open uncontrolled IV/oral series. [17] - Limitations: open-label, uncontrolled, single-group, decades old, conflicted — hypothesis-generating only. Not reproduced in modern controlled trials.

C5. Oral NADH for jet lag / sleep-deprived cognition. - Small double-blind trial: sleep-deprived subjects given NADH performed better on cognitive/wakefulness measures vs placebo; did not reduce daytime sleepiness or improve mood. [18] - Limitations: tiny, acute, not skin-relevant.

Oral summary: NADH's human evidence is small, dated, conflict-laden (Birkmayer), often combination or uncontrolled, and entirely outside dermatology. Even at its best it supports only modest fatigue/cognition effects. None of it transfers to a topical skin claim.

TIER D — Human TOPICAL

D1. Topical NADH — the gap. - No peer-reviewed human topical RCT (or even a controlled clinical study) testing an NADH cosmetic for wrinkles, tone, barrier, or skin NAD(H) was located. - The one marketed topical, Prof. George Birkmayer NADH Skin Serum (INCI: Aqua, Propylene Glycol, Alcohol, Sodium Bicarbonate, Hydrogenated Phosphatidylcholine, Disodium NADH, Cholesterol), makes appearance claims ("radiant appearance," "skin-caring fluid") and references the oral Birkmayer NADH legacy, but cites no clinical study for the topical and requires refrigeration at 3–8 °C, use within 3 months of opening — a transparency tell about stability [6]. - The closest controlled topical skin data in the family is for NAD+ (Ministrini 2025 liposomal NAD+, in-vitro/ex-vivo [5]) and niacinamide (extensive human RCTs — see nad-niacinamide-longevity.md) — neither is NADH.

D2. Verdict on the topical story. Any "topical NADH energizes/repairs your skin cells" or "raises your skin's NAD" claim is, for the NADH format, unsubstantiated — and partly counter-indicated by the reductive-stress finding (A1/A4). The energy biology is real; the chosen molecule and the human topical proof are not aligned.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • NADH is a poor passive-penetration candidate. It is large (~665–709 g/mol), highly polar, and carries multiple negative charges (two phosphates; disodium salt). This violates the rule-of-thumb "500 Dalton rule" for efficient stratum-corneum penetration and the small/moderately-lipophilic ideal (MW <500, logP 1–4) [19]. Pure NAD-family dinucleotides "struggle to penetrate skin on their own due to molecular size" [8][19].
  • Delivery systems are essentially mandatory. Liposomal/phosphatidylcholine encapsulation is the standard work-around; for NAD+, liposomal delivery improved penetration only ~30% vs free (single-donor ex-vivo, Ministrini 2025 [5]) — a modest gain off a low base, and that was NAD+, not NADH. The Birkmayer serum uses hydrogenated phosphatidylcholine + cholesterol (a liposome-type system) [6], consistent with the molecule needing help to enter.
  • Instability compounds the problem. NADH is oxidation- and light-sensitive and acid-labile (it degrades faster at low pH; the oxidized NAD+ is more acid-stable, the reduced NADH more base-stable). Refrigeration + short in-use life on the marketed serum [6] indicate that even the finished product is fighting decomposition. Any NADH that oxidizes in the jar becomes NAD+ (and ultimately can hydrolyze toward nicotinamide), so part of any in-use effect may not be NADH at all.
  • Bottom line: there is no human-skin penetration data for NADH specifically. Best case it behaves like NAD+ (poor passive penetration, modestly helped by liposomes); worst case it oxidizes before it gets anywhere.

6. Formulation

Parameter Practical guidance for NADH (Disodium NADH)
Concentration No evidence-derived efficacious topical level exists; vendor-driven only. Cosmetic inclusion is typically low (fractions of a % to low single digits), constrained by cost and stability rather than a dose-response curve.
pH NADH is acid-labile — favor near-neutral to mildly alkaline (~pH 7–8). The Birkmayer serum includes sodium bicarbonate (buffering toward alkaline) [6]. Low-pH systems (vitamin C, AHA serums) will accelerate degradation.
Stability Poor. Oxygen-, light-, heat- and acid-sensitive; oxidizes to NAD+, can hydrolyze further. Needs encapsulation (liposome), inert/opaque airless packaging, antioxidants (the serum adds tocopherol-type protection in analogous NAD formulas [5]), low water activity, and often cold-chain / refrigeration + short shelf life [6]. This is a hard, expensive ingredient to keep alive in a cream.
Pairings (conceptual) Layered with mitochondrial/energy-story actives (CoQ10, the one combination with human data albeit oral [16]); phosphatidylcholine/cholesterol liposomal carriers; humectants.
Conflicts Low-pH actives (L-ascorbic acid, glycolic/lactic acid), strong oxidizers, prolonged heat/light.
Sensory As supplied in serums: light, fast-absorbing, water-thin fluid; the disodium salt is water-soluble. NADH solutions can carry a faint yellow tint when partially oxidized.

Formulator's reality check: the combination of poor penetration + poor stability + no efficacious-dose data + reductive-direction mechanism means a lot of formulation effort and cost to deliver an active whose topical benefit is unproven. If the goal is a "cellular-energy / NAD" claim, niacinamide delivers the story with none of these problems.

7. Safety

  • Intrinsic toxicity: low. NADH is an endogenous coenzyme present in every human cell; oral human trials to ~10–20 mg/day report no serious adverse events (mild, if any) [14][16]. As a food-/biology-derived molecule, intrinsic irritancy is expected to be low.
  • Topical irritation/sensitization: no published human topical irritation/sensitization data for NADH specifically. Disodium NADH is not on EWG Skin Deep's restricted/unacceptable lists [8], and there is no CIR monograph specific to it (unlike niacinamide). Treat as low-concern but under-characterized; patch-test logic and conservative use levels apply.
  • Oxidative/reductive-balance caveat: the theoretical concern is not classic skin irritation but the reductive-stress / NOX-substrate angle (§2, A1/A3/A4) — i.e. whether chronically loading skin cells with a reductant could be net-unhelpful. This is mechanistic, not a demonstrated clinical harm, but it argues against high concentrations and against over-claiming "antioxidant."
  • Sun sensitivity: none expected (not a photosensitizer like retinoids/AHAs). NADH is itself UV/light-sensitive (a stability issue), not a phototoxicity issue.
  • Pregnancy: no specific contraindication identified for topical use; as an endogenous coenzyme, low theoretical concern — but with no data, defer to medical advice and avoid any therapeutic framing.
  • Sourcing quality: NADH is produced by enzymatic/fermentation and chemical routes for the lab/supplement market; purity and oxidation state vary (material can arrive partly oxidized to NAD+). Demand a CoA with assay (%NADH vs NAD+), water content, and storage conditions.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

The cosmetic/drug line. In the US (FDA) / EU, a cosmetic may improve appearance but must not claim to treat/prevent disease or alter the body's structure/function — that is drug territory. NADH's only real human data is in disease contexts (CFS, Parkinson's), which makes the claims tightrope especially narrow: the credible evidence is exactly the kind we cannot invoke for a cosmetic.

  • ✅ Plausibly safe cosmetic language (still needs our own substantiation for any efficacy claim): "formulated with NADH, a coenzyme involved in skin cells' natural energy processes," "supports a radiant, refreshed appearance," "conditions and softens skin" (matches the INCI emollient/skin-conditioning function [7]).
  • ⚠️ Risky / drug-like / unsubstantiated — flag for legal:
  • "boosts cellular energy / ATP in your skin," "recharges your skin's NAD" — not demonstrated for topical NADH; mechanism, not efficacy; and NADH ≠ NAD+.
  • "antioxidant protection" stated as fact — NADH is double-edged (NOX/ROS substrate [11]); the unqualified antioxidant claim is not defensible.
  • "activates sirtuins / longevity genes" — scientifically backwards for NADH (sirtuins consume NAD+, not NADH). Do not use.
  • "reverses aging / repairs cells / detoxifies," any borrowing of the Parkinson's / chronic-fatigue evidence — disease claims; off-limits for a cosmetic.
  • any "clinically proven" framing for a topical — there is no topical clinical trial to cite.
  • Compliance nuance unique to NADH: because the molecule is the reduced form, the "sirtuin/longevity-gene" marketing common to the NAD category is not just unsubstantiated for NADH — it is mechanistically wrong, and an informed reviewer/competitor/dermatologist could call it out. That is a credibility landmine for a science-forward brand.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • "NADH energizes your skin cells / more ATP = younger skin." Mechanism oversimplified; topical-human proof absent. Cellular ATP isn't limited by squirting NADH on the stratum corneum, and overloading the reduced side risks reductive stress [3][4][5]. Overreach.
  • "NADH is a powerful antioxidant." Half-true and context-dependent. NADH is a reductant but also a ROS-generating NOX/microsomal substrate [11]; "antioxidant" as an unqualified claim is not defensible.
  • "NADH activates your longevity genes (sirtuins)." Backwards. Sirtuins are NAD+-dependent; NADH is not their fuel. This is the clearest example of NAD-category marketing applied to the wrong molecule.
  • "Clinically proven NADH skincare." No topical clinical trial exists. The real NADH human data is oral, modest, conflicted (Birkmayer), and about fatigue/Parkinson's, not skin [14][16][17].
  • "Premium NADH does what cheap niacinamide can't." Unproven topically, and unstable; much of any delivered NADH may oxidize toward NAD+ in the pack. The proven NAD-axis actor is the cheap precursor, niacinamide.
  • What's NOT hype: NADH is a real, essential coenzyme; the NAD pool and cellular bioenergetics genuinely decline with age; "cellular energy" is a legitimate longevity theme. The error is operationalizing that theme with NADH rather than an NAD+-raising precursor.

11. Verdict for the brand

  • NADH (this dossier's target): ⚠️→❌ CONDITIONAL, leaning SKIP as a hero or named active.
  • Reasoning: NADH is the reduced member of the NAD couple, which means (a) it points in the opposite direction from the validated longevity lever (raise NAD+); (b) elevating it risks reductive stress [3][4][5]; (c) it is large, charged, and unstable, with no human topical penetration or efficacy data; (d) its only real human evidence is oral, modest, conflicted, and non-dermatological; and (e) the category's standard "sirtuin/longevity-gene" marketing is mechanistically wrong for it — a credibility risk for a science-forward brand.
  • If used at all: a tiny, encapsulated, well-protected "story garnish" layered on a niacinamide base, with claims limited to appearance/skin-conditioning and the energy mechanism kept as education, never as substantiated efficacy. Never claim it raises skin NAD+ or activates sirtuins.
  • Better same-story alternatives (prefer these):
  • Niacinamide — ✅ hero-capable. Proven (barrier/tone/appearance RCTs), cheap, safe, and an actual NAD+ precursor. Best longevity-story-to-risk ratio in the family.
  • NAD+ (liposomal) / NMN / NR — ⚠️ conditional "mechanism" actives with at least some in-vitro skin data and a directionally-correct (oxidized-side) story — still no human topical RCTs (see nad-niacinamide-longevity.md).
  • One-line reasoning: The "cellular-energy/NAD" story is worth telling — but tell it with niacinamide (or, secondarily, an NAD+ raiser), not NADH, which is the wrong oxidation state, unstable, and unproven on skin.

Longevity-relevance note. NADH is a textbook case of where the longevity narrative and the actual biology diverge. The hallmark "loss of cellular energy / NAD decline" is real and on-brand; but the science rewards raising NAD+ (and clearing CD38), not supplying its reduced partner. Leaning into NADH would let us sound longevity-forward while being quietly off-mechanism — exactly the kind of move that erodes the credibility this brand is being built on. Use it, if ever, with eyes open and claims tight.

Overall grade: D — unproven topically; the dossier's own (skeptical) conclusion is the defensible one

Grade D here is not a criticism of the dossier — it is agreement with it. The dossier already argues that topical NADH is unproven and partly counter-indicated, and the verification confirms that read. There is no human topical evidence for NADH of any tier (no RCT, no controlled clinical study, no human-skin penetration data). The only directionally-supportive topical-family data is for NAD+ (in-vitro/ex-vivo) and niacinamide — neither is NADH. The molecule's real human data is oral, small, dated, conflicted, and non-dermatological. On the A–D scale (A = strong human-topical RCT … D = unproven topically/hype), topical NADH is a clear D.

Note the unusual situation: most dossiers risk over-claiming. This one is the opposite — its bias runs against the ingredient, and that bias is well-founded. There were essentially no overstated pro-NADH claims to soften. The verification work therefore mostly consists of confirming that the skepticism is correctly sourced, plus one citation-metadata fix.

Main caveats (carry-forward)

  1. Zero human topical evidence. Nothing in this file demonstrates that topical NADH does anything on human skin. Every supportive data point is either NAD+ (not NADH), in-vitro/ex-vivo, animal, or oral. This is the governing fact and the dossier states it plainly.
  2. Borrowed-format AND borrowed-indication. NADH's human trials are oral and in disease (CFS/Parkinson's) — doubly non-transferable to a cosmetic appearance claim. The best-powered trial (Castro-Marrero) is a CoQ10 + NADH combination, so it cannot even isolate NADH.
  3. Conflict of interest is pervasive. The core oral human literature and the only marketed topical trace to Birkmayer (patent holder / brand owner). Treat as low-independence.
  4. Direction-of-bias is reversed (in a good way). Unlike typical hype dossiers, this one understates rather than overstates the ingredient. No pro-NADH claim required softening; the standing verdict (skip-to-conditional, anchor the NAD/energy story on niacinamide) is the defensible position.
  5. Unverified citations remain (already self-flagged): [17] Parkinson's open-label series, [18] jet-lag trial, and the Bos & Meinardi PMID inside [19]. These are reached via secondary summaries — confirm primaries before any external use. They are not load-bearing for the dossier's conclusion.
