Ingredient: Piperlongumine (INCI: not formally assigned — see §8)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Senotherapeutics & senolytics (natural amide alkaloid; ROS/electrophilic stress agent)

One-line honest summary: Piperlongumine (a.k.a. piplartine) is one of the most-cited "natural senolytics" in the longevity literature — but that reputation rests almost entirely on in-vitro cell-killing of cultured senescent fibroblasts plus oral/injected oncology and cognition models in mice. There is zero human topical skin evidence, no INCI assignment in the major cosmetic databases, and a reactive Michael-acceptor electrophile chemistry that is a genuine formulation/safety liability. Treat it as a narrative/early-science talking point at most — leaning toward SKIP for a near-term topical product, not a hero.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Piperlongumine (PL); synonym piplartine / piplartin.
  • INCI: None formally established in the primary cosmetic nomenclature sources checked (no CosIng/PCPC INCI entry surfaced). Trade/chemical catalogs list it only by chemical name. Some ingredient catalogs (e.g., The Good Scents Company) tag it as a "cosmetic agent" with claimed functions antioxidant / bleaching (skin-lightening) / skin-conditioning, but this is a catalog classification, not a regulatory INCI listing. [9][10]
  • CAS: 20069-09-4
  • Molecular formula / weight: C₁₇H₁₉NO₅ · ~317.3 g/mol (PubChem CID 637858). [10]
  • IUPAC / structure: 5,6-dihydro-1-[(2E)-1-oxo-3-(3,4,5-trimethoxyphenyl)-2-propen-1-yl]-2(1H)-pyridinone. Structurally it is a 3,4,5-trimethoxycinnamoyl group amide-linked to a 5,6-dihydropyridin-2(1H)-one (δ-lactam) ring. [10]
  • Chemical class: Amide alkaloid of the Piper genus (Piperaceae). The defining reactive feature: two α,β-unsaturated carbonyl (Michael-acceptor) electrophilic sites — the C2–C3 olefin on the lactam ring and the C7–C8 olefin on the cinnamoyl arm. This electrophilicity is the mechanism and the liability (see §2, §6, §7). [1][7]
  • Natural source: Fruit/root of long pepper (Piper longum L.), and related species (P. tuberculatum, P. retrofractum); a traditional Ayurvedic plant. Typically isolated/purified or made synthetically for research. [11]
  • Physical/chemistry notes: Lipophilic small molecule; poorly water-soluble (research uses DMSO/organic carriers); yellow-ish crystalline solid. Lipophilicity is a point in favor of skin penetration but a problem for aqueous cosmetic systems.
  • Key forms/derivatives: Plain PL; numerous synthetic analogues designed for higher senolytic potency (e.g., exocyclic-methylene "compound 49," EC₅₀ ~0.67 µM, ~12× more potent than PL) — these are research molecules, not cosmetic ingredients. [3]

2. Class & mechanism (how it works at the cell level)

Piperlongumine is best understood as an electrophilic, redox-active senolytic / pro-oxidant — it kills stressed cells by overwhelming their antioxidant defenses, and (in the senescence field) it appears to do this somewhat preferentially in senescent cells.

Mechanistic pillars (all established in cancer cells or cultured senescent fibroblasts, not human skin):

  1. GSTP1 inhibition → ROS accumulation (the original cancer mechanism). Raj et al. (Nature, 2011) identified PL in a screen as selectively killing cancer cells by binding and inhibiting glutathione S-transferase pi-1 (GSTP1) and related stress-response enzymes, causing reactive-oxygen-species (ROS) buildup specifically in cells already under oxidative stress, while sparing normal cells. [1]
  2. Michael-acceptor / thiol & selenol reactivity. The C2–C3 (and secondarily C7–C8) electrophilic olefins covalently react with cysteine/selenocysteine residues — forming glutathione adducts and irreversibly inhibiting antioxidant enzymes such as thioredoxin reductase 1 (TrxR1). This is a broadly reactive covalent mechanism (mass-spec found PL binds hundreds of proteins). [7][8]
  3. OXR1 degradation (the senolytic mechanism). Zhang et al. (Aging Cell, 2018) showed senescent fibroblasts upregulate oxidation resistance 1 (OXR1), a master regulator of antioxidant enzymes (GPX2, HO-1, catalase). PL binds OXR1 and drives its ubiquitin–proteasome degradation in a senescent-cell-specific manner, disarming their oxidative defenses and triggering apoptosis. [4]
  4. Apoptosis — partly ROS-independent. The foundational senolytic paper (Wang et al., Aging 2016) found PL kills senescent cells via caspase-3/PARP apoptosis, and — importantly — that ROS elevation was not required (antioxidant rescue didn't block killing). So the senolytic action is not purely "it makes free radicals." [2]
  5. NF-κB / NLRP3 anti-inflammatory effects (a separate axis). In non-skin models PL suppresses NF-κB signaling (covalent hit on IKKβ Cys179) and acts as an NLRP3 inflammasome inhibitor, lowering TNF-α/IL-1β/IL-6 — relevant to an "inflammaging" story but again proven in arthritis/asthma/neuro models, not skin. [12][13]
  6. Anti-melanogenic effect (the most skin-relevant in-vitro finding). PL suppresses α-MSH-induced melanogenesis by downregulating MITF and the pigment enzymes tyrosinase/TRP-1/TRP-2 — i.e., it appears to act mainly at the expression level (cellular tyrosinase activity fell alongside the gene/protein downregulation; the source does not establish a clean cell-free tyrosinase inhibition assay). [5]

Skin-relevant translation (honest): items 1–4 explain why PL is exciting as a senolytic concept, but every demonstration is in cultured cells or model organisms. Item 6 (pigment) is the only mechanism with cell + simple-organism skin-pigment data. Whether a topical cosmetic dose reaches living human senescent skin cells and selectively clears them is entirely unproven.

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

The single most important honesty point: Piperlongumine's entire anti-aging reputation is built on (a) in-vitro senolysis of cultured WI-38 fibroblasts and (b) oral/IP oncology + one cognition study in mice. For human skin of any kind — oral or topical — there is essentially nothing. A "piperlongumine senolytic serum" would be selling a cell-culture and mouse story as a finished topical product. Be brutally clear about that gap.

Tier 1 — In vitro (cell models)

  • Wang Y., Chang J., Liu X. et al., 2016, Aging (Albany NY) 8(11):2915–2926 (DOI 10.18632/aging.101100). The foundational senolytic paper. In human WI-38 lung fibroblasts made senescent three ways (ionizing radiation 15 Gy, replicative exhaustion ~passage 38, and oncogenic Ras), PL preferentially killed senescent cells with EC₅₀ ~6.2–8.0 µM (senescent) vs ~20.3 µM (non-senescent) — i.e., only ~2.5–3.3× selectivity (modest). Killing was caspase-3/PARP apoptosis and was ROS-independent (γ-tocotrienol antioxidant rescue did not prevent it). PL was strongly synergistic with the senolytic ABT-263 (CDI 0.02–0.41). SAR: the lactam-ring Michael acceptor is essential; trimethoxyphenyl ring tolerates modification. Limitations: a single cell type (WI-38), in vitro only, modest selectivity window, authors explicitly caution senescence is also needed for wound healing. [2]
  • Zhang X., Zhang S., Liu X. et al., 2018, Aging Cell 17(4):e12780 (DOI 10.1111/acel.12780). Mechanism paper. Identified OXR1 as a key PL target: senescent WI-38 cells upregulate OXR1 (and downstream GPX2/HO-1/catalase); PL (≈5 µM) binds OXR1 and triggers its proteasomal degradation specifically in senescent cells, raising ROS and killing them. Mass-spec found 172 candidate PL targets — so OXR1 is one of many. Limitations: in vitro only, single cell type, PL is promiscuous (binds hundreds of proteins). [4]
  • Liu X., Wang Y., Zhang X. et al., 2018, Bioorganic & Medicinal Chemistry 26(13):3925–3938 (DOI 10.1016/j.bmc.2018.06.013; PMID 29925484). Synthesized PL analogues; the exocyclic-methylene "compound 49" reached EC₅₀ ~0.67 µM (~12× more potent than PL) against IR-induced senescent WI-38, with modest selectivity gains, also via OXR1 reduction. Limitations: research analogues, in vitro; relevance to a cosmetic ingredient is conceptual. [3]
  • Kim H.-G. et al., 2019, Applied Biological Chemistry 62:60 (DOI 10.1186/s13765-019-0468-7). Most skin-relevant in-vitro work: in B16F10 murine melanoma cells, PL at 3–6 µM significantly reduced α-MSH-stimulated melanin, downregulating MITF, tyrosinase, TRP-1, TRP-2 (no cytotoxicity at those doses), and reduced pigmentation in zebrafish embryos at ~1 µM. Limitations: mouse melanoma line + zebrafish, not human skin; cell-culture/aquatic, not topical human use. [5]
  • Raj L., Ide T., Gurkar A.U. et al., 2011, Nature 475:231–234 (DOI 10.1038/nature10167). The original discovery: PL selectively kills cancer cells by inhibiting GSTP1 and the ROS stress response; active in mouse tumor xenografts without harming normal tissue. Foundational to PL's "selective for stressed cells" reputation. Limitations: cancer biology, not aging/skin; the in-vivo arm is tumor models. [1]

Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

  • Raj et al., 2011 (mouse tumor arm). PL inhibited tumor growth in multiple mouse xenograft models irrespective of p53 status, with low systemic toxicity. Oncology, systemic, not skin. [1]
  • Go J. et al., 2018, International Journal of Molecular Medicine 42(4):1875–1884 (DOI 10.3892/ijmm.2018.3782; PMID 30066827). Oral PL 50 mg/kg/day for 8 weeks in 23-month-old female C57BL/6J mice improved novel-object recognition and nest-building, increased hippocampal neurogenesis (doublecortin⁺ cells) and synaptic signaling (NR2B/CaMKIIα/ERK1/2). Limitation (key): the authors did not confirm senescent-cell clearance as the mechanism; oral; brain, not skin. [6]
  • Imiquimod psoriasis model — Mishra/Singh et al., 2019, Cell Death & Disease 10:21 (DOI 10.1038/s41419-019-2212-y). In imiquimod-induced psoriasis-like dermatitis in BALB/c mice, topical PL reduced keratinocyte hyperproliferation by inducing ROS-mediated apoptosis (↓PCNA/Ki-67/Cyclin D1/Bcl-2; ↓p-STAT3, ↓p-Akt) and modulated epigenetic marks. This is the only "topical PL on mammalian skin" study — but it's a disease/hyperproliferation model where killing dividing keratinocytes is the goal, NOT an anti-aging benefit; mouse; high local doses. [14]
  • Inflammation/arthritis & asthma models. PL (2.5–10 mg/kg) reduced TNF-α/IL-1β/IL-6 via NF-κB/OPG-RANKL in rat adjuvant arthritis; reduced ovalbumin-induced asthma — supporting an anti-inflammatory profile systemically, not in skin. [12][13]

Tier 3 — Human ORAL

  • None identified. No human clinical trials of oral piperlongumine for aging, skin, or any indication were found. (Contrast with spermidine/fisetin, which have human oral RCTs.) The human translation gap is total at this tier. [no source — absence]

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters for a skincare brand)

  • None. Zero. No human topical skin study of piperlongumine — RCT or otherwise — was found. There is no published human penetration, efficacy, or tolerability data for PL applied to human skin. Any topical claim would be 100% extrapolated from cell culture, mice, and other-format data. [no source — absence]

Bottom line by tier: Proven → PL selectively kills cultured senescent fibroblasts (modest selectivity) and cultured cancer cells; reduces pigment-enzyme expression in melanoma cells/zebrafish; improves cognition in aged mice (mechanism unconfirmed). Not proven for any human use, and entirely absent for human topical skin.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • In favor: Small (~317 g/mol) and lipophilic → on physicochemical grounds it should partition into the stratum corneum better than charged/large actives (peptides, polyamines, proteins).
  • Against / unknown: There are no published human (or even ex-vivo human skin) penetration data for PL. Lipophilicity helps SC uptake but can also mean it lodges in the SC rather than reaching viable epidermis/dermis where senescent fibroblasts live.
  • The reactivity problem dominates. PL is a covalent Michael acceptor that reacts with thiols (glutathione, cysteine residues) [7][8]. Skin is rich in glutathione and protein thiols, so a meaningful fraction of any applied PL is likely to be quenched/consumed at the surface and upper epidermis before reaching target cells — and that same reactivity is what creates sensitization risk (§7).
  • Aqueous formulation is hard. Poor water solubility means it needs solvents/oils/encapsulation; that adds cost and stability complexity (§6).
  • Practical read: Penetration to dermal senescent cells at an effective, selective concentration is plausible in theory but completely unvalidated, and the covalent-thiol chemistry actively works against clean delivery. This is a weaker bioavailability story than it first appears.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: No established topical human concentration. In-vitro actives were 3–8 µM (≈1–2.5 ppm) for senolysis/pigment and ~5 µM for the OXR1 mechanism — culture doses, not formulation guidance, and the senolytic selectivity window is narrow (senescent vs normal EC₅₀ only ~2.5–3×), so an "effective" dose is uncomfortably close to a "kills-everything" dose. There is no safe-and-effective topical dose-response to cite.
  • pH: No skin-specific data. The amide/enone is most stable around neutral; strongly acidic or basic conditions risk hydrolysis. Formulate near skin-neutral and test.
  • Stability: A real concern. The conjugated Michael-acceptor system is chemically reactive — prone to reacting with nucleophiles (amines, thiols, some preservatives, proteins/extracts), to oxidation, and likely to color/odor drift. The very feature that makes it bioactive makes it hard to keep intact and inert in a finished product. Expect to need encapsulation/anhydrous or oil-phase systems, antioxidants, and rigorous stability + compatibility testing. Limited public cosmetic-stability data — treat as a known unknown.
  • Pairings (conceptual only): In research it synergizes with ABT-263 (navitoclax) as a senolytic — irrelevant/inappropriate for cosmetics (ABT-263 is a chemo drug). For a narrative "senotherapy" stack you'd more sensibly pair safer, better-evidenced actives (e.g., niacinamide, peptides) and keep PL as a minor talking-point ingredient — not co-formulate it with other reactive electrophiles or thiol donors.
  • Conflicts: Avoid raw co-formulation with thiol/amine-rich actives and reducing agents (they'll react with it); watch preservative compatibility; vitamin-C and other redox-active partners may interact unpredictably given PL's pro-oxidant chemistry.
  • Sensory: No established cosmetic sensory profile; as a plant alkaloid it may carry slight color; oil-phase delivery will drive texture.

7. Safety

Honest framing: this is a bioactive, covalently-reactive, pro-oxidant cytotoxic molecule first characterized as a cancer-cell killer. That is a very different safety starting point from a humectant or a vitamin. The cosmetic safety dossier is essentially not built.

  • Irritation / cytotoxicity: At its active concentrations PL kills cells (that is the point). The senolytic selectivity window is narrow (~2.5–3×), so the margin between "clears senescent cells" and "damages healthy cells" is thin. No human topical irritation data exist. The psoriasis study used PL because it kills proliferating skin cells. [2][14]
  • Sensitization: Elevated theoretical risk. Strong Michael-acceptor electrophiles that covalently bind skin proteins are a classic structural alert for contact allergy/skin sensitization (haptenation). No human HRIPT/patch data exist to reassure here — this would have to be generated, and the structure-based prior is unfavorable. [7][8]
  • Genotoxicity: PL is reported as genotoxic in some assays (DNA-damage induction is part of its anticancer mechanism), which is a meaningful flag for a leave-on cosmetic. However, a 2024 in-vivo study of piplartine (from P. tuberculatum) at oral 25/50/100 mg/kg in mice found no DNA fragmentation, maternal, or embryo-fetal toxicity — so the data are mixed/context-dependent, not settled. Both signals must be disclosed. [15][16]
  • Pro-oxidant / mitochondrial: PL raises ROS and disrupts mitochondrial membrane potential to drive apoptosis — the opposite of an "antioxidant" benefit at active doses. Marketing it as an antioxidant is mechanistically backwards. [6][7]
  • Sun: No photostability or phototoxicity data on skin; given the conjugated chromophore and redox activity, photostability/photoreactivity should be tested before any leave-on/daytime use. [no source — absence]
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: Mixed signal — older literature flags genotoxic/cytotoxic potential; the 2024 mouse study found no teratogenicity at oral doses up to 100 mg/kg. With no human data, default to caution / avoid in pregnancy copy. [15][16]
  • Sourcing/allergen: Plant-derived (Piper longum); pepper-family botanical — relevant for botanical-allergen-aware labeling. Purity/standardization of natural isolates varies.
  • Overall: The safety burden of proof for a reactive cytotoxic senolytic in a leave-on cosmetic is high and currently unmet. This is arguably the strongest argument against near-term use.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic ingredient status: Weak/unclear. No formal INCI assignment surfaced in the cosmetic nomenclature sources checked; it appears in a chemical-catalog "cosmetic agents" listing (antioxidant/bleaching/skin-conditioning) but that is not a regulatory authorization. Before any use we'd need to confirm cosmetic legality and nomenclature per target market (EU CosIng, US, etc.) — do not assume it is an approved cosmetic ingredient. [9][10]
  • The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame): This molecule is the deepest claims trap in this dossier. "Senolytic," "kills senescent cells," "clears zombie cells," "anti-aging at the cellular level," "removes aged cells from your skin" are textbook structure/function (drug) claims — and worse, PL's literature is oncology/cell-death, so importing it into cosmetic copy is doubly risky. The entire "kills senescent cells" value proposition is, by nature, a drug-like claim.
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language (and even these are a stretch without our own data): "derived from long pepper (Piper longum), studied in longevity science," "inspired by senescence research," "helps improve the look of uneven tone" (the pigment angle is the only skin-mechanism with even in-vitro support, and still needs substantiation).
  • ⚠️ Risky / needs substantiation or legal review (most PL marketing): "senolytic," "clears senescent/zombie cells," "selectively kills aged skin cells," "reverses cellular aging," "antioxidant" (mechanistically wrong — it's a pro-oxidant), "boosts collagen." None are supported for human topical use.
  • Substantiation reality: There is no human topical literature to lean on. Any performance claim would require our own finished-formula human testing and a resolved safety/sensitization/genotoxicity position first. See ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Forms/suppliers: Available as a research chemical (Sigma-Aldrich/Merck, Cayman Chemical, Selleck, TargetMol, MedKoo, BOC Sciences) at ≥97% HPLC purity; also extractable from Piper longum. Cosmetic-grade supply chain is essentially undeveloped — these are reagent/research SKUs, not cosmetic raws with COAs, regulatory dossiers, and tox packages. [9][10][11]
  • Cost tier: Effectively high/uncertain for cosmetic use — research-grade alkaloid pricing plus the cost of building a cosmetic-grade, stabilized, encapsulated, safety-tested supply. Not a drop-in commodity active.
  • Supply/standardization risk: Botanical isolates vary in purity; reactive chemistry complicates storage/transport; no established cosmetic-grade standardization. High friction to source responsibly.

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • "Natural senolytic" halo. PL is genuinely a landmark research senolytic — but that is cell-culture and mouse science. The leap to "senolytic skincare" is unearned: no human skin data of any kind.
  • The selectivity is modest. The headline "selectively kills senescent cells" rests on only ~2.5–3× EC₅₀ selectivity in one fibroblast line [2] — a narrow window, not a clean scalpel.
  • "Antioxidant" is backwards. Catalogs and some marketing call PL an antioxidant; at active doses it is a pro-oxidant that raises ROS and disrupts mitochondria to kill cells [1][7]. Selling it as an antioxidant misrepresents the mechanism.
  • Oncology pedigree cuts both ways. Its fame comes from selectively killing cancer cells and being genotoxic [1][15] — exciting for a drug, a red flag for a leave-on cosmetic.
  • The cognition mouse study doesn't prove senolysis. Go 2018 improved aged-mouse cognition but did not confirm senescent-cell clearance as the cause [6] — so even the in-vivo "anti-aging" headline is mechanistically unproven.
  • The only topical-skin study is a disease model. Topical PL "worked" on psoriasis by killing hyperproliferating keratinocytes [14] — that is cytotoxic debulking, not skin rejuvenation.

11. Verdict for the brand

❌→⚠️ LEAN SKIP (at most a far-future, narrative-only "watch" ingredient). One-line: Piperlongumine is a scientifically fascinating research senolytic, but for a topical skincare brand it is the wrong stage and the wrong risk profile today — no human skin evidence, no settled INCI/cosmetic status, a reactive Michael-acceptor electrophile with sensitization and genotoxicity flags, narrow selectivity, and a value proposition ("kills senescent cells") that is inherently a drug claim. Use it, if at all, only as an educational talking point about where senolytic skin science is heading — never as a hero or a substantiated active.

Longevity-relevance note: PL has real longevity-science pedigree within the senolytics field (Nature 2011 cancer paper; the canonical Aging 2016 senolytic discovery; the Aging Cell 2018 OXR1 mechanism), which gives it genuine story value for a skin-longevity brand that wants to talk about cellular senescence. But the gap between "interesting senolytic in a dish/mouse" and "safe, effective, legal topical active" is enormous here — larger than for spermidine or fisetin. If we ever want a senolytic narrative ingredient with a less alarming safety profile and at least some skin-relevant data, fisetin or quercetin are far better-positioned candidates to research next; PL is better admired from a distance.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified: 2026-06-19 (independent re-check of every load-bearing citation against the primary literature).

Overall grade: D — unproven topically / narrative-only.

On the brand's scale (A = strong human-topical RCT; B = moderate human-topical; C = weak/indirect; D = unproven topically/hype), piperlongumine sits squarely at D for any topical-skincare use. There is zero human evidence of any kind (oral or topical) and zero human skin data. The entire reputation rests on (a) in-vitro senolysis of one cultured fibroblast line (WI-38), (b) cultured-cancer-cell and mouse-tumor oncology, (c) one cultured-melanoma + zebrafish pigment study, and (d) oral-mouse cognition and systemic anti-inflammatory models. The lone "topical on mammalian skin" study is a mouse psoriasis (disease/hyperproliferation) model, not an anti-aging readout. The existing dossier already states this clearly and accurately — this section confirms it rather than correcting a rosy picture.

Every benefit relevant to a topical anti-aging serum is borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral data. There is no human-topical column to grade.

Industry-funded or tiny?

Not the failure mode here. The key papers are academic (Mayo/Scripps-adjacent senolytics groups, Broad Institute, university labs) and not cosmetic-industry-funded — so there is no commercial-bias inflation to discount. The problem is the opposite: the science is genuine but entirely pre-clinical and oncology-rooted. Sample sizes are not the issue (these are cell/animal mechanistic studies, not underpowered human trials); the issue is stage and model, not funding or n.

Main caveats

  1. No human data at all — the human translation gap is total (Tier 3 and Tier 4 are empty).
  2. Modest selectivity — the "selective senolytic" headline rests on a ~2.5–3× window in one cell line; that is a narrow margin, not a scalpel.
  3. Reactive electrophile — covalent Michael acceptor (thiol/protein-binding) → structural alert for sensitization, plus formulation-stability and clean-delivery problems.
  4. Pro-oxidant, not antioxidant — at active doses PL raises ROS; any "antioxidant" framing is mechanistically backwards.
  5. Oncology/genotoxicity pedigree — fame comes from killing cancer cells and DNA-damage signals; genotoxicity evidence is mixed (one 2024 mouse study clean, earlier assays positive).
  6. No settled INCI / cosmetic-grade supply — regulatory and sourcing status is unbuilt.
  7. "Kills senescent cells" is inherently a drug claim — the value proposition itself is a structure/function claim.

Claims to soften / never make

  • ❌ Never: "senolytic," "clears/kills senescent (zombie) cells," "reverses cellular aging," "selectively removes aged skin cells" — drug claims, unproven for human topical use.
  • ❌ Never: "antioxidant" — mechanistically wrong (pro-oxidant at active doses).
  • ❌ Never: "boosts collagen," "repairs the barrier" — no data.
  • ⚠️ At most: "derived from long pepper (Piper longum), studied in longevity/senescence research" framed as narrative/heritage, with no implied finished-product benefit — and even the pigment/tone angle (the only mechanism with any skin-cell support) would need our own human substantiation.

Verdict

The dossier's existing call — LEAN SKIP / narrative-only "watch" ingredient — is fully supported by the evidence and, if anything, was already appropriately harsh. Grade D for topical skincare. No softening of the skip recommendation is warranted; two minor citation-accuracy fixes (tyrosinase wording in §2; reference [15] scope) were applied above. Fisetin/quercetin remain better-positioned senolytic-narrative candidates than PL.
