Ingredient: Polypodium Leucotomos Extract (PLE / "Fernblock") — (INCI: Polypodium Leucotomos Leaf Extract)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ⬜ considering / ☑ shortlisted (as supporting/conditional, not hero) / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Sunscreen / photoprotection filters & adjuncts (antioxidant photoprotective adjunct — not a UV filter in the regulatory sense)

One-line bottom line: The serious, repeatable human evidence for PLE is almost entirely for the ORAL capsule form (Fernblock / Heliocare). Evidence for TOPICAL PLE in finished cosmetics is thin — mostly in-vitro, decades-old, or as a sunscreen "booster" measured on plastic plates, not skin. Treat any topical anti-aging claim as largely borrowed from the oral data.

1. Identity

  • Common / trade names: Polypodium leucotomos extract (PLE / PL); branded hydrophilic extract Fernblock® (Cantabria Labs / IFC Group), sold orally as Heliocare. Older European drug name: Anapsos (used for psoriasis since the 1970s–80s). Sometimes conflated with Calaguala (a related fern preparation) — not identical.
  • INCI (cosmetic/topical): Polypodium Leucotomos Leaf Extract (also seen as Polypodium Leucotomos Extract). [Source 12]
  • Source organism: Polypodium leucotomos (a.k.a. Phlebodium aureum), a tropical fern native to Central/South America. The standardized aqueous (hydrophilic) extract is what carries the clinical data. [Sources 1, 13]
  • Key actives / markers: A mix of phenolic acids (HPLC-identified): ferulic acid, p-coumaric acid, caffeic acid, chlorogenic acid (3-caffeoylquinic acid), vanillic acid — predominantly cinnamic-acid–class polyphenols. Ferulic and caffeic acids show the strongest antioxidant potency in absorption models. [Source 1]
  • Important caveat: "PLE" is not one standardized substance. Extraction method changes the polyphenol profile and efficacy; the clinical evidence base is overwhelmingly for the Fernblock® preparation specifically, and does not automatically transfer to a generic "Polypodium leucotomos leaf extract" sourced for a cosmetic. [Source 6]

2. Class & mechanism

PLE is an antioxidant / biological-response photoprotective adjunct, not a sunscreen filter. It does absorb some UV, but its main action is downstream biology, not blocking photons. Documented mechanisms (mostly from in-vitro + animal + ex-vivo work): [Sources 1, 2, 5]

  • ROS scavenging: inhibits UV-induced generation/release of reactive oxygen species; preserves endogenous antioxidant enzymes (SOD, glutathione peroxidase, glutathione-S-transferase) and raises plasma ORAC (oral).
  • DNA protection: reduces cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers (CPDs) and 8-OH-dG; reduces UVA-driven mitochondrial "common deletions"; supports p53 activation and inhibits aberrant epidermal proliferation (cyclin D1, PCNA).
  • Anti-inflammatory / signaling: partially inhibits UV-activated NF-κB and AP-1; suppresses COX-2.
  • ECM / anti-photoaging (in-vitro): inhibits MMP-1/MMP-2/MMP-9, upregulates TIMP-1/TIMP-2, and stimulates type I, III, V collagen deposition in fibroblasts; modulates TGF-β.
  • Immune protection: preserves epidermal Langerhans cells; interferes with cis-urocanic acid isomerization (a UV immunosuppression trigger).

Mechanistically attractive across many aging pathways. The honest caveat: most of this is cell/animal data, and the route that delivered it clinically is oral, not topical.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core of this dossier)

Tier 1 — In-vitro (cell/biochemical)

Robust and broad. Demonstrates ROS scavenging, MMP inhibition, TIMP/collagen upregulation, NF-κB/AP-1/COX-2 suppression, CPD reduction, fibroblast/keratinocyte protection. - Philips, Gonzalez et al. / Parrado et al., 2016 — mechanistic review collating fibroblast collagen (I, III, V) stimulation and MMP-1/2 inhibition + TIMP upregulation in-vitro. [Source 1] - Gomes et al. (Fernblock vs UVA + benzo[a]pyrene pollutant), 2022 — protection against combined photo-pollution oxidative damage in cells. [Source 11] - Limitation: cell models ≠ finished topical product on living skin. These results justify interest, not claims.

Tier 2 — Animal

  • Hairless / XPC mouse models: oral/topical PLE reduced UV-induced tumor formation, CPDs, increased p53 activation. Supports a photocarcinogenesis-prevention hypothesis. [Source 1]
  • Limitation: doses/exposures not translatable to a cosmetic; cancer endpoints are out of scope for a cosmetic brand and must not be implied in copy.

Tier 3 — Human, ORAL (this is where the real clinical weight is)

Study n Design Dose Key result Limitation
González et al., 1997 (Photodermatol Photoimmunol Photomed) [S2] 21 Open, oral and topical varied ↑MED ~2.8×, ↑MPD 2.75–6.8×; protected Langerhans cells (both routes) Small, open-label, old
Middelkamp-Hup et al., 2004 (JAAD) [S3] 9 Within-subject, oral 7.5 mg/kg ↓erythema (p<.01); fewer sunburn cells, CPDs (p<.001), mast cells, proliferating cells Very small; surrogate endpoints
Middelkamp-Hup et al., 2004 (PUVA phototoxicity, JAAD) [S9] — Oral 7.5 mg/kg ↓psoralen-UVA phototoxicity, pigmentation, damage Small
Nestor, Berman, Swenson, 2015 (JCAD 8(2):19–23) [S8] 40 RCT, oral 240 mg ×2/day, 60 days ↑MED (PLE subjects ~22× more likely to show increased MED) & ↓erythema intensity; clean safety labs Surrogate endpoints; n=40; verify funding/industry ties
Kohli et al., 2017 (JAAD) [S4] 22 Clinical, oral PLE ↓UVB-induced changes clinically in 17/22, histologically in 22/22; some effect across VIS/UVA1/UVB No placebo arm reported; small
Ahmed et al., 2013 (JAMA Dermatol) — melasma RCT [S5] 40 (33 completed) Double-blind RCT + SPF55 240 mg ×3/day, 12 wk Negative: melanin index 28.8% vs 13.8% placebo, NS (p=.14); MASI NS (p=.62) Underpowered (16 vs 17 completers); sunscreen drove most benefit; PLE capsules donated by manufacturer (Cantabria)
Goh et al., 2018 (JCAD) — melasma Asian pilot [S10] small DB placebo-controlled pilot PLE Modest benefit signal Pilot, underpowered
Keršmanc et al., 2025 (Nutrients) [S7] 54 (52 completed) DB RCT 300 mg PLE + red orange + vits A/C/D/E, 8 wk ↑MED +23.8% (p<.05), ↓erythema −46.2% (p<.0001) at 8 wk; no pigment effect Combination product — can't isolate PLE; phototype I–III Caucasian-only; UVB-only; funded by the supplement maker (Tosla d.o.o.)

Systematic reviews of the oral data: - Prado, Teplitz, Winkelmann, Del Rosso, Rigel, 2018 (SKIN J Cutan Med): 18 studies (n=5–61); most showed ↑MED/photoprotection but mostly observational, not RCTs; benefit/safety "supported only for the Fernblock® formulation." [Source 6] - Zundell, Katz, Shah, Burshtein, Rigel, Zakria, 2025 (J Drugs Dermatol): 21 articles incl. 11 RCTs; concludes "strong therapeutic potential with an encouraging safety profile" as an adjuvant. [Source 8]

Honest read of the oral tier: Consistent short-term photoprotection signal (higher MED, less erythema, less acute DNA/histologic damage). But studies are small, many lack placebo control, endpoints are surrogate (MED/erythema, not wrinkles/long-term photoaging), and the one rigorously double-blinded anti-aging-adjacent clinical endpoint (melasma) was statistically negative. No long-term RCT shows reduced wrinkling or measured photoaging endpoints over time.

Tier 4 — Human, TOPICAL (the tier that matters for a topical brand — and it is weak)

  • González et al., 1997 [S2]: included a topical arm showing protection of Langerhans cells / phototoxicity — but old, open-label, and not a finished cosmetic.
  • Topical erythema inhibition reported at ~0.1% (w/v) in early work cited by the Parrado 2016 review [S1] — primary-source dosing detail is dated and not a modern RCT.
  • Aguilera et al., 2021 (Front Med) — "booster effect": 1% (w/w) PLE added to four sunscreen bases raised in-vitro SPF (+14.2%), UVA-PF (+9.3%), and immune-protection factors. Critically, this was measured on PMMA plates (ISO 24443), entirely in-vitro — no human or animal skin. Equally critical: this study was funded by Cantabria Labs (the Fernblock manufacturer); one author works in Cantabria's R&D department and S. González is disclosed as a Cantabria consultant — so the single most-cited "topical works" data point is both in-vitro AND industry-generated by the brand that sells the ingredient. [Source 14]
  • INCI databases list PLE leaf extract as a topical "antioxidant / skin-protecting" agent in 50+ products, but multiple dermatology references explicitly state "there is no conclusive evidence for its effectiveness or effective concentration in topical application," and that oral PLE reaches a depth topical antioxidants do not. [Sources 12, 13]

Bottom line for topical: There is no robust modern human RCT demonstrating that topical PLE in a finished cosmetic reduces photoaging, wrinkles, or even erythema at cosmetically realistic levels. The marketed topical story is borrowed from oral data and in-vitro booster experiments. This is the single most important honesty point in this file.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • The clinically validated route is oral — phenolic acids absorb ~70–100% in intestinal models, are metabolized via CYP450, and raise plasma antioxidant capacity. [Source 1]
  • For topical, penetration of the relevant polyphenol fraction from a finished emulsion is not well characterized in peer-reviewed human data. Many constituents are water-soluble small phenolics whose skin permeation/retention from a cosmetic vehicle is unproven for efficacy. Dermatology references note oral works "at a deeper cellular level not achieved with topically applied antioxidants." [Sources 12, 13] — treat topical penetration as an open question, not a solved one.

6. Formulation notes

  • Effective concentration (topical): Not established by human efficacy data. In-vitro/booster work used 1% (w/w); old erythema work cited ~0.1% (w/v). There is no validated "use level for benefit" in a leave-on cosmetic.
  • pH / stability: It's a polyphenol-rich aqueous extract — phenolic acids are oxidation-prone; expect colour drift (often amber/brown) and the need for antioxidant protection and opaque/airless packaging. Specific stability data for cosmetic finished goods is supplier-dependent and not well published.
  • Pairings: Conceptually complements other antioxidants (vitamin C, vitamin E, ferulic acid — note ferulic is literally one of its own constituents) and pairs naturally with SPF as an adjunct, never a replacement. [Source 13]
  • Conflicts: No well-documented topical incompatibilities, but high-polyphenol extracts can interact with metal ions / certain preservatives and can stain/discolor formulas.
  • Sensory: Aqueous extract; can contribute color and a faint herbal note depending on grade and level.

7. Safety

  • Oral safety is genuinely good. Across pooled human use, adverse events were mild and rare — pooled estimate ~16/1016 (≈2%), mostly mild GI upset and pruritus; no serious adverse events. [Sources 6, 8, 15]
  • Granular safety study (Nestor/Winscott 2015): 240 mg ×2/day for 56 days, 40 adults — no changes in physical exam, vitals, hematology, metabolic panel, or PT/PTT. [Source 8]
  • Long use history: oral PLE used in Europe since the 1980s with a clean reported record. [Source 13]
  • Topical irritation/sensitization: generally regarded as low-risk as a cosmetic botanical, but no strong dedicated topical safety dataset — botanical extracts carry baseline sensitization potential; patch-test-grade data should be requested from any supplier.
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: insufficient data — avoid / advise caution (this caution is for the oral supplement; standard cosmetic caution applies topically). [Sources 13, 15]
  • Children (<18): not tested. [Source 13]
  • Interactions (oral): theoretical caution with immunosuppressants/corticosteroids and possibly cardiac meds; not formally studied. [Sources 13, 15]
  • Sourcing/sustainability: wild-harvested tropical fern (Central/South America). Wild-collection raises sustainability/traceability questions; prefer cultivated/standardized, audited supply. [Source 13]

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • In a topical cosmetic, PLE is a cosmetic ingredient (antioxidant / skin-conditioning), NOT an approved UV filter. It cannot be labeled or implied as sunscreen/SPF on its own. In the US, sunscreen actives are OTC drugs on the monograph and PLE is not one. [Sources 12, 13]
  • Oral Fernblock/Heliocare is sold as a dietary supplement, not a drug — that distinction (and its evidence) does not license drug-like topical claims.
  • Do NOT claim (drug/over-reach): "sun protection / SPF," "prevents/treats skin cancer," "treats melasma," "replaces sunscreen," "repairs DNA," "boosts collagen by X%," "anti-cancer," "photo-immune protection."
  • Safer cosmetic language (if we use it topically): "antioxidant-rich fern extract," "helps defend the look of skin against environmental/oxidative stress," "supports skin's resilience to environmental aggressors," "visibly." Anything stronger needs substantiation/legal review.
  • The cleanest compliant story is to position any photoprotection benefit as oral-adjunct context / antioxidant defense, and to keep our actual anti-aging hero claims on better-evidenced actives (retinoids, niacinamide, ceramides, vitamin C, and SPF itself).

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Branded benchmark: Heliocare (Cantabria Labs), 240 mg capsules, 60-count, ~US retail commonly $25–$35/bottle (oral) — relevant only if we ever explore an oral adjunct SKU. [Source 16]
  • Cosmetic raw material: generic "Polypodium leucotomos leaf extract" is available to formulators but is not the Fernblock clinical material — buying the INCI does not buy the evidence. Expect mid-tier botanical-extract pricing; cost is not the constraint, evidence-to-claim risk is.
  • Demand standardization (defined polyphenol/ferulic markers), COA, allergen + sustainability/traceability documentation, and any topical safety/penetration data the supplier holds.

10. Hype vs. evidence

Marketing claim seen in the wild Reality
"Sunscreen in a pill / replaces SPF" False. Even proponents (DermNet) say it's an added layer, never a substitute. [S13]
"Topical fern extract = clinical photoprotection" Overreach. Human topical RCT evidence is essentially absent; data is in-vitro/booster + oral. [S12, S14]
"Boosts collagen, reverses photoaging" Collagen/MMP data is in-vitro fibroblast only; no human topical wrinkle/photoaging RCT. [S1]
"Proven for melasma" The one rigorous oral RCT was negative (p=.14). [S5]
"Clinically proven antioxidant defense" (topical) The clinical proof is for the oral Fernblock product, not topical cosmetics.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ CONDITIONAL / SUPPORTING — not a hero, and only with disciplined claims.

  • As a topical hero ingredient: skip. The human topical RCT evidence does not exist; we'd be borrowing oral data, which is exactly the credibility trap this brand is built to avoid.
  • As a supporting topical antioxidant in an SPF-adjacent or environmental-defense product: conditional yes — only with cosmetic-grade "antioxidant defense" language, standardized material, and no SPF/photoprotection claims.
  • As a potential future oral adjunct SKU (Heliocare-style): the strongest evidence lives here, but it's outside a lean topical-first lineup and carries supplement-regulatory overhead — park it.

Longevity relevance: Mechanistically it touches several real skin-aging hallmarks (oxidative stress, photoaging, ECM/MMP balance, inflammaging) and photoaging is the #1 evidence-based driver of skin aging — so the theme is on-strategy. But for a topical longevity brand, the evidence is too route-mismatched to lead with. Keep SPF + retinoid + barrier lipids as the photoaging-prevention backbone; treat PLE as an optional, honestly-framed supporting antioxidant at most.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Independent adversarial review, 2026-06-19. Each headline claim was checked against the cited primary source for: (a) route — human/topical vs. borrowed in-vitro/animal/oral; (b) does the study exist and say what's claimed; (c) sample size and industry funding. Citations were spot-verified against PubMed/PMC/JAMA/Frontiers full text.

Overall evidence grade — for TOPICAL anti-aging use: D (essentially unproven topically / hype)

This grade is route-specific and is the grade that matters for this brand, which is topical-first. There is no modern human topical RCT showing that PLE in a finished leave-on cosmetic reduces wrinkles, photoaging, or even erythema at realistic concentrations. The entire topical case rests on (1) in-vitro fibroblast/cell data, (2) a single in-vitro PMMA-plate sunscreen-booster study that is also manufacturer-funded, and (3) one old (1997), open-label, n=21 topical arm. That is the definition of weak/indirect-to-unproven.

For context, the ORAL form would grade roughly B−/C+: a consistent short-term photoprotection signal (↑MED, ↓erythema) across several small studies, but on surrogate endpoints (MED/erythema, not wrinkles), frequently underpowered, often without placebo control, and — see below — meaningfully entangled with the manufacturer. The one rigorously double-blind anti-aging-adjacent endpoint (melasma) was statistically negative. The dossier's pre-existing framing of "oral strong, topical thin" is directionally correct; this review tightens the conflict-of-interest and grading detail.

What checked out (the file was largely honest)

  • All 16 citations are real and correctly attributed to genuine papers, and the headline numbers match the sources (melasma p=.14 / p=.62 negative result; Keršmanc +23.8% MED / −46.2% erythema; Aguilera +14.2% SPF / +9.3% UVA-PF in-vitro; Parrado collagen/MMP data is explicitly in-vitro fibroblast).
  • The dossier does not smuggle in-vitro/animal/oral data as if it were human-topical in its bottom-line statements; the tiered structure correctly quarantines each route. The melasma "negative" claim is accurate, not spun.
  • The "no human topical RCT" honesty point is correct and verified.

Main caveats / what this review changed

  1. Industry funding was under-disclosed. Three of the most load-bearing studies are tied to the ingredient's maker: - Aguilera 2021 [S14] (the booster study) — funded by Cantabria Labs; an author is in Cantabria R&D; S. González is a Cantabria consultant. (Was unflagged; now flagged.) - Ahmed 2013 melasma RCT [S5] — PLE capsules donated by the manufacturer (Cantabria). (Now flagged. Note: this doesn't undermine the result — it was negative despite manufacturer involvement, which arguably strengthens the negative read.) - Keršmanc 2025 [S7] — funded by the supplement maker Tosla d.o.o. (Now flagged.)
  2. Recurring conflicted investigator. Salvador González is an author/contributor across S1 (Parrado review), S2 (1997), S4 (Kohli), and S14 (Aguilera) and is a disclosed Cantabria consultant. Much of the favorable PLE literature is not investigator-independent. The Parrado 2016 review [S1] declares "no conflict of interest," but its senior author is the same Fernblock-associated investigator — treat its enthusiasm accordingly.
  3. Citation attribution fix. S8 was cited as "Nestor / Winscott et al., 2015." The actual authors are Nestor, Berman, Swenson (JCAD 2015;8(2):19–23). "Winscott" appears spurious and has been corrected; the result itself (RCT, n=40, ↑MED) is real — the headline finding is that PLE subjects were ~22× more likely to show increased MED, which is a large odds ratio off a small n=40 and should not be over-read.
  4. Small samples throughout. Human studies cluster at n=9–54; only a handful are placebo-controlled RCTs, and the largest topical-relevant human data point is n=21 from 1997.
  5. Topical penetration remains unestablished — verified; no peer-reviewed human data characterizes efficacious skin delivery of the polyphenol fraction from a finished emulsion.

Claims that should be softened or dropped (beyond the file's own "Hype vs evidence" table)

  • Do not present the Keršmanc / Aguilera / Nestor results as independent corroboration of efficacy without noting they are manufacturer-funded (Keršmanc, Aguilera) or small surrogate-endpoint trials (Nestor).
  • Do not cite the Parrado 2016 review as neutral evidence — it is a manufacturer-associated narrative review of mostly in-vitro work.
  • Do not let "↑MED ~2.8×" (González 1997) read as a topical finding — that figure is the oral result; the topical arm only showed Langerhans-cell protection, open-label, n=21.
  • Keep the "↑23.8% MED" figure but cite it as p<0.05, not p<0.001 (the file overstated the significance level for the MED endpoint; erythema was p<0.0001, MED was p<0.05). Corrected in the Tier-3 table.
  • Any internal language calling topical PLE "clinically proven," "antioxidant defense (proven)," or "collagen-boosting" should be struck — the topical evidence does not support it.

Net verdict

The dossier's conclusion is sound and its skeptical posture is appropriate; the file was strong before review and is defensible after it. The corrections were about conflict-of-interest disclosure, one citation attribution, and one overstated p-value, not about a wrong bottom line. For a topical longevity brand, treat PLE as Grade D topically — at most an honestly-framed "antioxidant-rich fern extract" supporting player, never a hero, never with photoprotection/SPF/collagen claims.
