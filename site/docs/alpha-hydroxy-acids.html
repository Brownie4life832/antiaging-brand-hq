Ingredient: Alpha-Hydroxy Acids — Glycolic & Lactic Acid (+ salts)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ✅ shortlisted (SUPPORTING) · Class: Hydroxy acids & chemical exfoliants · Slug: alpha-hydroxy-acids

Exhaustive, citation-backed literature review for the brand research base. Evidence is graded by tier (in-vitro → animal → human oral → human topical), and benefits proven for topical human use are kept distinct from mechanistic or borrowed claims. Sources are numbered in §12; honest evidence-quality notes are throughout. Cosmetic-vs-drug claim lines flagged per ../../CLAUDE.md and ../../brand/glossary.md.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Alpha-hydroxy acids (AHAs). The two workhorses for this dossier are glycolic acid and lactic acid, plus their salts/derivatives.
  • INCI names:
  • Glycolic Acid (2-hydroxyacetic / hydroxyacetic acid; CAS 79-14-1). Smallest AHA (MW ~76 Da). Salts/derivatives in INCI: Sodium Glycolate, Ammonium Glycolate, Potassium Glycolate, Calcium Glycolate (partial-neutralization buffers); esters: Methyl/Ethyl/Propyl/Butyl Glycolate.
  • Lactic Acid (2-hydroxypropanoic acid; CAS 50-21-5). MW ~90 Da. Salts: Sodium Lactate (a humectant and core NMF component), Ammonium/Calcium/Potassium/TEA-Lactate; esters: Methyl/Ethyl/Lauryl/Myristyl/Cetyl Lactate.
  • Stereochemistry note (lactic acid): exists as L-(+), D-(−), and racemic D,L forms. L-lactic acid is the body's own isomer and the most-studied/most-active topically; isomer choice matters for barrier/ceramide effects (see §4).
  • Other AHAs in the class (context, not the focus): citric, malic, tartaric, mandelic (larger, gentler, lipophilic-leaning), and the related polyhydroxy acids (gluconolactone, lactobionic acid) which are gentler "next-gen" cousins.
  • Key forms used in finished product: free acid + partial neutralization to a target pH; or pre-neutralized salt blends ("buffered" AHA) that trade some potency for tolerability.

2. Class & Mechanism

AHAs are small, water-soluble organic acids. Their effects are pH- and concentration-dependent, driven mostly by the free-acid fraction:

  1. Corneodesmolysis (the core action). At low pH the protonated free acid penetrates the lower stratum corneum and weakens corneocyte cohesion — it disrupts the ionic/calcium-dependent bonds of corneodesmosomes and reduces desmosome/tonofilament density above the granular layer, accelerating desquamation (controlled shedding of dead surface cells). A leading mechanistic theory attributes this partly to calcium chelation: AHAs lower epidermal Ca²⁺ and strip Ca²⁺ from cadherins of desmosomes/adherens junctions, loosening cell adhesion. [6][7]
  2. Epidermal effects. Result is a thinner, more compact stratum corneum but a thicker viable epidermis, smoother surface, and more normalized differentiation. [8][2]
  3. Dermal/ECM effects (higher exposure, longer use). Histologic and gene-expression work shows increased collagen, glycosaminoglycans (GAGs)/hyaluronic acid, and improved elastic-fiber quality with sustained use — i.e., AHAs are not only surface exfoliants. [2][9][10][11]
  4. Hydration (lactic acid specifically). Lactic acid and its salt sodium lactate are components of the skin's own Natural Moisturizing Factor (NMF) and act as humectants; L-lactic acid also up-regulates keratinocyte ceramide synthesis (barrier lipids). [12]

Net: a dual identity — fast cosmetic resurfacing/radiance (surface) plus slower renewal/firming (dermal remodeling) at adequate exposure.

Honest read: AHAs map well onto the photoaging / texture / tone / hydration hallmarks. The trendy longevity hallmarks (senescence, mitochondria, glycation, autophagy) are not where AHA evidence lives — claims there would be overreach (§10).

4. Evidence Review by Tier

The core of this dossier. Read benefits as proven for topical human use only where the human-topical tier supports them. Mechanistic (in-vitro/explant) findings are suggestive, not proof of clinical benefit.

Tier 1 — In-vitro (cell/biochemical; mechanism only)

  • Moy, Howe & Moy (1996), Dermatol Surg. Normal human dermal fibroblast cultures incubated with glycolic acid for 24 h; collagen production indexed by radioactive hydroxyproline. Glycolic acid elevated collagen production — a "specific stimulatory effect." Limitation: monolayer fibroblasts, no barrier/penetration context; cannot predict in-vivo dose-response. [13]
  • Rawlings et al. (1996), Arch Dermatol Res. Keratinocyte/SC work: L-lactic acid increased keratinocyte ceramide synthesis in vitro and SC ceramide levels in vivo; D-lactic acid did not — isomer-specific barrier effect. (Spans in-vitro + in-vivo.) [12]
  • Calcium-chelation / corneodesmolysis mechanistic work underpinning §2. Limitation: explains action, not efficacy. [6][7]

Tier verdict: Consistent mechanistic support for collagen stimulation (glycolic) and ceramide/barrier support (L-lactic). Hypothesis-generating only.

Tier 2 — Ex-vivo / explant (human tissue, not living subject)

  • Narda, Trullas, Brown, Piquero-Casals, Granger & Fabbrocini (2021), J Cosmet Dermatol 20(2):513–521. Human skin explants, glycolic acid 8/10/15/25% adjusted to pH 4. All concentrations induced concentration-dependent desquamation and increased total collagen, with no significant change in pro-inflammatory TNF-α — i.e., renewal signal without an inflammatory signal. Limitation: ex-vivo tissue, not a clinical endpoint; industry-affiliated authors (ISDIN/Cantabria). [4]

Tier 3 — Animal

  • No pivotal mouse/rat efficacy studies drive AHA's human use; the field moved straight to human skin. Photo-co-carcinogenicity and UV-sensitivity work informing the FDA position used human skin / human subjects (see §7), not classic rodent efficacy models. No anti-aging benefit should be cited from animal data — there essentially isn't a meaningful animal efficacy tier here.

Tier 4 — Human, ORAL

  • Not applicable. AHAs are not used as oral anti-aging supplements; no benefit may be borrowed from an oral format. (Glycolic acid is a known metabolite/contaminant in some contexts but that's toxicology, not skincare benefit.)

Tier 5 — Human, TOPICAL — RCTs (highest-value tier)

  • Stiller, Bartolone, Stern, Smith, Kollias, Gillies & Drake (1996), Arch Dermatol 132(6):631–636. Double-blind, vehicle-controlled RCT, 74 women (67 completed), ages 40–70, moderately severe facial photodamage; 8% glycolic and 8% L-lactic creams twice daily for 22 weeks. Face: ≥1-grade improvement in 76% (glycolic), 71% (lactic) vs 40% (vehicle) (P<.05). Forearm: glycolic > vehicle for overall severity + sallowness; lactic > vehicle for overall severity, mottled hyperpigmentation, sallowness, roughness. Authors called benefits "modestly useful" and partial — honest, not dramatic. [1]
  • Newman et al. / "50% glycolic acid" (1996), double-blind vehicle-controlled. 41 volunteers, 50% glycolic peel-strength applied 5 min weekly × 4 weeks to half-face/forearms/hands. Significant reductions in rough texture and fine wrinkling, fewer solar keratoses, slight lightening of lentigines; histology showed SC thinning + epidermal thickening. Limitation: small n, short, peel-format (professional-strength) not daily leave-on. [14]
  • 5% glycolic daily formulation, double-blind RCT (~1998). ~75 volunteers, 5% glycolic vs placebo to face/neck × 3 months: trends toward improvement across photoaging measures (signal weaker at low daily %). Limitation: under-powered for low-dose effect. [15]
  • Melasma RCTs (serial peels + topicals). Recalcitrant-melasma RCT (n=28, 20 wks): serial glycolic peels + topical azelaic/adapalene beat topical-only on MASI. Recent RCT (n=60): 35% glycolic peel vs 4% retinol peel — both improved melasma, no significant between-group difference. Limitation: peels are clinical procedures, not consumer leave-on; combination designs muddy attribution. [16][17]
  • Network meta-analysis — Comparative efficacy of topical interventions for facial photoaging (2025), Scientific Reports. 23 RCTs, 3,905 participants. Retinoids (isotretinoin/tretinoin/retinol/tazarotene) led for fine/coarse wrinkles and hyperpigmentation; glycolic acid specifically reduced roughness but ranked lower on wrinkles and carried a higher adverse-event risk than tretinoin. Key strategic takeaway: AHAs are a texture/roughness/radiance play, not the strongest wrinkle/collagen actor — a retinoid is. [5]

Tier 5 — Human, TOPICAL — lower-tier clinical (pilot / open-label / histologic)

  • Ditre, Griffin, Murphy et al. (1996), J Am Acad Dermatol 34(2 Pt1):187–195. Pilot clinical + histologic + ultrastructural study; 25% glycolic, lactic, or citric lotion ~6 months. ~25% increase in epidermal + dermal thickness, increased acid mucopolysaccharides (GAGs), improved elastic-fiber quality, increased collagen density, no inflammation. Most-cited basis for "AHAs remodel dermis" — but it's a small pilot, open in design; treat as supportive, not definitive. [2]
  • Smith (1996), J Am Acad Dermatol — "Epidermal and dermal effects of topical lactic acid." 5% vs 12% L-lactic twice daily × 3 months. 12% increased epidermal + dermal firmness/thickness and improved smoothness/lines; 5% gave epidermal/clinical changes but no dermal change. Establishes a lactic dose-threshold for dermal benefit (~12%). [10]
  • Bernstein, Lee et al. (2001), Dermatol Surg 27(5):429–433. 20% glycolic lotion to forearm twice daily × 3 months: increased type I collagen mRNA and epidermal+dermal hyaluronic acid. Forearm (not face), surrogate molecular endpoints — mechanistic confirmation in living human skin. [9]
  • Comprehensive review — Almeman (2024), Clin Cosmet Investig Dermatol 17:1661–1685. Modern clinical + legal review concluding AHAs (glycolic/lactic) improve texture, tone, photoaging and have an acceptable safety profile within regulated limits; consolidates concentration/pH and regulatory guidance. Narrative review — inherits the limitations of its primary studies. [3]
  • Kornhauser, Coelho & Hearing reviews (2010/2012). Class reviews of AHA applications, mechanisms, and photoactivity/sun-exposed skin; useful for the nuanced UV story (§7). [18][19]

What is genuinely PROVEN for topical human use: smoother texture / reduced roughness; improved appearance of photodamage, sallowness, and mottled pigmentation; (lactic) hydration + barrier support. Suggested but softer: measurable collagen/GAG/thickness gains (pilot + molecular + explant evidence converge, but no large long-term RCT with biopsy-confirmed firmness as the primary endpoint). Not established topically: senescence clearance, mitochondrial/autophagy benefit, anti-glycation.

5. Topical Bioavailability / Penetration

  • Penetration is governed by molecular size, concentration, and especially pH/free-acid fraction. Glycolic (MW ~76) penetrates faster/deeper than lactic (~90) and far more than larger AHAs.
  • pH is the master dial. In-vitro percutaneous absorption of 4% glycolic: ~13.5% penetration at pH 2.0 vs ~2.2% at pH 3.8 — lowering pH dramatically increases delivery (and irritation). [20]
  • The SC has a pH gradient (acidic surface → ~neutral viable epidermis); as AHA diffuses inward it ionizes to a polar, less-permeant form — self-limiting depth.
  • Free acid vs salt: SC is far more permeable to the neutral free acid than to ionized salt. Buffering toward the pKa (glycolic ≈ 3.8; lactic ≈ 3.86) balances efficacy vs tolerability — at pH ≈ pKa roughly half is free acid, often the sweet spot of benefit-to-irritation. [21]
  • Systemic absorption is low for leave-on consumer concentrations; negligible for rinse-off. Supports the pregnancy-tolerability view (§7). [22]

6. Formulation

  • Effective leave-on concentrations:
  • Glycolic 5–10% (8% is the classic RCT dose; 5% works but signal is weaker; 10%+ trends to peel territory).
  • Lactic 5–12% (5% ≈ hydration/barrier + surface; ~12% needed for dermal firming per Smith 1996).
  • pH: typically ~3.5–4.0 for leave-on to keep adequate free acid while limiting irritation; glycolic at pH 4 still drives desquamation + collagen without raising TNF-α (Narda 2021). Going below ~3.5 sharply raises both efficacy and irritation/penetration.
  • Buffering/neutralization: partial neutralization with the corresponding salt (sodium glycolate / sodium lactate) sets pH and softens the irritation curve; sodium lactate doubles as a humectant.
  • Stability: AHAs are chemically robust, water-soluble, easy to formulate, long shelf life; main job is holding target pH.
  • Sensory: can sting/tingle on application; well-buffered formulas feel light and "polishing." Lactic feels more cushioned/hydrating than glycolic.
  • Pairs well with: humectants + barrier lipids — hyaluronic acid, glycerin, ceramides, niacinamide, panthenol (offset dryness/irritation and reinforce the barrier-repair thesis). PM use preferred (sun sensitivity).
  • Conflicts / sequencing: don't layer same-step with strong retinoids or high-strength L-ascorbic acid (compounding irritation; pH clashes) — alternate nights or AM/PM split. Avoid stacking multiple acids (AHA+BHA+retinoid) for sensitive skin. Benzoyl peroxide + AHA = irritation risk.

7. Safety

  • Irritation: the dominant adverse effect — dose/pH-dependent stinging, erythema, dryness, peeling. Risk climbs >10% and <pH 3.5. The 2025 network meta-analysis flagged glycolic among the higher adverse-event topicals vs tretinoin. [5]
  • UV / photosensitivity (the big one): topical AHAs increase skin sensitivity to UV during use and for up to ~1 week after stopping. FDA's review found exposure of control sites to 1 MED produced sunburn cells in 71/72 subjects; AHA use lowered the UV threshold. Daily broad-spectrum SPF is non-negotiable with AHAs, and a sunburn-alert label is recommended in the US (§8). [23][24]
  • Nuance: class reviews (Kornhauser) note the UV story is dose/formulation-dependent and that some hydroxy-acid formulations on sun-exposed skin can be made safer with added photoprotection/antioxidants — but the prudent, label-aligned default is "AHAs increase sun sensitivity; pair with SPF." [18][19]
  • Allergic contact dermatitis / sensitization: rare — glycolic/lactic are irritants, not meaningful sensitizers. Reactions are almost always irritant (pH/dose), not allergy. [25]
  • Pregnancy/lactation: topical glycolic/lactic are generally considered acceptable — low/minimal systemic absorption; ACOG views OTC topical glycolic as acceptable. Prudent guidance favors ≤10% glycolic / ≤5% lactic. Caveat: no dedicated pregnancy RCTs exist; recommendations are precautionary, so standard "consult your provider" boilerplate applies. [22][26]
  • Skin of color: glycolic peels are used in melasma in Fitzpatrick IV–VI but carry post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) risk if over-aggressive; lower %, careful pacing, and SPF mitigate. [16][17]
  • Sourcing / sustainability: glycolic acid is largely synthetic (also in sugarcane); lactic acid is commonly fermentation-derived (corn/beet sugar) — a credible natural/biotech-origin story. Both are commodity, low supply risk.

8. Regulatory & Claims

  • Cosmetic vs drug line: As leave-on cosmetics for appearance (smoothness, radiance, look of tone), AHAs sit in cosmetic territory. Claims to treat/prevent disease or alter skin structure/function (e.g. "increases collagen by X%," "treats sun damage," "peels away years") risk reclassification as an unapproved drug (US FDA). Peels at clinical strength are professional procedures, out of consumer-cosmetic scope.
  • US — FDA AHA guidance (final 2005): recommends a "Sunburn Alert": "This product contains an alpha hydroxy acid (AHA) that may increase your skin's sensitivity to the sun and particularly the possibility of sunburn. Use a sunscreen, wear protective clothing, and limit sun exposure while using this product and for a week afterwards." FDA's referenced CIR safe-use envelope for consumer AHAs: ≤10% AHA, final pH ≥3.5, with sun-protection directions/formulation. [23][24][27]
  • US — CIR Expert Panel: consumer use safe at ≤10% glycolic/lactic, pH ≥3.5; salon/professional up to 30%, pH ≥3.0. [27][3]
  • EU — SCCS/SCCNFP (precautionary): historically glycolic ≤4% at pH ≥3.8 and lactic ≤2.5% at pH ≥5 for consumer products — notably stricter than US. Confirm current EU status before any EU launch; SCCS continues to revisit acids. [28][3]
  • Claim-language cheat sheet (this ingredient):
  • ✅ Safe cosmetic: "gently exfoliates," "smooths and refines the look of texture," "reveals brighter, renewed-looking skin," "improves the appearance of dullness and uneven tone," "(lactic) hydrates and supports the moisture barrier."
  • ⚠️ Risky / drug-like — flag for substantiation/legal: "peels away years," "boosts/increases collagen by X%," "treats sun damage/melasma," "reverses aging." Any AHA SKU should carry the sun-sensitivity/SPF messaging (US) and respect EU limits.

9. Sourcing & Cost

  • Cost tier: inexpensive / commodity. Both are high-volume industrial acids; raw-material cost is a rounding error vs packaging and brand.
  • Suppliers: widely available cosmetic-grade from major specialty-chemical distributors; pre-buffered AHA blends and "encapsulated/time-release" glycolic systems exist at modest premium.
  • Supply risk: low. Lactic acid's fermentation (bio-based) origin is a genuine natural/sustainability narrative; glycolic is mostly synthetic.
  • On-thesis with "buy commodity bases, invest in brand": AHAs are exactly the kind of well-understood, cheap, effective active where money goes to formulation finesse (pH, buffering, sensory, barrier pairings) and brand — not exotic raw materials.

10. Hype vs. Evidence

  • "AHAs boost collagen" → Partly true, oversold. In-vitro (Moy), explant (Narda), molecular in-vivo (Bernstein), and pilot histology (Ditre) converge on a real ECM signal — but there is no large long-term RCT with biopsy-confirmed firmness as a primary endpoint, and the network meta-analysis ranks glycolic below retinoids for wrinkles. Frame as "supports renewal / the look of firmness," never a numeric collagen claim.
  • "Anti-aging at the cellular/longevity level" → Unsupported. No credible topical evidence AHAs clear senescent cells, fix mitochondria, induce autophagy, or reduce glycation/AGEs. Marketing that borrows "longevity science" language for AHAs is overreach.
  • "Natural fruit/milk acids = gentle" → Misleading. Potency tracks pH and concentration, not provenance; low-pH AHA is a real irritant and a genuine UV-sensitizer.
  • "No need for sunscreen / it's just exfoliation" → False and unsafe. AHAs measurably increase UV sensitivity (FDA). SPF pairing is mandatory, not optional.
  • "Higher % = better" → Diminishing returns + rising risk. Daily leave-on benefit plateaus while irritation/PIH climb; peel-strength is a clinical procedure, not a daily routine.

11. Verdict for the Brand

✅ SUPPORTING — A cheap, well-evidenced texture / radiance / tone (and, via lactic, hydration + barrier) active that complements — but does not outrank — a retinoid hero for wrinkles/collagen.

  • One-line reasoning: Strong topical-RCT evidence for smoothing and improving the appearance of photodamage and tone; favor L-lactic acid (gentler, humectant, ceramide-supporting, on-brand for a "kind-to-skin, barrier-respecting" premium line) as the lead AHA, with glycolic as an optional booster.
  • Longevity-relevance note: AHAs are a photoaging/surface-renewal play, not a longevity-mechanism actor. They earn their place on real, provable appearance benefits — do not position them under senescence/mitochondria/glycation framing. Pair any AHA SKU with mandatory SPF messaging (the single most evidence-based anti-aging step) and barrier ingredients to fit the barrier-repair thesis.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified: 2026-06-19 · Independent adversarial check of every headline efficacy claim against the primary literature. Each cited study was pulled and read for (a) evidence tier — is it HUMAN + TOPICAL, or quietly borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral?, (b) does the source exist and say what the dossier claims?, (c) is it industry-funded or very small?

Overall evidence grade: B (moderate human-topical)

Grade scale: A = strong human-topical RCT evidence; B = moderate human-topical; C = weak/indirect; D = essentially unproven topically / hype.

Why B, not A: The appearance/texture/radiance/tone benefits rest on genuine human-topical RCTs — but the anchor trial (Stiller 1996) is a single ~22-week study whose authors explicitly called the benefit "modestly useful," and the strongest recent synthesis (2025 network meta-analysis) ranks glycolic below retinoids for wrinkles and flags it for higher adverse-event risk. The deeper collagen/dermal-remodeling story rests on a small open-ish pilot + molecular surrogate endpoints + an industry-funded explant study — convergent and plausible, but not RCT-grade with a clinical firmness endpoint. So: solidly real, moderate, honest — a high B, not an A. The dossier's own internal grading (Strong/Moderate/Weak per hallmark) is well-calibrated and was largely confirmed.
