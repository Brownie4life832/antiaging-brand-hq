Ingredient: Beta-Glucan (INCI: Beta-Glucan; and source-specific variants — see below)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ✅ shortlisted (supporting active; conditional hero — see Verdict) Class: Humectants & hydration actives (also functions as a film-former, soothing/barrier active, and — depending on source — an immunomodulatory wound-healing agent)

1. Identity

  • Common name: Beta-glucan (β-glucan; "the molecule that holds 20x its weight in water" is the common marketing shorthand — caution, see Hype section).
  • What it is: A naturally occurring polysaccharide — a long chain of glucose (sugar) units — found in the cell walls of yeast, certain mushrooms/fungi, algae, and the bran of cereal grains (oat, barley). It is not a single molecule; structure, branching, molecular weight, and solubility vary enormously by source, and these differences materially change skin behavior.
  • Core structural distinction (this matters for evidence transfer):
  • Cereal β-glucans (oat, barley): linear chains of β-(1→3) and β-(1→4) linked glucose. Oat β-glucan is ~70% (1→4) / ~30% (1→3), water-soluble, very high MW (~1 × 10⁶ Da / 1000 kDa typical). This is the dominant skincare/cosmetic form (the anti-wrinkle and hydration data are mostly here).
  • Yeast / fungal β-glucans (Saccharomyces cerevisiae, Lentinula edodes, Ganoderma, Aureobasidium pullulans): β-(1→3) backbone with β-(1→6) side branches. This branched (1,3/1,6) structure is the immunologically active form — it is the ligand for the Dectin-1 receptor and drives the wound-healing/immune data.
  • Key forms / derivatives you will see on COA/INCI lists:
  • Beta-Glucan (generic INCI; usually oat- or yeast-derived).
  • Sodium Carboxymethyl Beta-Glucan / Carboxymethyl Glucan (CM-Glucan) — a chemically modified, water-soluble, lower-MW derivative (e.g., Mibelle's CM-Glucan Forte). Better solubility/handling; this is the form behind several cosmetic in-vivo claims.
  • Yeast Beta-Glucan, Oat (Avena sativa) Beta-Glucan, (1→3)-β-glucan, (1→3),(1→6)-β-D-glucan, Lentinan (from shiitake), Pleuran (from oyster mushroom), Zymosan (crude yeast cell-wall prep).
  • Molecular formula (idealized repeating unit basis): ~C₁₈H₃₂O₁₆ per disaccharide unit; appears as white/off-white powder or clear-to-amber viscous liquid.

⚠️ First and most important honesty note: "Beta-glucan" on an ingredient list tells you very little. An oat β-glucan, a yeast (1,3/1,6) β-glucan, and a carboxymethyl glucan derivative are functionally different ingredients. Evidence does not automatically transfer between them. This dossier flags source throughout.

2. Class & mechanism

Beta-glucan is best understood as a multi-functional active with (at least) four distinct, partly source-dependent modes of action on skin:

  1. Humectant + film-forming moisturizer (physical / surface). As a high-MW hydrophilic polysaccharide, it binds and holds water and forms a thin, breathable, moisture-retentive film on the stratum corneum. This reduces transepidermal water loss (TEWL) and gives an immediate smoothing/plumping sensory. This is the most robustly demonstrable topical mechanism and is largely physical (does not require deep penetration).

  2. Soothing / anti-inflammatory (immunomodulatory). Branched (1,3/1,6) β-glucans are recognized by pattern-recognition receptors — chiefly Dectin-1 (a C-type lectin), but also Complement Receptor 3 (CR3), Langerin on Langerhans cells, and TLR2 — which are expressed on skin keratinocytes, Langerhans cells, dendritic cells and macrophages (Goodridge 2009; van den Berg 2009/PMC2837148; de Jesus 2020/JID). Binding modulates cytokine signaling and can calm reactive, sensitized skin. This is a signaling effect, not just a barrier effect.

  3. Pro-repair / pro-collagen (cell-signaling, indirect). In wound and skin models, β-glucan activates macrophages and fibroblasts (via Dectin-1 → NF-κB and related transcription factors), driving release of growth factors and cytokines, fibroblast proliferation/migration, collagen deposition, and re-epithelialization (Majtan & Jesenak 2018/PMC6017669). The proposed anti-wrinkle mechanism (Pillai 2005) is that β-glucan reaching the dermis stimulates fibroblast collagen synthesis directly and via macrophage-mediated cytokines.

  4. Antioxidant (modest, source-dependent). Some β-glucans show free-radical scavenging and can reduce UVB-induced ROS in dermal cells in vitro, dampening the MMP-1 / collagen-degradation cascade (e.g., Lentinus β-glucan, 2025; Ganoderma polysaccharide studies). This is real but generally mild relative to dedicated antioxidants (vitamin C, etc.).

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core)

Tiers are separated deliberately. The single most important caveat: most of the strong β-glucan evidence is from (a) oral immune supplementation or (b) wound care (a damaged-skin, occlusive-dressing context) — not from topical use on intact, healthy aging skin. Anti-aging cosmetic data for topical β-glucan is thin and largely industry-linked.

TIER 1 — In vitro (cells / biochemistry)

  • Receptor biology: Dectin-1 is the principal β-glucan receptor; it is expressed on keratinocytes, Langerhans cells, macrophages and dendritic cells. β-glucan size controls the magnitude of Dectin-1–mediated cytokine (IL-1β) responses in human dendritic cells — particulate/larger β-glucans are more immunostimulatory than soluble small ones (Elder et al., J Biol Chem/PMC5500631, 2017). Langerin on Langerhans cells also binds β-glucans (de Jong et al., PMC2837148, 2010).
  • Keratinocyte proliferation / barrier turnover: Carboxymethyl-glucan (CM-glucan, baker's yeast) reported to promote keratinocyte growth and stratum-corneum renewal (Zulli et al., 1998 — manufacturer-associated; foundational but old).
  • Antioxidant / photoaging: β-glucan/polysaccharides reduce UVB-induced ROS, inhibit MAPK signaling, downregulate MMP-1 and preserve type I procollagen in human dermal fibroblasts (Ganoderma polysaccharide studies; Lentinus β-glucan, Int J Biol Macromol, 2025 — alleviated UVB-induced fibroblast senescence and photoaging, in vitro + mouse).
  • Limitation: In-vitro effects often use particulate yeast/fungal β-glucan at concentrations and delivery (direct to cells) not achievable through intact skin from a topical cream. High mechanistic plausibility ≠ topical efficacy.

TIER 2 — Animal

  • Diabetic wound healing (db/db mice): Aminated β-1,3-D-glucan improved healing (Berdal et al., Wound Repair Regen, 2007). A water-soluble β-glucan / cross-linked hyaluronate hydrogel accelerated db/db wound closure to ~91% by day 14 (2024/2025 study).
  • Yeast β-glucan + macrophage polarization: accelerated diabetic wound healing via M2 macrophage polarization and anti-inflammatory modulation (Int J Biol Macromol-type study, PubMed 41632999, 2024/2025).
  • Photoaging: Lentinus β-glucan reduced UVB photoaging markers in mouse skin (2025).
  • Limitation: Wound/diabetic models are damaged-skin, often injected or applied to open wounds — strong for wound care, weak as evidence for intact aging skin cosmetic use.

TIER 3 — Human ORAL (supplement)

This is where β-glucan's strongest RCT evidence lives — but it is immune/systemic, not dermatologic.

  • Upper respiratory tract infection (URTI): Multiple systematic reviews/meta-analyses of yeast (1,3/1,6) β-glucan find reduced incidence, number and/or duration of URTIs in healthy/stressed adults and older adults (e.g., systematic review & meta-analysis, Eur J Nutr, 2021, link.springer 10.1007/s00394-021-02566-4; Auinger et al.; Talbott & Talbott 2009/2010 stressed-subjects RCT; Fuller et al., older adults, Nutrition, 2017/PMID 28606567). Doses ~2.5–1000 mg/day (commonly 250–500 mg), often Wellmune (Saccharomyces).
  • Caveat for us: These are oral, immune outcomes. They are evidence that β-glucan is bioactive and immunomodulatory in humans — they say nothing about topical skin benefits and must never be cited to support a skincare claim.

TIER 4 — Human TOPICAL

(a) RCT / controlled cosmetic-efficacy data — thin and sponsor-linked:

  • Pillai R, Redmond M, Röding J. "Anti-Wrinkle Therapy: Significant New Findings in the Non-Invasive Cosmetic Treatment of Skin Wrinkles with Beta-Glucan." Int J Cosmet Sci, 2005; 27(5). The headline anti-aging study. n = 27 subjects, oat β-glucan ~0.1%, 8 weeks. Digital analysis of silicone replicas showed significant reduction in wrinkle depth/height and skin roughness; fluorescent-labeling work argued β-glucan penetrated into epidermis/dermis despite high MW. Limitations: small n; industry-conducted and -funded (Ceapro, the oat β-glucan supplier; authors affiliated to Symrise) [verified 2026-06-19]; single study, not independently replicated in 20 years; the placebo/blinding/control protocol is not clearly reported by modern standards (publicized partly via company press release). Treat as suggestive only, not established — this one paper is the entire topical anti-wrinkle case.
  • CM-Glucan Forte (Mibelle) in-vivo data: Manufacturer-reported double-blind, placebo-controlled study (~28 days, cream) showing increased skin hydration and smoothness and reduced TEWL (improved barrier). Limitation: manufacturer marketing dossier, not an independent peer-reviewed RCT — directionally consistent with the humectant mechanism but low on the evidence ladder.

(b) Human topical wound-care data (controlled, but wound context, not anti-aging):

  • Systematic review & meta-analysis — Somboonchokephisal et al., Wound Pract Res, 2025; 33(4):166–175: included 4 clinical studies (mix of RCTs and retrospective cohorts), analysed as two separate pooled meta-analyses: a chronic-wound pool of 2 studies (~354 participants) and an acute-wound pool of 2 studies (~290 participants). The chronic-wound pool showed a ~twofold increase in healing at 12 weeks; the acute-wound result was inconclusive / non-significant (high heterogeneity). The pooled RCTs were rated high/uncertain risk of bias. Mushroom-derived performed most favorably. [Verified 2026-06-19 — real, and the chronic vs acute split is as stated; note the "twofold" figure rests on only 2 chronic-wound studies.]
  • Venous ulcers — Medeiros et al., 2012 (PMC3430226): non-randomized, no control, n = 12 patients / 13 ulcers; 3% insoluble S. cerevisiae (1→3)-β-glucan cream daily up to 90 days. Area reduction ~55% at 90 days (one healed; a 15-yr-old ulcer reduced ~68%); significant rise in plasmocytes (p=0.018). Limitations: tiny, uncontrolled, single-arm.
  • Diabetic foot ulcers: randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled study of soluble β-1,3/1,6-glucan reported good safety and promising healing acceleration (cited in Majtan & Jesenak 2018 review, PMC6017669).
  • Pediatric partial-thickness burns: (1→3),(1→6)-β-glucan effective as a topical dressing with high rate of good cosmetic results (cited in same review).

Bottom line on transferability: - Proven for topical human use (intact skin): hydration, reduced TEWL/barrier support, smoothing, soothing sensory — supported by mechanism + the CM-Glucan in-vivo data + the 0.1% oat trial. Confidence: moderate (small/sponsor-linked but mechanistically sound and low-risk). - Suggested but not established for topical: anti-wrinkle / collagen (one small 2005 sponsor study); antioxidant/photoaging (in-vitro/animal only). - Strong, but borrowed from another format: immune/URTI benefit is oral; wound-healing benefit is damaged-skin wound care. Neither justifies an anti-aging cosmetic claim on intact skin.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • The honest position: Native cereal/yeast β-glucans are very high MW (often ~1,000 kDa) — far above the ~500 Da "rule of 500" heuristic for easy stratum-corneum penetration. The default expectation is that most topically applied β-glucan stays on the surface as a film/humectant, which is sufficient for the hydration/barrier benefit.
  • Penetration claims: Pillai 2005 argued (via fluorescent labeling on excised abdominal skin) that β-glucan reaches the epidermis/dermis through intercellular channels — the basis for the deeper collagen claims. This is contested and rests on a single industry study: the work was conducted/funded by Ceapro (the oat β-glucan supplier) with authors affiliated to Symrise, and the widely repeated "first to prove β-glucan penetrates skin" line traces to a 2005 company press release, not to independent replication [verified 2026-06-19]. There is no independent confirmation that intact-skin penetration to the dermis occurs at cosmetically meaningful levels. Lower-MW/carboxymethylated derivatives (CM-glucan) are designed for better solubility and plausibly better partitioning, but rigorous independent human penetration-depth data are sparse to absent.
  • Practical read: Credit β-glucan with surface hydration/film + soothing with high confidence. Treat "penetrates to the dermis and rebuilds collagen" as plausible but not robustly proven — useful for formulation rationale, risky for hard copy claims.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration:
  • Cosmetic hydration/soothing: typically 0.1–2% of the active (note many trade liquids are sold at low % active, e.g., 0.5–2% solids — read the COA, not the trade-name dose). The Pillai anti-wrinkle trial used ~0.1% oat β-glucan; CM-glucan often used at low single-digit %.
  • Suppliers commonly recommend 0.5–3% of powder/liquid forms; wound-care topical studies used up to 3% insoluble yeast glucan.
  • pH: Broadly stable and compatible across the typical cosmetic range (~pH 3–8). Soluble forms tolerate the acidic pH of vitamin C / AHA systems reasonably; confirm with supplier specs per grade.
  • Stability / handling: Generally stable; high-MW soluble forms are viscous and can be gelling/film-forming at higher use levels (sensory and viscosity implications). Powders need proper hydration/dispersion. Watch microbial load on natural-derived liquids — preserve adequately.
  • Texture / sensory: Light, "cushiony," slightly slippery film; gives an immediate smoothing/plumping skin feel and a softening "second-skin" finish. Pleasant in serums and creams; pairs well with a barrier-repair cream texture.
  • Pairings (synergistic / complementary):
  • Hyaluronic acid — complementary humectant; β-glucan's film + soothing rounds out HA's pure water-binding (common, sensible pairing).
  • Niacinamide, panthenol, ceramides — barrier/soothing stack; strong fit for a barrier-repair cream.
  • Retinoids / exfoliating acids — β-glucan is a good buffering/soothing partner to offset irritation.
  • Conflicts: No notable hard incompatibilities. Mainly viscosity/aesthetic management at higher concentrations, and potential film interactions with high electrolyte loads — formulate and stability-test per grade.

7. Safety

  • Irritation / tolerance: Excellent. Widely regarded as non-irritating, non-sensitizing at typical use levels; often used to reduce irritation. The Cosmetic Ingredient Review (CIR) has assessed β-glucan as safe for cosmetic use at the concentrations used (commonly cited as safe; verify current CIR report wording before publishing a claim).
  • Comedogenicity: Rated ~0 (non-comedogenic); fine for oily/acne-prone skin.
  • Sun sensitivity: None known — does not increase photosensitivity (contrast with retinoids/AHAs).
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: Topical β-glucan is generally considered low-risk in pregnancy (minimal systemic absorption from intact skin). Not medical advice; defer to the user's clinician, and avoid disease/treatment phrasing.
  • Sensitization / allergy: Low risk. Caveat: individuals with documented yeast sensitivity may prefer oat-derived β-glucan; oat-derived may be relevant to those avoiding gluten-adjacent cereal proteins (β-glucan itself is not gluten, but cereal extracts can carry trace protein — check supplier purity). EWG hazard generally rated low.
  • Sourcing / sustainability: Derived from renewable, often by-product/co-product streams (oat bran, brewer's/baker's yeast, mushroom). Generally a good sustainability story (upcycled agricultural/fermentation side-streams), subject to verifying the specific supplier.

8. Regulatory & claims (cosmetic vs. drug — per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic-safe (substantiable) language: "hydrates," "helps support the skin barrier," "soothes / helps calm the look of redness," "improves the appearance of smoothness and softness," "helps reduce the look of fine lines," "moisture-locking film," "lightweight, second-skin hydration." These align with the demonstrable humectant/barrier/soothing effects.
  • ⚠️ Drug-like / high-risk — flag for substantiation or legal review:
  • "Boosts/rebuilds collagen," "heals wounds," "treats/cures eczema, ulcers, dermatitis," "stimulates the immune system," "repairs" (structure/function) — these cross toward drug claims and/or rest on wound-care or oral data that does not apply to a cosmetic on intact skin.
  • "Penetrates deep to the dermis to regenerate" — penetration is contested; avoid as a hard claim.
  • Any immune messaging — the immune evidence is oral; importing it to a topical cosmetic is both scientifically and legally unsound.
  • Net: Beta-glucan is easy to use compliantly if copy stays in the hydration/barrier/soothing/appearance lane. The temptation (and the trap) is borrowing its impressive oral-immune and wound-healing literature for topical anti-aging claims.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: Low to moderate (commodity-adjacent). Oat and yeast β-glucan are inexpensive and widely available; specialized branded/derivatized forms (CM-Glucan Forte, high-purity 1,3/1,6 yeast glucans, premium mushroom β-glucans, marketed "high-bioavailability" grades) command a premium.
  • Common suppliers / trade names: Mibelle Biochemistry (CM-Glucan Forte), Wellmune (oral immune), Lonza, plus numerous oat/yeast β-glucan suppliers (MakingCosmetics, specialty actives houses). Marine/algal (e.g., Euglena paramylon) and mushroom (lentinan/pleuran) grades exist for differentiation.
  • Supply considerations: Stable, renewable supply (oat bran, fermentation yeast). Main spec items: source, structure (1,3/1,4 vs 1,3/1,6), MW, % active solids, solubility, purity/endotoxin, protein/gluten trace. Always buy on COA, not trade name.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • "Holds up to 20x its weight in water / out-hydrates hyaluronic acid." Marketing line. β-glucan is a genuinely good humectant + film-former, but the "beats HA" framing is not supported by robust head-to-head data; it is comparable/complementary, not categorically superior. Use as a complement to HA, not a giant-killer claim.
  • "Clinically proven anti-aging / collagen booster." Overreach. Rests substantially on one small (~27-subject), sponsor-associated 2005 trial at 0.1% oat β-glucan, not independently replicated. Real signal, weak proof.
  • "Penetrates deep to the dermis." Contested; high MW argues against it; the claim leans on industry data. Don't hard-claim it.
  • "Immune-boosting skincare." The immune/Dectin-1/URTI evidence is strong but ORAL. Importing it to a face cream is the most common and most misleading β-glucan overreach in the category.
  • "Heals/repairs skin." Wound-care evidence is for damaged skin under dressings, with small, high-bias RCTs — not a license for "repairs" on a cosmetic for intact skin.
  • Where the hype is actually fair: it is a safe, well-tolerated, pleasant, multi-tasking hydrator/soother with a good barrier and sustainability story — and a strong supporting sensory/calming player in a barrier-repair formula.

11. Verdict for the brand

✅ SUPPORTING active — conditional hero.

One-line: A safe, elegant, well-tolerated humectant/film-former/soother with strong mechanism and a great barrier-and-sustainability story — but its anti-aging "collagen/penetration" case is thin and its best evidence (immune, wound-healing) is borrowed from oral/wound formats, so use it as a credibility-friendly supporting hydrator/soother in the barrier-repair cream rather than the headline anti-aging hero.

  • Fit with the barrier-repair thesis: Excellent. Hydration + TEWL reduction + soothing + barrier support is exactly the brand's core lane, and β-glucan pairs naturally with ceramides, niacinamide, panthenol and HA.
  • Why not full hero: The marquee anti-aging (collagen/wrinkle/penetration) evidence is one small sponsor-linked study; over-leaning on it risks the claims discipline the brand depends on.
  • Longevity-relevance note: Its longevity tie-in is honest but indirect — it targets inflammaging (Dectin-1 immune modulation), barrier decline, and hydration, which are legitimate skin-aging hallmarks, plus weak in-vitro signals on photoaging/senescence. It is not a collagen-rebuilding or senescence-clearing "longevity" actor in any proven topical sense; frame it as barrier/calm/hydration, not as a regenerative claim.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19 by independent re-check of the primary literature (live source search). Scope: do the headline efficacy claims rest on human + topical data, or on borrowed in-vitro / animal / oral / wound-care data; are the cited studies real and faithfully described; and how strong/independent is the funding and sample base.

Overall evidence grade: C (weak / indirect for topical anti-aging)

For β-glucan's honest core lane (hydration / TEWL reduction / barrier support / soothing) the evidence is mechanistically sound and consistent but human-topical proof is limited and largely industry-sourced → roughly a B−. For the marquee anti-aging claims (collagen synthesis, wrinkle reduction, "penetrates to the dermis," photoaging/senescence reversal) the topical human evidence is C, sliding to D — it is essentially one small, non-replicated, industry-conducted study plus in-vitro/animal data. The strongest β-glucan evidence (immune/URTI, wound healing) is real but wrong-format (oral, or damaged-skin wound care) and must not be used for a cosmetic on intact skin. Weighted across all headline claims, the defensible composite grade is C.

  • Medeiros 2012 (Int J Mol Sci 13(7):8142, PMC3430226), Du/Bian/Xu 2014 (Phytother Res, PMID 23494974), Majtan & Jesenak 2018 (Molecules, PMC6017669), Elder 2017 (PMC5500631), Fuller 2017 (PMID 28606567). All real and accurately characterized. Medeiros is correctly flagged as tiny (n=12), uncontrolled, single-arm.
  • CM-Glucan Forte / Mibelle (#14) and Zulli 1998 (#17). Manufacturer-associated; #17's exact citation remains [unverified] — already flagged. These should never be cited as independent proof.

No fabricated or misattributed citations found. The dossier did not quietly pass off in-vitro/animal/oral data as human-topical — on the contrary, it tiers the evidence honestly and repeatedly warns against the borrow. This is a well-constructed, skeptical dossier.

Main caveats (the things that keep this at C, not B)

  1. One sponsor study carries the whole topical anti-aging story. Every "anti-wrinkle / collagen / penetrates-to-dermis" claim ultimately traces to Pillai 2005 — small, industry-funded, never replicated. Treat as a hypothesis, not evidence.
  2. The strong evidence is the wrong format. Immune/URTI = oral; wound healing = damaged skin under dressings with small, high-risk-of-bias RCTs. Neither transfers to a cosmetic on intact, healthy aging skin.
  3. Source ≠ source. Oat β-glucan, yeast (1,3/1,6) β-glucan, and CM-glucan are different ingredients; the dossier is right that evidence does not auto-transfer, and a brand must spec by COA.
  4. High MW vs. penetration. ~1,000 kDa argues strongly against meaningful dermal penetration; the contrary claim rests on a press-release-grade source. Default assumption should be surface film/humectant, which is sufficient for the real benefit.
  5. "Out-hydrates HA" / "20x its weight in water" / "immune-boosting skincare" are marketing lines with no robust topical head-to-head or topical-immune support.
