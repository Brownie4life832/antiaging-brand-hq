Ingredient: Black Ginger Methoxyflavones (Kaempferia parviflora) — INCI: Kaempferia Parviflora Rhizome Extract

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering (conditional — see verdict) · ⬜ shortlisted · ⬜ rejected · ⬜ in a product Class: NAD+ / sirtuin / AMPK / cellular-energy actives · Slug: black-ginger-methoxyflavones

One-line honesty flag up front: Black ginger has a genuinely interesting skin-aging mechanism story (AMPK / SIRT1 / PGC-1α mitochondrial biogenesis, senolytic-adjacent fibroblast effects, MMP suppression) and a real, reproducible in-vitro + ex-vivo dataset across several independent labs. But every piece of in-vivo skin evidence is from the WRONG format for a cream: the human RCTs are oral and measure body fat / exercise, not skin; the only animal "skin photoaging" study dosed the extract by mouth; and the headline topical human "clinical trial" published on the cosmetic-grade extract was a safety patch test, not an efficacy trial. Topical human efficacy for skin aging is, as of 2026-06, essentially unproven in the peer-reviewed record. Treat accordingly.

1. Identity

  • Common names: Black ginger, Thai black ginger, black galingale, krachai dam / kra chai dum (Thai). Sometimes "Thai ginseng."
  • Botanical source: Kaempferia parviflora Wall. ex Baker (family Zingiberaceae — the ginger family), a rhizomatous herb native to Thailand, Laos and the highlands of SE Asia. The rhizome is the part used.
  • INCI (cosmetic): Kaempferia Parviflora Rhizome Extract. Established cosmetic INCI; listed on EU CosIng / COSMILE Europe and EWG Skin Deep; used in marketed products (e.g. Adipeau, Storyderm, Kraam). Default INCIDecoder-classified function: antioxidant. [13][14][20]
  • The actives — "methoxyflavones" (polymethoxyflavones, PMFs): The biological identity of black ginger is a suite of lipophilic flavones whose hydroxyls are methylated (methoxy groups), unusual among dietary flavonoids. The principal/abundant ones reported across the chemistry literature: [1][2]
  • 5,7-dimethoxyflavone (5,7-DMF / DMF) — the most abundant single PMF; commercial oral extracts are often standardized to ≥2.5–10% 5,7-DMF.
  • 5,7,4′-trimethoxyflavone (TMF)
  • 3,5,7-trimethoxyflavone (3,5,7-TMF)
  • 3,5,7,4′-tetramethoxyflavone and 5,7,3′,4′-tetramethoxyflavone
  • 3,5,7,3′,4′-pentamethoxyflavone (PMF)
  • Plus methylated apigenin/luteolin derivatives (e.g. 7,4′-dimethylapigenin, tetramethylluteolin).
  • Standardized trade/commercial forms (relevant because almost all clinical work uses a specific extract, not "black ginger" generically):
  • BG100 — a standardized rhizome extract reported at ~62% total polymethoxyflavones; the form behind the 2021 ex-vivo skin paper and the 2024 cosmetic safety paper (Thai groups; NSTDA/BCG-funded). [4][8]
  • SIRTMAX® (Tokiwa Phytochemical, Japan) — the oral "sirtuin-activator" supplement brand; behind the 2025 body-fat RCT. [10]
  • KPFORCE™ (Maruzen, Japan) — oral supplement form behind a safety RCT. [11]
  • In cosmetics, the marketed face product most associated with topical K. parviflora is Adipeau Volume Cream (positions the extract as an "adipo-volumizing" / dermal-fat active). [13][14]
  • Chemistry note: PMFs are small, highly lipophilic (fully methylated → no free phenolic OH groups → far more oil-soluble and membrane-permeant than typical flavonoids like quercetin). That lipophilicity is the single most important formulation/penetration fact about this ingredient (see §5).

2. Class & mechanism

Black ginger sits in the "cellular-energy / longevity-pathway" active class alongside NAD+ precursors, resveratrol analogs, urolithin A and spermidine. Its mechanistic pitch is built on three intertwined pathways:

  • AMPK activation (energy sensor). K. parviflora extract and its PMFs activate AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK), the cell's low-energy sensor, and downstream phosphorylate acetyl-CoA carboxylase — driving fat oxidation and glucose uptake in muscle. This is the basis of the metabolic/exercise literature and feeds into mitochondrial biogenesis. [1][9]
  • SIRT1 → PGC-1α → mitochondrial biogenesis. In rodent muscle and myotube models, the extract upregulates SIRT1, AMPK, PPARδ and PGC-1α, plus the PGC-1α downstream program (NRF-1, ERRα, Tfam), increasing mitochondrial number/density. SIRTMAX is literally marketed as a "sirtuin activator." [9][10] In skin fibroblasts, the standardized extract raised PGC-1α and increased ATP production (~+35% in vitro) while lowering ROS — a "restore cellular energy" mechanism directly analogous to the NAD+/urolithin story. [3]
  • PPARα/γ activation. 5,7-DMF is a dual PPARα/γ activator, which in fibroblasts suppresses UVB-induced MMPs and boosts the antioxidant enzyme catalase. [5] (PPARγ activation is also why the same molecule shows up in adipocyte differentiation studies — see §3 volume note and §10.)

Secondary, skin-relevant mechanisms (well documented in cells): - MMP suppression / collagen protection — PMFs (esp. 5,7-DMF, TMF, 3,5,7-TMF) downregulate MMP-1, -2, -3, -9, -13 via blocking MAPK → AP-1 (c-Jun/c-Fos) and NF-κB signaling, and upregulate type I procollagen (COL1A1). [3][5][6][7] - Anti-senescence / SASP modulation — reduces SA-β-gal+ cells, downregulates p16/p21/p53/pRb, suppresses the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) in dermal fibroblasts. [3][4] - Antioxidant / NRF2-adjacent — lowers intracellular ROS, induces catalase and heme-oxygenase-1 (HO-1). [5][6][12] - Anti-inflammatory — suppresses COX-2, PGE2, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α signaling (NF-κB / AP-1). [6][7][12] - Melanogenesis modulation — CONTRADICTORY (see §3 + §7): some PMFs inhibit tyrosinase/melanin in B16F10 cells [15], yet 5,7-DMF has separately been reported as pro-melanogenic elsewhere. [1] Do not build a tone claim on this. - PDE5 inhibition — 5,7-DMF inhibits PDE5 (IC50 ~10.6 μM); the basis of black ginger's traditional "vitality/circulation" reputation. Not skin-relevant per se, but it is why the plant is sometimes hyped as "natural Viagra." [1]

Plain-language: Skin cells run on mitochondria; with age and UV they make less energy, leak more ROS, slip into senescence, and tip the collagen balance toward breakdown. Black ginger's methoxyflavones flip several of those switches at once in a dish — wake up the AMPK/SIRT1/PGC-1α "energy + repair" program, calm ROS and inflammation, and brake the MMPs that chew up collagen. The open question is whether enough of them reach living human skin from a cream to do any of that.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

Read this before believing any marketing. I separate what is proven for the format you would actually sell (a topical cosmetic) from what is borrowed from oral supplements, animal feeding studies, or cell culture. The headline finding: the in-vitro and ex-vivo skin science is solid and multi-lab; the in-vivo skin science is all oral; the topical human science is a safety test, not an efficacy test.

Tier 1 — In-vitro (cell studies) — the strongest and most reproducible tier, across independent groups

  • Park, Woo, Kim, Kim & Hwang (2017), Evidence-Based Complement. Altern. Med. 2017:6861085. H₂O₂-senescent human dermal fibroblasts (Hs68) + standardized KPE 1/5/10 μg/mL. ↓SA-β-gal (17.6–33%), ↓p53/p21/p16/pRb, ↑ATP (+35.3% at 10 μg/mL), ↓ROS (~13%), ↑PGC-1α/mitochondrial-biogenesis genes. This is the keystone "skin = mitochondria" paper. (Also includes an oral mouse arm — see Tier 2.) No COI declared. DOI: 10.1155/2017/6861085 [3]
  • Klinngam, Rungkamoltip, et al. (2021), Biomedicine & Pharmacotherapy 145:112461. Purified PMFs (5,7-DMF, 5,7,4′-TMF, 3,5,7,3′,4′-PMF) in primary human dermal fibroblasts and an ex-vivo human skin model. In fibroblasts: ↓cellular senescence, ↓ROS, ↓SASP, protection against mitochondrial dysfunction, ↑tropocollagen. In ex-vivo human skin (chronological-aging model): reported ↑lamin B1, ↑collagen type I, ↑fibrillin-1, ↑hyaluronic acid. This is the single most "skin-tissue-like" dataset (see Tier 4 caveat on ex-vivo ≠ in-vivo). Thai/NSTDA groups (later overlap with the BG100 commercial program). DOI: 10.1016/j.biopha.2021.112461 [4]
  • Kim et al. (2012), Experimental Dermatology 21(3):211-216. 5,7-DMF (5/10/20 μM) in UVB-irradiated Hs68 fibroblasts. Dual PPARα/γ activator; strongly ↓MMP-1/-2/-9 (mRNA, protein, activity); ↑catalase → ↓ROS → ↓MAPK; ↓IκBα phosphorylation, ↓NF-κB p65 translocation, ↓IL-6/IL-8. Mechanistically the cleanest single-molecule photoaging paper. DOI: 10.1111/j.1600-0625.2011.01435.x (PMID 22379967) [5]
  • Phung et al. (2021), Antioxidants 10(10):1609. PMFs (5,7-DMF, 5,7,4′-TMF, 3,5,7,3′,4′-PMF; 6.25–12.5 μM) vs TNF-α–stimulated human dermal fibroblasts. 5,7,4′-TMF most potent: ↓MMP-1, ↑COL1A1, ↓ROS, ↓COX-2/IL-1β/IL-6, blocks MAPK/AP-1/NF-κB. DOI: 10.3390/antiox10101609 [7]
  • Lee, Jang, Kim & Kang (2022), Antioxidants 11(2):425. 3,5,7-trimethoxyflavone (50/100 μM) vs TNF-α–stimulated NHDFs. ↓MMP-1 secretion, ↑procollagen I, ↓ROS (~48% at 100 μM), ↓IL-1β/6/8, ↓MAPK/Akt, ↑HO-1. DOI: 10.3390/antiox11020425 [6]
  • Klinngam et al. (2024), Biomolecules 14(7):776 — 3D-model arm. BG100 (0.1%, 0.2%) on UV-exposed human full-thickness 3D skin (EFT-400). ↑Collagen I (~+35–38%), ↑hyaluronic acid (+46–73%), ↓ROS (~33–35%), ↓DNA damage 8-OHdG (32–45%); benchmarked vs 0.5% retinol and 2% ethyl ascorbic acid. (IL-6 not significantly reduced.) The strongest "topical-format" efficacy signal — but it is reconstructed tissue, not living humans. NSTDA/BCG-funded; "no COI" declared. DOI: 10.3390/biom14070776 [8]
  • Huo et al. (2023), Plants 12(5):1183. Anti-melanogenic screen in IBMX-stimulated B16F10: 7,4′-dimethylapigenin and 3,5,7-TMF ↓tyrosinase/melanin (12.5–100 μM); C-5 methoxy is key. Note: does NOT test 5,7-DMF, which is reported pro-melanogenic elsewhere → the pigmentation story is unsettled. DOI: 10.3390/plants12051183 [15]

Verdict on Tier 1: Unusually strong for the in-vitro tier — multiple independent labs (Thailand, Korea), multiple purified single molecules, consistent direction (↓senescence, ↓ROS, ↓MMP, ↑collagen, ↑mitochondria). This is better cell-level evidence than many botanicals have. But in-vitro fibroblast/3D data is still the weakest tier for predicting what a finished cream does on intact stratum corneum — concentrations of 5–100 μM in a dish, with the compound applied directly to fibroblasts, do not represent delivered dose through skin.

Tier 2 — Animal — exists for skin, but the efficacy study is ORAL, not topical

  • Park, Pyun, Woo, Jeong & Hwang (2014), Photodermatol. Photoimmunol. Photomed. 30(5):237-245. UVB-photoaging in SKH-1 hairless mice, K. parviflora ethanol extract given ORALLY (100/200 mg/kg/day, 13 weeks). Significantly prevented wrinkle formation and collagen loss (↑type I/III/VII collagen genes), ↓MMP-2/-3/-9/-13 (via ↓c-Jun/c-Fos), ↑catalase, ↓NF-κB/IL-1β/COX-2. Excellent skin endpoints — but it is a feeding study, so it speaks to oral black ginger, not a cream. DOI: 10.1111/phpp.12097 (PMID 24313661) [16]
  • Park et al. (2017) mouse arm [3]: middle-aged SKH-1 mice, oral KPE 200 mg/kg/day × 24 weeks. ↓SA-β-gal (25.5%), ↓p53/p21/p16, ↓wrinkle area/number/length/depth, ↑skin thickness, ↑collagen (hydroxyproline + Masson's trichrome), ↑elasticity. Again oral.
  • Lee et al. (2018), Oxidative Medicine and Cellular Longevity 2018:5967150. The one genuine TOPICAL animal study. SKH-1 hairless mice, topical KPE 50–100 mg/kg applied before simulated-solar-UV. ↓COX-2, ↓PGE2, ↓ROS, ↓MAPK, ↓NF-κB/AP-1 in skin + JB6/HaCaT cells. But the endpoint is acute UV-inflammation/photoprotection, NOT a chronic anti-aging/wrinkle outcome. Korea Food Research Institute funded; no COI. DOI: 10.1155/2018/5967150 [12]

Verdict on Tier 2: Real, fairly high-quality rodent skin data showing wrinkle reduction, collagen preservation and MMP suppression — but the two efficacy studies are oral feeding studies. The only topical-route animal study demonstrates anti-inflammatory photoprotection, not anti-aging remodeling. So even in animals, topical black ginger is unproven for skin aging.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL — the only randomized controlled trials that exist — and none are about skin

These establish the molecule's systemic biology and a clean safety record. None has a skin endpoint. Include for credibility/context, not as skin proof.

  • Sung et al. (2025), Frontiers in Nutrition 12:1576024 (SIRTMAX®). RCT, double-blind, placebo-controlled; 100 randomized / 83 analyzed; oral 100 mg/day of SIRTMAX extract (20% standardized ≈ 50 mg KP ethanol extract) × 12 weeks. ↓body-fat mass vs placebo (p=0.0054); framed via energy expenditure / fat utilization. Funded by Daehan Chemtech; multiple author COIs (Daehan, Tokiwa = the SIRTMAX maker). No skin outcomes. DOI: 10.3389/fnut.2025.1576024 [10]
  • Yoshino et al. (2019), Journal of Medicinal Food 22(x) (KPFORCE™). RCT, double-blind, placebo-controlled; n=52 healthy Japanese; oral 750 mg/day × 4 weeks. Primary aim safety: no treatment-related adverse events or lab abnormalities. Maruzen-affiliated (the maker). DOI: 10.1089/jmf.2018.4372 (PMID 31517555) [11]
  • Broader oral program (context): multiple human/animal studies on exercise performance, muscular endurance, metabolic syndrome, erectile/vascular function — driven by the AMPK/SIRT1/PGC-1α and PDE5 mechanisms reviewed in Chen et al. 2018. [1] All systemic; none dermatologic.

Verdict on Tier 3: For oral black ginger there are genuine placebo-controlled RCTs with a body-fat signal and a good short-term safety record — but they are sponsor-run, short, and completely silent on skin. They tell us the molecule is bioactive and orally tolerable; they tell us nothing about a cream's performance.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL — the tier that matters for a skincare SKU, and it is the thinnest

  • Klinngam et al. (2024), Biomolecules 14(7):776 — human arm. Despite the title ("…and Clinical Trial"), the human portion is a SAFETY study, not an efficacy study. It was a topical irritation + sensitization patch test of 0.2% BG100 in n=52 volunteers (induction + challenge phases): Mean Cumulative Irritation Index 0.08 ("non-irritating"), 0 sensitization reactions ("non-sensitizing"), plus in-vitro genotoxicity-negative (OECD TG487). No wrinkle, hydration, elasticity, or firmness efficacy was measured in humans here. All efficacy in this paper is from the 3D model (Tier 1). NSTDA/BCG-funded; "no COI." DOI: 10.3390/biom14070776 [8]
  • Supplier/marketing topical "efficacy" claim — [unverified, NOT peer-reviewed]. Trade and supplier write-ups describe a "28-day study in 63 Chinese women" with once-daily facial BG100 emulsion reporting +33.7% hydration, −15.6% TEWL, +14.0% elasticity, −73.7% facial redness, plus dermatologist-graded wrinkle/firmness improvement. I could not locate this as a peer-reviewed publication — it appears to be a supplier marketing dataset, and the −73.7% redness figure in particular is implausibly large for a 28-day botanical. Treat as manufacturer-reported, unverified. [19][20]
  • Adipeau Volume Cream ("adipo-volumizing") — brand claim. A marketed face cream positions K. parviflora rhizome extract as restoring dermal/facial fat-pad volume via adipocyte modulation, citing "Karolinska studies." I found no peer-reviewed human topical trial supporting facial-volume restoration, and the underlying adipocyte science is contradictory (see §10): some studies show PMFs induce adipogenesis (PPARγ↑) [17] while others show they suppress adipogenesis / reduce adipocyte hypertrophy via AMPK [18]. The "fills out your face" mechanism is therefore not established and the human evidence is brand-level only. [13][14]

Verdict on Tier 4: For a topical cosmetic, the peer-reviewed human dataset is one safety patch test (clean — genuinely useful for tolerability) and zero peer-reviewed efficacy trials. Topical human anti-aging efficacy is unproven in the refereed literature; the only efficacy numbers are supplier-reported.

Explicit borrowed-vs-proven map: - Proven for topical human use (peer-reviewed): tolerability only — non-irritating, non-sensitizing at 0.2% (patch test). - Strong but in the wrong format (do NOT claim for a cream): "↓body fat," "↑exercise/energy," "sirtuin activator," "mitochondrial biogenesis," any oral-supplement benefit. - Cell/tissue & oral-animal signals (directional only, not consumer claims): ↓senescence, ↓MMP, ↑collagen, ↑mitochondrial ATP, wrinkle reduction in mice. - Brand/supplier only (no peer-reviewed human topical support): facial-volume restoration, +33.7% hydration / −73.7% redness, pigmentation/tone benefit.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • The good news: PMFs are small, fully methylated, highly lipophilic flavones — physicochemically well suited to partitioning into and through the stratum corneum (far more so than glycosylated/hydroxylated flavonoids). This is the most favorable thing about the ingredient for topical use, and it's why oral 5,7-DMF has been explored with transdermal penetration enhancers (oleic acid, menthol) specifically to bypass poor oral absorption. [1]
  • The caveat: I found no published independent skin-permeation (Franz cell) study quantifying how much 5,7-DMF / PMF crosses human skin from a finished cosmetic, nor any topical PK. The ex-vivo human-skin gene/protein changes [4] imply something penetrates living tissue, but delivered dose to dermal fibroblasts is [unverified].
  • Oral PK is poor and irrelevant to topical: rat oral bioavailability of PMFs is only ~1–14% (rapid demethylation, sulfation, glucuronidation; first-pass loss). [1] This low oral bioavailability is actually a (weak) argument FOR the topical route — local delivery avoids first-pass metabolism — but it is not proof of topical delivery.
  • Net: Penetration is plausible and physicochemically favorable, but not independently quantified for a cosmetic. Do not make mechanistic "penetrates to the dermis to repair fibroblasts" claims.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration (topical): The only topical data points are 0.1–0.2% of the standardized BG100 extract (3D model + patch test) [8] and the supplier emulsion (~0.2%) [19]. As a whole-extract, expect a typical cosmetic use level of roughly 0.1–0.5% standardized extract; if dosing on PMF content, BG100 at ~62% PMF means 0.2% extract ≈ 0.12% total PMFs. No optimized concentration is established for human efficacy. [partly unverified — get supplier TDS]
  • pH: Methoxyflavones lack free phenolic OH groups, so they are less pH-sensitive than typical polyphenols and should tolerate the mild-acidic-to-neutral range of most serums/creams. No published optimal-pH window located — confirm with supplier. [unverified]
  • Stability: Methylation makes PMFs more oxidatively stable than hydroxy-flavonoids (fewer oxidizable phenols); lipophilic and reasonably heat-stable. Still, standard light/air-protective, opaque/airless packaging is prudent. Whole-rhizome extracts can carry color (yellow/brown) and odor. Concrete cosmetic-stability data are supplier-held. [partly unverified]
  • Format: Strongly favors anhydrous or emulsion (oil-phase) systems given lipophilicity — load into the oil phase. Good fit for a barrier-repair cream's lipid phase.
  • Pairings (rational, evidence-aligned):
  • Niacinamide — barrier/tone workhorse; complementary, no conflict.
  • Ceramides / cholesterol / fatty acids — on-thesis for the brand's barrier-repair cream; lipophilic PMFs sit comfortably in a lipid matrix.
  • Other "cellular-energy" actives (NAD precursors, CoQ10, resveratrol analogs, urolithin A) — narratively coherent "mitochondrial-longevity" stack; mechanistically complementary (AMPK/SIRT1 + NAD+).
  • Vitamin C / E — antioxidant synergy (NRF2/catalase axis).
  • Retinoids — MMP-suppression + collagen mechanisms are complementary; sequence to manage irritation (the retinoid, not the PMF, drives irritation).
  • Conflicts: No documented hard incompatibilities. Whole-extract color/odor is the main sensory risk; a purified PMF fraction mitigates this at higher cost. [unverified]
  • Sensory: Whole rhizome extract = potential faint yellow tint + herbaceous/spicy ginger note at higher loads; purified PMFs are near-neutral. Verify in the actual base.

7. Safety

  • Topical (human): The one human topical study [8] is reassuring: non-irritating (MCII 0.08), non-sensitizing (0/52), and non-genotoxic in vitro (OECD TG487) at cosmetic levels (0.2% extract). EWG Skin Deep lists the INCI with low hazard / low allergenicity. [14][20] As a ginger-family (Zingiberaceae) botanical, theoretical cross-reactivity/sensitization in ginger-sensitive individuals can't be fully excluded — recommend in-house RIPT on the finished formula.
  • Oral safety (context): KPFORCE RCT (750 mg/day × 4 wk) showed no adverse events [11]; SIRTMAX RCT (100 mg/day × 12 wk) well tolerated [10]; broadly good tolerability in the supplement literature.
  • Sun: No evidence of photosensitization; mechanistically photoprotective in cells/mice (↓UVB-MMP, ↓UV-inflammation). [5][12] Not a sunscreen — does not replace SPF.
  • Pregnancy/lactation: No topical pregnancy-safety data. Note: black ginger is traditionally an aphrodisiac / vasoactive (PDE5-inhibiting) herb and some sources caution against high-dose oral use in pregnancy. For a cosmetic topical at <0.5%, systemic exposure is expected to be negligible, but default to cautious cosmetic language and "consult your doctor" for any oral form. [unverified for topical pregnancy]
  • Endocrine/PPAR note: PMFs are PPARα/γ ligands and have systemic metabolic activity orally; at cosmetic topical concentrations this is not expected to be clinically meaningful, but it's worth flagging for a brand that values claims discipline.
  • Pigmentation caution: Because 5,7-DMF has been reported as pro-melanogenic in some work while other PMFs are anti-melanogenic [1][15], do not assume a "brightening" safety/benefit direction — it could theoretically darken in some contexts. Unsettled; test if tone is a target.
  • Sourcing/sustainability: Cultivated rhizome crop (mainly Thailand). Agricultural supply → batch-to-batch PMF variability is the real quality risk; insist on a standardized extract (e.g. % total PMFs / % 5,7-DMF) with CoA. No major sustainability red flags identified; it is a cultivated, fast-growing rhizome, not a threatened wild species.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic vs drug line: Kaempferia Parviflora Rhizome Extract is an established cosmetic INCI (CosIng/EWG-listed). Used to support appearance claims it stays cosmetic. Language implying it treats/prevents disease or alters skin structure/function — "boosts collagen," "activates sirtuins/mitochondria in your skin," "reverses aging," "restores facial fat," "increases skin elasticity by X%" — risks crossing into drug territory (US FDA) and would need substantiation/legal review.
  • The acute substantiation problem for THIS ingredient: there is no peer-reviewed human topical efficacy trial. Borrowing the oral RCT results ("clinically proven sirtuin activator," "reduces body fat") onto a cream would be misleading and a clear claims-discipline violation. The supplier "63 women" efficacy numbers are not peer-reviewed and should not be presented as established without seeing (and ideally re-running) the study.
  • Safer cosmetic phrasing (✅): "a polymethoxyflavone-rich botanical studied for skin-cell energy and antioxidant defense," "helps visibly improve the look of skin affected by environmental stress," "supports the appearance of firmness," "antioxidant botanical." Pair any "longevity/mitochondrial" narrative with cell-study framing, not consumer-outcome promises.
  • Avoid / flag for legal (⚠️): "boosts collagen by X%," "activates sirtuins/AMPK in your skin," "clinically proven anti-aging," "restores facial volume," "reverses wrinkles," "increases elasticity X%," and any oral cross-format benefit (body fat, energy, libido) on a skincare label.
  • See ../../brand/glossary.md claim cheat-sheet.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Forms to buy: standardized cosmetic extract (BG100, ~62% PMFs — Thai supplier program) [4][8]; oral/nutraceutical standardized extracts (SIRTMAX® Tokiwa, KPFORCE™ Maruzen) [10][11]; or generic standardized K. parviflora rhizome extracts from Thai botanical houses (e.g. Specialty Natural Products). [Supplier landscape — verify current cosmetic-grade availability and standardization.]
  • Cost tier: Moderate (botanical extract, not a synthesized specialty molecule like urolithin A or NR). Whole standardized extract should be far cheaper per kg than NAD-precursor actives, fitting a premium cream's budget comfortably at 0.1–0.5%. A purified PMF fraction (for color/odor/standardization) costs more. Raw $/kg not publicly listed — request supplier quote. [unverified]
  • Quality lever: because the actives vary with the crop, the standardization spec (% total PMFs and/or % 5,7-DMF) is the thing to pay for. Insist on CoA + standardization; cheap unstandardized "black ginger extract" may have little of the active fraction.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • Hype: "Sirtuin-activating skincare," "mitochondrial longevity for skin," "Thai ginseng that reverses aging," "fills out your face / restores youthful volume," "nature's NMN/Viagra." Much of this conflates the oral metabolic/exercise RCTs and the cell-culture mechanism with a cream's performance.
  • Reality check #1 — wrong format. The randomized human evidence (SIRTMAX body fat, KPFORCE safety) is oral and not about skin. [10][11] The wrinkle/collagen in-vivo skin data is from oral mouse feeding studies. [3][16]
  • Reality check #2 — the "clinical trial" is a safety test. The flagship topical paper's human arm is an irritation/sensitization patch test, not an efficacy trial; all its efficacy is from a 3D model. [8]
  • Reality check #3 — the volume story is built on contradictory science. "Adipo-volumizing" rests on adipocyte studies that disagree: some show PMFs induce adipogenesis via PPARγ [17], others show they suppress it / shrink adipocytes via AMPK [18]. You cannot have a reliable "adds facial fat" claim from a literature that also says it "reduces fat." And there is no peer-reviewed human topical volume trial. [13][14]
  • Reality check #4 — pigmentation is unsettled. 5,7-DMF is reported pro-melanogenic in places, anti-melanogenic PMFs in others. [1][15] No tone claim is safe yet.
  • What's genuinely strong: the in-vitro + ex-vivo skin mechanism is real, coherent and multi-lab (↓senescence, ↓MMP, ↑collagen, ↑mitochondrial ATP, ↓ROS); the molecule is lipophilic and penetration-favorable; and the topical safety/tolerability is clean. That is a legitimately good foundation — it's the human topical efficacy link that's missing.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ CONDITIONAL — a supporting "longevity-science / antioxidant" active, not a hero and not a primary efficacy claim driver.

One line: Mechanistically gorgeous and perfectly on-thesis for a skin-longevity brand (AMPK/SIRT1/PGC-1α + senescence + MMP), with strong multi-lab cell/ex-vivo data, favorable lipophilic penetration, clean topical tolerability and moderate cost — but with NO peer-reviewed human topical efficacy trial, it cannot carry hard efficacy claims the way retinol, niacinamide or vitamin C can.

  • Best role: a supporting "cellular-energy / antioxidant longevity" active layered into the barrier-repair cream alongside proven workhorses (ceramides, niacinamide, retinoid, vitamin C, SPF in routine). It adds a credible, differentiated mitochondrial/sirtuin narrative and pairs naturally with NAD precursors / urolithin A / resveratrol analogs for a coherent "longevity stack" story.
  • Promote toward hero only if (a) an independent, vehicle-controlled human topical RCT with real skin endpoints (wrinkles/elasticity/firmness/hydration) is published or commissioned by the brand, AND (b) claims stay in safe cosmetic-appearance language.
  • Skip if the brand needs every active to have human topical RCT backing, or wants a brightening/"facial volume" claim (both unsettled/unproven topically).
  • Longevity relevance: Very high in concept — it targets mitochondrial decline, cellular senescence, oxidative stress and inflammaging, four core hallmarks, via the same AMPK/SIRT1/PGC-1α axis as the marquee longevity molecules. That conceptual richness is exactly why claims discipline matters here: the story is strong enough to oversell well past the topical evidence.

Log under ../../ideas/considering.md as a conditional/supporting active pending independent human topical efficacy data.

Overall evidence grade for TOPICAL skin efficacy: C− / D+ (weak / indirect — mechanistically rich but no human topical efficacy)

Rationale: The tier that matters for a cream — human topical efficacy — does not exist in the peer-reviewed literature. The one human topical study ([8]) is a safety patch test; its efficacy is from a 3D reconstructed-skin model. Everything stronger is either in-vitro/ex-vivo (genuinely good and multi-lab, but the weakest tier for predicting cosmetic performance) or oral (RCTs for body fat/safety; oral-mouse for wrinkles). It stays out of pure "D/hype" because (i) the mechanism is real and reproduced across independent groups, (ii) there is a clean ex-vivo human-tissue signal, and (iii) topical tolerability is established. But with zero human topical efficacy RCTs it cannot reach B. For oral black ginger the grade would be ~B− (real but sponsor-run RCTs, non-skin endpoints); for topical skin efficacy it is C−/D+.

What verified out cleanly

  • 5,7-DMF / PPARα/γ / MMP (Kim 2012) [5] — REAL; 5–20 μM, MMP-1/2/9 down, in-vitro. Correctly tiered.
  • Klinngam 2021 ex-vivo [4] — REAL; in-vitro fibroblast + ex-vivo human-skin endpoints (lamin B1, collagen I, fibrillin-1, HA). Correctly labeled ex-vivo (not in-vivo).
  • Klinngam 2024 BG100 [8] — REAL; key correction confirmed: the human arm is a safety patch test, NOT an efficacy trial — efficacy is 3D-model only. The dossier states this explicitly.
  • Park 2014 [16] and Park 2017 mouse arm [3] — REAL; both wrinkle/collagen mouse studies are ORAL feeding, not topical. Correctly flagged.
  • Lee 2018 [12] — REAL; the one topical animal study, but endpoint is acute UV-inflammation, not chronic anti-aging. Correctly limited.
  • SIRTMAX 2025 [10] / KPFORCE 2019 [11] — REAL oral RCTs; body-fat / safety; no skin endpoints; sponsor COIs. Correctly quarantined from skin claims.
  • Adipogenesis contradiction [17] vs [18] — REAL and genuinely opposing → the "facial volume" mechanism is unsettled. Correctly flagged. Strengthened on re-verification: the systemic human and animal evidence points to fat reduction, not gain — a separate oral human RCT reports K. parviflora purified PMF reduces visceral fat in overweight Japanese adults (PMID 33475663), consistent with the body-fat RCT [10] and the AMPK-mediated adipogenesis suppression of [18]. So the dominant, best-evidenced metabolic direction is anti-adipogenic, which makes a topical "adds facial fat / restores volume" claim not merely unproven but directionally counter to the stronger systemic data. Avoid it firmly.

Caveats to carry into any marketing/claims work

  1. No human topical efficacy trial exists (peer-reviewed). Do not say "clinically proven" for the cream.
  2. The strong RCTs are oral and non-skin. Borrowing "sirtuin activator / reduces body fat / boosts energy" onto a cream is the central trap.
  3. The "clinical trial" in the flagship paper's title is a safety test. Do not cite it as efficacy.
  4. "Facial volume / adipo-volumizing" is brand-level and built on contradictory adipocyte science — and the stronger systemic data point the OPPOSITE way (fat reduction). Avoid the claim firmly (see "What verified out cleanly" above; human visceral-fat RCT PMID 33475663).
  5. Pigmentation direction is unsettled (5,7-DMF possibly pro-melanogenic). No brightening claim.
  6. Supplier "63-women" topical efficacy numbers are unverified and one figure (−73.7% redness) is implausibly large.
  7. No independent topical skin-penetration data — penetration is physicochemically plausible (lipophilic PMFs) but unquantified for a cosmetic.

Bottom line

The mechanism and the cell/ex-vivo science are a legitimately strong foundation and the molecule's lipophilicity makes it a better topical candidate than most polyphenols. But the chain to a finished cream is missing its final, decisive link: a human topical efficacy trial. Use it as a supporting longevity-narrative active with carefully bounded cosmetic-appearance + antioxidant claims; do not let the (real) oral/longevity science or the (real) cell-level mechanism leak into topical efficacy promises. Topical-efficacy grade: C−/D+.
