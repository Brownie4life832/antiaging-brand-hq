Ingredient: Alpine Rose extract (INCI: Rhododendron Ferrugineum Extract / Rhododendron Ferrugineum Leaf Cell Culture Extract)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Senotherapeutics & senolytics (botanical / flavonoid-based)

One-line honest summary: Alpine Rose is the cosmetic industry's first "senolytic" marketing story — a genuinely interesting flavonoid-rich leaf extract (taxifolin, farrerol, hyperoside, naringenin) pitched as clearing "zombie cells" from skin. But the evidence is almost entirely single-supplier (Mibelle) and either in-vitro or published in trade/manufacturer-funded outlets, not independent peer-reviewed human RCTs. The one peer-reviewed paper (Hubert 2022) is a chemistry paper that did no efficacy testing of its own — it merely proposes which molecules might explain a senolytic activity reported elsewhere. The whole "senolytic" claim rides on borrowed evidence: cancer-cell apoptosis data for the individual flavonoids plus structural similarity to fisetin. Treat it as a strong narrative/supporting active with a great longevity story, not a proven topical hero.

0. Disambiguation — there are TWO different "Alpine Rose" actives (do not conflate)

This matters because vendors and bloggers blur them constantly:

Alpine Rose Active (Mibelle/Helvecos) Rhododendron Ferrugineum Extract (in glycerin/water or caprylic/capric triglyceride) Senolytic — glycerol/water leaf extract said to kill senescent fibroblasts 1–3%
PhytoCellTec™ Alp Rose (Mibelle) Rhododendron Ferrugineum Leaf Cell Culture Extract "Plant stem cell" — callus/dedifferentiated cell-culture extract for epidermal stem-cell protection & barrier, dehydrins 0.4–1%

PhytoCellTec Alp Rose (barrier/stem-cell) is covered briefly where relevant. There is also a senocelltec dossier in this repo — that is the combination concept (alpine rose senolytic + apple + grape stem cells, "clear and fill"); read the two together. 1. Identity

  • Common name: Alpine rose / rusty-leaved alpenrose / Rostblättrige Alpenrose (German); rhododendron des Alpes (French).
  • Botanical source: Rhododendron ferrugineum L. (Ericaceae) — an evergreen subalpine shrub of the European Alps/Pyrenees, growing 1,700–2,300 m. Adapted to extreme cold, UV and drought; leaves accumulate protective dehydrins (cold/UV-protective proteins) and a rich phenolic load. [1][9]
  • INCI:
  • Rhododendron Ferrugineum Extract (glycerin/water leaf extract — the senolytic "Alpine Rose Active"). [3]
  • Rhododendron Ferrugineum Leaf Cell Culture Extract (the PhytoCellTec callus-culture "stem cell" version). [10]
  • Also seen: Rhododendron Ferrugineum Leaf Extract, …Flower Extract.
  • Forms supplied: water-soluble (glycerin/water) and oil-soluble (caprylic/capric triglyceride) liquids; cell-culture version as a water-soluble powder (with isomalt + lecithin). Standard versions are COSMOS/NATRUE-certified, Ecocert/“bioknospe” organic, alcohol-free, preservative-free. [3][10]
  • Key actives (phytochemistry, from the primary paper): the glycerol/water leaf extract was profiled by 13C-NMR dereplication (CARAMEL) + LC/MS and yielded fourteen identified metabolites, dominated by flavonoids: [1]
  • Taxifolin 3-O-arabinoside (leading flavonoid, two stereoisomers) and free taxifolin (a dihydroflavonol)
  • Hyperoside (quercetin-3-O-galactoside), plus quercetin glycosides (rhamnoside, pentoside, acetyl-hexoside)
  • (+)-Catechin / (−)-epicatechin
  • Flavanones: naringenin, poriol, farrerol (and dihydrofarrerol by LC/MS)
  • Orcinol, methyl-orcellinate, phloroacetophenone, benzoic & isovanillic acids, and the triterpene lupeol
  • The four flavonoids singled out as the probable senolytic drivers: hyperoside, taxifolin, naringenin, farrerol. [1]

2. Class & mechanism

Alpine Rose Active is positioned as the first cosmetic ingredient marketed on the "senolytics" concept. [4][7]

What senolytics actually are (the real science it borrows from): - "Senolytics" was coined by Zhu et al. 2015 (Aging Cell). Senescent cells resist their own pro-apoptotic environment by upregulating senescent-cell anti-apoptotic pathways (SCAPs) — notably the BCL-2 family (BCL-2, BCL-xL, BCL-W). Drugs/molecules that inhibit these tip senescent cells into apoptosis (senolysis) while sparing healthy cells. [12] - BCL-2/BCL-xL inhibition (e.g., navitoclax/ABT-263) selectively kills senescent cells (Yosef 2016; Zhu 2016). [16][17] - Flavonoids are credible senolytic candidates: fisetin is the most potent of 10 flavonoids screened and extends health/lifespan in mice (Yousefzadeh 2018); quercetin (with dasatinib) is the canonical D+Q cocktail. [13][14]

How Alpine Rose is claimed to fit this: 1. The leaf extract's flavonoids — especially farrerol (shown to reduce Bcl-2 in gastric cancer cells), taxifolin (↓Bcl-2 / ↑Bax, Wnt/β-catenin in colorectal cancer), hyperoside (↓Bcl-2 in breast cancer, Bcl-2 docking modeled in silico), and naringenin (pro-apoptotic) — are proposed to act as BCL-2-downregulating senolytics in skin fibroblasts. [1] 2. Structurally, taxifolin and farrerol resemble fisetin, the validated senolytic flavonoid — Mibelle leans heavily on this analogy. [11(SOFW)]

The crucial mechanistic caveat: every one of those Bcl-2/apoptosis citations is from cancer cell lines, not senescent skin cells. The Hubert 2022 paper itself is explicit that it is a phytochemical study correlating composition to a senolytic activity "previously reported" elsewhere — it offers a plausibility argument, not mechanistic proof in skin. The actual senescent-fibroblast killing data live in a separate manufacturer source (see §4). [1][11]

Secondary mechanisms (lower-key, also largely vendor/in-vitro): - Antioxidant: the flavonoid/phenolic load (taxifolin, catechins, quercetin glycosides) is a credible ROS scavenger; vendor claims UVA protein-carbonyl protection (preventing premature stress-induced senescence). [3][4] - Anti-inflammatory: reducing senescent cells should lower the SASP (inflammatory cytokines/proteases) → less "inflammaging"; vendor reads the clinical redness drop as an inflammation proxy. [11] - Barrier / dehydrins (mostly the PhytoCellTec version): dehydrin proteins behave as water-retaining "sponges" protecting cell membranes against cold/dehydration. [9][10]

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

The single most important honesty point: There is one peer-reviewed journal article on this extract — Hubert et al. 2022, Cosmetics (MDPI) — and it contains NO efficacy experiment at all. It is a chemistry/dereplication paper that identifies the molecules and then argues by analogy that they could explain a senolytic effect reported in a trade publication. The actual senolytic assay and the human clinical results are reported only in manufacturer-authored trade-magazine articles (SOFW Journal; Skin Care HPC Today), which are not peer-reviewed RCT literature. All key data are single-source (Mibelle/Helvecos) with declared funding from Mibelle. No independent lab has replicated the senolytic skin effect.

Tier 1 — In vitro (cell models)

  • Hubert, Kotland, Henes, Poigny, Wandrey, 2022, Cosmetics 9(2):37 (DOI 10.3390/cosmetics9020037). Peer-reviewed, open access, Mibelle-funded. Phytochemical profiling of the glycerol/water leaf extract by 13C-NMR (CARAMEL) + high-res LC/MS → 14 metabolites identified, flavonoid-dominated. Concludes that hyperoside, taxifolin, naringenin and farrerol "can explain" the senolytic activity via known pro-apoptotic / Bcl-2-downregulating behavior. Limitations: this paper ran no senolytic, antioxidant, or clinical assay; "Institutional Review Board Statement: Not applicable"; the senolytic activity is cited to ref [7] = a trade article; mechanism is inferred from cancer-cell-line literature, not senescent skin cells. [1]

  • Baumann, Wandrey, Zülli, 2022, SOFW Journal 148(10):28–32 — senolytic assay. Trade journal, manufacturer-authored, not peer-reviewed. This is where the actual numbers live. Method: normal human dermal fibroblasts given 500 µM H₂O₂ × 2 h to induce oxidative-stress premature senescence; after 3 days a mixed culture (28.1% senescent) was treated 48 h with 1% alpine rose extract vs Navitoclax (positive control) vs untreated; senescence-associated β-galactosidase assay, 400 cells counted. Result: senescent fraction fell 28.1% → 10.1% with 1% extract, vs 12% with Navitoclax, with healthy cells largely unaffected — reported as selective senolysis comparable to a pharma senolytic. Note this is a single manufacturer-run assay in a non-peer-reviewed trade journal, so "comparable to Navitoclax" is the vendor's framing of one unreplicated experiment, not an established equivalence. [11]

  • Critical caveats: single in-vitro experiment, one cell type, induced (not naturally aged) senescence, manufacturer lab, trade outlet, no replication, no statistics reported in the article text, no dose-response. Impressive if it holds, but it is the entire in-vitro efficacy basis.

  • Borrowed flavonoid evidence (NOT alpine rose, NOT skin): farrerol → apoptosis/↓Bcl-2 in gastric cancer SGC-7901 (Liu 2015); taxifolin → cell-cycle arrest/↓Bcl-2/↑Bax, tumor regression (Razak 2018); hyperoside → ROS-mediated apoptosis/↓Bcl-2 in breast cancer (Qiu 2019) + in-silico Bcl-2 docking (Pirvu 2018); naringenin → pro-apoptotic synergy (Rakhshan 2021). These underpin the mechanistic story but are cancer-cell, not senescent-skin, data. [1 refs 23–27]

Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

  • None for alpine rose. No animal study of Rhododendron ferrugineum senolytic skin activity was found.
  • Historical pharmacology of the genus: an aqueous leaf/aerial extract showed anti–herpes-simplex-1 activity (Gescher 2011) and anti-Porphyromonas gingivalis adhesion (Löhr 2015); a chloroform leaf extract was cytotoxic to HL-60 leukemia cells (Seephonkai 2011, ferruginenes A–C). These are ethnopharmacology/cytotoxicity, not skin-aging evidence, and the genus also contains toxic grayanotoxins in some species (see §7). [1 refs 4,5,10,11]

Tier 3 — Human ORAL

  • Not applicable / none for alpine rose. Alpine Rose Active is a topical cosmetic; no oral human data exist.
  • Context — senolytics ARE real in humans, but via different molecules/routes: Hickson et al. 2019, EBioMedicine 47:446–456 — first-in-human senolytic trial: oral dasatinib + quercetin (D+Q) in diabetic kidney disease reduced senescent cells in human adipose tissue AND in the epidermal basal layer of skin. This proves the category works in humans — but it is oral D+Q, not topical alpine rose, and quercetin ≠ the alpine-rose flavonoid mix. [15]
  • Fisetin (the flavonoid alpine rose is structurally compared to) extends lifespan in mice (Yousefzadeh 2018) and is in human senolytic trials (e.g., COVID/aging pilots) — again oral, and fisetin itself is not a major component of the alpine rose extract. [13]

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters — and it's a single manufacturer trial in a trade outlet)

  • Baumann/Wandrey/Zülli, 2022, SOFW Journal — clinical redness study. Trade journal, manufacturer-authored, not peer-reviewed; no published statistics/CIs. Randomized, placebo-controlled; 44 female volunteers, age 40–65, with dull complexion + cheek redness; 2% alpine rose extract cream vs placebo, twice daily, whole face, 14 days. Result: statistically significant reduction in skin redness of 8.4% (portable spectrophotometer CM-700d), read as a proxy for reduced SASP-driven inflammation. [11]
  • Critical caveats: small (44), women-only, single-center, manufacturer-run, very short (14 days), only redness reported with a number (the elasticity result is mentioned only as "data not shown" in the companion firmness study — and that firmness trial actually tested the apple stem-cell extract, n=21, not alpine rose). 8.4% redness reduction is a modest effect, and redness is an indirect senescence readout.
  • "Increased elasticity" + "UVA photoaging / protein-carbonyl protection" claims [3][4][6]: these appear on Mibelle's product pages and in trade write-ups but I could not locate published sample sizes, durations, or effect sizes for them; the cosmeticsdesign-europe source that carried the protein-protection claim is now offline (HTTP 410). Treat as unsubstantiated vendor claims until a data sheet with numbers is obtained. [unverified]
  • PhytoCellTec Alp Rose (different product): vendor reports in-vivo barrier protection and anti-wrinkle (crow's-feet) effects, but again without published independent trial detail. [10]

Bottom line by tier: Proven for the senolytic category in humans → oral D+Q (Hickson 2019), oral fisetin in mice. For topical alpine rose specifically → one in-vitro senolysis assay + one 44-person 14-day redness trial, both manufacturer-authored and in trade outlets, plus one peer-reviewed paper that did no efficacy testing. Independent replication: none found.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Actives are small flavonoid aglycones/glycosides (taxifolin ~304 Da, farrerol ~300 Da, naringenin ~272 Da, hyperoside ~464 Da) — within the range that can cross the stratum corneum, especially the more lipophilic aglycones (taxifolin, farrerol, naringenin). Glycosides (hyperoside, taxifolin-arabinoside) are more hydrophilic and penetrate less readily.
  • No published skin-penetration / depth-of-delivery data exist for this extract. The in-vitro senolysis was a direct cell-culture dosing (1% in medium) — it tells us nothing about whether a topical cream delivers enough flavonoid to viable dermis to kill senescent fibroblasts in vivo (fibroblasts sit in the dermis, the hardest target to reach topically). This is the central unproven leap: a dermal senolytic mechanism delivered through a topical cosmetic.
  • The glycerin/water carrier aids spreadability and humectancy but is not a penetration enhancer for dermal delivery. An oil-soluble version exists for lipophilic systems. [3]
  • Practical read: penetration of some flavonoid into the epidermis is plausible; meaningful dermal senolysis from a topical dose is unproven and mechanistically demanding.

6. Formulation

  • Use level: Alpine Rose Active 1–3% (in-vitro active at 1%; clinical cream at 2%). PhytoCellTec Alp Rose 0.4–1%. [3][10][11]
  • pH: No published constraint from the supplier; flavonoid/phenolic extracts are generally most stable in mildly acidic to neutral (≈4–6) ranges, which also suits an anti-redness positioning. Confirm against the specific COA.
  • Stability: Polyphenols/flavonoids are prone to oxidation and color drift (yellow→brown) with light, heat, high pH and metal ions. The glycerin/water matrix is stabilizing; add a chelator (e.g., EDTA/phytate) and consider antioxidant co-stabilizers; protect from light; run stability + challenge testing. The standard grade is supplied preservative-free, so the finished formula must carry the full preservation load.
  • Pairings (conceptual / on-brand):
  • Niacinamide (barrier + anti-redness + the NAD/longevity story) — complementary anti-inflammatory.
  • Other antioxidants (vitamin E, ferulic, resveratrol) — reinforce the oxidative-stress/photoaging angle.
  • Centella/azelaic/soothing actives — support the redness-reduction claim.
  • PhytoCellTec stem-cell extracts (apple/grape) — the "clear-and-fill" SenoCellTec pairing Mibelle itself promotes (see senocelltec.md).
  • Peptides / barrier lipids — fit the brand's repair thesis.
  • Conflicts: strong oxidizers, high pH, and unchelated metal ions (color/stability). No specific incompatibility published; standard polyphenol care applies.
  • Sensory: liquid, easy to incorporate; at use levels color/odor contribution should be low but the extract can carry a faint botanical tint — check in white/anhydrous bases.

7. Safety

  • Irritation/sensitization: No specific irritation or sensitization signal reported at cosmetic use levels; the extract is a low-percentage botanical and the products are positioned for redness-prone/sensitive skin. No independent HRIPT/patch data located — generate our own. [3]
  • Genus toxicity caveat (worth knowing, not alarmist): the genus Rhododendron is famous for grayanotoxins (the "mad honey" neurotoxins) and for general plant toxicity if ingested. R. ferrugineum leaf material has been used medicinally (decoctions) but the plant is not for ingestion, and an ethnopharmacology/toxicology review of the genus exists (Popescu & Kopp 2013). For a topical, low-level, characterized leaf extract this is not a flagged safety problem, but sourcing a well-characterized, toxin-screened, cosmetic-grade extract matters — don't treat "natural alpine flower" as automatically benign. [1 ref 10]
  • Sun sensitivity: none indicated; mechanistically the antioxidant/photoaging-protection story is the opposite of photosensitizing. No human photo-data. [4]
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: no topical pregnancy data; flavonoid antioxidants are generally low-concern topically, but with no data, use conservative copy ("consult your doctor"). Do not extrapolate the oral senolytic-drug cautions (D+Q, navitoclax have real systemic toxicities) to a topical botanical — but also don't imply the topical does what those drugs do.
  • Sourcing/sustainability: wild-harvested in the Swiss Alps under organic "bioknospe" (pesticide-free); leaves regenerate the following year (claimed sustainable). The cell-culture (PhytoCellTec) version avoids wild harvesting entirely and is the more defensible sustainability story. [1][10]

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic ingredient status: straightforward botanical INCI (Rhododendron Ferrugineum Extract / …Leaf Cell Culture Extract); listed in EWG Skin Deep and cosmetic ingredient dictionaries. No special restriction found for topical cosmetic use. [18]
  • The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame) — this ingredient is a claims minefield. The entire value proposition is built on "senolytics," "eliminating senescent/zombie cells," "rejuvenation," "anti-aging at the cellular level." Telling consumers a cream removes cells, kills senescent cells, or reverses aging is squarely structure/function (drug) language, and importing the medical senolytic literature (D+Q, fisetin lifespan extension) into topical claims is the central legal trap. "Zombie cell"/"senolytic" copy also invites substantiation demands the current single-source evidence can't meet.
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "inspired by senolytic / cellular-longevity science," "an Alpine botanical rich in protective flavonoids (taxifolin, farrerol)," "helps visibly reduce the look of redness," "supports a more even, calmer-looking complexion," "helps defend skin against environmental/oxidative stress," "helps skin look more elastic/rejuvenated." Keep even the redness claim modest — the only number is 8.4% in 14 days from a manufacturer trial.
  • ⚠️ Risky / substantiation-or-legal-review: "removes/clears senescent (zombie) cells from your skin," "senolytic," "kills aged cells," "reverses aging," "rejuvenates at the cellular level," "increases elasticity by X%," any borrowing of fisetin/D+Q lifespan claims. None are supported for purified topical human use by independent evidence.
  • Substantiation reality: to make real performance claims we'd need our own finished-formula human testing; the existing literature (one trade redness study + one no-efficacy chemistry paper) will not substantiate a "senolytic serum." See ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Primary supplier: Mibelle Group Biochemistry (Switzerland) owns both branded versions (Alpine Rose Active; PhytoCellTec Alp Rose), developed with Helvecos AG. Distributed through standard personal-care channels (UL Prospector, regional distributors). This is effectively a single-source / branded active, which is a strategic dependency (price, supply, and the marketing story are all controlled by one vendor).
  • Cost tier: mid-to-premium — a branded, certified-organic Swiss alpine active with a patented/marketed concept commands a premium over commodity botanical extracts. The cell-culture (PhytoCellTec) version typically costs more than the simple leaf extract.
  • Supply/standardization risk: flavonoid content of wild-harvested leaf material varies with season/altitude; rely on the supplier COA for standardization. Being single-source means no easy second supplier for the branded story (a generic Rhododendron ferrugineum leaf extract could be sourced, but without Mibelle's senolytic data or marketing).

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • "The first senolytic skincare ingredient." True as a marketing first, but "senolytic" implies the drug-grade mechanism (selective senescent-cell killing) demonstrated for oral D+Q in humans and fisetin in mice — not validated for this topical botanical by any independent study.
  • The peer-reviewed paper proves it works — false. Hubert 2022 is a chemistry paper with no efficacy experiment; it explicitly says senolytic activity was "previously reported" (in a trade article) and only argues which molecules could be responsible. A peer-reviewed citation here is doing less work than it appears to.
  • The evidence is single-source. In-vitro senolysis and the clinical redness result both come from Mibelle-authored trade-journal articles (SOFW; HPC Today), manufacturer-funded, never independently replicated. That's the weakest tier of "clinical" support.
  • The mechanism is borrowed from cancer biology. Farrerol/taxifolin/hyperoside ↓Bcl-2 data are from tumor cell lines, not senescent skin fibroblasts. Plausible analogy ≠ demonstrated skin senolysis.
  • The fisetin halo. "Structurally similar to fisetin" is doing heavy lifting; fisetin is the validated senolytic flavonoid — and it is barely present, if at all, in the alpine rose extract. Similar ≠ same.
  • Dermal delivery hand-wave. Senescent fibroblasts live in the dermis; a 1% in-dish result says nothing about whether a topical cream delivers enough flavonoid that deep. No penetration data exist.
  • Conflated products. Bloggers mix the senolytic leaf extract with the PhytoCellTec stem-cell version and with the SenoCellTec blend; their evidence and claims are different. Keep them separate.
  • Modest real number. The one quantified human result is −8.4% redness over 14 days — pleasant and on-trend for sensitive skin, but a long way from "clears zombie cells and reverses aging."

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ SUPPORTING / CONDITIONAL — superb longevity narrative, thin independent evidence. One-line: Alpine Rose extract is arguably the most on-theme botanical for a skin-longevity brand — it lets us talk credibly about cellular senescence and "senolytics," the hottest hallmark in aging biology — but its skin-efficacy proof is single-supplier, in-vitro + one short trade-journal redness trial, so use it as a science-forward supporting/story active with strictly honest copy, not as an evidence-backed hero, and ideally validate it in our own finished-formula testing before making any performance claim.

Why it's tempting (and on-brand): the senescence/senolytics hallmark is central to the longevity thesis, the Alpine provenance and flavonoid chemistry are genuinely distinctive, and the soothing/anti-redness angle pairs naturally with a barrier-repair cream and sensitive-skin positioning. It is a far better narrative differentiator than most "stem cell" botanicals.

Why it can't be a hero today: the headline "senolytic" claim is mechanistically borrowed (cancer cells, oral drugs, fisetin analogy), the human data are one 44-person 14-day manufacturer redness study, the peer-reviewed paper ran no efficacy test, there's no independent replication, no penetration data, and the whole concept is single-source (Mibelle) — both an evidence weakness and a supply/strategic dependency. The claims surface is also legally hazardous (senolytic = drug-flavored language).

Longevity-relevance note: Among botanicals, this has one of the cleanest conceptual ties to a true hallmark of aging (senescent-cell clearance), which Hickson 2019 confirms is a real, achievable target in human skin — but via oral D+Q, not this cream. That gap (validated category, unvalidated specific topical) is exactly the spermidine situation in this repo: a brilliant education/differentiation ingredient whose honest use is mechanism-framed, not proof-claimed. If we want real "senolytic" claims, the path is our own human testing (and possibly a delivery-enhanced form), not the existing literature.

Suggested role: supporting/“story” active in a soothing or barrier-repair product (e.g., 1–3% in a calming cream), with copy hedged to redness/“rejuvenated look”/“inspired by senolytic science,” pending our own substantiation. Consider pairing with, or choosing between, this and the other repo senolytics (eterwell-youth-epilobium.md, senocelltec.md) so the brand isn't over-indexed on one single-supplier story.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified: 2026-06-19. Independent check of every load-bearing citation via web search + primary-source fetch (MDPI, ResearchGate/Semantic Scholar full text, PubMed/PMC, Mibelle media downloads). Findings below; the dossier's own honesty framing held up well and only minor softenings were needed.

Overall grade: C → leaning C/D for the headline "senolytic" claim

  • C (weak / indirect) is the fair grade for alpine rose as a soothing/anti-redness antioxidant botanical: there is one real human topical signal (−8.4% redness, 14 days), but it is small, short, single-center, manufacturer-run, and published only in a trade journal.
  • D (unproven topically / hype) is the fair grade for the specific "senolytic — clears senescent/zombie cells from your skin" claim: there is zero independent, human, topical evidence that this cream performs senolysis in skin. The category is real in humans (Hickson 2019, oral D+Q), but that is borrowed evidence, not evidence for this product.

This is not an A or B ingredient. No independent human-topical RCT exists. Every efficacy datapoint is single-supplier (Mibelle/Helvecos).

Main caveats (for the brand)

  1. Single-source evidence. Every efficacy datapoint traces to Mibelle/Helvecos. No independent lab has replicated topical senolysis. This is both an evidence weakness and a supply/strategic dependency.
  2. Trade journals ≠ RCTs. The two papers that actually contain numbers (SOFW; HPC Today) are manufacturer-authored and not peer-reviewed. The one peer-reviewed paper (Hubert 2022) contains no efficacy experiment.
  3. Borrowed mechanism + borrowed humans. "Senolytic" rests on cancer-cell Bcl-2 data + a fisetin structural analogy + oral-D+Q human data — none of which is topical alpine rose in skin.
  4. Dermal-delivery gap unaddressed by any data. A 1% in-dish result says nothing about whether a cream reaches dermal fibroblasts. No penetration data exist.
  5. A newer 2024 peer-reviewed paper exists but does not help the topical case — it studies the SenoCellTec blend in-vitro (RNA splicing in dermal stem cells), is Mibelle-sponsored, and is neither a senolysis nor a human/topical study (added as ref 21; belongs to senocelltec.md).

Claims to soften / already softened

  • Do not call the in-vitro senolysis or the redness number "clinical proof" of senolysis — they are an induced-senescence cell assay and an indirect redness proxy, both manufacturer/trade-tier.
  • Keep the only defensible performance number as −8.4% redness over 14 days (manufacturer trial) and frame all senolytic/longevity language as "inspired by" science, per §8. The dossier already does this; edits below tighten two spots that slightly over-credited the in-vitro result.
