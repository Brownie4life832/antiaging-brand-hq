Ingredient: Ectoin (+ Hydroxyectoine) (INCI: Ectoin; Hydroxyectoine)

Researched: 2026-06-19 (upgraded from 2026-06-18 draft) · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Humectants & hydration actives (more precisely: extremolyte / compatible-solute cell protectant) Evidence verdict at a glance: Genuinely good evidence for hydration + barrier + soothing; the strongest human RCT evidence is for medical-device topical (atopic dermatitis) and inhaled/nasal respiratory use, NOT for cosmetic anti-wrinkle skin use. Anti-aging / photoprotection claims rest on in-vitro + mechanism + a small number of manufacturer-linked cosmetic trials. Honest grade: moderate topical viability.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Ectoine (also spelled "Ectoin," the trademarked cosmetic-grade form from bitop AG / now part of the Givaudan-adjacent extremolyte supply chain).
  • INCI: Ectoin (chemically: (4S)-2-methyl-1,4,5,6-tetrahydropyrimidine-4-carboxylic acid; a cyclic amino-acid derivative / tetrahydropyrimidine). CAS 96702-03-3.
  • Key derivative: Hydroxyectoine (INCI: Hydroxyectoine; CAS 165542-15-4) — the 5-hydroxy analog. Hydroxyectoine is often a more potent thermo/desiccation stabilizer than ectoine in microbiology, and the two are frequently co-produced; in cosmetics ectoine dominates, but hydroxyectoine appears in some "next-gen extremolyte" blends. Mechanistically they are siblings.
  • Class of molecule: "Compatible solute" / "extremolyte" — an osmolyte produced by halophilic/extremophilic microbes (e.g. Halomonas elongata) to survive high salt, heat, desiccation, and radiation. Discovered 1985 in the bacterium Ectothiorhodospira halochloris from an Egyptian (Wadi El Natrun) salt lake.
  • Physical: White, odorless, water-soluble crystalline powder. Used as a single ingredient or as fermentation lysates (e.g. "Thermus thermophilus + ectoine" cofermentation products).

2. Class & mechanism

Ectoine is a kosmotropic compatible solute. Its biology is unusually well understood for a cosmetic active:

  • Preferential exclusion / preferential hydration. Ectoine is preferentially excluded from the immediate surface of proteins and membranes. This forces water to be retained at the biomolecular surface, building a structured "hydration shell" (the marketed "Ectoine Hydro Complex"). The net thermodynamic effect stabilizes the folded/native state of proteins and the ordered state of lipid bilayers — i.e. it protects macromolecules and membranes against stress (heat, dryness, osmotic shock, surfactants, UV-driven damage) without binding them directly. This is textbook compatible-solute behavior and is the core, real mechanism.
  • Water binding / humectancy. By structuring and holding water, it acts as a humectant in the stratum corneum and reduces water loss — the basis of the hydration/TEWL benefits.
  • Membrane stabilization. It orders and stabilizes lipid bilayers and has been shown in vitro to protect cell membranes from surfactant-induced damage (Graf et al. 2008).
  • Anti-inflammatory signaling. In airway/lung models, ectoine blunts ceramide-/lipid-raft-dependent inflammatory signaling triggered by particulate matter (carbon nanoparticles), reducing downstream neutrophilic inflammation (Sydlik/Unfried group). This is the proposed basis for "pollution protection."
  • Photoprotection (mechanistic). In keratinocytes, ectoine reduced UVA-induced second-messenger release, AP-2 activation, ICAM-1 expression, and mitochondrial DNA mutation (Buenger & Driller 2004 — in-vitro keratinocytes, Merck-authored). It is described as a singlet-oxygen quencher / indirect antioxidant. (A frequently-repeated "prevents UV-induced loss of Langerhans cells" claim is NOT found in the abstracts of either Buenger & Driller 2004 or the Graf 2008 review and could not be sourced — treat it as [unverified] and do not use it.) Note: it is not a UV filter — it does not absorb UV or replace SPF.

One-line mental model: Ectoine doesn't "repair" or "regenerate" — it is a protective hydration shield that helps skin cells and the barrier withstand environmental stress and hold water.

Bottom line on hallmarks: Ectoine is a resilience/defense and hydration/barrier actor. It does not credibly target the "regenerative" hallmarks (senescence, collagen synthesis, glycation) in any human-proven way.

4. Evidence review by tier (core of the dossier)

The single most important honest framing: ectoine has a large clinical literature, but most of the strongest human RCT evidence is in respiratory (nasal/inhaled) and medical-device atopic-dermatitis indications — not cosmetic anti-aging on healthy skin. Benefits proven for one format do not automatically transfer to a cosmetic face cream. Below, formats are kept strictly separate.

Tier 1 — In-vitro / ex-vivo (mechanism; abundant, foundational, not proof of cosmetic benefit)

  • Buenger & Driller, 2004, Skin Pharmacol Physiol 17(5):232–237 — "UVA stress model" in human keratinocytes/skin: ectoine prevented UVA-induced ceramide release, second-messenger/AP-2 activation, ICAM-1 expression, and mitochondrial DNA mutation. Limitation: cell/ex-vivo model; mechanistic, not a clinical outcome.
  • Graf, Anzali, Buenger, Pfluecker, Driller, 2008, Clin Dermatol 26(4):326–333 (review of in-vitro + early in-vivo) — membrane protection from surfactants; preferential-exclusion mechanism; in-vivo TEWL reduction after an O/W ectoine emulsion; "superior moisturizer with long-term efficacy." Limitation: review by manufacturer-affiliated authors; mixes mechanism with marketing.
  • Wang et al., 2025, J Cosmet Dermatol (PMC12186289) — Ectoine–Thermus thermophilus cofermentation product ("D-Ectoine," 20% ectoine + lysates) in human dermal fibroblasts and a 3D epidermal model (EpiKutis): enhanced GSH-PX (note: SOD and CAT were NOT significantly changed vs control — the dossier's earlier "SOD/CAT/GSH-PX" wording overstated this), suppressed LPS-induced IL-6/IL-8, promoted collagen I, inhibited UVB-induced MMP-1, reduced 8-OHdG. Limitation: purely lab/tissue models; tests a blend (and a 20%-ectoine one), not the 0.5–2% pure ectoine we'd use; no human data; supplier-affiliated authors (Umotor).
  • Carbon-nanoparticle / lung-cell signaling work (Sydlik et al.; Part Fibre Toxicol 2012, 9:48) — ectoine interrupts ceramide-/lipid-raft signaling that drives particle-induced inflammation. Relevance to skin: mechanistic analogy only.

Tier 2 — Animal

  • Sydlik/Unfried group, multiple papers (2009–2015) — in rats and mice, ectoine reduced carbon-nanoparticle-induced neutrophilic lung inflammation and downstream allergic sensitization (e.g. PLoS One / Part Fibre Toxicol / PMC4491258, 2015). Relevance to skin: this is the mechanistic and dosing basis for the human inhalation work; it is lung, not skin.
  • No strong, independent rodent skin photoaging model is the anchor of the cosmetic claims — the cosmetic case leans on in-vitro + human cosmetic tests, which is a weakness.

Tier 3 — Human, ORAL

  • No meaningful oral ectoine evidence for skin. (The 2025 "antiaging" trial below pairs oral probiotics with topical ectoine — the oral arm is probiotics, not ectoine.) Treat oral ectoine for skin as unsupported.

Tier 4a — Human, RESPIRATORY (inhaled / nasal) RCTs — the strongest controlled human data, but NOT skin

  • Unfried et al., 2016, Int J COPD 2016;11:2573–2583 (PMC5076798) — double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover; 33 elderly women (mean ~76 y, mild COPD); inhaled 1.3% ectoine in saline, 2.5 mL/day, 28 days/phase, 7-wk washout. Results: the pre-specified primary outcome (reduction of sputum neutrophils and IL-8) was NOT met in the combined analysis — neutrophil % p≈0.31, IL-8 p≈0.68; FEV1, FVC, QoL also not significantly changed. The one significant signal was sputum nitrate/nitrite (quotient 0.65, 95% CI 0.45–0.93, p≈0.02), and an ancillary, period-1-only analysis suggested a neutrophil reduction. Significant period/carryover effects (neutrophils p≈0.08; IL-8 p≈0.06) confounded interpretation. Funded by bitop AG (manufacturer); one author employed by bitop, group grant-/consultancy-funded by sponsor. [Corrected 2026-06-19: earlier draft stated neutrophil p=0.035 as a clean positive result; the source reports the primary neutrophil endpoint as non-significant.]
  • Allergic rhinitis / rhinosinusitis trials (Werkhäuser, Bilstein & Sonnemann 2014, J Allergy (Cairo); Eichel et al. 2014, controlled vs. comparator; Bilstein 2021 review, BioMed Res Int) — ectoine nasal spray/eye drops non-inferior or comparable to azelastine/cromoglycate for symptom relief. Mostly non-inferiority / observational / comparator-controlled (not all placebo-RCT), several industry-linked.
  • Throat/pharyngitis & bronchitis sprays/inhalations (e.g. Eur Arch Otorhinolaryngol 2016; observational bronchitis study 2019) — supportive but lower-tier (observational/comparator).
  • Why this matters for us: it proves ectoine is safe on mucosa and biologically active as an anti-inflammatory in humans — credibility-supporting — but says little directly about cosmetic facial outcomes.

Tier 4b — Human, TOPICAL SKIN — medical-device / dermatology (best on-skin human data)

  • Marini, Reinelt, Krutmann & Bilstein, 2014 (online 2013), Skin Pharmacol Physiol 27(2):57–65 — randomized, intra-individual, double-blind, comparator-controlled, multicenter; n=65 adults, mild-moderate atopic dermatitis; ectoine cream (5.5–7%) vs. a non-steroidal barrier comparator, twice daily 28 days. Result: ectoine equivalent to comparator on SCORAD/IGA/pruritus; well tolerated. Limitation: no placebo arm; comparator (not vehicle) controlled; AD, not anti-aging; manufacturer-affiliated authors.
  • Alexopoulos et al., 2023, Pediatr Dermatol (PMID 36038984) — randomized, observer-blind, vehicle-controlled, multicenter; children 2–18 y, mild-moderate AD; 1% ectoine + 0.1% hyaluronic acid medical device (ECZAID) vs. vehicle, twice daily 4 wks. Result: EHA cream superior to vehicle; minor adverse events. Limitation: combo product (ectoine + HA); short; calls for long-term data; device, not cosmetic.
  • Kauth & Trusova, 2022, Dermatol Ther (Heidelb) — systematic review (PubMed/Cochrane through Oct 2021): 6 clinical studies, 525 patients (447 AD, 78 retinoid dermatitis); ectoine 5.5–7%. SCORAD improvements (e.g. Wilkowska 42→15; Marini 7.5→3.8); TEWL improved in Hon et al. (p=0.035); hydration +15.1% vs +7.3% control (Tlish & Shavilova); reduced need for pharmacotherapy in 2 pediatric studies. Certainty of evidence graded "very low" to "moderate"; authors flag heterogeneity, small/underpowered samples, and call explicitly for high-quality RCTs. Excellent tolerability (1.5% dropouts for transient stinging). This is the most useful single summary of on-skin human evidence — but it is NOT independent: lead author M. Kauth is an employee of bitop AG (the ectoine maker) and bitop funded the publication. Treat its conclusions as manufacturer-aligned even though it honestly grades the evidence as low. (525-patient total reported by the dossier could not be independently re-verified from open abstracts; the 6-study / 5-AD + 1-retinoid / 5.5–7% structure is confirmed.)
  • CO2-laser recovery (Molecules 2025, MDPI 30(11):2470) — ectoine applied post-ablative-laser improved skin parameters (hydration/erythema recovery). Small, niche, post-procedure.

Tier 4c — Human, TOPICAL SKIN — cosmetic / anti-aging on healthy skin (the claim most relevant to us — and the thinnest)

  • Heinrich, Garbe & Tronnier, 2007, Skin Pharmacol Physiol 2007;20(4):211–218 — "In vivo assessment of Ectoin: a randomized, vehicle-controlled clinical trial"; monocentric, double-blind; n=104 healthy women; 2% ectoine vs. vehicle vs. untreated; assessed moisturization, skin-surface structure, elasticity, signs of aging. Important caveat: the headline result is that subjects preferred ectoine on Wilcoxon rank-sum analysis — i.e. a subjective preference signal more than a battery of hard, quantified instrumental endpoints with effect sizes. No adverse effects. Limitation: the foundational cosmetic RCT, but single-center, conducted at a dermatology test institute associated with ingredient/cosmetic testing, with limited public numeric detail and healthy-skin cosmetic endpoints; reads as efficacy-supportive but not a strong, magnitude-quantified anti-aging proof.
  • Nobile et al. (Complife Italia), 2025, Cosmetics 12(2):34 — randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, n=96, mild-moderate facial aging; oral probiotics + topical (ectoine + sodium hyaluronate), 8 wks. Results: hydration +16.6% / moisturization up to +21.4% in combined group; TEWL ~5.8× greater decrease vs placebo; wrinkle depth −9.2% and radiance +19.1% at 1 h post-application. Major limitation: the topical (ectoine+HA) effect cannot be isolated from the oral probiotic and the HA; Caucasian 45–65 only; no durability follow-up; industry-conducted.

What is genuinely proven for TOPICAL HUMAN SKIN cosmetic use: - ✅ Hydration increase and TEWL reduction — real, repeatedly observed (though best numbers come from combo/medical-device formats and manufacturer trials). - ✅ Soothing / very high tolerability on compromised, sensitive, post-procedure and pediatric skin — strong, consistent. - ⚠️ Barrier support — supported, partly via AD/device data and TEWL. - ❓ Anti-wrinkle / firming / "anti-aging" — weak and confounded (combo products, manufacturer trials, short-term/1-hour measurements that reflect plumping/hydration, not true remodeling). - ❌ Borrowed-not-proven for cosmetic skin: "pollution/PM2.5 protection," "UV/photoprotection," "DNA protection," "anti-inflammatory" — these come from lung/in-vitro work; do not present them as proven facial-skincare outcomes.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Ectoine is small (MW ~142) and highly water-soluble/hydrophilic — which means it functions largely at and within the stratum corneum (humectant, hydration shell, surface/upper-layer protection) rather than needing deep dermal delivery for its core hydration/barrier benefit.
  • High hydrophilicity also limits deep transdermal penetration through the lipid barrier; the proven benefits (hydration, TEWL, soothing, surface protection) are consistent with predominantly upper-epidermal action, so deep penetration is not required for its evidenced effects.
  • There is little dedicated, peer-reviewed human penetration/pharmacokinetic data for cosmetic ectoine specifically — claims about "reaching living cells" are inferred from in-vitro keratinocyte models, not from quantified in-vivo skin-penetration studies. Mark deep-cellular delivery claims as [partly unverified].

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: Cosmetic serums/creams typically 0.3–2%; the cosmetic RCT used 2%. Medical-device/dermatology formats use 5.5–7% (AD) or 1% + 0.1% HA (pediatric device). For a cosmetic hydration/barrier active, 0.5–2% is the sensible, evidence-aligned range; going higher mainly matters for therapeutic (drug/device) positioning we should avoid.
  • pH: Broadly pH-tolerant and stable across the cosmetically relevant range; compatible with low-pH (vitamin C) and higher-pH systems.
  • Stability: Excellent — thermally stable, oxidation-resistant, long shelf life. One of the easiest "premium" actives to formulate. Water phase, add cold or hot.
  • Pairings (synergistic / compatible): Hyaluronic acid and glycerin (layered humectancy), ceramides and niacinamide (barrier), panthenol/allantoin (soothing), peptides, and antioxidants. Pairs well alongside (not instead of) SPF for an "environmental defense" story. Combo with HA is the best-evidenced pairing.
  • Conflicts: No notable incompatibilities reported. Plays nicely with most actives — a genuine advantage.
  • Sensory: Light, non-tacky, odorless, colorless; adds a comfortable "cushioned" feel without heaviness. Excellent for sensitive-skin sensory.

7. Safety

  • Irritation/sensitization: Excellent profile. In the systematic review (Kauth & Trusova 2022), only ~1.5% dropped out for transient stinging/tingling/redness, mostly resolving after first use; no serious adverse events. Used safely on infants, children, atopic and post-procedure skin, and on mucosa (nasal/ocular sprays).
  • Sun sensitivity: None — it is photo-stable and, if anything, photo-protective in vitro. But it is not a sunscreen and must not be positioned as sun protection.
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: No specific contraindication identified; it is a benign osmolyte used in pediatric and OTC products. (Standard caution: defer to a clinician; not a known risk.)
  • Sensitization/allergens: No recognized allergen status; no fragrance/preservative concerns intrinsic to the molecule.
  • Sourcing/sustainability: Produced by microbial fermentation (classically Halomonas elongata via "bacterial milking" — osmotic-shock release into low-salt medium; increasingly via engineered E. coli/other hosts). Vegan, non-animal, biotech-fermentation origin — a genuine sustainability/clean-biotech story. Caveat: traditional high-salinity fermentation creates briny wastewater/corrosion challenges (an industrial, not consumer, issue).

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic vs. drug/device line is the key risk here. Much of ectoine's strongest evidence sits in medical-device (AD creams, nasal sprays) and therapeutic contexts. If our copy borrows that language ("treats eczema/dermatitis," "treats allergic rhinitis," "anti-inflammatory therapy," "reduces lung inflammation"), we cross into drug/device claims that require registration/substantiation. Keep strictly to cosmetic appearance/feel language.
  • ✅ Safe cosmetic language: "deep, long-lasting hydration," "helps the skin hold onto moisture," "helps strengthen and protect the look and feel of the skin barrier," "helps skin stay comfortable and resilient against daily environmental stress," "soothing," "supports a healthy-looking, well-hydrated barrier," "derived from extremophile biology."
  • ⚠️ Risky / needs substantiation or avoid: "protects against pollution / blue light / UV" (protection = often borrowed from lung/in-vitro; UV/sun protection is drug/SPF territory), "repairs DNA," "anti-inflammatory," "boosts collagen," "treats/soothes eczema or dermatitis," "antioxidant that prevents aging." If we want any "environmental defense" angle, soften to appearance/resilience and be ready to cite — and never imply sun protection.
  • Maintain claims discipline per ../../brand/glossary.md: "supports / helps / visibly," not "treats / prevents / boosts X%."

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: Mid. Markedly pricier than glycerin/basic humectants; cheaper than exotic peptides, growth factors, or rare longevity actives. At 0.5–2% use levels the cost-in-formula is modest for the credibility it buys.
  • Supply: Reliable, scalable fermentation; multiple suppliers; cosmetic-grade widely available (bitop's "Ectoin natural" is the reference grade; commodity and Asian fermentation sources exist). Hydroxyectoine is available but less common in cosmetics and somewhat pricier.
  • Value: Strong value-for-evidence among hydration actives — one of the few where the marketing story and the science substantially align for hydration/barrier.

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • "Up to 200% hydration increase" / "reduces TEWL 15–72%" / "−38% redness in days": These specific eye-catching numbers circulate widely on supplier/brand blogs and trace back to manufacturer materials and best-case single studies — treat as cherry-picked, not consensus. The direction (hydration up, TEWL down) is real; the headline magnitudes are marketing-grade.
  • "Pollution / PM2.5 / blue-light protection": The robust data is in lungs/airways and in-vitro cells, not facial skin clinical trials. Borrowing it for a face cream is the most common overreach.
  • "UV / photoaging protection": In-vitro and mechanistic; ectoine is not a UV filter and never replaces SPF. Claiming sun/photo protection is both unsubstantiated for cosmetic skin and a regulatory trap.
  • "Anti-wrinkle / collagen boost": Wrinkle-depth and collagen signals come from 1-hour post-application plumping, combo products (ectoine+HA+probiotics), or in-vitro — not from isolated long-term ectoine remodeling. Hydration-driven smoothing ≠ structural anti-aging.
  • "Microbiome support": Plausible and on-trend, but skin-microbiome clinical evidence for ectoine specifically is thin.
  • Conflict-of-interest pattern (pervasive): The favorable ectoine literature is dominated by manufacturer authorship/funding. Verified examples: the foundational mechanism papers (Buenger & Driller 2004; Graf et al. 2008) are Merck KGaA-authored; the COPD trial (Unfried 2016) is bitop AG-funded with a bitop employee author; the cofermentation paper (Wang 2025) involves the D-Ectoine supplier (Umotor); the pediatric AD device trial (Alexopoulos 2023) is funded by the device maker (BODERM SA); and even the systematic review (Kauth & Trusova 2022) has a bitop-employee author and bitop funding — so there is no large, fully-independent clinical anchor. Weight all efficacy magnitudes accordingly; the most reassuring independent signal is the broad safety/tolerability record (corroborated across many settings), not the efficacy claims.

Net: Ectoine is one of the more honest "trendy" actives — but only if we sell what's proven (hydration, barrier comfort, soothing, resilience) and resist the borrowed protection/anti-aging halo.

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️→✅ SUPPORTING (strong supporting, not hero). One line: Ectoine is a low-risk, easy-to-formulate hydration/barrier/soothing active with genuinely solid human safety and tolerability and real (if modest) hydration/TEWL evidence — an excellent credibility- and comfort-builder for a barrier-repair formula, but not a longevity headliner and not a basis for pollution/UV/anti-wrinkle claims.

  • Why supporting and not hero: Its proven benefits (hydration, barrier, soothing) overlap with cheaper, equally-evidenced actives (glycerin, HA, niacinamide, panthenol); its distinctive value is the "extremophile resilience" narrative plus excellent tolerability — story and feel, more than a unique clinical effect.
  • Why not skip: It genuinely fits the barrier-repair cream thesis, is exceptionally safe for sensitive/compromised skin, formulates trivially, and lets us tell a differentiated, science-true resilience story.
  • Longevity-relevance note: Maps to the resilience/defense side of skin-longevity (helping cells and the barrier withstand environmental stress and stay hydrated), not the regenerative side (senescence, collagen, glycation). Frame it as "helping skin stay resilient," never as a regenerative/anti-aging hero.
  • Recommended use: 0.5–2%, paired with HA + ceramides/niacinamide in a barrier/sensitive-skin format, claims kept to hydration/comfort/resilience.

Are the studies real, and do they say what's claimed?

All 18 sources are real and resolve; designs, sample sizes, concentrations and durations are accurately reported. The dossier's tier structure (in-vitro / animal / oral / respiratory / topical-medical / topical-cosmetic) is honest and is the single best thing about it — most overreach in the wider literature comes from collapsing those tiers, and this dossier does not. Three specific accuracy errors were found and corrected (below).

Main caveats

  1. No independent clinical anchor exists. Every efficacy study cited is manufacturer-authored or manufacturer-funded: mechanism papers = Merck KGaA; COPD trial = bitop AG (employee author); cofermentation = Umotor (supplier); pediatric AD device = BODERM SA; and the "systematic review" = bitop (employee author M. Kauth + bitop-funded). The only thing corroborated across truly varied, lower-COI settings is safety/tolerability, not efficacy magnitude.
  2. Evidence-type laundering risk. "Pollution/PM2.5 protection," "DNA protection," "anti-inflammatory," and "photoprotection" are lung-cell / in-vitro findings, not facial-skin outcomes. The dossier already flags this; keep it flagged hard.
  3. Format ≠ transfer. The strongest controlled human data are respiratory (mucosa) and AD (compromised skin) — they prove ectoine is safe and bioactive, not that a cosmetic face cream delivers anti-aging benefit.
  4. Small, short studies. Cosmetic/AD trials are n≈65–104, ≤4–8 weeks, with no long-term remodeling data.

Claims corrected in the dossier body (this pass)

  • Unfried 2016 neutrophil result: earlier text said neutrophils were "significantly lower (p=0.035)." The pre-specified primary endpoint (neutrophils + IL-8) was NOT met (neutrophil p≈0.31; IL-8 p≈0.68); only sputum nitrate/nitrite reached significance (p≈0.02), with significant period/carryover effects. Corrected.
  • Kauth & Trusova 2022 called "independent": it is not — lead author is a bitop AG employee and bitop funded it. References to it as the "independent" anchor were removed/relabeled as manufacturer-aligned. Corrected.
  • "Prevents UV-induced loss of Langerhans cells" (Buenger & Driller 2004 / Graf 2008): not present in either source's abstract; could not be sourced. Marked [unverified]; do not use.
  • Wang 2025 antioxidant claim: "higher antioxidant capacity (SOD/CAT/GSH-PX)" overstated — only GSH-PX rose significantly; SOD and CAT were not significantly changed. Corrected; also clarified it tests a 20% blend, not the 0.5–2% pure ectoine we'd use.
  • Heinrich 2007 missing volume/pages added (20(4):211–218); reframed as a subjective-preference result, not quantified anti-aging proof.
