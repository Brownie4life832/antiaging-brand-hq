Ingredient: Galactomyces / Saccharomyces (Pitera) Ferment Filtrate (INCI: Galactomyces Ferment Filtrate; Saccharomyces Ferment Filtrate)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product Class: Autophagy & mitochondrial-targeted actives (yeast-ferment postbiotic; AHR/NRF2-axis antioxidant & barrier conditioner)

One-line honest summary: A yeast-fermentation "postbiotic" broth (the SK-II "Pitera" molecule) with a genuinely interesting, AHR/NRF2-anchored mechanistic story for barrier, antioxidant defense and pigment/tone — but the human evidence is almost entirely manufacturer-affiliated, open-label (no placebo/vehicle arm), and the mechanism is "antioxidant/barrier," not actually autophagy or mitochondria despite the taxonomy slot it was filed under. Credible supporting/feel-good barrier-and-glow ingredient, not an evidence-backed longevity hero, and not a clean fit for an "autophagy/mitochondrial" narrative.

1. Identity

  • Common names: Galactomyces ferment filtrate (GFF); Saccharomyces ferment filtrate; "Pitera" (SK-II's trademarked name); "sake yeast filtrate"; galactomyces "essence." Often marketed as a postbiotic or ferment.
  • INCI:
  • Galactomyces Ferment Filtrate (COSING/CIR; the form with the strongest published science)
  • Saccharomyces Ferment Filtrate (a distinct INCI; chemically related yeast-ferment broth, far less studied in skin)
  • Related/adjacent INCIs in the same family: Saccharomyces/Saccharomycopsis Ferment Filtrate, Saccharomyces Cerevisiae Extract, Bifida Ferment Lysate (different organism — do not conflate).
  • Naming history — important and genuinely confusing: SK-II originally listed "Pitera" as Saccharomyces / Saccharomycopsis ferment filtrate; on later taxonomic re-analysis the yeast was reclassified and the INCI changed to Galactomyces ferment filtrate. So "Saccharomyces (Pitera)" and "Galactomyces (Pitera)" in marketing refer to the same product lineage at different times, but Saccharomyces Ferment Filtrate and Galactomyces Ferment Filtrate are separate INCIs today with very different evidence bases. [12][13]
  • What it physically is: the filtrate (clarified liquid) left after a yeast (genus Galactomyces, historically tied to sake/rice-wine brewing) ferments a substrate; the yeast cells are removed, leaving a watery broth of yeast metabolites — small peptides/amino acids, oligosaccharides, organic acids (e.g., pyruvate/lactate-type), B-vitamins, minerals, and trace antioxidants. "Postbiotic" is the accurate category (metabolites of microbes, no live organisms). [3][6][11]
  • Composition reality check: It is an undefined, lot-variable mixture — "~50 micronutrients" is a marketing figure, not a characterized actives panel. Most of the filtrate is water; the bioactive fraction is small and not standardized to any single marker compound. This is a core scientific limitation, not a footnote. [6][11][14]
  • Physical/chemistry notes: water-like, mildly acidic-to-neutral, water-soluble; used at very high "as-supplied" percentages (10–100%) precisely because it is mostly water. [11][14]

2. Class & mechanism (how it's claimed to work vs. what's shown)

Taxonomy caveat first (be honest in copy and internally): this ingredient was filed in the brand's master index under "Autophagy & mitochondrial-targeted actives," presumably riding the fermentation/longevity halo. The published mechanism is not autophagy induction and not mitochondrial biogenesis. No primary study shows GFF inducing autophagy (ATG genes, LC3 lipidation, TFEB) or mitochondrial biogenesis (PGC-1α, mitophagy) in skin. If we keep it in that class slot, we must flag the mismatch. Its real mechanistic identity is an AHR/NRF2-axis antioxidant + barrier/tight-junction conditioner.

The documented mechanistic pillars (almost all in keratinocytes/melanocytes in vitro, much of it from one academic group — Furue/Tsuji, Kyushu University — and from P&G):

  1. Aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AHR) agonism. GFF behaves as an antioxidative AHR agonist, upregulating the canonical AHR target CYP1A1 (mean fold-change ~1.78 across 3 lots in tKC keratinocytes). AHR activation is the upstream switch for several downstream skin effects below. Knocking down AHR abolishes the effects, proving AHR-dependence. [1][2][4]
  2. NRF2 / antioxidant-response element (ARE) activation. GFF drives nuclear translocation of NRF2, the master antioxidant transcription factor, upregulating detox/antioxidant enzymes NQO1, HMOX1 (HO-1), GPX2, GST, and suppressing reactive oxygen species (ROS) from UV, pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-13), and oxidative stressors. [5][8][9]
  3. Barrier / terminal-differentiation enhancement. Via AHR, GFF upregulates filaggrin (FLG) and loricrin (LOR) and caspase-14 (which processes filaggrin into natural moisturizing factor / NMF), and tight-junction proteins claudin-1, claudin-4, occludin, ZO-1, increasing transepithelial electrical resistance (TER) and cell–cell adhesion in cultured keratinocytes and 3D epidermal models. [4][7][10]
  4. Anti-"inflammaging" signaling. GFF downregulates inflammaging-linked genes CXCL14 and IL6R, and (AHR-dependently) raises anti-inflammatory IL-37 while suppressing pro-allergic IL-33 — the same axis as the prescription drug tapinarof. [8][16]
  5. Anti-senescence (epidermal). Through NRF2, GFF downregulates CDKN2A (p16^INK4a), a senescence marker over-expressed in aged keratinocytes — a plausible (but in-vitro only) "anti-senescence" signal. [5][8]
  6. Pigment/tone. In melanocytes, GFF reduces melanin synthesis and oxidative stress (NRF2-ARE), suggesting brightening/even-tone potential. [9][15]
  7. Mechanical/structural (newest). A 2025 in-vitro study reports GFF upregulates anchoring junctions (integrins α2/α3/β1) and stabilizes the actin cytoskeleton (calponin-2), raising keratinocyte Young's modulus (stiffness/resilience) and protecting against actin destabilization. [17]

Honest translation: This is a coherent, repeatedly-replicated antioxidant + barrier + tone mechanism in cell and 3D models. It is not an autophagy/mitochondrial mechanism. Whether a topical filtrate raises these pathways in living human skin is largely inferred, not directly measured.

4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

The single most important honesty point: GFF has an unusually deep in-vitro mechanistic file (rare for a "ferment") — but its human data are essentially all (a) manufacturer-affiliated (Procter & Gamble / SK-II), (b) open-label single-arm or split-face without a true placebo/vehicle control, and (c) testing finished multi-ingredient SK-II products, not isolated GFF. There is, to my searching, no independent, vehicle-controlled, randomized topical trial of GFF as an isolated active. The mechanism is also antioxidant/barrier, not autophagy/mitochondria.

Tier 1 — In vitro (cell & 3D models) — this is the ingredient's real strength

  • Takei, Mitoma, Hashimoto-Hachiya, … Furue, 2015, Clinical & Experimental Dermatology (DOI 10.1111/ced.12635). Normal human keratinocytes + 3D epidermal equivalents: GFF produced dose-dependent upregulation of CYP1A1, FLG, LOR; restored filaggrin suppressed by Th2 cytokines (IL-4/IL-13); effects abolished in AHR-knockdown cells → AHR-dependent. Limitation: in vitro/3D; no living-human readout. [4]
  • Woolridge, Koshoffer, Kadekaro, Wickett, Boissy, Hakozaki (P&G), 2014, J Am Acad Dermatol (S0190-9622(14)00529-5). Normal human melanocytes (light + dark skin) + melanoma cells: 5% and 10% GFF every other day reduced melanin 35% and 55% by day 25 (60% in melanoma cells by day 12); reduced oxidative stress. Viability unaffected ≤15%; 20%/30% cut viability 20%/50%. Limitation: cell culture; P&G authorship. [9][15]
  • Cooper (Jkw), Koshoffer, Kadekaro, Hakozaki, Boissy (Univ. Cincinnati + P&G), 2019, "GFF Suppresses ROS … via Nrf2-ARE Pathway" (J Clin Cosmet Dermatol 3(1):138, Sciforschen). Human melanocytes: 10% GFF increased NRF2 ~70% over 16 days and upregulated GST (NRF2 downstream) over 8 days; suppressed ROS. Limitation: in vitro; low-tier open-access journal; P&G-affiliated authorship. (Note: this melanocyte paper is from the Cincinnati/P&G group, NOT the Furue/Kyushu lineage — corrected on verification.) [9]
  • Nakajima et al. (P&G), 2022, J Clin Med (DOI 10.3390/jcm11164645). Transcriptomics of immortalized keratinocytes (tKC), 3 GFF lots, 10%: 99 genes consistently modulated (22 up, 77 down), incl. CYP1A1 (FC 1.78), SPRR1A/B, KRT6/16, CLDN1/4, IL6R, CXCL14. Authors' own limitations: no proteomics; gene roles not fully understood; AHR-dependence of the 99 genes "largely unknown." [1]
  • Tsuji, Hashimoto-Hachiya, … Furue, Nakahara, 2022, Frontiers in Immunology (DOI 10.3389/fimmu.2022.745997). NHEK + 3D + murine skin: tapinarof and GFF both upregulate IL-37 (anti-inflammatory) and suppress IL-33 (pro-allergic) AHR-dependently. Frames GFF alongside a real Rx drug mechanism. Limitation: no mouse IL-37 homolog → limited in-vivo validation; in vitro. [16]
  • Yan, Tsuji, Hashimoto-Hachiya, Furue, 2022, J Clin Med (DOI 10.3390/jcm11216338) — review/synthesis. Pulls the keratinocyte story together: NRF2→GPX2/NQO1/HMOX1; ↓CDKN2A (anti-senescence); ↑caspase-14, claudin-1/-4; ↓CXCL14/IL6R (anti-inflammaging). Limitation: it's a narrative review, not new controlled data; same author group. [8]
  • 2024, Journal of Dermatological Science (DOI 10.1016/j.jdermsci.2024.…; PubMed 39013659): "Enhancement of skin barrier function and augmentation of epidermal cell-cell interactions by GFF." Keratinocytes: ↑claudin-1/-4, occludin, ZO-1, ↑TER, enhanced cell–cell attachment. Limitation: in vitro. [7]
  • Crabtree et al., 2025–26, Int. J. Cosmetic Science (DOI 10.1111/ics.70009; PubMed 40708474; online 2025, assigned to a 2026 issue). HaCaT + 3D skin + fibroblasts, 8.775% GFF: upregulated integrins α2/α3/β1 and calponin-2 (actin-stabilizing); raised keratinocyte Young's modulus and protected vs latrunculin-B actin disruption. Authors' own limitations: complex mixture, actives unidentified; single-cell mechanics ≠ tissue firmness; needs human validation. [17]

Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

  • Sparse. Some AHR/IL-33 work used murine epidermis as adjunct to the IL-37 paper [16], but there is no dedicated rodent aging-skin or wound-healing study establishing GFF efficacy in animals. This tier is effectively empty for GFF, which is unusual and worth stating plainly.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL

  • None relevant. GFF/Pitera is a topical-only ingredient; there is no oral-supplement literature to borrow from (contrast spermidine, which has extensive oral data). So unlike many "longevity" actives, there is no oral evidence to either import or be skeptical of — the human story stands or falls on topical work.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (what matters for us — and the honesty problem lives here)

All of the following are P&G/SK-II-affiliated, test finished SK-II products (not isolated GFF), and lack a true randomized placebo/vehicle control arm. They are real published human data, but they are the weakest design tier (open-label / within-subject), the highest conflict-of-interest tier, and they cannot isolate GFF from the rest of the formula.

  • Miyamoto, Inoue, Yan, Yagi, Suda, Furue, 2023, J Clin Med (DOI 10.3390/jcm12031168) — the "11-year / 12-month" study. n = 86 Japanese women; their facial aging was tracked 1999→2010 (worsening wrinkles/spots/roughness, ↓hydration, ↑TEWL, all p<0.01), then all applied three SK-II GFF products twice daily for 12 months. Result: hydration, TEWL, wrinkles (0.37→~0.2), spots (0.28→~0.19), roughness (0.86→~0.73) returned toward 1999 baseline; authors estimated "−9.23 ± 3.19 years" apparent-age reversal.
  • Critical caveats: open-label, single-arm, no placebo/vehicle/control group (subjects are their own historical control); three products at once (cannot attribute to GFF, or even to one product); all authors are P&G employees except the consultant (Furue); P&G-funded; Japanese-women-only; regression-to-the-mean and seasonal/lifestyle confounds uncontrolled; "−9.23 years" is a derived, headline-friendly figure. This is the flagship human "proof," and it is methodologically weak. [6]
  • Miyamoto et al., 2021, J Clin Med (DOI 10.3390/jcm10112502) — daily-fluctuation pore/redness/roughness. Two studies, n = 47 and n = 57 young Japanese women; 4-week baseline, then GFF ("Pitera") formula twice daily ×4 weeks. Reported reduced intensity and day-to-day fluctuation of facial redness, roughness, pore area. Caveats: within-subject before/after, no vehicle control; P&G; short; finished formula. (An independent dermatologist notes the pore changes weren't cleanly quantified as true size reduction.) [18][20]
  • "Two independent clinical trials" cited as showing GFF reduced facial erythema/roughness/pore dilation — these are the 2021 fluctuation studies above (and related P&G work), repeatedly re-cited; not independent of P&G despite the "independent" phrasing. [1][18]
  • Mask-related fluctuation study (Miyamoto/P&G, 2022, J Clin Med jcm11082121) — moisturizer (GFF-containing) normalized mask-induced pore/redness/TEWL fluctuation. Same design limitations. [19]

Bottom line by tier: GFF's mechanistic (in-vitro) file is strong and repeatable for antioxidant/AHR/NRF2/barrier/pigment effects. Its human topical file is real but low-quality by design (open-label, no controls, manufacturer-run, whole-product not isolated GFF). There is no autophagy/mitochondrial evidence at all, and no independent vehicle-controlled RCT of GFF as an isolated active.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • The molecule is a mixture, so "penetration" is ill-defined. Effects are attributed to small metabolites (amino acids, organic acids, small peptides, oligosaccharides) — individually plausible to penetrate or to act at the stratum-corneum surface (humectancy, mild exfoliating acids) — but no published transdermal/penetration study quantifies what reaches viable epidermis or whether intracellular AHR/NRF2 are engaged in living human skin.
  • The mechanism partly explains itself topically: AHR and tight-junction/filaggrin effects are keratinocyte (epidermal) events that don't require deep dermal delivery, which is mechanistically convenient — but the in-vitro doses (5–10% GFF bathing cells) are not equivalent to what penetrates from a leave-on at the surface.
  • Lot-to-lot variability is the real bioavailability problem: because the filtrate is uncharacterized and unstandardized, the "active dose" differs between suppliers, fermentation runs, and even SK-II vs K-beauty knock-offs. [11][14]
  • Practical read: Plausible surface + upper-epidermal activity; deep-dermal anti-aging delivery is unproven; consistency depends entirely on supplier QC.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: No validated finished-product efficacy %; because it is mostly water it is used at high "as-supplied" levels (commonly 10–100%; up to ~90.7% in some leave-on moisturizers per CIR use data). A "90% galactomyces essence" can still contain only a trace of actives — % on the label is not potency. [11][14]
  • pH: Mildly acidic to near-neutral as supplied; it carries organic acids, so it nudges formulas acidic — generally compatible with a skin-friendly pH ~4.5–6. Confirm per supplier COA.
  • Stability: It's a fermentation broth of reactive small molecules → watch microbial load, color/odor drift, and lot variability; requires robust preservation and challenge testing. Limited public stability data — treat as a known unknown.
  • Pairings (conceptual): Niacinamide (barrier/tone/B3 — complementary mechanisms), hyaluronic acid/glycerin (humectant synergy with NMF story), ceramides (barrier thesis), antioxidants (vitamin C/E — additive NRF2/antioxidant framing), peptides. As a watery "first essence," it slots ahead of creams.
  • Conflicts: Few hard incompatibilities reported; as a postbiotic broth, avoid harsh chelation/high-heat processing that could degrade metabolites. Co-formulating with strong cationics or very high electrolyte loads may affect clarity — bench-test.
  • Sensory: Watery, light, fast-absorbing "essence" texture; can have a faint fermented/yeasty odor that needs masking. Pleasant, "lightweight glow" sensory is part of its consumer appeal.

7. Safety

  • Overall: Well-tolerated in general use; long real-world consumer history (SK-II + K-beauty). The independent Cosmetic Ingredient Review (CIR) assessed yeast-derived ingredients (incl. Galactomyces & Saccharomyces ferment filtrates) and concluded the group "safe as used" for those with adequate data (final assessments published 2024; tentative report Jan 2024), with documented use up to ~90.7% leave-on. [11][14]
  • Irritation/sensitization: Low rate, but yeast/ferment metabolites can sensitize a minority; reactive/sensitive users report occasional redness, itching, or breakouts. Patch-test advised; we should run our own HRIPT on finished formula. [11]
  • Fungal-acne (Malassezia) nuance — flag this: GFF is a yeast-derived ferment, and its AHR activation + metabolite "food" can, anecdotally, aggravate Malassezia folliculitis ("fungal acne") in predisposed users. Not a universal problem, but a real caution for oily/acne-prone marketing and for any "good for all skin" claim. [21]
  • Sun sensitivity: No photosensitivity signal; mechanistically antioxidant/AHR effects are framed as photo-protective in vitro (not a substitute for SPF). [5][9]
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: No specific topical red flags; it's a cosmetic postbiotic. Default to conservative copy ("consult your doctor") given absence of dedicated data.
  • Sourcing/allergen: Fermentation substrate varies (rice/sake heritage); disclose source for sensitive customers. "Postbiotic / no live organisms" is accurate and reassuring.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic ingredient status: Valid INCIs (Galactomyces Ferment Filtrate, COSING; Saccharomyces Ferment Filtrate), CIR-reviewed "safe as used," functions listed as skin-conditioning / humectant / (antioxidant per supplier). Legal as a cosmetic ingredient in major markets. [11][14]
  • The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame): The mechanism vocabulary is a claims minefield. "Activates NRF2/AHR in your skin," "anti-senescence," "anti-inflammaging," "reverses aging −9 years," "boosts collagen," "repairs the barrier" all flirt with structure/function (drug) claims — and the "−9.23 years" figure comes from an uncontrolled manufacturer study we cannot stand behind.
  • ✅ Safer cosmetic language: "fermentation-derived (postbiotic) essence," "antioxidant-rich yeast ferment," "helps skin feel hydrated, smooth and comfortable," "supports the look of an even, radiant tone," "supports the feel of a healthy moisture barrier," "visibly refines the look of pores/texture." Hedge even these — our support would be mechanistic + category, not our own RCT.
  • ⚠️ Risky / substantiation-or-legal-review: "activates your skin's antioxidant defense system," "anti-aging at the cellular level," "reduces senescence," "reverses X years of aging," "increases collagen by X%," "clinically proven to repair the barrier." None are supportable for our product on existing (manufacturer, uncontrolled) literature.
  • Substantiation reality: To make performance claims we'd need our own finished-formula, vehicle-controlled human testing; the existing human literature is open-label, conflicted, and tests competitors' multi-ingredient products. See ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Forms/suppliers: Widely available cosmetic raw material from Asian fermentation specialists (e.g., Korean/Japanese ferment houses such as Quegen Biotech and others) and ingredient distributors; sold as Galactomyces Ferment Filtrate or Saccharomyces Ferment Filtrate broths. "Pitera" specifically is SK-II/P&G proprietary and not sold as a raw material.
  • Cost tier: Low-to-mid as a raw material (it's largely water from fermentation) — the brand premium (SK-II) is marketing/heritage, not raw-material cost. Commodity GFF is cheap; this is exactly the "buy commodity bases, invest in the brand" dynamic in CLAUDE.md.
  • Supply/standardization risk: High variability — no marker-compound standardization, fermentation-run and supplier dependent. Require COA, microbial specs, and ideally batch consistency data; consider Galactomyces (more studied) over generic Saccharomyces if we use it.

10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

  • The "Pitera miracle / sake brewers' hands" origin story is anecdote, not evidence. It is a brilliant brand myth (SK-II's whole foundation) — but it is marketing folklore, not a study.
  • "Clinically proven to reverse aging by ~9 years." Comes from an open-label, single-arm, P&G-funded study testing three SK-II products at once with no control group [6]. That is not proof a GFF essence reverses aging.
  • "Two independent clinical trials." They are P&G-affiliated, within-subject, no vehicle control [18] — "independent" is misleading.
  • "Postbiotic that activates your skin's antioxidant/anti-senescence machinery." True in cultured cells; unproven that a topical filtrate does this in living human skin at consumer use levels.
  • The autophagy/mitochondria framing (incl. this brand's class slot). There is no autophagy or mitochondrial evidence for GFF. Selling it as an "autophagy/mitochondrial" longevity active would be unsubstantiated and mechanistically wrong.
  • "More % = more powerful" (e.g., 95–100% galactomyces toners). The filtrate is mostly water; high % ≠ high active dose; lot variability dominates.
  • Independent derms are lukewarm. Multiple evidence-based dermatologists, after personal long-term use and literature review, report no clear benefit and steer patients to retinoids/vitamin C instead [20][22].

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ SUPPORTING / CONDITIONAL — a credible "barrier-glow-and-tone" postbiotic with a great story, but weak independent human proof and the WRONG class label.

One-line: Galactomyces ferment filtrate is a likable, well-tolerated, mechanistically-interesting antioxidant + barrier + tone postbiotic that can earn a place as a supporting sensory/feel-good active in a barrier-repair or essence step — but it is not a hero, its human evidence is open-label and manufacturer-conflicted, and it must not be marketed as an autophagy/mitochondrial "longevity" active (no such evidence exists).

Two specific brand actions: 1. Reclassify it internally. It does not belong under "Autophagy & mitochondrial-targeted actives." Move it to a postbiotic / antioxidant-barrier / AHR-NRF2 class in the master index, with a note explaining the correction. Keeping it in the autophagy slot invites an unsubstantiated claim. 2. If used, lead with barrier/tone/sensory, hedge hard, and consider a more-characterized alternative. Niacinamide already covers barrier/tone with stronger, cleaner evidence (per glossary). GFF's marginal value is narrative + sensory + the postbiotic/fermentation trend, which suits a premium essence but doesn't strengthen the core barrier-repair-cream thesis.

Longevity-relevance note (honest): Among "longevity-adjacent" actives, GFF's pedigree is mechanistic and consumer-cultural, not geroscience. Its only true longevity-flavored signals (↓p16/CDKN2A senescence, ↓inflammaging cytokines, NRF2 antioxidant defense) are in-vitro keratinocyte findings from a single research lineage, with zero autophagy/mitochondrial data and zero independent human RCTs. For a skin-longevity brand it is a reasonable supporting ingredient and a strong brand-story ingredient — but the credible-science differentiation should be carried by better-evidenced actives, with GFF playing a clearly-hedged supporting role.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified: 2026-06-19. Independent adversarial re-check of every headline claim and citation in this dossier against primary sources (PubMed/PMC, MDPI, Wiley, JAAD, Frontiers, CIR, Sciforschen).

Overall grade: C (weak / indirect for living human skin)

Justification: GFF has an unusually deep, internally-consistent in-vitro mechanistic file (AHR→NRF2→barrier/antioxidant/pigment), which is real and replicated — but every human-topical study is (a) manufacturer-affiliated (P&G/SK-II), (b) open-label / within-subject with no placebo or vehicle arm, and (c) tests finished multi-ingredient SK-II products, never isolated GFF. There is no independent vehicle-controlled RCT of GFF as an isolated active, and zero autophagy or mitochondrial evidence despite the master-index class slot. By the grading rubric (A = strong human-topical RCT; B = moderate human-topical; C = weak/indirect; D = unproven/hype), GFF sits at C: the mechanism is well-supported in cells, but the human-skin efficacy claim is inferred from manufacturer open-label data, not demonstrated by controlled trial. It is rescued from a D only because the human studies, weak as they are, do exist and the in-vitro story is genuinely robust.

Human vs. borrowed evidence (the core question)

  • Human + topical? Only [6], [18], [19] — all P&G, all open-label/within-subject, all finished SK-II products. None isolate GFF; none have a vehicle/placebo control.
  • Borrowed from in-vitro? The entire mechanism (AHR/NRF2/barrier/pigment/senescence/inflammaging) — [1][4][7][8][9][15][16][17]. Robust as cell biology, but it is the dossier's strength, not human proof.
  • Animal / oral? Effectively none (a little murine adjunct in [16]); no oral literature at all. The dossier states this correctly.
  • Industry-funded / tiny? Yes on both counts where it matters: all efficacy data is P&G; the flagship human study is n=86 open-label; [9] is in a low-tier pay-to-publish journal; [15] is a conference abstract.

Main caveats (carry these forward)

  1. Class label is wrong. No autophagy/mitochondrial evidence exists. The dossier already says this loudly — keep it. Do not market GFF as an "autophagy/mitochondrial longevity active."
  2. "−9.23 years" is not proof. It is a derived figure from an uncontrolled, P&G, multi-product, single-arm study. Never use it as a brand claim or even as internal justification for efficacy.
  3. Human efficacy is inferred, not demonstrated. No independent vehicle-controlled RCT of isolated GFF exists. Any "clinically proven" framing is unsupportable for our product.
  4. % on label ≠ potency. Uncharacterized, lot-variable filtrate that is mostly water.
