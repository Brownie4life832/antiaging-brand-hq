Ingredient: Trehalose (INCI: Trehalose)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Class: Autophagy & mitochondrial-targeted actives · Status: ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product

Brutally honest one-liner: Trehalose is a cheap, ultra-safe, genuinely well-evidenced humectant / barrier-and-desiccation protector with a real, interesting mechanism (it stabilizes proteins/membranes by water-replacement + glass-formation). Its glamorous "longevity" credential — autophagy induction — is real but almost entirely bench-and-systemic (oral/IV); there is no good human evidence that a topical cream raises autophagy in living skin, and the autophagy effect in cells needs millimolar (often ~100 mM) concentrations that a leave-on product can't credibly deliver to the dermis. Treat it as an honest, low-risk supporting hydration/protection active with a believable "resilience/anti-glycation" story — not an autophagy "hero."

1. Identity

  • Common name: Trehalose (α,α-trehalose; "mushroom sugar," "tremalose," "mycose"). Trade names include Treha / Trehalose 100 (Nagase/Hayashibara), Trehalose SG.
  • Chemistry: A non-reducing disaccharide of two α-glucose units joined by an unusual α,α-1,1-glycosidic bond. MW ≈ 342.3 Da (anhydrous); commonly supplied as the dihydrate (~378 Da). CAS 99-20-7. The 1,1 linkage locks both anomeric carbons, making trehalose non-reducing (cannot itself participate as the reactive sugar in Maillard/glycation reactions) and unusually stable to heat and acid.
  • INCI: Trehalose (humectant / skin-conditioning / antioxidant-stabilizer functions per cosmetic INCI databases).
  • Key forms / derivatives:
  • α,α-Trehalose — the natural, bioactive form (the only one meant when "trehalose" is used).
  • Glycosyl Trehalose — enzyme-extended oligosaccharide blend (often co-sold with hydrogenated starch hydrolysate); used as a milder humectant/skin conditioner.
  • Sodium Trehalose Sulfate — a sulfated derivative with its own (small) clinical dataset (see §4).
  • Silanetriol Trehalose Ether, Sodium Trehalose Octenylsuccinate — niche conditioning/emulsion-stabilizer derivatives.
  • TREHA® (food grade) — the same molecule sold as a food ingredient; relevant to the safety/sourcing story.
  • Where it occurs naturally: baker's/brewer's yeast, mushrooms, the "resurrection plant" Selaginella lemginosella, tardigrades, brine shrimp (Artemia), and many desiccation-tolerant organisms — where it is the chemical that lets them survive near-total water loss (anhydrobiosis). This biology is the true story behind trehalose and is the most defensible marketing narrative.

2. Class & mechanism

Trehalose is filed here under autophagy & mitochondrial-targeted actives because of its longevity-literature fame, but mechanistically it wears two hats, and they have very different evidence weights for skin.

Hat 1 — Physical chemistry: bioprotection by water-replacement + vitrification (the strong, skin-relevant story)

  • Water-replacement hypothesis: as a cell dries, trehalose's many hydroxyls hydrogen-bond to proteins and membrane phospholipid head-groups in place of departing water, preserving native 3-D structure and membrane spacing. This is the same trick anhydrobiotic organisms (tardigrades, brine shrimp, resurrection plants) use to survive desiccation.
  • Vitrification / glass-formation hypothesis: concentrated trehalose dries into an amorphous "glass" with a high glass-transition temperature (Tg > ~100 °C), immobilizing biomolecules and arresting chemical/physical degradation. (Caveat from the biophysics literature: essentially every dried system vitrifies — vitrification is necessary but not sufficient for protection; trehalose is unusually good at it, not uniquely capable.)
  • Humectancy: like glycerin, trehalose binds and holds water in the stratum corneum, but it appears to outperform glycerin in low-humidity conditions in supplier testing (where glycerin can lose water to dry air).
  • Antioxidant / membrane stabilization: modest direct ROS-scavenging and protection of membranes/proteins against UV- and oxidation-driven damage in cell models.
  • Non-reducing → anti-glycation by abstention + competition: because trehalose cannot itself glycate proteins and may help stabilize them, it is positioned as an "anti-AGE" sugar (see §3, §4).

Hat 2 — Cell signaling: mTOR-independent autophagy induction (the famous, but skin-unproven, story)

  • Trehalose is one of the best-known mTOR-independent autophagy inducers in cell biology. In human keratinocytes it raised autophagy markers (LC3-II) without inhibiting mTORC1/mTORC2 [3].
  • Mechanism (resolved in liver/hepatocyte work): trehalose enters cells via the transporter SLC2A8 (GLUT8), and inside the cell it inhibits SLC2A glucose transporters (a pseudo-starvation / glucose-restriction signal), which activates AMPK and triggers autophagy; mTORC1 suppression alone was not sufficient without AMPK/GLUT8 [1][2].
  • In keratinocytes specifically: the autophagy effect has been linked to TFEB nuclear translocation and to TIMP3/Beclin-1 interaction + ATG9A trafficking [4][5].
  • Why this matters for honesty: every one of these findings used cells bathed in 50–200 mM trehalose or systemic (oral/IP/IV) dosing in animals. Inducing autophagy in living human dermis from a topical cosmetic is not demonstrated.

Net framing: trehalose is a protective osmolyte/humectant first (well-supported for skin) and an autophagy inducer second (well-supported in cells/systemically, unsupported topically in humans).

Trehalose's honest lane = hydration + desiccation/oxidative protection (resilience), with a secondary "non-glycating, anti-AGE-adjacent" angle. The autophagy/mitochondrial/senescence column is where marketing overreaches.

4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

Read this first. Trehalose's evidence is inverted vs. its longevity hype. The deepest, most exciting biology (autophagy, proteostasis, neuroprotection, lifespan) is in-vitro and systemic (oral/IV) — not topical. For topical human skin, the only reasonably supported benefit is hydration/barrier (and even there the cleanest RCT is on a derivative, sodium trehalose sulfate, and a supplier study). No independent, double-blind, placebo-controlled RCT of plain topical trehalose with anti-aging endpoints (wrinkles, firmness, collagen) was found in this review. Autophagy "rejuvenation" claims for a face cream are not substantiated.

Tier 1 — In-vitro / mechanism (strong, consistent — but high concentrations)

  • Mayer, Higgins, … DeBosch (2016), Science Signaling 9(416):ra21. doi:10.1126/scisignal.aac5472. [2] — Landmark mechanism paper: trehalose inhibits SLC2A (GLUT) glucose transporters, creating a pseudo-fasting state that activates AMPK and induces autophagy; mTORC1 suppression alone was insufficient without AMPK. Established that trehalose is a glucose-transport blocker, not a passive sugar. (Hepatocyte/mouse-liver system.)
  • Mayer et al. (2016), Scientific Reports 6:38586. doi:10.1038/srep38586 [companion] — SLC2A8 (GLUT8) identified as the mammalian trehalose transporter required for trehalose-induced autophagy; GLUT8-deficient cells/mice resisted trehalose's AMPK/autophagy effects.
  • Chen, Li, … Gu (2016), Scientific Reports 6:28423. doi:10.1038/srep28423 [3] — In human keratinocytes (HaCaT, primary, A431, HeLa), trehalose, sucrose, and raffinose (but not maltose/glucose/sorbitol) induced autophagy at 50–200 mM, mTOR-independently. Authors' own caveat: "the mechanism … is unclear." Concentration note: 100–200 mM = a bench condition.
  • Li, Chen, Chen, Chen, Gu (2022), Oxidative Medicine and Cellular Longevity 2022:9366494. doi:10.1155/2022/9366494 [5] — Keratinocytes (HaCaT, A431, primary) + UVB 50 mJ/cm². Trehalose (100 mM, 24 h pretreatment) raised LC3-II 4.71-fold (P<0.001), cut UVB-induced LDH release/cell death, via TIMP3/Beclin-1 interaction + ATG9A ER→lysosome translocation. Limitation: in-vitro; authors noted RT-qPCR vs. IHC inconsistencies; 100 mM dose.
  • Taira/Chen et al. (2020), Journal of Functional Foods 74:104198. doi:10.1016/j.jff.2020.104198 [6] — "Trehalose against UVB-induced skin photoaging by suppressing MMP expression and enhancing procollagen I synthesis in HaCaT cells." Trehalose scavenged UVB-induced ROS, suppressed MMP via blocking MAPK/AP-1/NF-κB, and supported procollagen I. Limitation: single keratinocyte line, in-vitro; no human skin.
  • Anhydrobiosis / biophysics literature (e.g., desiccation-tolerance reviews; Clegg, Crowe, and "molecular glasses" reviews such as Chem Rev 2024, doi:10.1021/acs.chemrev.3c00752) [7] — robust, decades-deep support for water-replacement + vitrification as the core protective mechanism. Honest caveat from this same literature: vitrification is necessary but not sufficient for protection.

Verdict (Tier 1): Mechanism is real and well-replicated for autophagy (high mM) and bioprotection (biophysics). The autophagy data's Achilles' heel is concentration realism in a topical setting.

Tier 2 — Animal

  • Wu, Chen, Zheng, et al. (2019), Cell Death & Disease 10(7):483. doi:10.1038/s41419-019-1704-0 [8] — C57BL/6 mice, random-pattern dorsal skin flaps. Trehalose 2 g/kg/day intraperitoneal improved flap survival area (days 3 & 7), increased microvessel density/VEGF, and reduced oxidative stress (↑SOD1, HO-1, eNOS) via TFEB-mediated autophagy (blocked by TFEB shRNA). Limitations: injected (IP), not topical; surgical/ischemia model, not cosmetic aging; single species. This is the closest "skin" animal data and it is systemic-dose, disease-model.
  • Neurodegeneration / aging models (large, supportive, but not skin): trehalose (typically 2% in drinking water or IP/IV) reduces protein aggregation and improves outcomes in Huntington's, tauopathy, ALS, and other proteinopathy mouse models (reviewed by Emanuele 2014, Curr Drug Targets 15(5):551–557 [9]; Hosseinpour-Moghaddam et al. 2022, Pharmacol Res [10]). d-galactose aging models: trehalose engaged Nrf2 and reduced aging phenotypes [11]. Relevance to skin: mechanistic encouragement only — all systemic, none topical.

Verdict (Tier 2): Strong, repeated proof that systemically delivered trehalose induces autophagy and protects tissue. None of it is a topical-cosmetic analog.

Tier 3 — Human, ORAL / systemic (establishes the autophagy story is systemic, not topical)

  • Kaplon, … Seals (2016), Aging 8(6):1167–1183. doi:10.18632/aging.100962 [12] — Randomized, double-blind, parallel trial; 32 healthy adults 50–77 (15 trehalose vs. 17 maltose control), 100 g/day oral, 12 weeks. No change in conduit-artery FMD overall; resistance-artery function improved ~30% in weight-stable subjects. Takeaway: even at a massive 100 g/day oral dose, vascular effects were modest/mixed (and this NIH-funded trial reported FMD/resistance-artery endpoints, not absorption). Separately, the pharmacology literature notes that most ingested trehalose is cleaved to glucose by intestinal trehalase and little is absorbed intact (figures in the low-percent range are cited), which undercuts "oral autophagy" claims at normal dietary intakes [9][19]. (The exact "<0.5%" figure is not from the Kaplon trial — treat it as an order-of-magnitude point, not a precise number from this study.)
  • IV trehalose in OPMD (Bioblast "Cabaletta," HOPEMD), Phase 2 open-label, n≈25 (Argov et al., Neurology 2016 abstracts S28.004 / P7.068) [13] — IV trehalose was safe and improved swallowing time (−35.3%, p<0.0001) and some muscle measures. Caveat: open-label, no placebo, IV route, rare disease; later development did not yield an approved drug. Also studied in SCA3 and the HEALEY ALS platform (SLS-005, NCT05136885).

Verdict (Tier 3): Human data exist only for oral/IV trehalose in disease or vascular-aging contexts, are mixed and mostly uncontrolled/early, and require enormous systemic doses. They say nothing about a topical skin benefit, and they actually highlight trehalose's poor oral bioavailability.

Tier 4 — Human, TOPICAL (the tier that matters — and the thinnest)

No independent, double-blind, placebo-controlled RCT of plain topical trehalose with anti-aging (wrinkle/firmness/collagen) endpoints was found. What exists:

  • Sodium trehalose sulfate — randomized, placebo-controlled, double-blind topical study (Maeda, "Functional properties and skin care effects of sodium trehalose sulfate," Skin Res Technol 2024;30(4):e13666; doi:10.1111/srt.13666; PMID 38606717) [14] — over 4 weeks, a lotion/emulsion with sodium trehalose sulfate produced a significant decrease in TEWL and increase in stratum-corneum water content vs. placebo in subjects with low SC hydration; a companion 3D-epidermis arm showed upregulated barrier/NMF factors (ABCA12, filaggrin, ceramide). Caveats: it's a sulfated derivative (not plain trehalose) — so the result does not transfer directly to plain trehalose; it is a peer-reviewed paper but appears manufacturer-linked (industry author/affiliation), so weigh accordingly. Hydration/barrier endpoints only — no anti-aging endpoints.
  • Trehalose 100 (Nagase) supplier study [15] — 14-day study in women with dry skin: a formula with 3% Trehalose 100 significantly improved hydration vs. placebo and outperformed glycerin in low-humidity conditions. Caveats: supplier/marketing source, not an independent peer-reviewed RCT; hydration endpoint only.
  • Chmielewski, Lebiedowska, Barańska-Rybak (2025), Int J Mol Sci 26(10):4747. doi:10.3390/ijms26104747; PMID 40429894 [16] — Ex-vivo human skin explants, methylglyoxal-glycation model: a dual-weight HA + trehalose injectable reduced CML (AGE) by 69% (d6, p=0.008), 68% (d8), 61% (d12) vs. MG alone, without harming viability. Caveats: ex-vivo (not living subjects), injectable (not leave-on), HA+trehalose combo (trehalose not isolated), and manufacturer-funded (URGO). Anti-glycation signal is interesting but cannot be claimed for a topical trehalose cream alone.
  • HA + trehalose "anti-AGE" narrative review (Clin Cosmet Investig Dermatol / Dovepress 2024, S476362) [17] — synthesizes the glycation/oxidative-stress rationale for aesthetic-medicine (injectable) HA+trehalose; review/positioning, not primary topical-efficacy data.

What is actually proven for human TOPICAL trehalose: - Reasonably supported (hydration/barrier): topical trehalose (and its sulfate derivative) improves stratum-corneum hydration and reduces TEWL [14][15]; consistent with strong humectant/biophysics rationale. - Suggested but confounded (anti-glycation): AGE reduction shown only ex-vivo, in a combo, injected, manufacturer-funded [16]. - NOT established topically: autophagy induction in living skin; wrinkle/firmness/collagen improvement; "cellular rejuvenation"; mitochondrial/senescence benefits; photoaging protection in vivo (keratinocyte-only data [5][6]).

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • For its hydration/barrier job, trehalose does not need to penetrate — it works at and within the stratum corneum as a surface/SC humectant and water-replacement agent. This is why the hydration claim is the most defensible: it's a surface effect.
  • For its autophagy/anti-glycation jobs, trehalose would need to reach living epidermis/dermis at high concentration, and here the problems stack up:
  • It is large-ish (342 Da), highly hydrophilic, and uncharged-but-very-polar → poor passive permeation through the lipid stratum corneum.
  • The cell-biology effects require ~50–200 mM intracellular exposure and GLUT8-mediated uptake [1][2][3] — conditions a leave-on cream realistically cannot create in dermal fibroblasts.
  • The ex-vivo anti-glycation result that did reach the dermis used an injectable route [16], bypassing the barrier entirely.
  • Researchers building topical trehalose products explicitly acknowledge its "low skin permeability" and have turned to liposomal encapsulation to get it into keratinocytes: Emanuele et al. (2014), Biomed Rep [19], found trehalose-loaded liposomes out-protected free trehalose against UVB-induced DNA/oxidative damage markers (CPDs, 8-OHdG, protein carbonyls) in HaCaT cells — confirming both the penetration problem and that encapsulation helps (in vitro).
  • Delivery implication: if the brand wants any credible "deeper" action, an encapsulated/penetration-enhanced system would be needed [19] — and even then the autophagy claim would remain unproven for living human skin. For honest formulation, lean on the surface hydration/protection mechanism, which needs no penetration.

6. Formulation

  • Use level: commonly 0.5–5% in skincare (supplier guidance up to ~5%; ~3% used in the cited hydration study). Higher levels add humectancy/viscosity but offer no proven extra benefit and can feel tacky.
  • Solubility: freely water-soluble; water-phase ingredient. Not oil-soluble.
  • pH / stability: exceptionally stable — non-reducing bond resists heat, acid, and Maillard browning; broad pH compatibility (~3–9); long shelf life; resists hydrolysis. One of the easiest actives to formulate.
  • Sensory: light, non-greasy humectant; mild clean taste/odor (food grade). At higher % can feel slightly sticky/tacky (typical humectant behavior) — pair with emollients/film-formers.
  • Pairings (synergy / common combos):
  • Hyaluronic acid — classic humectant + water-replacement pairing (and the format used in the anti-glycation data) [16][17].
  • Glycerin / panthenol / betaine / urea — complementary humectant blends; trehalose helps in low-humidity where glycerin weakens [15].
  • Ceramides / barrier lipids — supports the barrier-repair thesis (membrane stabilization + lipid scaffolding).
  • Ectoine — another extremolyte/osmolyte; a natural "resilience" co-star with overlapping desiccation-protection biology.
  • Antioxidants (vitamin E, niacinamide) — complementary protection.
  • Freeze/anhydrous & "resurrection"/dehydrated-format products — trehalose's glass-forming/cryo-stabilizing biology is a genuine technical asset (e.g., stabilizing labile actives, powder-to-liquid formats).
  • Conflicts: none notable. It is chemically inert and friendly. (It will not "boost" reactive actives, but it won't fight them either.)
  • Functional bonus: acts as a stabilizer/cryoprotectant for fragile co-actives (peptides, enzymes, probiotics, vitamin C systems) — a real formulation utility beyond skin benefit.

7. Safety

  • Excellent safety profile. GRAS (FDA, affirmed 2000; GRN-supported); JECFA "ADI not specified"; widely consumed in food. Non-irritating, non-sensitizing at cosmetic levels; suitable for sensitive, compromised, and post-procedure skin.
  • Irritation / sensitization: no meaningful topical irritation or allergenicity signal at cosmetic concentrations.
  • Sun sensitivity: none; not a photosensitizer (it is mildly photo-protective in cell models, but do not claim sun protection — see §8).
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: no specific topical contraindication; it's a benign food sugar. (General "consult your clinician" stance per brand policy; no known issue.)
  • Eyes/mucosa: well tolerated (trehalose is an active in marketed dry-eye drops), reinforcing its gentleness.
  • Oral/dietary caveats (NOT topical, but relevant to brand honesty & sourcing optics):
  • Trehalase deficiency / intolerance: a small subset of people lack sufficient intestinal trehalase and can get osmotic GI symptoms from large dietary trehalose — irrelevant to a face cream, but worth knowing.
  • C. difficile controversy: Collins et al. (2018), Nature 553:291–294, doi:10.1038/nature25178 [18] proposed that the rise of dietary trehalose helped epidemic C. difficile ribotypes (RT027/RT078) thrive. This is debated and contested (e.g., Eyre/Walker group found utilization genes not associated with severe outcomes) and concerns gut/dietary exposure — not topical skincare. Mention only to avoid being blindsided; it does not affect cosmetic safety.
  • Sourcing/sustainability: produced by an enzymatic process from starch (corn/tapioca) (Hayashibara/Nagase process), or by microbial fermentation — both scalable, vegan-compatible, and sustainable. No animal-derived or ethically fraught supply.

8. Regulatory & claims (cosmetic vs. drug)

  • Status: a cosmetic ingredient (humectant / skin conditioning / antioxidant-stabilizer). Using it as a moisturizer/skin-conditioner is squarely cosmetic.
  • ✅ Defensible cosmetic language:
  • "Provides long-lasting hydration," "helps lock in moisture / reduce moisture loss," "helps support the skin barrier," "helps skin resist environmental/dehydration stress," "soothing," "inspired by the desiccation-resilience biology of resurrection plants / tardigrades" (framed as the ingredient's natural story, not a skin claim).
  • "Helps defend the appearance of skin against dryness and environmental stress."
  • ⚠️ Risky / drug-like or unsubstantiated — flag for legal/substantiation:
  • Anything invoking "autophagy," "cellular self-cleaning/renewal," "recycles damaged cells," "mitophagy," "rejuvenates cells at the cellular level," "anti-senescence," "longevity at the cellular level." These imply structure/function/drug effects, are not proven topically, and are the #1 overreach in trehalose marketing. If used at all, they must be explicitly about the ingredient's biology in lab studies, not a promise about the user's skin — and ideally avoided.
  • "Boosts collagen," "reverses wrinkles," "anti-AGE/de-glycates skin," "repairs sun damage," "protects from UV." The collagen/MMP/anti-glycation data are in-vitro / ex-vivo / combo / injectable — not topical-human-proven. UV/photo claims risk OTC sunscreen (drug) territory.
  • "Treats" any condition (eczema, dermatitis): drug territory.
  • Bottom line: sell trehalose as hydration + resilience + a great story, keep autophagy/longevity language to ingredient lore (clearly bench-stage), and route any anti-aging/anti-glycation claim through substantiation review. 9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: LOW. Trehalose is a mass-produced food-grade commodity sugar (orders of magnitude cheaper than peptides, exosomes, or the exotic longevity actives). Excellent value: real mechanism + great safety + low price.
  • Suppliers: Hayashibara (Nagase) is the dominant producer (TREHA® food grade; Trehalose 100 cosmetic grade); also available from numerous fine-chemical/cosmetic distributors (DKSH, dermo-suppliers, generic disaccharide vendors). Easy, reliable global supply.
  • Provenance story: enzymatic-from-starch or fermentation; vegan, plant/microbe-derived, sustainable — clean supply narrative with no ethical flags.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • The hype: marketed as a "longevity sugar" / "autophagy activator" / "cellular renewal" / "anti-AGE" active that makes skin "clean out old cells." Brands lean hard on the tardigrade/resurrection-plant and Huntington's/autophagy literature.
  • The reality: 1. Autophagy is real but systemic/bench. Every human autophagy-relevant result is oral or IV at gram-scale doses (and oral is <0.5% absorbed). No evidence a face cream raises autophagy in living human skin. The cell data need ~100 mM — not a topical reality. 2. Anti-aging endpoints (wrinkles/firmness/collagen) are not shown topically. MMP/procollagen and anti-glycation data are keratinocyte-only, ex-vivo, combo, or injectable [5][6][16]. 3. The genuinely strong claim is the humble one: hydration + barrier + desiccation/oxidative protection. That's well-grounded in biophysics and small human topical data [14][15] — and it's enough to justify inclusion. 4. Great story ≠ great skin efficacy. The tardigrade/resurrection-plant narrative is true ingredient biology and excellent for storytelling — but it describes how the molecule protects desiccating organisms, not a proven anti-wrinkle effect in people.
  • Honest summary: trehalose is the rare ingredient that is under-hyped for what it actually does (hydration/resilience) and over-hyped for what it doesn't (topical autophagy/longevity).

11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️→✅ SUPPORTING / CONDITIONAL. Include as a low-cost, ultra-safe supporting humectant + barrier/desiccation-protection active with a compelling "resilience" story — NOT as an autophagy/longevity hero.

  • One-line reasoning: Genuinely good evidence and mechanism for hydration, barrier support, and stress/desiccation resilience at near-zero cost and risk, paired with one of the best ingredient stories available (tardigrades, resurrection plants) — but its headline "autophagy/longevity" credential is unproven for topical skin and must be kept out of claims.
  • Longevity-relevance note: trehalose is a bona fide longevity-science molecule (mTOR-independent autophagy via GLUT8/AMPK, proteostasis, neuroprotection) — which makes it strategically attractive for a skin-longevity brand's narrative — provided the brand is disciplined: the longevity biology lives in the ingredient-lore/education layer (clearly lab-stage), while on-pack/efficacy claims stay on hydration + resilience. Pairs naturally with ectoine, HA, ceramides, niacinamide. Best deployed as a credibility-and-comfort supporting active and a storytelling asset, in the same tier as ectoine. Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified: 2026-06-19. Independent adversarial fact-check of the headline claims and citations above. Method: each load-bearing study was located at its primary source (PubMed/PMC/journal) and checked for (a) human vs. in-vitro/animal/oral, (b) topical vs. systemic/injected, (c) whether the paper actually says what the dossier claims, and (d) sample size / industry funding.

Overall evidence grade: B− for the hydration/barrier lane; D for the autophagy/longevity lane

There is no single grade because trehalose's evidence is genuinely bimodal, and this dossier is one of the few that says so honestly. Splitting by claim:

  • Topical hydration / barrier / TEWL → grade B− (moderate human-topical). Two human studies support it ([14] Maeda 2024, a peer-reviewed randomized double-blind placebo-controlled 4-wk study, and [15] a Nagase supplier study), plus very strong biophysics. Downgraded from a clean B because the cleanest RCT [14] is on a sulfated derivative (sodium trehalose sulfate), not plain trehalose, and is industry-affiliated; the only plain-trehalose human data is a non-peer-reviewed supplier study [15]. No independent RCT of plain topical trehalose with even hydration endpoints was found.
  • Topical anti-aging (wrinkles / firmness / collagen / autophagy / "rejuvenation") → grade D (essentially unproven topically / hype). Zero human-topical evidence. Every supporting study is in-vitro keratinocyte ([3][5][6]), animal-injected ([8]), oral/IV ([12][13]), or ex-vivo injected combo ([16]). This matches the dossier's own §4/§10 framing.
  • Anti-glycation → grade C (weak/indirect). The one human-tissue study [16] is real and its numbers are exact, but it is ex-vivo + injectable + HA-combo + manufacturer-funded — not transferable to a leave-on trehalose cream.

Citation audit (all real; descriptions accurate unless noted)

Spot-checked 8 of the most load-bearing citations against primary sources — all exist, are correctly attributed, and say what the dossier claims:

  • [2] Mayer/DeBosch 2016 Sci Signal ra21 — real; SLC2A/GLUT-inhibition → AMPK → autophagy mechanism accurate; hepatocyte/mouse-liver (correctly labeled not-skin).
  • [3] Chen/Gu 2016 Sci Rep 28423 — real; mTOR-independent keratinocyte autophagy; sucrose/raffinose positive, glucose/maltose/sorbitol negative — all accurate; in-vitro; government-funded (not industry).
  • [5] Li/Gu 2022 Oxid Med Cell Longev — real; LC3-II 4.71-fold, P<0.001, 100 mM, UVB 50 mJ/cm², HaCaT/A431/HEK — exact match; cell-culture only; China NSFC-funded (not industry).
  • [8] Wu 2019 Cell Death Dis — real; C57BL/6, random-pattern flap, 2 g/kg/day intraperitoneal, surgical-ischemia (not cosmetic-aging), TFEB autophagy — exact match; systemic injection, not topical.
  • [12] Kaplon 2016 Aging — real; 32 adults 50–77, RCT double-blind, 100 g/day oral 12 wk, maltose control, no conduit-FMD change, ~30% resistance-artery gain only in weight-stable subgroup — exact match; NIH-funded, no COI.
  • [14] Maeda 2024 Skin Res Technol — real and peer-reviewed (the dossier originally understated this as "ResearchGate/industry literature, peer-review status not established" — corrected above). RCT design and ↓TEWL/↑SC-water results confirmed; sulfated-derivative + industry caveats stand.
  • [16] Chmielewski 2025 IJMS 26(10):4747 — real; CML −69% (d6, p=0.008), −68% (d8), −61% (d12) vs MG — exact match; 36 explants, intradermal injection, HA+trehalose combo (trehalose not isolated), funded by URGO with an URGO-employed author who influenced design/interpretation — all correctly caveated. (Note: a near-duplicate paper by the same sponsor exists at IJMS 26(7):3217 — same product line; treat the two as one industry dataset, not independent replication.)
  • GRAS 2000 / Richards 2002 [19][20] — confirmed; GRAS affirmed 2000 (FDA had no questions on GRN 000045).

Main caveats

  1. The evidence is "inverted" and the dossier says so — good. The famous longevity biology (autophagy, proteostasis, neuroprotection, lifespan) is systemic/bench only; the boring claim (hydration/resilience) is the only human-topical one. No correction needed to that thesis.
  2. Concentration realism is the killer for autophagy. All cell autophagy data use 50–200 mM intracellular exposure via GLUT8 uptake — not achievable in living dermis from a leave-on cosmetic. Correctly stated.
  3. The "best" human-topical RCT is on a derivative, not the molecule. [14] tests sodium trehalose sulfate; results should not be silently transferred to plain trehalose.
  4. Two of the three strongest "skin" sources are industry-linked ([14] industry-affiliated; [16] URGO-funded). The single plain-trehalose human study [15] is a non-peer-reviewed supplier dossier. Independent confirmation is thin.
  5. One sourcing slip (now fixed): the "<0.5% oral absorption" figure was placed next to the Kaplon trial [12], which does not report it; it is reattributed to the pharmacology/safety literature as an order-of-magnitude point.

Claims softened / corrected in this pass

  • §4 / Sources [14]: upgraded the sodium-trehalose-sulfate study from "peer-review status not established" to its real peer-reviewed citation (Maeda 2024, Skin Res Technol) and added the explicit caveat that it is a derivative, not plain trehalose, so it does not directly support plain-trehalose claims.
  • §4 Tier 3 / Sources: removed the implication that the "<0.5% absorbed" bioavailability figure comes from the Kaplon trial; reframed as a general pharmacology point.
  • Sources [20]: corrected an imprecise GRAS Notice number (was "GRN 000727") to the actual GRN 000045 (2000).
  • No efficacy claim required hard softening — the dossier was already appropriately hedged (arguably more skeptical than the evidence demands for the hydration lane). The net direction of error in the original was under-stating the hydration evidence (mislabeling a peer-reviewed RCT), not over-stating efficacy.

Bottom line

This is a high-integrity dossier. Its central honest claim — "under-hyped for hydration/resilience, over-hyped for topical autophagy/longevity" — survives adversarial checking. Keep all autophagy/longevity language in the clearly-labeled "ingredient lore (lab-stage)" layer and route any anti-glycation/anti-aging on-pack claim through substantiation review. The only defensible efficacy claims for a finished topical product are hydration, moisture-loss reduction, and barrier support — and even those rest partly on a derivative and on industry data, so they warrant the brand's own corroboration before they go on a label.
