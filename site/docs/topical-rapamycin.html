Ingredient: Rapamycin (Sirolimus) / rapalogs (INCI: Sirolimus)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ☑ rejected (for cosmetic use) — see Verdict Slug: topical-rapamycin · Class: Senotherapeutics & senolytics (mTOR inhibitor / gerosuppressant)

Headline for a cosmetic brand: This is a fascinating longevity molecule with the single most interesting human topical aging dataset in the senotherapeutic class — and it is also an FDA-approved prescription drug (Hyftor, sirolimus 0.2% gel, approved 2022) and a systemic immunosuppressant. That combination makes it a near-certain non-starter as a cosmetic ingredient in the US/EU. It is reviewed here in full because it is the reference compound the whole "senolytic skincare" category borrows credibility from. Skip for our formulations; understand it cold for competitive/claims literacy.

1. Identity

  • Common names: Rapamycin; Sirolimus (the approved drug name / INN).
  • INCI: Sirolimus (this is also the pharmaceutical name — itself a red flag for cosmetic use; see §8).
  • Chemistry: Macrolide lactone, C51H79NO13, MW ≈ 914 g/mol, logP ≈ 4.3, water solubility ≈ 2.6 µg/mL, pKa ≈ 9.96. A large, very lipophilic, poorly water-soluble molecule. [9][5]
  • Origin: Natural product (secondary metabolite) of the soil bacterium Streptomyces hygroscopicus, first isolated from soil collected on Rapa Nui (Easter Island) in the 1960s–70s — hence "rapamycin." Produced commercially by fermentation. [10][11]
  • Key forms / derivatives ("rapalogs"):
  • Sirolimus — parent compound; oral (Rapamune) for transplant; topical (Hyftor 0.2% gel).
  • Everolimus — oral rapalog (oncology, transplant).
  • Temsirolimus — IV rapalog (oncology).
  • Nab-sirolimus (albumin-bound) — IV oncology.
  • All share the same core mechanism (mTOR inhibition); none is a cosmetic ingredient.

2. Class & mechanism

Rapamycin is the prototypical mTOR inhibitor and the founding molecule of the gerosuppressant / senomorphic approach to aging (distinct from senolytics, which kill senescent cells; rapamycin instead prevents and quiets them).

  • It binds the intracellular protein FKBP12; the rapamycin–FKBP12 complex then inhibits mTORC1 (mechanistic target of rapamycin complex 1), the master nutrient/growth signaling hub. [3][4]
  • Geroconversion / gerosuppression model (Blagosklonny): cellular senescence is framed as "twisted growth" — when the cell cycle is arrested but mTOR stays active, the cell converts into a bloated, inflammatory senescent state. Rapamycin slows this geroconversion (in vitro by roughly ~3-fold), keeping cells in a younger, quiescent state rather than removing them. [3]
  • At the skin/cell level: mTOR inhibition (a) promotes autophagy (cellular "cleanup"), (b) suppresses the SASP — the senescence-associated secretory phenotype, the inflammatory cytokine cocktail (IL-6, IL-1α, etc.) that senescent cells spew and that drives "inflammaging" — and (c) reduces senescence markers such as p16INK4A. The SASP suppression is mechanistically specific: mTOR drives translation of IL1A, which feeds NF-κB-driven SASP; rapamycin blocks that. [7][7a]

4. Evidence review by tier (the core of this dossier)

Brutal-honesty summary: The mechanistic and animal case is genuinely strong and unusually well-pedigreed (rapamycin is the most reproducibly life-extending drug in mammals). But the human topical aging evidence rests on essentially ONE small, exploratory, high-dropout, industry-conflicted trial (lead authors hold equity in a topical-rapamycin company) with no independent replication. Almost every confident "rapamycin reverses skin aging" claim online is borrowed from oral/animal/in-vitro work, not proven for a topical cosmetic context.

Tier 1 — In vitro

  • Laberge, Sun, Campisi et al. (2015), Nature Cell Biology: mTOR inhibition by rapamycin suppresses the pro-tumorigenic SASP by reducing IL1A translation and NF-κB activity; rapamycin-treated senescent fibroblasts lost their ability to stimulate tumor growth. Foundational for the "rapamycin quiets senescent cells" thesis. [7a] (PubMed 26147250)
  • Wang et al. (2017), Aging Cell: rapamycin inhibits the secretory phenotype of senescent cells via an Nrf2-independent mechanism. [12] (PMC5418203)
  • Pospelova / Blagosklonny group — UVA-irradiated human fibroblasts (Oncotarget, 2017): rapamycin and Torins prevented the UVA-induced senescent phenotype (↓SA-β-gal, ↓SASP) while reinforcing cell-cycle arrest — i.e., decoupling arrest from geroconversion. [13] (PMC5752566)
  • Rapamycin protects skin fibroblasts from UVA photoaging by inhibiting p53 and phospho-HSP27 and reducing ROS (2018/2021). [14] (PMC7892968; PubMed 29705807)
  • H2O2-induced oxidative senescence in human skin fibroblasts (2024, Tissue Eng Regen Med): rapamycin attenuated oxidative-stress senescence. [15]
  • Limitation of the whole tier: monolayer cells in dishes at controlled concentrations — says nothing about whether a topically-applied cream reaches dermal fibroblasts at active doses in living skin.

Tier 2 — Animal

  • Harrison, Strong, Miller et al. (2009), Nature — NIA Interventions Testing Program: oral rapamycin extended median and maximal lifespan in genetically heterogeneous mice even when started late in life. This is the landmark result behind rapamycin's entire longevity reputation — but it is systemic (oral), whole-organism lifespan, not skin. [6]
  • Strong et al. (2020), Aging Cell: late-life and intermittent dosing also extend mouse lifespan, with sex-specific effects. [6a]
  • Topical/UV mouse skin models: rapamycin reversed UVB-induced collagen-fiber breakage, blocked UVB-induced MMP upregulation (preserving type-I collagen), and reduced ROS via autophagy (Hspb2-mediated) in mouse skin/ear photodamage models. [14a] (Mol Biol Rep 2023)
  • mTOR inhibitors slow skin carcinogenesis but impair wound healing (2011, PubMed 21895616): important double-edged safety note — the same pathway inhibition that suppresses senescence also slows healing. [16]
  • Limitation: mouse skin ≠ human skin (thinner, different barrier); doses/vehicles not consumer-realistic.

Tier 3 — Human ORAL (systemic)

  • Decades of transplant use (Rapamune) and growing off-label "longevity" use establish systemic pharmacology, but oral rapamycin carries dose-dependent immunosuppression, stomatitis/mouth ulcers, dyslipidemia, impaired wound healing, and edema. [17]
  • Relevance to skin aging: oral data demonstrate the organism-level aging biology but do not transfer to a cosmetic skin claim. Blagosklonny himself argues most skin aging reflects organismal aging and that systemic dosing would do more — while conceding topical is the more palatable route. [3] These are explicitly drug contexts.

Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL

(a) The one anti-aging RCT — Chung, Robbins, et al. (2019), GeroScience (Drexel). "Topical rapamycin reduces markers of senescence and aging in human skin: an exploratory, prospective, randomized trial." [1][2] - Design: exploratory, prospective, randomized, vehicle-controlled; applied to dorsal hands (one hand active, one vehicle). - Formulation/dose: ~10 µM rapamycin (~0.001%), ~0.5 cc applied every 24–48 h at night, for 6–8 months. - Sample size (the key weakness): 36 enrolled → only 17 completed; biopsy analysis on ~8 samples from 13 consenting subjects. 77% female, 75% White. - Conflict of interest (the other key weakness, verified 2026-06-19): three authors — including the lead author — are disclosed shareholders of Boinca Therapeutics LLC, a company commercializing topical rapamycin. The single human-topical aging study is therefore both tiny and financially conflicted; its positive result must be read with that in mind. No independent replication exists. - Results: significant reduction in p16INK4A (P = 0.008, primary outcome); increase in collagen VII (P = 0.0077); reduced solar/actinic elastosis on histology; clinician-noted improvement in fine wrinkles, dermal volume, tone, sagging, and vein/tendon visibility in most completers (11 of 13 by ~4 months). No detectable systemic rapamycin in blood. - Limitations (per the authors): tiny completed N and high dropout; limited senescence-marker panel (small punch biopsies); p16INK4A is not specific to senescence; hands-only; no validated wrinkle-grading instrument as primary endpoint; not powered for cosmetic efficacy claims. This is hypothesis-generating, not confirmatory.

(b) Topical sirolimus in disease — the robust human topical dataset (but NOT anti-aging): - TREATMENT RCT — Koenig, Bell, Hebert et al. (2018), JAMA Dermatology 154(7):773–780: multicenter, randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled; 179 patients with TSC facial angiofibromas; 1% vs 0.1% rapamycin vs vehicle for 6 months. Angiofibroma Grading Scale improvement: 16.7 (1%) vs 11.0 (0.1%) vs 2.1 (vehicle), P<.001; photos rated "better" in 81.8% / 65.5% / 25.5%. Well tolerated; systemic absorption not detected in 500+ blood samples. [8] - Systematic review / network meta-analysis (2022, PMC9025300): across trials (~261 patients), topical mTOR inhibitors significantly reduced angiofibroma severity; adverse effects mainly local; blood levels within safe limits. [18] - 2026 review — Lapa & Sandre, J Cutan Med Surg: off-label topical sirolimus across dermatologic conditions (angiofibroma, vascular/lymphatic malformations, etc.); reiterates poor passive penetration and the need for vehicle engineering. [19] - Why this matters for honesty: these prove topical sirolimus works on a vascular/proliferative lesion and is locally safe — they do not prove it rejuvenates normal aging skin. Do not let TSC efficacy be quoted as anti-aging proof.

Bottom line — proven for topical human use vs borrowed: - Borrowed from oral/animal/in-vitro: lifespan extension, broad senolytic/SASP/autophagy benefits, photoaging prevention, antioxidant effects. - Shown for topical human (disease): angiofibroma reduction; local tolerability; negligible systemic absorption. - Shown for topical human (aging) — only weakly, one small conflicted trial: ↓p16INK4A, ↑collagen VII, ↓solar elastosis, clinician-rated cosmetic improvement on hands. (Single exploratory study, 17 completers, author equity stake, unreplicated.)

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

This is a serious physicochemical obstacle, independent of the regulatory problem.

  • Rapamycin violates the "500-Dalton rule" for skin penetration: MW ≈ 914, logP ≈ 4.3, very low aqueous solubility (2.6 µg/mL). It does not efficiently cross intact stratum corneum; studies find little to none within the SC of untreated skin ex vivo. [5][9]
  • At skin pH (~4.8) its ionized fraction further limits passive diffusion. [9]
  • Penetration-enhancement strategies under research: serine-protease pretreatment (improved permeability shown by scanning transmission X-ray microscopy) [5], redox-sensitive nanocarriers / liposomes [20], and the gel vehicle in approved Hyftor. A consumer-grade cream is unlikely to deliver an active dermal dose reliably without sophisticated delivery engineering — which a cosmetic brand cannot claim.

6. Formulation notes

  • Concentrations in evidence: anti-aging trial ~10 µM ≈ 0.001%; TSC trials 0.1–1%; approved Hyftor gel 0.2%.
  • Stability: rapamycin is notoriously oxidation- and light-sensitive and degrades readily; pharmaceutical formulations require antioxidants and protective packaging. A challenge even for drug formulators.
  • pH: low aqueous solubility and high logP make aqueous cosmetic systems awkward; typically formulated in lipid/solvent or gel vehicles.
  • Pairings/conflicts (academic only, given we won't use it): would conceptually conflict with strong actives that compromise the barrier or add irritation (high-strength retinoids, acids) given delayed wound healing; no established cosmetic synergy data.
  • Sensory: not a relevant consideration — not viable as a cosmetic.

7. Safety

  • Topical (disease use) is locally well-tolerated: most common AEs in TSC trials were mild application-site pain, pruritus, erythema; systemic absorption essentially undetectable at 0.1–1% on the face. [8][18]
  • Class is a systemic immunosuppressant. The same mTOR inhibition that suppresses senescence can impair wound healing (documented for mTOR inhibitors) [16] and, systemically, causes immunosuppression, mouth ulcers, and dyslipidemia. Even if topical systemic exposure is low, the mechanism is immunomodulatory, which is categorically inappropriate for an unsupervised cosmetic.
  • Sun sensitivity: not a classic photosensitizer, but UV/skin-cancer interplay is complex (mTOR inhibition both slows carcinogenesis and impairs repair) — another reason it belongs under medical supervision.
  • Pregnancy/lactation: systemic sirolimus is embryo/fetotoxic in animals and is contraindicated; effective contraception is required before/during/after oral therapy. Topical systemic exposure is low, but no human topical pregnancy safety data exist and the label warns of possible fetal exposure. Treat as avoid in pregnancy/breastfeeding. [21][22]
  • Sensitization: local irritation reported; not a notable contact allergen, but irrelevant given the above.
  • Sourcing/sustainability: fermentation-derived; no special sustainability concern — but it is a controlled pharmaceutical supply chain, not a cosmetic raw-material one.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

This is the decisive section for the brand.

  • Sirolimus is an approved prescription DRUG, including topically. Oral Rapamune (FDA 1999) and, critically, Hyftor (sirolimus topical gel 0.2%) — FDA-approved March 22, 2022 as the first topical Rx for TSC facial angiofibroma. [23] An ingredient that is the active in an approved topical drug is, by definition, drawing regulatory scrutiny toward "drug," not "cosmetic."
  • In the US, putting a recognized drug active into a "cosmetic" and marketing aging benefits would almost certainly render the product an unapproved new drug / misbranded under FDA rules. The EU bans use of substances that are medicinal products in cosmetics; sirolimus would not survive a cosmetic safety assessment as a self-applied active.
  • Claims: even the human topical aging data describe senescence biology and structure/function changes (p16, collagen VII, elastosis) — these are inherently drug-type structure/function claims, exactly the language CLAUDE.md flags as high-risk ("repairs," "boosts collagen," "reverses aging"). There is no compliant cosmetic claim we could make about rapamycin's mechanism.
  • Net: this fails the cosmetic-vs-drug line on both the ingredient identity and the claims it would invite.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Produced by S. hygroscopicus fermentation; bulk pharmaceutical-grade sirolimus is available but priced and regulated as an API (active pharmaceutical ingredient), not a cosmetic raw material. [10][11]
  • Compounding pharmacies sell oral rapamycin for off-label longevity use; topical compounded rapamycin exists in the wellness/longevity grey market. None of this is a legitimate cosmetic supply route. Expect API-tier cost plus regulatory/insurance overhead — economically and legally unsuited to a retail cosmetic.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • Hype: "Rapamycin is the most powerful anti-aging molecule — now in a cream." Marketing routinely fuses (a) the oral mouse lifespan result, (b) in-vitro senescence/SASP data, and (c) the single small topical hand trial into an implied "clinically proven topical skin rejuvenation." That fusion is not supported.
  • Reality checks: 1. The flagship lifespan data are oral, in mice — not topical, not skin, not human. 2. The only human topical aging trial had 17 completers, was explicitly "exploratory," and was authored in part by equity holders of a topical-rapamycin company — and has never been independently replicated. (It is best described as a randomized exploratory trial, not a confirmatory "RCT.") 3. The molecule barely penetrates skin without specialized delivery. 4. It is a prescription immunosuppressant drug — selling it as a cosmetic is a regulatory dead end.
  • A credible brand should treat rapamycin skincare as a cautionary tale about senolytic-skincare overreach, not a template to copy.

11. Verdict for the brand

❌ SKIP (as a cosmetic ingredient/active).

  • One-line reasoning: Rapamycin is an FDA-approved prescription drug and systemic immunosuppressant whose only human topical aging evidence is a single tiny exploratory trial of a barely-penetrating molecule — it cannot be a compliant or safe cosmetic active for us.
  • Longevity-relevance note: It is nonetheless the gold-standard reference compound of the gerosuppressant/senotherapeutic field and the credibility anchor competitors lean on. Worth knowing cold for competitive positioning and claims literacy. If the brand ever wants a legitimate cosmetic expression of the same biology, look to non-drug senomorphics/autophagy-supporters (e.g., spermidine, certain peptides/polyphenols) rather than rapamycin itself — to be evaluated separately on their own (currently thinner) evidence.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19 by independent re-check of the primary sources for the headline efficacy claims (tests: is the evidence human AND topical, or borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral; are the cited studies real and accurately described; are key studies industry-funded or very small).

Overall evidence grade for the brand-relevant claim ("topical rapamycin rejuvenates aging human skin"): D — essentially unproven topically.

This grade is for the anti-aging cosmetic claim, the only one that matters for this brand. The grade is not a knock on the dossier, which is itself rigorous and largely accurate — it reflects the genuine state of the evidence.

  • The mechanistic / in-vitro / animal case is strong and real (would be A if the claim were "mTOR inhibition is a validated longevity mechanism in model systems").
  • The human topical disease evidence (TSC angiofibroma) is genuinely strong (A for that indication — large multicenter double-blind RCT, n=179, verified) but is not an anti-aging claim and must never be cited as one.
  • The human topical aging evidence is a single small exploratory trial that is also industry-conflicted and unreplicated → D for the cosmetic-rejuvenation claim. (One could argue C; the undisclosed-until-now author equity stake plus zero replication pulls it to D.)

What the adversarial check confirmed (the dossier is largely sound)

  • Citations are real and accurately described. Spot-verified against primary sources: Chung 2019 GeroScience [1] (36 enrolled / 17 completed / 8 samples / 0.001% / dorsal hands / p16 P=0.008 / collagen VII P=0.0077 / no detectable blood levels / "exploratory" — all match exactly); Koenig 2018 JAMA Dermatol [8] (n=179, AGS 16.7 vs 11.0 vs 2.1, P<.001, photos better 81.8/65.5/25.5%, 500+ blood samples negative — all match exactly); Harrison 2009 Nature [6] (oral, whole-mouse median+maximal lifespan +9–14%, late-life dosing — correctly framed as systemic, not skin); Laberge 2015 Nat Cell Biol [7/7a] (IL1A-translation/NF-κB SASP mechanism, in-vitro human fibroblasts + mouse xenograft — correctly placed in Tier 1). No fabricated citations found.
  • The tiering is honest. The dossier already explicitly separates "borrowed from oral/animal/in-vitro" from "shown for topical human," already flags the 17-completer weakness, and already warns against quoting TSC efficacy as anti-aging proof. Good practice.

Main caveats (the gaps the check surfaced)

  1. The single human-topical-aging trial is industry-conflicted — and the dossier originally omitted this. Three authors of Chung 2019 (including the lead author) are disclosed shareholders of Boinca Therapeutics LLC, a topical-rapamycin company. Combined with n=17 completers and no independent replication, this is the textbook "small + conflicted" red flag. Now disclosed in §4(a), §12[1], and the table. This is the most important correction.
  2. The entire human-topical aging case = one study. Every "topical rapamycin reverses skin aging" claim traces to this one trial. There is no second study, no larger study, no independent group reproducing it.
  3. Disease ≠ aging. The robust human-topical data (TSC angiofibroma) prove activity on a vascular/proliferative lesion, not rejuvenation of normal aged skin. Correctly handled by the dossier; flagged here because it is the most common way this molecule is oversold.
  4. Penetration gap is real and undisputed (MW 914, logP 4.3, "500-Dalton rule" violation). Even if the aging effect were real, a consumer cream is unlikely to deliver an active dermal dose. Not a claim that needed softening.
  5. The molecule remains an Rx drug / systemic immunosuppressant (Hyftor 0.2% gel, FDA 2022) — verified-adjacent and decisive for the SKIP verdict regardless of efficacy.

Claims softened in this pass

  • §3 table: "Cellular senescence" topical-evidence rating Moderate → Weak (rests on one conflicted trial); "Collagen/ECM" Weak–moderate → Weak.
  • §4(a): added the Boinca COI disclosure and "no independent replication."
  • §4 brutal-honesty summary: "ONE small, exploratory, high-dropout trial" → added "industry-conflicted … with no independent replication."
  • §4 bottom-line bullet: annotated the topical-aging result as conflicted/unreplicated.
  • §10 reality-check #2: added the author equity stake + no-replication note; flagged that the study is better called an "exploratory trial," not a confirmatory "RCT."
  • §12[1]: added the verified shareholder/COI note.

Net

The dossier's SKIP verdict and its reasoning are fully supported — if anything the adversarial check strengthens it: the one piece of human-topical-aging evidence is weaker than it first appears (single, tiny, conflicted, unreplicated). No claim in the dossier was found to be fabricated or to misrepresent a source; the only material defect was the omitted conflict-of-interest disclosure, now corrected. For the brand, treat topical rapamycin's anti-aging story as Grade D / unproven topically and a cautionary example of senolytic-skincare overreach.
