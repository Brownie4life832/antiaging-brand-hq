Ingredient: Niacinamide (Vitamin B3) (INCI: Niacinamide)

Researched: 2026-06-19 · Status: ✅ shortlisted (strong SUPPORTING / near-hero) Class: Antioxidant (also: barrier/lipid modulator, NAD⁺ precursor, depigmenting agent) Evidence verdict at a glance: One of the best-evidenced cosmetic actives — multiple human topical RCTs for tone/barrier/wrinkle appearance, plus a phase-3 ORAL RCT for skin-cancer chemoprevention. Brutally honest caveat: most topical trials are industry-run, often on combination formulas, and effect sizes are modest. It is a reliable workhorse, not a miracle.

1. Identity

  • Common name: Niacinamide; nicotinamide; vitamin B3 (amide form).
  • INCI: Niacinamide.
  • Chemistry: Pyridine-3-carboxamide (the amide of nicotinic acid). Water-soluble, MW ~122 g/mol, small and relatively polar.
  • NOT the same as: Niacin / nicotinic acid — the acid form causes prostaglandin-D2–mediated flushing/vasodilation; the amide (niacinamide) does not. This distinction matters for both safety messaging and the old "don't mix with vitamin C" myth (see §10).
  • Key forms / derivatives:
  • Niacinamide itself — the standard cosmetic active.
  • Nicotinic acid (niacin) — flushing agent, not used as a cosmetic brightener.
  • Myristyl nicotinate / tocopheryl nicotinate — lipophilic prodrug esters designed for better penetration; far less clinical data.
  • NAD⁺ / NMN / NR (nicotinamide riboside) — downstream/related NAD⁺-boosters marketed in "longevity" supplements; topical cosmetic evidence is thin and mostly mechanistic/marketing. Do not conflate niacinamide's solid topical record with NMN/NR hype.

2. Class & mechanism

Niacinamide is a precursor to the redox coenzymes NAD⁺/NADH and NADP⁺/NADPH via the salvage pathway (niacinamide → NMN via NAMPT → NAD⁺). Aged and UV-stressed skin shows depleted NAD⁺; topically supplying niacinamide is thought to replenish these pools, which feed essentially every energy-, repair-, and antioxidant-dependent process in the cell. The leading mechanistic review (Boo 2021) is candid that "there is no convincing evidence that nicotinamide has specific molecular targets" — it is a broad metabolic substrate, not a precision drug. Proposed mechanisms:

  • NAD⁺/energy & antioxidant defense. Regenerates NADPH (pentose phosphate pathway) → drives glutathione reductase → replenishes reduced glutathione (GSH). Also a modest direct singlet-oxygen scavenger in vitro. This is the basis for its "Antioxidant" class assignment.
  • Barrier / lipid synthesis. Upregulates keratinocyte synthesis of ceramides, other sphingolipids (glucosylceramide, sphingomyelin), free fatty acids, and cholesterol, plus involucrin and filaggrin — i.e., it helps the skin make its own "mortar" and corneocyte envelope. This is the mechanistic spine of the barrier-repair story.
  • Pigmentation. Does not inhibit tyrosinase or melanin synthesis. Instead it inhibits melanosome transfer from melanocytes to keratinocytes (35–68% inhibition in coculture, reversible) — an indirect, keratinocyte-side mechanism (Hakozaki 2002).
  • ECM / photoaging. In cell/reconstructed-skin models, upregulates collagen (I, III, V) and elastin and downregulates MMP-1/3/9 by damping ROS→AP-1/NF-κB signaling.
  • DNA repair / photoimmune protection. As a PARP and sirtuin substrate, supports poly(ADP-ribose) DNA-repair cycling; enhances nucleotide-excision-repair gene expression and prevents UV-induced ATP depletion / immunosuppression. This is the mechanistic basis for the oral chemoprevention data (§4D).
  • Sebum. Some evidence it lowers sebum excretion rate / casual sebum levels.

Bottom line on hallmarks: Strongest, most defensible topical claims are tone/pigmentation, barrier/TEWL, and overall photoaging appearance. Collagen, senescence, mitochondrial, and microbiome claims rest on mechanism and should NOT be stated as proven topical benefits.

4. Evidence review by tier

Reading guide. Quality climbs left→right: in-vitro → animal → human ORAL → human TOPICAL. Within topical, RCT/vehicle-controlled > open-label. Caveat across the whole field: many topical trials are manufacturer-sponsored, small, short (8–12 wk), and test combination formulas, so isolating niacinamide's solo contribution is often impossible.

4A. In-vitro (cell culture / reconstructed skin) — mechanism only

  • Hakozaki et al. 2002 (BJD): no effect on mushroom tyrosinase or melanocyte melanogenesis; 35–68% inhibition of melanosome transfer in melanocyte–keratinocyte coculture; reduced pigmentation in pigmented reconstructed-epidermis (PREP) model. Establishes the depigmentation mechanism.
  • Keratinocyte studies (reviewed in Boo 2021): upregulation of ceramide/sphingolipid/FFA/cholesterol synthesis, involucrin, filaggrin; downregulation of MMP-1/3/9; upregulation of collagen I/III/V and elastin; direct singlet-oxygen scavenging (k ≈ 1.8 × 10⁸ M⁻¹s⁻¹).
  • Fibroblast studies: extended replicative lifespan, reduced senescence-associated β-gal — hypothesis-generating only.
  • Limitation: intracellular niacinamide in mammalian cells may not reach the ~50 µM threshold needed to inhibit sirtuins/PARP, so some proposed mechanisms may not operate at physiologic doses. The niacinamide transporter is unidentified.

4B. Animal — sparse and not the basis of claims

Most preclinical work is in cell/reconstructed-skin models rather than whole-animal dermatology. Photo-immunoprotection and DNA-repair work includes mouse UV models supporting the chemoprevention rationale, but animal data are not load-bearing for this ingredient; human data exist and supersede it.

4C. Human — ORAL (strongest single RCT for this molecule)

  • Chen J, Martin AJ, Damian DL et al. 2015 — the ONTRAC trial (N Engl J Med 373:1618–1626; DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa1506197). Phase-3, double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled. n = 386 high-risk patients (≥2 nonmelanoma skin cancers in prior 5 yr). Oral nicotinamide 500 mg twice daily vs placebo, 12 months. Result: ~23% lower rate of new nonmelanoma skin cancers (95% CI 4–38, p = 0.02) and ~11–20% fewer actinic keratoses over the trial. Benefit disappeared after stopping the supplement. Well tolerated, no excess adverse events.
  • Meta-analysis / systematic review (PMC9125143, 2022) pooled chemoprevention data: confirms a real but modest effect in high-risk populations, good safety.
  • Chen AC, Martin AJ, Dalziell RA, Halliday GM, Damian DL. 2016 — oral nicotinamide & TEWL (Br J Dermatol 175(6):1363–1365): in an RCT (n≈292, nicotinamide 500 mg BID vs placebo, 12 mo) oral nicotinamide reduced forehead TEWL by ~5–6% — statistically real but a small effect, and crucially ORAL, not topical. (Earlier drafts mis-attributed this to "Surya R" — corrected; it is the same Sydney group behind ONTRAC.)
  • CRITICAL FOR THE BRAND: ONTRAC is the strongest, most-cited evidence behind niacinamide — but it is ORAL, at pharmacologic dose, in immunocompromised/high-risk patients, for cancer prevention. It is NOT evidence that topical niacinamide cream prevents skin cancer or "repairs DNA" in healthy users. Do not borrow this halo into cosmetic claims. (A follow-up NEJM transplant-recipient trial, Allen et al. 2023, NEJMoa2203086, did not show benefit in that population — further reason to be cautious.)

4D. Human — TOPICAL

RCT / vehicle- or active-controlled (the strongest topical tier): - Bissett DL, Oblong JE, Berge CA. 2005 (Dermatol Surg 31:860–865) — 12-wk double-blind, vehicle-controlled, split-face RCT, n = 50 women (40–60). 5% niacinamide significantly improved fine lines/wrinkles, hyperpigmented spots, red blotchiness, sallowness (yellowing), and elasticity vs vehicle. Effects modest but statistically significant. Industry (P&G) authors. - Bissett DL et al. 2004 (Int J Cosmet Sci 26:231–238; DOI 10.1111/j.1467-2494.2004.00228.x) — companion paper, same program; topical niacinamide reduced yellowing, wrinkling, red blotchiness and hyperpigmented spots in aging facial skin. - Hakozaki et al. 2002 (clinical arms, BJD 147:20–31): 5% niacinamide, 8 wk, significantly reduced hyperpigmentation/increased lightness vs vehicle from week 4; a separate 2% in-sunscreen split-face arm (n = 120) also significant. - Fu JJJ, Hillebrand GG et al. 2010 (Br J Dermatol 162:647–654; DOI 10.1111/j.1365-2133.2009.09436.x) — randomized parallel-group, n = 196 women, 8 wk: a cosmetic 5% niacinamide + peptides + 0.3% retinyl propionate regimen vs prescription 0.02% tretinoin. The niacinamide regimen showed non-inferior to slightly better wrinkle improvement at 8 wk with far better tolerability (less erythema/dryness, preserved TEWL); equivalent by 24 wk. Confound: combination formula + unblinded self-assessment + tretinoin's known irritancy → cannot isolate niacinamide. - Acne (anti-inflammatory) RCTs: Khodaeiani 2013 (Int J Dermatol) — 4% niacinamide gel vs 1% clindamycin, n = 80, comparable efficacy (niacinamide better on oily skin). Shahmoradi 2013 (PMC3724370) — 5% niacinamide vs 2% clindamycin gel, n = 60, no significant difference. Earlier Shalita 1995 — 4% niacinamide ≈ 1% clindamycin. Supports anti-inflammatory/sebum claims. - Pigment-disorder RCTs: Navarrete-Solís 2011 (PMC3142702) — 4% niacinamide vs 4% hydroquinone for melasma, n = 27, comparable MASI reduction with less irritation; Castanedo-Cazares — 4% niacinamide vs 0.05% desonide, both > placebo. Combination work: niacinamide + N-undecylenoyl phenylalanine (Bissett 2008), + N-acetylglucosamine, + tranexamic acid all outperform niacinamide alone.

Barrier / TEWL (topical, mostly smaller/biophysical): - Forearm study (n = 41, 2% niacinamide, 24 d): increased stratum-corneum ceramide & FFA, decreased TEWL in dry skin. - Mechanistic biophysical work (e.g., Int J Pharm 2013, stratum-corneum molecular/biophysical properties; Sci Rep 2025 stratum-corneum hydration/structure [unverified — full text not accessed]) generally supports hydration/barrier effects.

Honest summary of the topical tier: Tone/pigmentation and barrier/TEWL have multiple vehicle-controlled human RCTs → "strong" for a cosmetic. Wrinkle/photoaging appearance is "moderate" (significant vs vehicle but modest, often combo formulas, mostly one research group/sponsor). Collagen, senescence, mitochondrial, microbiome, glycation = not proven topically.

5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

  • Small, water-soluble molecule that penetrates the stratum corneum reasonably well; readily taken up and converted intracellularly (the niacinamide transporter is still unidentified).
  • Penetration appears non-linear with dose — more on the label does not mean proportionally more in the skin. Widely-repeated secondary sources cite ~68% stratum-corneum penetration at 5% dropping to ~52% at 10%, but these specific figures could not be traced to a primary peer-reviewed study and are NOT in the cited Boo 2021 review — treat as indicative, not established. The defensible point stands: niacinamide is hydrophilic and partitions poorly into the SC lipid matrix, so increasing the label % does not yield proportional uptake. This supports keeping concentrations modest rather than chasing "10%+" (see §10).
  • Lipophilic ester prodrugs (myristyl/tocopheryl nicotinate) were designed to improve delivery but lack the clinical track record of plain niacinamide.

6. Formulation

  • Effective concentration: 2–5% is the well-supported sweet spot — this is where the human RCTs actually cluster (4–5%). A commonly-cited "~3.2% saturates the conversion enzymes" figure circulates in secondary skincare sources but could not be traced to a primary study; treat it as folklore, not fact. What IS defensible: published vehicle-controlled efficacy data sit at 2–5%, and above ~5% there is little controlled human evidence of added benefit while irritation/flushing risk rises in sensitive skin. Recommend formulating at 4–5%.
  • pH: Effective and stable across ~5–7; not pH-fragile like L-ascorbic acid or AHAs. Most stable around pH 6.
  • Stability: Very stable, long shelf life, heat-tolerant in normal processing, water-soluble — easy and cheap to formulate. (Avoid prolonged low-pH + heat, which can hydrolyze a little niacinamide to nicotinic acid — see flushing note.)
  • Pairs well with: essentially everything — retinoids (buffers irritation, complementary), peptides, hyaluronic acid, ceramides (synergistic barrier story), and vitamin C (the "they cancel out / cause flushing" claim is an outdated myth in modern formulas — see §10).
  • Conflicts: none significant. The only real-world issue is trace nicotinic-acid impurity in low-grade raw material or formulas held hot at low pH → transient flushing. Use cosmetic-grade purified niacinamide and a near-neutral pH.
  • Sensory: lightweight, non-greasy, plays well in serums, essences, lotions, and creams; no color/odor issues.

7. Safety

  • Irritation/sensitization: Very low. CIR/clinical testing: no stinging up to 10%, no irritation in 21-day cumulative tests up to 5%; not a sensitizer or photosensitizer. Occasional transient flushing at high % or with impure material.
  • Sun sensitivity: None — does not increase photosensitivity (if anything, photoprotective via DNA-repair support).
  • Pregnancy/lactation: Considered low-risk topically — minimal systemic absorption through intact skin, not an ECHA/CIR reproductive hazard; niacinamide is a normal B-vitamin and a routine prenatal nutrient. Standard "consult your doctor" boilerplate still applies; we do not give medical advice.
  • Sensitization/allergens: Low allergen risk; no common cross-reactors.
  • Oral note (context, not a topical claim): oral nicotinamide is well tolerated to ~1 g/day; hepatotoxicity only at extreme doses (>3 g/day); isolated thrombocytopenia in dialysis patients. Irrelevant to topical cosmetic use but useful for FAQ accuracy.
  • Sourcing/sustainability: Manufactured synthetically at large scale (vitamin/feed industry overlap); no major sustainability or supply-chain red flags.

8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

  • Cosmetic vs drug line: Niacinamide is a cosmetic ingredient. Claims must stay in appearance/support territory. The ORAL skin-cancer data and DNA-repair mechanism sit firmly on the drug side of the line and must not migrate into cosmetic copy.
  • ✅ Safe cosmetic language: "supports the skin's moisture barrier," "helps visibly even skin tone," "for a smoother, more refined look," "improves the appearance of enlarged-looking pores," "helps comfort and visibly strengthen the look of skin," "antioxidant support."
  • ⚠️ Risky / drug-like — avoid or flag for substantiation/legal: "increases ceramide synthesis / boosts collagen" (structure/function → keep to "supports the barrier"), "treats hyperpigmentation/melasma/acne/rosacea," "repairs DNA," "prevents skin cancer," "reduces wrinkles by X%." Anything borrowing the ONTRAC cancer-prevention halo.
  • See ../../brand/glossary.md.

9. Sourcing & cost

  • Cost tier: very low. Abundant commodity active, excellent cost-to-benefit — arguably the best "credibility per dollar" ingredient available.
  • Suppliers: Lonza, Jubilant, Vertellus, and numerous others; widely second-sourced. Low supply risk. Specify cosmetic/pharma grade with low nicotinic-acid impurity.

10. Hype vs. evidence

  • "10%+ is stronger." ❌ Mostly marketing — but argue it carefully. The solid part: human efficacy RCTs use 2–5%, and there is little-to-no controlled evidence that 10% outperforms 5%, while higher % buys irritation risk. The shakier part: the popular "enzymes saturate at 3.2%" and "penetration drops 68%→52% from 5% to 10%" figures are not traceable to primary studies (see §5, §6) — use the absence-of-benefit-evidence argument, not those precise numbers.
  • "Don't combine with vitamin C — they cancel out / cause flushing." ❌ Outdated myth from a 1960s study using niacin (the acid), high concentration, high heat/low pH. Modern niacinamide + vitamin C formulations at skin-friendly pH are fine and widely co-formulated.
  • "Boosts collagen / repairs DNA / NAD⁺ longevity in a jar." ⚠️ Mechanistically plausible, not proven for topical cosmetic use. NAD⁺/NMN/NR "longevity" framing is borrowed hype — keep niacinamide's claims to its real topical wins (tone, barrier, appearance).
  • "Prevents skin cancer." ⚠️ True-ish for ORAL 500 mg BID in high-risk patients (ONTRAC) — not a topical cosmetic claim, and not even replicated in transplant recipients (2023). Never use in product copy.
  • "Cure for acne/melasma." ❌ It performs comparably to some actives in trials and is well-tolerated, but "cure/treat" is drug language.

11. Verdict for the brand

✅ SUPPORTING (hero-grade). Niacinamide is the connective tissue of the barrier-repair/renew thesis: it is the rare active that is gentle, cheap, stable, layer-with-anything, and genuinely evidence-backed for the brand's two most defensible claim spaces — barrier/TEWL and tone. It is a near-default inclusion across the line and a legitimate co-hero (with ceramides) for a barrier-renewal product. One-line: broadly proven, well-tolerated barrier/tone multitasker with unbeatable credibility-per-dollar — include at 4–5%, but resist borrowing its oral/NAD⁺ longevity halo into cosmetic claims.

Longevity relevance: Strong thematic fit (NAD⁺ precursor, DNA-repair/sirtuin/PARP substrate, the "skin-longevity" molecule par excellence) — but be disciplined: the longevity mechanism is mostly oral/cellular, while the topical wins are barrier and tone. Use the longevity story for brand narrative, not for product efficacy claims.

Evidence verification (adversarial)

Verified 2026-06-19 by an adversarial fact-check pass. Method: each headline efficacy claim was tested for (a) HUMAN + TOPICAL provenance vs. quietly-borrowed in-vitro/animal/oral data, (b) whether the cited study is real and says what is claimed, (c) industry funding / small-n / single-group concentration. Primary checks done against PubMed/NEJM/Wiley/Oxford/PMC and the Boo 2021 review full text.

Overall evidence grade: B (moderate human-topical)

Niacinamide is genuinely one of the better-evidenced cosmetic actives, but it is not grade A. Grade A would require multiple large, independent, vehicle-controlled topical RCTs on the isolated active. What actually exists: a handful of vehicle-controlled topical RCTs (tone/pigmentation strongest), with effect sizes that are modest, run largely by one sponsor (P&G) / overlapping author teams, often on combination formulas, and short (8–12 wk). The single most impressive trial behind the molecule's reputation (ONTRAC) is oral, pharmacologic-dose, in high-risk patients, for cancer prevention — a different product, route, dose, and population. The dossier already handles most of this honestly; this section records the verification and the residual soft spots.

Grade by claim space: | Claim space | Best evidence type | Grade | |---|---|---| | Pigmentation / even tone | Multiple human topical vehicle-controlled RCTs (Hakozaki 2002; Navarrete-Solís 2011) | B+ (strong for a cosmetic) | | Barrier / TEWL / hydration | Smaller human topical biophysical studies + strong mechanism; best RCT TEWL data is ORAL | B– | | Photoaging appearance (lines, sallowness, blotchiness) | Human topical RCTs, but modest, mostly P&G, often combo | B–/C+ | | Acne / anti-inflammatory | Human topical RCTs vs clindamycin (Khodaeiani 2013; Shahmoradi 2013) — but "treat acne" is drug language | B (as comparison data), not a cosmetic claim | | Collagen / ECM, senescence, mitochondrial, microbiome, glycation | In-vitro / NAD⁺ rationale only | D topically | | DNA repair / skin-cancer prevention | ORAL phase-3 RCT (ONTRAC); null in transplant recipients (Allen 2023) | A for ORAL / D as a topical cosmetic claim |

What checked out (real studies, accurately represented)

  • ONTRAC (Chen 2015, NEJM, NEJMoa1506197): real. n=386, oral nicotinamide 500 mg BID, ~23% fewer NMSCs (p=0.02), benefit lost on stopping. Correctly flagged as ORAL and walled off from cosmetic copy. ✅
  • Allen 2023 (NEJM, NEJMoa2203086): real, and correctly described as a null result in transplant recipients — a genuinely honest inclusion that cuts against the ingredient. ✅
  • Hakozaki 2002 (BJD 147:20–31): real. 35–68% melanosome-transfer inhibition; no tyrosinase effect; 5% clinical arm reduced pigmentation. Accurately represented. ✅
  • Bissett 2005 (Dermatol Surg, vehicle-controlled split-face, n=50, 5%): real; findings (fine lines, spots, blotchiness, sallowness, elasticity) accurate. P&G authors — correctly disclosed. ✅
  • Fu 2010 (BJD 162:647–654, n=196): real; niacinamide/peptide/retinyl-propionate regimen ≈/> 0.02% tretinoin at 8 wk, equal by 24 wk. Dossier's "cannot isolate niacinamide — combination formula + tretinoin irritancy confound" caveat is correct and important. ✅
  • Navarrete-Solís 2011 (n=27, 4% niacinamide vs 4% HQ): real; comparable pigment improvement, less irritation. Accurately represented (note: small n=27, split-face). ✅
  • Khodaeiani 2013 (Int J Dermatol, n=80) & Shahmoradi 2013: real; niacinamide ≈ clindamycin. Accurate. ✅
  • Boo 2021 review: the load-bearing quote — "there is no convincing evidence that nicotinamide has specific molecular targets" — is verbatim accurate, as is the 1.8×10⁸ M⁻¹s⁻¹ singlet-oxygen rate constant and the "mainly formulated at 4–5%" point. ✅

Main caveats (the honest reading)

  1. Sponsor/author concentration. The topical photoaging evidence is dominated by P&G-affiliated authors (Bissett, Oblong, Hakozaki, Fu, Hillebrand) and the chemoprevention/TEWL evidence by one Sydney group (Damian/Halliday/Martin/Chen). Independent replication is thinner than the citation count suggests. Effect sizes are modest even where significant.
  2. Oral ≠ topical — the biggest borrowed-halo risk. The molecule's prestige comes from ORAL data (ONTRAC; the TEWL RCT is also oral). The dossier resists borrowing this into cosmetic claims — good — but the brand must hold that line in marketing.
  3. Combination-formula confound. The strongest "anti-wrinkle vs tretinoin" result (Fu 2010) is a multi-ingredient regimen; niacinamide's solo contribution is unprovable from it.
  4. Two quantitative claims were NOT primary-sourced (corrected in-text this pass): the "~68% penetration at 5% → ~52% at 10%" figures and the "~3.2% enzyme saturation" figure trace only to secondary/commercial skincare content, not to the cited Boo 2021 review or any primary study found. The directional argument (non-linear uptake; 2–5% is the evidenced range) survives; the precise numbers should not be asserted as fact.
  5. Author mis-attribution corrected: the 2016 oral-TEWL RCT (source #14) was credited to "Surya R"; the actual first author is Chen AC (same group as ONTRAC). Study real, claim accurate, citation now fixed.
  6. Two sources remain flagged [unverified] by the dossier itself (Snaidr review #9; Sci Rep 2025 #16) — appropriate; they are corroborative, not load-bearing.
