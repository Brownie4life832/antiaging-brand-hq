# Competitor: The Ordinary (DECIEM)

**Researched:** 2026-06-18 · **Site:** https://theordinary.com · **Tier:** $ (mass / drugstore-priced, ingredient-first)

## Positioning
"Clinical formulations with integrity." The Ordinary launched in 2016 under parent company DECIEM (founded 2013 by Brandon Truaxe and Nicola Kilner) as a deliberate disruptor: take high-concentration, well-studied actives, name each product literally after its active and percentage (e.g. "Niacinamide 10% + Zinc 1%"), strip out marketing markup, and sell at drugstore prices. The core promise is **radical transparency + accessibility** — exposing the beauty industry's pricing games by selling clinical-grade ingredients for a few dollars. DECIEM brands itself "The Abnormal Beauty Company." Now majority-owned by Estée Lauder, which acquired full control in a deal valuing DECIEM at ~$2.2B (76% stake reached in 2021 for ~$1B, full acquisition completed June 2024 in a ~$1.7B total transaction). The Ordinary is the volume engine of DECIEM and one of the most recognized skincare brands in the world.

## Audience
Ingredient-literate, value-driven, "do-it-yourself routine" skincare users. DECIEM explicitly targets "the educated, the curious and the intelligent." In practice the base skews young and digital-native: Gen Z and younger millennials who discovered the brand via TikTok/Reddit, want efficacious actives without paying prestige prices, and are comfortable building their own multi-step regimens from single-active products. Broadly unisex and global. Strongly over-indexes on entry-level and budget-conscious buyers, students, and the "skincare curious" — i.e. trial buyers acquired cheaply. Anti-aging buyers exist within the base but the brand is a generalist (acne, hydration, brightening, texture, barrier) rather than an aging/longevity specialist.

## Brand voice & aesthetic
**Voice:** Clinical, literal, deadpan, anti-marketing. Products are named by active + percentage, not by aspirational benefit names — the naming convention *is* the brand message (utility over hype). DECIEM's own tagline leans into provocation ("The Abnormal Beauty Company"; "Everything Is Chemicals"). Education-forward and community-driven: the brand has reformulated and even commissioned clinical studies in response to viral customer behavior (e.g. testing its Glycolic Acid 7% Toner as a scalp treatment after TikTok use). Tone is transparent, slightly contrarian, and unpretentious.

**Aesthetic:** Minimalist to the point of austerity. Plain white/clear bottles and droppers, monospace/typewriter-style labels, lab/pharmacy cues, no gloss, no glamour. The look deliberately signals "no money spent on packaging, all of it in the formula." Highly recognizable and screenshot-friendly, but intentionally cold, utilitarian, and un-luxurious.

## Strengths
- **Unbeatable price-to-active value.** Clinical-grade actives at $6–$20 is the brand's moat. Almost impossible to out-cheap on commodity actives.
- **Transparency as identity.** Active + % naming and "expose the markup" framing built deep trust and a sense of honesty competitors can't easily copy without looking derivative.
- **Massive cultural reach & cult status.** TikTok/Reddit-driven virality, global recognition, repeat-purchase hero SKUs (Niacinamide, HA) that act as acquisition machines.
- **Community-led product development.** Listens to and acts on customer behavior, even funding studies — generates authenticity and earned media.
- **Estée Lauder backing.** Scale, distribution (Ulta, Sephora, global), supply chain, and R&D resources behind a brand that still reads as indie.
- **Low-friction trial.** $6 entry price removes purchase risk and drives enormous top-of-funnel volume.

## Weaknesses / gaps
- **No emotional story, no soul, no ritual.** The brand is pure utility. It owns "cheap + honest actives" but not meaning, identity, sensory experience, or the *feeling* of caring for aging skin. Zero ritual or indulgence.
- **DIY burden / decision fatigue.** Single-active products require the customer to self-diagnose, build, and layer their own routine. Intimidating and error-prone for less-expert buyers; no curated "do this" simplicity for aging skin specifically.
- **Generalist, not an aging/longevity authority.** Anti-aging lives scattered across a sprawling single-active catalog (peptides, retinoids, etc.). No focused, credible "skin longevity" positioning or flagship aging system.
- **Aesthetic is intentionally un-premium.** The lab-austere look caps perceived value and price ceiling; it cannot credibly command a premium or sit on a luxury vanity. Cold and clinical by design.
- **Commoditized & widely cloned.** The "active + %" budget-actives model is now imitated by many drugstore lines; the brand's once-unique angle is increasingly generic.
- **Quality/consistency perception risk.** Frequent reformulations, occasional formulation criticism, and the inherent "you assembled it yourself" risk (irritation from over-layering actives) create variability.
- **Brand-narrative baggage.** Founder Brandon Truaxe's public unraveling and death (2019) plus the Estée Lauder takeover have, for some, eroded the original indie-disruptor mystique.

## What Kintsugi can beat them on
1. **Premium emotional meaning vs. cold utility.** The Ordinary is the definition of soulless efficacy — it has no story, no philosophy, no ritual. Kintsugi can own the *meaning* of aging (the kintsugi worldview: repair and gild your skin's history) — an entire emotional and aesthetic dimension The Ordinary structurally rejects.
2. **Curated "fewer, better" system vs. DIY active overload.** The Ordinary makes the customer the formulator. Kintsugi can win the anti-/pro-aging buyer who wants *guidance* — a tight, intelligently sequenced repair-and-renewal regimen (barrier, peptides, renewal) designed for them, no chemistry degree required.
3. **Design-led luxury aesthetic vs. lab austerity.** The Ordinary's white-pharmacy look is a price ceiling. Kintsugi's gold/wabi-sabi premium design can justify and own the $$–$$$ "informed indulgence" tier The Ordinary cannot reach.
4. **Focused skin-longevity authority vs. scattered generalist catalog.** The Ordinary spreads aging across dozens of single-active SKUs. Kintsugi can be *the* specialist for aging well — a coherent longevity story with a clear hero franchise, not a wall of percentages.
5. **Sensorial ritual + experience.** The Ordinary offers none. Kintsugi can deliver texture, ritual, and "informed indulgence" while keeping claims honest — the experience The Ordinary deliberately strips away.
6. **Pro-aging, dignified framing.** The Ordinary is benefit-neutral and transactional. Kintsugi's warmer "age beautifully, don't fight it" stance gives it emotional and PR territory The Ordinary never even attempts.

**Where NOT to fight them:** price (do not try to out-cheap $6 actives), raw active accessibility, or volume/scale. The Ordinary owns the bottom of the market on price-per-active. Beat them on meaning, curation, design, focus, and experience — i.e. by playing a completely different (premium, emotional, longevity-specialist) game, and ideally by *converting* their value-acquired customers as they trade up and seek a real anti-aging system with a story they believe in.
