# Competitor: Eighth Day

**Researched:** 2026-06-18 · **Site:** https://eighthdayskin.com · **Tier:** $$$ (entry ultra-luxury / "informed indulgence" top end)

> **Note on the brief's hint:** The prompt associated Eighth Day with Dr. Jason Diamond. That is incorrect — Dr. Jason Diamond's stem-cell/growth-factor brand is **Metacine** (Dr Diamond's Metacine, launched 2023). **Eighth Day** is a separate brand founded by **Dr. Antony (Tony) Nakhla**, a board-certified dermatologic / reconstructive skin-cancer surgeon. The two are frequently cross-shopped and compared in the "growth factor serum" category, which likely caused the mix-up. This teardown covers Eighth Day (Nakhla). [unverified whether any formal connection exists between the two — none found].

## Positioning
"We speak the language of skin." A surgeon-founded, science-forward luxury brand built on the premise that **aged skin is wounded skin that can be healed**. Sells "real science, real results, real change" via bioidentical, skin-native ingredients that signal cells to renew — positioned as the antidote to luxury skincare that charges $300+ for "glycerin, water and no rationale" (founder's own framing). Effectively: clinical-grade regenerative/wound-healing science, repackaged as luxury anti-aging.

## Audience
Affluent, results-driven 40+ consumers (skewing women) who are considering or already getting in-office aesthetic procedures (injectables, lasers, facials) and want a clinical-grade at-home regimen to match. The founder describes them as people "who demand the best and don't mind paying for it" — appearance/fitness/fashion-conscious, brand-agnostic but efficacy- and credential-driven. Sold through prestige retail (Violet Grey, Niche Beauty, med-spas/skin studios) and DTC.

## Brand voice & aesthetic
- **Voice:** Clinical authority softened with poetic, aspirational lines ("Discover the Power of Renewal," "We speak the language of skin"). Founder leans on vivid, accessible metaphors — Peptide-rich Plasma® as "a protein shake for the skin." Heavy emphasis on the surgeon's credentials, 10,000+ surgical patients, and clinical-trial percentages.
- **Aesthetic:** Sophisticated minimalism — clean white/neutral palette, cellular/scientific imagery, refined product photography. Reads "medical-luxury" (derm office meets prestige counter). Press-logo trust signals (Vogue, Allure, Harper's Bazaar).
- **Proof style:** Quantified clinical claims (97%, 100%, % improvement) plus retailer/editorial endorsements.

## Strengths
- **Credible founder + origin story.** A reconstructive skin-cancer surgeon who derived the tech from real wound-healing practice (PRP, dried cell membranes). "Aged skin = wounded skin" is a memorable, defensible narrative.
- **Ownable proprietary tech.** Peptide-rich Plasma® is patented/patent-pending, branded, and consistently messaged — a clear "why us."
- **Clean reformulation done well.** Evolved from placental-inspired "Active Placental Matrix" (600+ peptides, mirroring placental proteins) to a **synthesized, vegan, cruelty-free** Peptide-rich Plasma® (no actual placental/animal tissue) — and markets it as the "safest, cleanest" route. Also gluten-/paraben-/sulfate-/phthalate-/PEG-/fragrance-free.
- **Strong efficacy reputation.** Overwhelmingly positive independent reviews (Violet Grey, Who What Wear, Editorialist) and clinical-trial data; several reviewers call the serum "holy grail."
- **Prestige distribution + trust signals.** Stocked in luxury/derm-adjacent channels; broad editorial coverage.
- **Smart price laddering.** Multiple serum sizes (incl. value 50/100 ml), duos, minis, and a 15%-off first order lower the entry barrier into a $300+ product.

## Weaknesses / gaps
- **Category undifferentiation at the top.** Sits in a crowded, increasingly hot "growth factor / regenerative serum" pack (Metacine, Calecim, Angela Caglia, BIOEFFECT, etc.). The Peptide-rich Plasma® story is good but the consumer-visible promise ("firmer, smoother, renewed") is near-identical to rivals.
- **Price/value friction.** $325/30 ml flagship (up to ~$525/50 ml at some retailers); several signature sets sold out, and pricing is inconsistent across retailers (confusing, can read as opaque). Independent reviewers consistently flag it as expensive.
- **Results are gradual, not dramatic.** At least one honest review notes improvements "build gradually rather than dramatically" — a vulnerability against rivals showing dramatic before/afters.
- **Faintly clinical/cold brand world.** "Medical-luxury" minimalism is competent but generic; little emotional or philosophical hook beyond "science + renewal." No distinctive worldview a customer can belong to.
- **Origin-story baggage / messaging drift.** The placenta-inspired heritage (and the shift to synthesized "plasma") can confuse or unsettle some shoppers; the tech naming has changed over time (Active Placental Matrix → Peptide-rich Plasma®), which muddies the story.
- **Fear/correction framing.** "Wounded skin," "turn back the clock," "10 signs of aging" is conventional anti-aging language — emotionally extractive, not affirming.

## What Kintsugi can beat them on
1. **Emotional/philosophical worldview.** Eighth Day is all clinical authority and zero feeling. Kintsugi's gold/wabi-sabi "gild your history, don't erase it" reframe directly counters their fear-based "aged skin = wounded skin / turn back the clock." We can own the *meaning* layer they leave empty while matching them on science underneath.
2. **Pro-aging vs. correction.** Reframe the exact same actives (peptides, barrier repair, renewal) as *honoring* skin rather than *fixing damage* — a more PR-friendly, less commoditized story than Eighth Day's "10 signs of aging."
3. **Aesthetic differentiation.** They live in clinical-white/cellular minimalism (so does everyone). A gold/warm, tactile, kintsugi-inspired design system stands out sharply on shelf and feed.
4. **Radical claims & pricing honesty.** Eighth Day's pricing is inconsistent across retailers and its claims are big-number-heavy (97%/100%). Kintsugi can win trust with transparent, consistent pricing and honest "here's what's proven vs. not" copy — the skeptical 35–55 reader they both target rewards this.
5. **Beat the gradual-results vulnerability.** Pair an honest "real change takes time" narrative (fits the kintsugi/repair philosophy) with a genuinely better immediate sensory/finish experience, neutralizing the "builds slowly, not dramatically" knock against them.
6. **Lower-friction entry at true premium-not-ultra tier.** Eighth Day's hero is ~$325; positioning Kintsugi's hero in the $$–$$$ band with a believable story can capture buyers who find $325 (and $425 night cream) hard to justify without an emotional reason to commit.
