# Ingredient: Secondary AHAs — Malic, Tartaric & Citric Acid (INCI: Malic Acid; Tartaric Acid; Citric Acid)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product
**Class:** Hydroxy acids & chemical exfoliants (the "secondary" / fruit-derived AHAs, sitting behind the two workhorses glycolic and lactic acid)

> **One-line honest summary:** These three "fruit acids" are real alpha-hydroxy acids that share glycolic/lactic acid's exfoliation mechanism, but with **far less direct human clinical evidence as standalone actives**. Their genuine, well-evidenced role in a modern formula is usually **pH adjustment, buffering, chelation/antioxidant support, and gentle co-exfoliation** — not headline anti-aging performance. Citric acid has the single best skin-thickness study of the three; malic has the best antioxidant/pigment-enzyme data; tartaric is mostly a pH-buffer/blend ingredient with almost no standalone human skin trials. Treat as **supporting/functional**, not a hero.

---

## 1. Identity

These are three distinct molecules grouped because they are the most common *non*-glycolic, *non*-lactic AHAs used in cosmetics, all naturally occurring "fruit acids." Note an important classification subtlety: citric, malic and tartaric each carry more than one carboxyl and/or hydroxyl group, so they are **poly-functional hydroxy acids** — citric is sometimes described as having both alpha- and beta-hydroxy character, and all three are often marketed loosely as "fruit AHAs." Their dominant cosmetic behaviour is AHA-like (exfoliation at low pH) plus acid/chelant functionality. [1][14]

## 2. Class & mechanism

All AHAs act by the same core mechanism, established by Van Scott & Yu: at low pH the **un-ionized acid** interferes with intercellular ionic bonding between corneocytes, weakening corneodesmosomal cohesion in the lower stratum corneum, accelerating desquamation and normalizing epidermal differentiation ("keratolysis"/exfoliation). [3][2] Downstream of chronic use, AHAs are reported to increase epidermal thickness, dermal glycosaminoglycans (GAGs), and (in some studies) collagen — the same histologic signature seen with glycolic acid, ammonium lactate and even retinoic acid. [9][10]

Two mechanistic facts dominate everything practical about these molecules:

1. **They work in the un-ionized (acid) form, so pH is decisive.** Maximum corneocyte turnover is obtained around **pH ~3**, with the effective window roughly **pH 2.8–4.8**; above each acid's pKa the molecule ionizes and exfoliation falls off sharply. [4 (SCCNFP)] This is *more important than concentration* for both efficacy and irritation. [4]
2. **Molecular size modulates penetration and gentleness.** Glycolic (76 g/mol) penetrates best; lactic is next; malic (134), tartaric (150) and especially citric (192) are larger, so they penetrate the stratum corneum less readily — generally meaning **gentler, more superficial action** but also weaker per-mole exfoliation than glycolic. [5][6]

**Where the three diverge from glycolic/lactic — their real differentiators:**

- **Citric acid is primarily a chelator and antioxidant-enabler.** Its three carboxyls **sequester pro-oxidant trace metals** (Fe, Cu, Ca, Mg), which both stabilizes formulas (prevents metal-catalyzed oxidation/rancidity, protects other antioxidants and preservatives) and provides indirect antioxidant benefit. It is also the standard **pH adjuster/buffer** across cosmetics (effective buffer roughly pH 2–8). [7][8] Much of citric acid's presence in skincare is functional, not "active."
- **Malic acid has the strongest direct antioxidant / pigment-enzyme data of the three** (DPPH radical scavenging; inhibition of DOPA auto-oxidation), discussed in §4. [12]
- **Tartaric acid is mostly a pH buffer / blend acid** with antioxidant claims from its grape origin but very little standalone mechanistic skin work. [1]

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

| Hallmark | Plausibility for these acids | Best evidence tier behind it |
|---|---|---|
| **Photoaging (texture, fine lines, dyschromia)** | Plausible — AHA-class effect; high-concentration (20–25%) citric / citric-in-blend altered photoaging *histology* markers (epidermal thickness, GAGs), not blinded clinical wrinkle scores | Human topical (citric in Ditre/Bernstein, both at 20–25%); class-level RCTs are mostly glycolic/lactic [9][10][11] |
| **Collagen/ECM loss** | Plausible (↑ dermal GAGs, collagen density with chronic AHA) | Human topical biopsy (citric 20%, AHA 25% incl. citric) [9][10] |
| **Barrier decline / hydration** | Mixed — low-pH AHAs can transiently *impair* barrier; malic/tartaric act as humectants; but exfoliation ≠ barrier repair | Mechanistic + in vitro; AHAs can raise TEWL [4][16] |
| **Pigmentation / tone** | Plausible — exfoliation evens tone; malic & citric inhibit tyrosinase / melanogenesis in vitro | In vitro (malic IC50 data); human via general AHA exfoliation [12] |
| **Oxidative stress** | Plausible — citric chelates pro-oxidant metals; malic/tartaric scavenge radicals in vitro | In vitro / formulation-level [7][12] |
| **Microbiome / acid mantle** | Plausible — restoring/maintaining acidic surface pH supports barrier enzymes & flora | Mechanistic (pH biology) [4] |
| **Cellular senescence** | No direct evidence for these acids | — |
| **Mitochondrial decline** | No skin evidence (malate/citrate are TCA-cycle metabolites systemically, but that is irrelevant to topical skin) | — |
| **Inflammaging** | No clean evidence; low-pH acids can be pro-irritant rather than anti-inflammatory | — |
| **Glycation / AGEs** | **No direct evidence** these acids inhibit skin glycation despite "fruit-acid antioxidant" marketing | — |

## 4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

> **The single most important honesty point:** Almost all of the *strong human topical* AHA evidence belongs to **glycolic and lactic acid**. For malic, tartaric and citric specifically, the human topical record is thin: **citric acid has two real biopsy studies** (one standalone, one as part of an AHA mix), **malic acid's best data is in-vitro antioxidant/anti-tyrosinase**, and **tartaric acid has essentially no standalone human skin trial** — it rides on the AHA *class* and on its formulation (buffer/chelant) role. When a "fruit acid" serum implies retinoid-like remodeling, it is borrowing from glycolic/lactic RCTs.

### Tier 1 — In vitro / mechanistic

- **Van Scott & Yu, 1984, *J Am Acad Dermatol* 11:867–879.** Foundational mechanism: AHAs reduce corneocyte cohesion by interfering with intercellular ionic bonding → desquamation/keratolysis. Establishes the whole class mechanism (not acid-specific). [3]
- **Smith, 1996, *Int J Cosmet Sci* 18:75–83 — "Comparative effectiveness of α-hydroxy acids on skin properties."** Directly compared glycolic, L-(+)/D-(−) lactic, **citric, malic** and hydroxybutyric acids on cell renewal, stinging, moisture and lines/wrinkles (≈0.5–1.15 M, ~6 weeks). **Conclusion: L-(+) lactic and glycolic were the most effective and among the least irritating; the larger fruit acids (incl. malic, citric) were less effective per equimolar dose.** This is the most direct head-to-head and it does *not* favour the fruit acids on efficacy. [13] (Popular blogs cite "malic acid increases cell renewal 18%/10%/5% at pH 3/5/7" — this figure traces to Smith-era cell-renewal work but I could **not verify the exact malic-specific numbers in the primary text** [partially unverified].)
- **Carboxylic-acid tyrosinase study, 2025, *PMC11990924*.** **Malic acid** inhibited mushroom tyrosinase (IC50 ≈ **3.91 mM**, mixed-type) and was the most potent inhibitor of **L-DOPA auto-oxidation (IC50 ≈ 0.38 mM)** among the carboxylic acids tested; mechanism judged to be largely **environmental acidification** (3 mM → pH 4.6; 6 mM → pH 3.5) plus some direct effect, confirmed in B16-F10 melanoma cell melanin assays. **Citric was *not* tested in this 2025 paper**; the **citric IC50 ≈ 1.615 mM** (and succinic ≈ 2.943 mM) figure is the authors' *prior* mushroom-tyrosinase data quoted within it, not a result of this study. **Tartaric not tested.** Malic is GRAS (FEMA); oral LD50 1600–3200 mg/kg. *Limitation: enzyme/cell models, not human skin; "acidification" mechanism means any low-pH AHA would do similar.* [12]
- **Citric acid as chelator/antioxidant (formulation literature).** Citric sequesters Fe/Cu/Ca/Mg, suppressing metal-catalyzed oxidation and protecting co-formulated antioxidants/preservatives (weaker than EDTA/GLDA but "clean-label" acceptable). [7][8] *This is a genuine, repeatedly documented function — but it is a* formulation *benefit, not a skin-biology claim.*

### Tier 2 — Animal

- No meaningful malic/tartaric/citric-specific animal skin-aging studies were identified. Class-level animal work exists for glycolic (penetration, UV photo-toxicity testing in hairless guinea pigs — see §7) but does not validate the fruit acids individually. [4] *Honest gap.*

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL

- Not applicable to a topical skin claim. Malate, citrate and tartrate are dietary organic acids / TCA-cycle intermediates (citrate, malate) with food-additive (GRAS) status; **no oral data is relevant to topical skin performance** and should never be cited as if it were. Including this only to pre-empt the misuse of "it's a Krebs-cycle molecule" marketing. [Identity sources 1][12]

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters — and it is led by citric, thin for malic, near-empty for tartaric)

- **Bernstein et al., 1997, *Dermatologic Surgery* 23(8):689–694 — "Citric Acid Increases Viable Epidermal Thickness and Glycosaminoglycan Content of Sundamaged Skin."** **20% citric acid lotion vs vehicle**, applied to sun-damaged skin, biopsies after **3 months**. Image analysis showed **increased viable epidermal thickness and increased dermal glycosaminoglycans** in treated skin; authors concluded citric acid produces changes **similar to glycolic acid, ammonium lactate and retinoic acid**. *This is the best standalone human study for any of the three acids. Limitations: small dermatology-surgery study, exact n and pH not confirmed from the abstract retrieved, single-acid but high (20%) concentration, surrogate histologic endpoints rather than blinded clinical wrinkle scoring.* [9]
- **Ditre, Griffin, Murphy, … Yu, Van Scott, 1996, *J Am Acad Dermatol* 34(2):187–195 — "Effects of α-hydroxy acids on photoaged skin."** Forearm split-design: lotion with **25% glycolic, lactic, OR citric acid** vs placebo, ~**6 months**. Clinical + histologic + ultrastructural analysis showed **~25% increase in skin thickness**, thicker papillary dermis, **increased GAGs/acid mucopolysaccharides, improved elastic-fiber quality, increased collagen density**, with no inflammation. **Citric acid was one of the three actives**, so this is real (if pooled) human topical support for citric at high concentration. *Limitations: pilot study, small, forearm (not face), three acids analyzed together at a high 25% level, no isolation of citric-only magnitude.* [10]
- **Stiller et al., 1996, *Arch Dermatol* 132:631–636.** 22-week double-blind vehicle-controlled RCT, **n=74 women (40–70 y)**, **8% glycolic OR 8% L-lactic** creams: "modestly useful" improvement in photodamage, well tolerated. **Does NOT include malic/tartaric/citric** — included here as the benchmark for what a *rigorous* AHA RCT looks like, and to show the fruit acids have nothing equivalent. [11]
- **Tartaric acid:** No standalone human topical RCT identified. It appears in **multi-acid peel/blend** contexts (secondary sources cite a "Kornhauser et al." chemical-peel report using tartaric in combination [partially unverified]) and as a pH buffer. Its skin claims rest on the AHA *class* and on its formulation role, not on its own trials. [1]
- **Malic acid:** No robust standalone human topical anti-aging RCT identified; human use evidence is essentially "it's an AHA + the in-vitro antioxidant/tyrosinase data + safety patch testing." [12][CIR malic, source 17]

**Bottom line by tier:**
- **Citric** → 1 standalone human biopsy study (20%) + inclusion in a 25% AHA human study; plus strong *formulation* (chelant/antioxidant/buffer) evidence. **Best-evidenced of the three for skin remodeling, but at high concentrations and via histology.**
- **Malic** → best *in-vitro* antioxidant/anti-tyrosinase profile; no strong standalone human aging trial.
- **Tartaric** → weakest; a buffer/blend acid with class-borrowed claims.
- Any retinoid-comparison language is honest only at **high (20–25%) concentrations under occlusive/long study conditions**, not at the **low fractional-percent levels** these acids usually appear at in finished consumer products.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **pH governs everything.** AHAs penetrate and act far better in the **un-ionized form** (below pKa). In vitro human-skin diffusion work shows AHA absorption is dramatically higher at **pH 3.0 vs 7.0**, and for glycolic, 4% at pH 2.0 deposits significantly more into skin than at pH 3.8. [6][4] For these acids with first pKa ~2.9–3.4, a meaningful un-ionized fraction requires a genuinely acidic formula (pH ~3–4).
- **Size penalty.** Penetration tracks inversely with molecular size: glycolic (76) > lactic (90) > malic (134) > tartaric (150) > citric (192). In a homologous AHA penetration series, total absorption fell from ~27–30% (glycolic/lactic) to ~19–21% for the larger AHAs. [5] Practically, the fruit acids are **more superficial** actors — a plus for gentleness, a minus for deep remodeling.
- **Penetration-enhancer caveat (safety-relevant).** AHAs can increase penetration of *other* ingredients; regulators flagged that AHAs "markedly increased the skin penetration of Benzophenone-3 (oxybenzone)" in one formulation, though glycolic did **not** enhance hydroquinone/musk-xylol absorption in guinea pig testing. [4] Co-formulation should be assessed for unintended enhanced absorption of partners.
- **Practical read:** Bioavailability is *real but pH- and size-limited.* At skin-friendly pH and the low concentrations typical of consumer "fruit acid" products, expect modest surface exfoliation + buffering/chelation, not the high-concentration histologic remodeling seen in Bernstein/Ditre.

## 6. Formulation

- **Concentration:**
  - *As actives/exfoliants:* effective human histologic data exist at **citric 20%** [9] and the **25% AHA blend** [10]; gentler exfoliation in finished products typically uses low single digits. There is **no validated low-concentration dose-response** for malic/tartaric as standalone anti-agers — do not imply one.
  - *As functional ingredients:* **citric acid** is used at **~0.1–1% (often <0.5%)** purely for **pH adjustment/chelation**; the 2014 CIR found leave-on citric up to ~4% (see §7). Malic max leave-on use ~**2.1%**; both citric and malic patch-tested clean at ≤4%/1% respectively (§7). [15][17]
- **pH:** Target **~3.5–4.0** for AHA exfoliation while respecting the FDA/CIR consumer guardrail of **pH ≥ 3.5 at ≤10%** (§8). For *non-exfoliating* use (citric as a pH adjuster) the acid simply trims formula pH to the desired window. The EU SCCNFP precautionary view is even more conservative for the two workhorse acids (glycolic ≤4% pH ≥3.8; lactic ≤2.5% pH ≥5). [4][8]
- **Stability:** All three are stable, water-soluble solids. **Citric is itself a stabilizer** (chelates oxidation-catalyzing metals, extends antioxidant/preservative life). [7] Caution: as a polyfunctional acid, citrate can interact with cationic/charged actives; tartaric/citric buffers must be balanced so the final pH actually lands in range. Avoid Maillard-type discoloration risk in systems with reducing sugars + amines over time (general organic-acid caution).
- **Pairings (conceptual):**
  - **Citric + vitamin C / other antioxidants** — chelation protects the antioxidant and lowers pH (note: L-ascorbic itself needs low pH).
  - **Malic/tartaric/citric as "buffer/secondary acids" in a glycolic- or lactic-led exfoliant** — to soften peak acidity, add a gentler exfoliating fraction and round the sensory.
  - **AHA + niacinamide / ceramides** — pair surface renewal with barrier support (counters AHA's transient barrier/TEWL hit; see §3).
  - **AHA + daily SPF** — non-negotiable given UV-sensitivity data (§7).
- **Conflicts:** Strong direct acids (L-ascorbic, high-strength glycolic) plus these acids can stack irritation and over-drop pH; charged/cationic systems may interact with citrate/tartrate. Test final pH and challenge stability.
- **Sensory:** Low color/odor at typical levels; well-known, "clean-label/natural fruit acid" story is a sensory/marketing positive. At higher exfoliating strengths expect tingle/sting on application.

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation / pH-dependence:** Irritation is driven by **pH more than concentration**: as pH rises 3→7 irritation falls sharply, and a higher-% acid at higher pH can be *milder* than a lower-% acid at lower pH (e.g., 13% glycolic pH 4.4 < 8% glycolic pH 3.25). [4] Expect transient sting/tingle; the larger fruit acids are generally gentler than glycolic at equal pH/strength.
- **Sensitization (good news, acid-specific):**
  - **Citric:** 2014 CIR (Fiume et al.) — citric acid, citrate salts and alkyl citrates **safe as used**; repeated-insult patch testing at **4% leave-on** showed **no irritation and no sensitization**. [15]
  - **Malic:** CIR — **1% in formulation did not induce sensitization (HRIPT)**; however **no sensitization data at the 2.1% max leave-on level** (a real data gap). [17]
  - **Tartaric:** CIR **deferred** to FDA's review; tartaric is metabolically inert / GRAS as related tartrate salts. Standalone cosmetic sensitization dataset is thinner. [1]
- **Sun sensitivity (the defining AHA safety issue — class-level, applies to these acids):** FDA-sponsored clinical studies showed daily **10% glycolic at pH 3.5 for 4 weeks** significantly **lowered the minimal erythema dose** and **increased UVB-induced sunburn cells (a DNA-damage marker)** — i.e., increased UV sensitivity; effect **returned to normal ~1 week after stopping**. [4] FDA's consumer guidance states AHAs may increase sun sensitivity by **up to ~18%** during use and for up to a week after, and recommends a **"Sunburn Alert"** label and daily sun protection. [18] **This is the central safety story for any AHA product — including fruit-acid ones.**
- **Barrier:** Low-pH AHA use can transiently impair the barrier / raise TEWL; one study found **no barrier disruption with 5% glycolic at pH 3.8**, suggesting moderate, well-buffered formulas are reasonable. [4] Pair with barrier support.
- **Pregnancy:** Topical AHAs are generally considered low-risk in pregnancy (minimal systemic absorption), but with limited dedicated data the conservative cosmetic line is **"consult your doctor."** No specific malic/tartaric/citric pregnancy concern identified, but don't assert safety affirmatively in copy.
- **Sourcing/allergen:** All three are common food acids (GRAS); "fruit-derived" framing is generally trust-positive. Most cosmetic-grade material is produced by **fermentation/synthesis**, not literal fruit pressing — avoid implying artisanal fruit extraction unless true.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Cosmetic status:** Malic, tartaric and citric are all established cosmetic INCIs with pH-adjuster / buffering / chelating / (for citric) antioxidant and (all) skin-conditioning functions. Citric and malic have CIR safety assessments; tartaric is CIR-deferred to FDA. [1][15][17] As **exfoliants/anti-agers** they fall under the AHA framework.
- **AHA-specific labeling guardrails:**
  - **US FDA (cosmetic, not approved drugs):** AHAs are considered safe for consumer use at **≤10% and final pH ≥3.5**, *provided* the product is formulated to avoid increased sun sensitivity **or** directions advise daily sun protection; FDA recommends the **"Sunburn Alert"** statement. [18] These are cosmetics, *not* OTC drugs, so structure/function and disease language is off-limits.
  - **CIR (1997/1998):** glycolic & lactic safe **≤10% / pH ≥3.5** consumer; **≤30% / pH ≥3.0** professional, rinse-off, with sun protection. [4]
  - **EU SCCNFP precautionary position (2000):** for the two main AHAs, **glycolic ≤4% pH ≥3.8; lactic ≤2.5% pH ≥5**, with eye-contact and UV-protection warnings — a stricter benchmark to respect for EU sales. [4]
- **The drug/cosmetic line:** "Exfoliates," "smooths," "helps improve the look of texture/tone," "supports radiance," "helps maintain skin's natural pH," and (for citric) "antioxidant/chelating support" are defensible cosmetic claims. ⚠️ Drug-territory / substantiation-or-legal-review: **"reverses photoaging," "boosts/increases collagen by X%," "rebuilds the dermis," "anti-aging," "treats melasma/hyperpigmentation."** Note that even the genuine human studies (Bernstein, Ditre) used **20–25%** and measured **histology**, not blinded wrinkle scores at consumer concentrations — so "retinoid-like remodeling" is *not* substantiated for a typical low-% fruit-acid product.
- ✅ **Safer cosmetic language:** "gentle fruit-acid exfoliation," "helps smooth and refine the look of skin," "helps even the appearance of tone," "naturally derived AHA," "helps keep the formula fresh and balanced" (citric chelation/pH). See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).
- ⚠️ **Risky:** any collagen-percentage, "anti-aging," disease, or "as effective as retinol" claim; and **never omit** the sun-sensitivity guidance.

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Cost tier: LOW.** Citric, malic and tartaric are bulk commodity food/industrial acids — among the cheapest functional ingredients in any formula. Widely available cosmetic/USP/food grades from global suppliers; citric is ubiquitous as a pH adjuster.
- **Sourcing notes:** Most cosmetic-grade citric and malic are **fermentation- or synthesis-derived**; tartaric is often a **wine-industry by-product** (cream of tartar lineage), which is a genuine upcycling/sustainability angle if sourced that way. Specify L-isomers where relevant and require a COA. Supply is deep and stable (these are megaton-scale commodities), so **no meaningful supply risk** — they are the opposite of a single-source specialty active.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

- **"Fruit acids = a gentler retinol."** The retinoid-comparison comes from Bernstein/Ditre at **20–25%** measuring **histology**; it does **not** transfer to a 1–2% "apple/grape acid" serum. Honest framing: gentle surface renewal, not dermal remodeling.
- **"Antioxidant fruit acids fight aging."** Citric's antioxidant value is mainly **metal chelation in the bottle** (stabilizing the formula), and malic/tartaric radical-scavenging is **in-vitro**. There is **no human evidence** these acids meaningfully reduce skin oxidative damage in use, and **no evidence at all** for anti-glycation/AGE benefit despite "fruit antioxidant" marketing.
- **"Malic brightens / fades dark spots."** Based on **in-vitro tyrosinase/DOPA-oxidation** data whose mechanism is largely **acidification** — i.e., generic to low-pH acids, not a special malic property — plus general exfoliation. No standalone human pigment RCT. [12]
- **"Tartaric acid is a clinically proven anti-ager."** It has essentially **no standalone human topical trial**; it's a buffer/blend acid. Claims rest on the AHA class.
- **"Krebs-cycle / cellular-energy molecule."** Citrate and malate are metabolic intermediates *systemically*; this is **irrelevant to topical skin** and is a classic marketing non-sequitur.
- **The omission problem.** The best-evidenced, most certain fact about these acids — **they increase UV sensitivity** — is the one marketing tends to bury. Leading with sun protection is both honest and on-brand for a science-forward label.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ **SUPPORTING / FUNCTIONAL — not a hero.**
**One-line:** These three fruit acids earn their place as **gentle co-exfoliants and (especially citric) as a pH-buffer/chelator/antioxidant-protector**, but their standalone human anti-aging evidence is modest (best for citric, thin for malic, near-absent for tartaric) and the headline AHA performance belongs to glycolic/lactic — so use them in a supporting role with honest, mechanism-true copy.

**How they fit the barrier-repair / skin-longevity thesis:**
- **Citric acid is almost a guaranteed inclusion** — but as a **formulation workhorse** (pH adjuster, chelator that protects other antioxidants/preservatives, gentle co-exfoliant), not as a marketed hero.
- **Malic and/or tartaric** make sense as **gentler secondary acids in a multi-acid exfoliant** (softening a glycolic/lactic peak, adding a "natural fruit acid" + humectant/antioxidant story) — *if* the brand decides exfoliation belongs in the lean routine at all. Given the thesis favors **barrier repair over barrier disruption**, any AHA product must be paired with barrier support (ceramides/niacinamide) and mandatory SPF messaging.
- They are **cheap, stable, commodity, low-supply-risk** — fully consistent with the "buy commodity bases, invest in the brand" principle. The risk is purely **claims discipline**, not cost or sourcing.

**Longevity-relevance note:** Of the recognized aging hallmarks, these acids credibly touch **photoaging (texture/tone via exfoliation)** and, for citric, **oxidative stress (formula-level chelation)** and **acid-mantle/barrier pH** — modest, real, mechanism-honest. They do **not** have evidence for senescence, mitochondrial, glycation, or inflammaging benefit, and should not be positioned as "longevity actives." Their honest longevity contribution is **enabling** (keeping vitamin C / antioxidant systems stable, maintaining healthy surface pH) and **cosmetic surface renewal** — supporting cast to genuine repair actives, with sun protection as the real anti-photoaging lever.

**Main caveats (what a skeptic should hold onto):**
1. **No fruit acid here has a standalone, blinded, clinical (wrinkle-scored) human RCT at consumer concentrations.** The two supportive human studies (Bernstein, Ditre) are at **20–25%** and read out **histology**, and Ditre pools citric with glycolic/lactic. Everything stronger is glycolic/lactic, not these acids.
2. **Malic's "brightening" and the tyrosinase data are in-vitro and mechanistically acidification-driven** — i.e., not a malic-specific bioactivity. Any low-pH acid would do similar in that assay; this should never be sold as a unique malic benefit.
3. **Funding/size:** the supportive human studies are small academic/dermatology-surgery studies from the Van Scott/Yu lineage (the AHA-patent originators) — not independent large RCTs. Not disqualifying, but not the gold standard either. The dossier does not overstate them.
4. **Oral / TCA-cycle framing is irrelevant to topical skin** — the dossier already pre-empts this misuse correctly.

- §3 photoaging row reworded so it no longer reads as "blends reverse photoaging markers" (which edges toward a drug claim); it now specifies **high-concentration, histologic** markers, not clinical wrinkle reversal.
