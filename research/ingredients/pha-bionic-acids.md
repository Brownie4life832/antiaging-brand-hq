## 1. Identity

**Polyhydroxy acids (PHAs)** and **bionic acids** are the "next-generation," larger-molecule cousins of alpha-hydroxy acids (AHAs). Chemically they *are* AHAs — a hydroxy/carboxyl in the alpha position — but they carry **multiple additional hydroxyl groups** (PHAs) or **a second sugar unit** (bionic acids). The extra hydroxyls make them bigger, slower-penetrating, more humectant, and markedly gentler.

| Name | INCI | Type | MW (g/mol) | pKa | Source / chemistry |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Gluconolactone** (glucono-δ-lactone, GDL) | Gluconolactone | PHA | ~178 | ~3.6 | Cyclic ester of gluconic acid; oxidation of glucose; hydrolyzes in water to gluconic acid. The workhorse PHA. |
| **Gluconic acid** | Gluconic Acid | PHA | ~196 | ~3.6 | Open-chain acid form of the above; the two interconvert in solution. |
| **Lactobionic acid** | Lactobionic Acid | Bionic acid | ~358 | ~3.8 | Oxidation of lactose = **galactose + gluconic acid**. Used in organ-transplant preservation fluid (antioxidant chelator). |
| **Maltobionic acid** | Maltobionic Acid | Bionic acid | ~358 | ~3.86 | Oxidation of maltose = **glucose + gluconic acid**; plant-derived. |
| **Galactose** | Galactose | Monosaccharide moiety | ~180 | n/a (a sugar, not an acid) | The sugar released from lactobionic acid; implicated in proteoglycan/procollagen synthesis. |

**Key forms/derivatives in commerce:** Gluconolactone is the most common stand-alone PHA. Lactobionic and maltobionic are the headline "bionic acids." Galactose and gluconic acid usually appear *as part of* the bionic molecules rather than as primary actives. NeoStrata holds much of the foundational IP and most published human data.

> Plain-language: **gentle exfoliating acids that double as humectants and antioxidants.** Think "AHA for sensitive skin" — the smoothing/renewal of glycolic/lactic, with far less sting and a moisturizing bonus.

## 2. Class & mechanism

PHAs/bionics act through several overlapping routes:

1. **Exfoliation (desquamation).** Like AHAs, they lower stratum-corneum pH and weaken corneocyte cohesion, accelerating shedding of dull surface cells → smoother texture, more even tone. Because they are larger (MW 178–358 vs glycolic's 76), they penetrate **more slowly and superficially**, which is why they exfoliate with much less stinging/irritation. (Grimes 2004; clinical-textbook chapter.)
2. **Humectancy / barrier support.** Multiple hydroxyl groups bind and hold water. Lactobionic acid forms a hydrating gel matrix and is reported to retain water better than glycerin or sorbitol. Unlike classic AHAs, gluconolactone and glucoheptonolactone have been shown to **strengthen** stratum-corneum barrier function (reduced TEWL and erythema after a chemical challenge). (Grimes 2004.)
3. **Antioxidant / metal chelation.** Gluconolactone scavenges free radicals (comparable in an in-vitro assay to ascorbic acid and α-tocopherol) and chelates metals; lactobionic acid complexes Fe(II), suppressing hydroxyl-radical generation (the basis of its use in organ-preservation fluid). (Bernstein 2004.)
4. **MMP inhibition.** Lactobionic and maltobionic acids inhibit matrix metalloproteinases (MMP-9 in particular) — the enzymes that degrade collagen/elastin with age and UV. (Green 2008; maltobionic in-vitro work.)
5. **Anti-glycation.** Bionic/polyhydroxy acids dose-dependently inhibit non-enzymatic protein glycation in vitro, with potency similar to the reference inhibitor aminoguanidine. (Green 2014.)

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

| Hallmark | Relevance | Evidence tier (for **topical human** use unless noted) |
|---|---|---|

## 4. Evidence review by tier  ← **core of this dossier**

> **Brutal-honesty framing.** PHAs/bionics have a *real but thin and conflicted* human evidence base. Almost every human topical study is **manufacturer-affiliated (NeoStrata: Green, Edison, Sigler, Wildnauer)**, published in non–top-tier or trade journals (*Cutis*, *Cosmetic Dermatology*, *Dermatologic Surgery*), and **most are uncontrolled "controlled-usage" studies, not vehicle-controlled RCTs.** The one comparative trial benchmarks PHA against AHA (active comparator) rather than against placebo. So: the *tolerability* advantage over AHAs is well-supported; the *anti-aging efficacy* claims rest on small, industry, lower-tier studies plus mechanistic in-vitro work. Treat efficacy as **moderate-at-best, gentleness as strong**.

### 4a. In-vitro
- **Bernstein et al. (2004), *Dermatol Surg* 30:189–195** — Transgenic (elastin-promoter) model of photoaging. Gluconolactone gave **up to ~50% protection** against UV-induced elastin-promoter activation; acts via radical scavenging/chelation, not UV screening. Also reported no significant rise in sunburn cells. *Limitation: in-vitro/reporter model; "50% protection" is not a clinical SPF and must never be framed as sun protection.* [1]
- **Green, Edison, Bojanowski, Weinkauf (2014), *JAAD* 70(2):AB22 / 10.1016/j.jaad.2014.01.092** — Bionic/polyhydroxy acids produced **dose-dependent, statistically significant (P<0.05) inhibition of non-enzymatic glycation** in vitro, comparable to aminoguanidine; linked mechanistically to reduced skin sallowness. *Limitation: in-vitro; conference/abstract-level; industry authors.* [2]
- **Maltobionic acid in cultured cells** — inhibits MMP activity, lipid peroxidation, and melanogenesis in B16 melanocytes (supplier/technical literature; supports collagen-protection, antioxidant, and pigment claims at the cell level). *Limitation: supplier-sourced, not independent peer review.* [3]
- **Zerbinati et al. (2023), *Pharmaceuticals* 16(5):655** — In-vitro on a **multi-ingredient** gluconolactone+mandelic+salicylic+phytic+peptide lotion (not isolated gluconolactone): high cell viability (RHE 105%, IL-1α 2.52 pg/mL = non-irritant range), ↑COL1A1/elastin at 1.25–2.5 mg/mL, ↑involucrin, and ↓senescent-cell markers (~44–54%) post-UVB. *Limitation: formula not the molecule; in-vitro; can't attribute effects to PHA alone.* [4]

### 4b. Animal
- **No dedicated topical anti-aging animal studies of note.** (Lactobionic acid's best-known systemic use is as a component of University-of-Wisconsin organ-preservation solution — relevant only as a mechanism analogy, not a skin claim.) Evidence tier here is effectively empty for skin aging.

### 4c. Human — ORAL
- **Not applicable / no relevant evidence.** Gluconic acid/gluconolactone appear in foods (acidulant, e.g. tofu coagulant) and lactobionic/maltobionic in some functional-food contexts, but **there is no credible oral evidence for skin anti-aging.** Do not borrow any oral framing.

### 4d. Human — TOPICAL

**RCT-level (vehicle-/placebo-controlled, blinded):** **Essentially none specific to PHAs.** This is the central weakness — there is **no published double-blind, vehicle-controlled RCT** isolating a PHA/bionic acid for anti-aging. The strongest design is an *active-comparator* trial:

- **Edison, Green, Wildnauer, Sigler (2004), *Cutis* 73(3 Suppl):14–17** — 12-week, comparative, controlled trial: **gluconolactone (PHA) regimen vs glycolic-acid (AHA) regimen.** Both produced significant anti-aging benefits (clinical grading, silicone-replica texture, pinch-recoil resiliency). AHA edged PHA on sallowness (17.1% vs 12.4%) and recoil (13.5% vs 10.2%) at wk 12, **but the PHA regimen was significantly better tolerated** (less stinging/burning/sensitivity). *Limitations: active comparator (no vehicle/placebo arm), NeoStrata-affiliated, supplement issue, sample size not stated in abstract.* [5]

**Lower-tier (uncontrolled / "controlled-usage" / open-label / biophysical):**
- **Green, Edison, Sigler (2008), *Cosmetic Dermatology* 21(2):76–82** — **Lactobionic acid 8%**, 12-week controlled-usage. **n=31 women (29 white, 2 Asian), age 39–60, Fitzpatrick II–III.** Significant improvement in *all* clinically graded parameters; significant **reduction in pre-existing mild irritation**; significant ↑ in **skin firmness and thickness**. Arm-biopsy histology: **reduced MMP-9 activity** and **increased GAG staining**. Reported clarity ↑ (~100% of subjects), fine-line ↓ (~37%), firmness ↑ (~14.5%). *Limitations: no control/vehicle arm, single-arm usage study, small n, industry authors, trade journal.* [6]
- **Maltobionic acid 8% (pH 3.8), 12-week study, n=28 Caucasian women age 35–58** — improved fine lines/wrinkles, elasticity, firmness, texture, dryness, clarity; ~7.5% ↑ skin thickness; histology ↑ epidermal thickness + GAG, more compact SC. *Limitations: same as above — uncontrolled, industry/supplier-sourced.* [3][7]
- **Warowna, Strzelecka & Kręcisz (2025), *J Clin Med* 14(5):1619** — **40% lactobionic vs 40% mandelic acid peels** on hydration/elasticity in women aged 30–40 and 50–60. Independent (Polish academic) split-design; both acids + moisturizer improved hydration and elasticity; lactobionic favored on hydration in the older group. *Limitations: this is a series of **procedure-strength chemical peels**, not a leave-on cosmetic; biophysical endpoints only; active comparator (no vehicle arm); short.* [8] *(NB: an earlier draft mis-cited this as "Tolosa et al." — corrected 2026-06-19.)*
- **Jarząbek-Perz et al. (2023), *J Cosmet Dermatol* 22:...** — **10% vs 30% gluconolactone chemical peels**, biophysical study: changes in **sebum, pH, and TEWL**. Supports gluconolactone's barrier/hydration behavior at peel strengths. *Limitations: peel concentrations (procedure, not leave-on), biophysical only.* [9]
- **Foundational reviews / textbook synthesis** — Grimes, Green, Wildnauer, Edison (2004), *Cutis* 73(2 Suppl):3–13, "The use of polyhydroxy acids (PHAs) in photoaged skin"; and Green & Edison's chapter "PHAs and Bionic Acids: Next-Generation Hydroxy Acids" (in *Cosmetic Dermatology*, Wiley). These collate the gentleness, humectancy, barrier, and sensitive-skin-compatibility (rosacea, atopic dermatitis, post-procedure) story. *Limitations: narrative reviews authored by the IP holders.* [10][11]

**What is PROVEN for topical human use vs borrowed:**
- ✅ **Proven (human topical, even if lower-tier):** gentle exfoliation/smoothing; reduced fine-line and improved firmness/clarity over ~12 wks; **superior tolerability vs AHAs**; humectant hydration; reduces (rather than causes) irritation; histologic ↑ thickness / ↑ GAG / ↓ MMP-9 (small n).
- ⚠️ **Mechanistic / in-vitro only (not yet a human-topical claim):** anti-glycation/AGE reduction; antioxidant radical scavenging; UV-elastin-promoter "protection"; melanogenesis inhibition; senescence-marker reduction.
- ❌ **Borrowed / not applicable:** any oral benefit; any "sun protection/SPF" framing; systemic/organ-preservation antioxidant data.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **Larger molecules = shallower, slower penetration.** Gluconolactone MW ~178 and bionics ~358 vs glycolic 76. They concentrate in/near the stratum corneum, which is *why* they exfoliate gently and rarely sting — but also why deep dermal remodeling claims should be modest.
- **Free-acid availability is pH-driven** (pKa ~3.6–3.9): at formula pH ~3.5–4 a useful fraction is in the active free-acid form. Gluconolactone exists in lactone↔acid equilibrium in water.
- **Humectant film** on the surface contributes to measured hydration/TEWL benefits independent of any dermal action.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration (leave-on):** Gluconolactone commonly **~10%** (CIR max leave-on reported 15%); lactobionic and maltobionic studied at **8%**; combined PHA/bionic blends ~12%. Peels run higher (10–30% gluconolactone) but that's procedure territory, out of scope for premium daily leave-on.
- **pH:** Target **~3.5–4.0** for exfoliating efficacy; can be buffered higher for very sensitive/barrier-support positioning (trades exfoliation for humectancy).
- **Stability:** Robust and easy to formulate. Gluconolactone hydrolyzes to gluconic acid in water (expected, not a defect); both are stable. Bionic acids are stable, water-soluble.
- **Pairs well with:** Ceramides + HA (amplifies the barrier/hydration story), niacinamide, azelaic acid (studied together for rosacea), gentle/encapsulated retinoids and bakuchiol (PHA's gentleness offsets retinoid dryness), post-procedure soothing routines. Good AM **or** PM (see sun-sensitivity nuance below).
- **Conflicts:** Few. Less stacking risk than AHAs, but combining with strong glycolic/retinoid/high-% vitamin C in the same step still raises cumulative irritation — alternate for sensitive skin.
- **Sensory:** Light, hydrating, "gel-matrix" slip from lactobionic; minimal sting — a genuine premium sensory advantage and a credible "kind acid" story.

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation:** **Low** — the headline advantage. HRIPTs found no irritation/sensitization for gluconolactone across products at 0.04%, ~1.5%, and 15%; studies repeatedly show *less* stinging/burning than AHAs and even reduction of pre-existing mild irritation. Suitable for rosacea-prone, atopic, and post-procedure skin per the review literature.
- **Sensitization:** Not observed in available HRIPT data; gluconic acid family rated safe by CIR.
- **Sun sensitivity — important nuance:** Industry data argue PHAs do **not** increase UV sensitivity (no rise in sunburn cells; gluconolactone is antioxidant). **However, the US FDA explicitly declined to extend its AHA "Sunburn Alert" labeling to PHAs *only because it lacked data*, not because PHAs were shown safe** — a comment requested the extension but provided no definition or supporting data, and FDA stated it "does not have data" on PHAs. So "PHAs don't increase sun sensitivity" is **plausible and industry-supported but not regulator-affirmed.** Daily SPF messaging remains the right, safe default. [12]
- **Pregnancy:** No specific red flags; topical hydroxy acids with low systemic absorption are generally considered acceptable. Use standard boilerplate / defer to clinician.
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** Derived from sugars (glucose, lactose→galactose, maltose) — fermentation/oxidation chemistry supports a clean, naturally-derived narrative. Lactobionic is milk-sugar-derived (note for vegan positioning); maltobionic is plant-derived (vegan-friendly alternative).

## 8. Regulatory & claims

- **Cosmetic, not drug** — exfoliation, smoothing, hydration, "appearance of" claims sit comfortably in cosmetic territory.
- ✅ **Safe cosmetic language:** "gently exfoliates," "smooths and refines the look of texture," "hydrates and supports the moisture barrier," "improves the appearance of fine lines and dullness," "a gentle acid suitable for sensitive skin," "visibly even, clearer-looking skin."
- ⚠️ **Risky / drug-like or over-reach (flag for substantiation/legal):**
  - "boosts/builds collagen," "increases skin thickness by X%," "inhibits MMPs," "reverses glycation/AGEs," "anti-glycation" as a consumer benefit claim — all **structure/function or mechanism claims** resting on in-vitro or small uncontrolled data.
  - **Any sun-protection framing** ("up to 50% UV protection," "photoprotective") — the 50% figure is an in-vitro reporter result, NOT sun protection; using it in copy would be a serious claim violation.
  - "Senolytic" / "kills senescent cells" — from one in-vitro multi-ingredient formula; not substantiated.
- **Sunburn-alert label:** Not currently *required* by FDA for PHAs (unlike AHAs), but given the regulatory ambiguity, including SPF guidance is prudent and on-brand for claims discipline.

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Cost tier: low–moderate.** Gluconolactone is inexpensive and abundant. Lactobionic and maltobionic are pricier specialty actives (and partly NeoStrata-IP-associated) but still far from exotic. Low supply risk; multiple suppliers (e.g., Global Calcium "Malocal" for maltobionic; widely available gluconolactone).
- **Story value > cost:** the differentiation is the *gentle-acid* and *sugar-derived* narrative, not raw rarity.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

- **Marketing overreach to avoid:**
  - **"Anti-glycation / fights AGEs"** — heavily marketed; rests on **in-vitro** glycation inhibition only. No human-topical proof that it reduces skin AGEs or sallowness via that pathway.
  - **"Antioxidant as strong as vitamin C"** — from a single in-vitro radical assay; not a clinical antioxidant claim.
  - **"Photoprotective / 50% UV protection"** — in-vitro reporter model; **not** sun protection and dangerous to imply.
  - **"Builds collagen / increases skin thickness"** — small, uncontrolled, industry histology; real signal, weak proof.
  - **"No sun sensitivity, unlike AHAs"** — likely true but **not** regulator-confirmed; FDA simply has no data.
- **Where the hype is actually fair:** PHAs/bionics genuinely **are gentler than AHAs** while delivering comparable smoothing — that comparative-trial + tolerability story is the one well-earned headline.
- **Conflict-of-interest flag:** the human evidence base is dominated by one company's investigators in lower-tier journals. Independent vehicle-controlled RCTs are missing.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

✅ **SUPPORTING — and a strong candidate for a "conditional hero" in a gentle-exfoliant / sensitive-skin SKU.** The defensible, on-brand claim is **kind, hydrating renewal**: AHA-grade smoothing with barrier support and minimal irritation — ideal as the acid layer in a lean, premium routine and a natural companion to a retinoid hero and a barrier-repair cream.

**One-liner:** *Use PHAs/bionics as the gentle, hydrating exfoliant that lets a premium brand offer "acid for everyone" without the sting — lead with tolerability, never with the in-vitro anti-glycation/UV hype.*

**Longevity relevance:** Indirect but real — its best longevity-adjacent mechanisms (MMP inhibition to protect collagen, antioxidant/chelation, anti-glycation, barrier integrity) map onto ECM preservation, oxidative-stress, glycation, and barrier hallmarks. But these are **mostly in-vitro**; the human longevity story is "protect and maintain the barrier/ECM gently," not "reverse aging." Pair with proven anti-photoaging actives (retinoid, SPF) rather than positioning PHA as the engine.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Verified:** 2026-06-19 · **Method:** independent citation check (PubMed/journal/PMC/Federal Register) of every headline claim; primary-source text pulled and read for the two load-bearing human studies (Green 2008 lactobionic; Edison 2004 comparative). **Reviewer stance:** hostile — assume hype until proven.

### Overall evidence grade: **B− (leaning C for "anti-aging efficacy"; B for tolerability)**

There is no single grade that fits, because the two halves of the PHA story sit at different tiers:

- **Tolerability / gentleness vs AHAs → B (solid).** This is the one claim backed by a *controlled, active-comparator* human trial (Edison 2004: PHA regimen significantly better tolerated than a glycolic regimen) plus consistent HRIPT and low-irritation usage data. It is the only headline I'd let marketing lead with.
- **Anti-aging *efficacy* (fine lines, firmness, "thickness," clarity) → C (weak/indirect).** Real human signal, but every primary study is single-arm "controlled-usage" (no vehicle/placebo), small (n=28–31), NeoStrata-funded, NeoStrata-authored, and published in trade/supplement venues (*Cutis* supplement, *Cosmetic Dermatology*). There is **no double-blind, vehicle-controlled RCT isolating a PHA/bionic for anti-aging** — confirmed, not just asserted.
- **Mechanistic claims (anti-glycation, antioxidant "as strong as vitamin C," MMP inhibition, melanogenesis, senescence, "50% UV protection") → C/D.** All in-vitro or reporter-model only. None is a defensible human-topical claim.

So the dossier's own self-grade ("efficacy moderate-at-best, gentleness strong") is **fair and, if anything, slightly generous on efficacy.** Grade the *molecule's proven topical anti-aging power* at **C**; grade the *gentleness positioning* at **B**.

### What checked out (citations are real and say what's claimed)
Every one of the 13 sources is a **real publication** — no fabricated studies, no invented DOIs. Spot-verified in detail:
- **Edison 2004 (Cutis 73 Suppl):** real; 12-wk PHA-vs-AHA comparator, **no placebo arm**, NeoStrata-affiliated, supplement issue — exactly as the dossier states. ✓
- **Green 2008 lactobionic (Cosmet Dermatol 21:76–82):** full text read. **n=31 (29 white, 2 Asian), age 39–60, Fitzpatrick II–III; 14.5% firmness; no vehicle control (untreated contralateral forearm only); NeoStrata employees; NeoStrata-funded; MMP-9↓ + GAG↑ in an n=16 biopsy subset.** All numbers match the dossier. ✓ (Minor nuance: "skin thickness" is largely **caliper/"plumping"** measurement [6.9% vs baseline]; histology showed increased *epidermal* thickness — the dossier already separates these correctly.)
- **Bernstein 2004 (Dermatol Surg 30:189–195):** real; in-vitro/reporter photoaging model — correctly flagged as NOT sun protection. ✓ (Abstract paywalled; identity/journal/pages confirmed.)

### Main caveats (the honest read)
1. **Single-sponsor capture.** The entire *human* efficacy base is essentially one company's (NeoStrata) investigators — Green, Edison, Sigler, Wildnauer — who also hold the IP. That is a structural conflict, not a footnote. Treat efficacy magnitudes (37% fine lines, 14.5% firmness, "~100% clarity") as **sponsor-reported, uncontrolled** figures; never quote them as if independently established.
2. **No vehicle-controlled RCT exists.** Improvements vs *baseline* in a single arm cannot separate the active from the cream base, regression to the mean, or seasonal/behavioral effects. The only controlled comparison is *against another acid*, which can't prove efficacy over placebo.
3. **Procedure vs leave-on conflation risk.** Two of the human data points (Jarząbek-Perz 10–30% peels; Warowna 40% peels) are **in-clinic peel** studies, not daily leave-on cosmetics. Their hydration/barrier readouts support the *molecule's behavior* but should not be cited as evidence for a leave-on product's anti-aging effect.
4. **Maltobionic "8% clinical" data is not independently published.** It lives in supplier monographs (Global Calcium/Qvsiete), NeoStrata patents, and the Green/Edison book chapter — confirmed; there is no standalone peer-reviewed primary paper. Lowest-reliability tier in the dossier; keep it labeled as such.
5. **One citation was misattributed (now fixed).** Source [8] was credited to "Tolosa de Talamoni"; the actual authors are **Warowna, Strzelecka & Kręcisz**. The paper is real and the conclusion is unchanged, but the prior author name was wrong — corrected in §4d and §12. (The dossier had pre-flagged it "author list unverified," which is exactly why it should never have shipped to copy uncorrected.)

### Claims to soften / never make in marketing
*(Most are already flagged in §8/§10 — this consolidates and reinforces the hard "no" list.)*
- ❌ **"Boosts/builds collagen," "increases skin thickness by X%," "inhibits MMPs," "anti-glycation / fights AGEs," "antioxidant as strong as vitamin C," "reduces senescent cells."** Each is structure/function or mechanism language resting on in-vitro or small uncontrolled sponsor data. Keep to "supports," "helps protect the look of," "appearance of" framing only.
- ❌ **Any sun-protection / "50% UV protection" / "photoprotective" framing.** In-vitro reporter result; using it would be a serious claim violation.
- ❌ **"Clinically proven to reverse aging" / quoting the 37% / 100% / 14.5% figures as established efficacy.** They are sponsor-reported, uncontrolled. Soften to "in a company-sponsored usage study, users saw…" with the conflict disclosed — or omit.
- ⚠️ **"Increases skin thickness/plumps":** if used at all, tie to the caliper "plumping" appearance, not dermal remodeling.
- ✅ **Fair to keep:** "gentle exfoliation suitable for sensitive skin," "smooths and refines texture," "hydrates and supports the moisture barrier," and — the one genuinely earned comparative claim — **"the smoothing of an AHA with far less sting"** (Edison 2004 supports this; still avoid implying superiority on *efficacy*).

The correct posture is the one it already takes: **lead with tolerability (B-grade evidence), treat anti-aging efficacy as a C-grade "supporting" story, and quarantine every mechanistic/in-vitro claim out of consumer copy.**
