# Ingredient: Sea fennel (Crithmum maritimum) extract (INCI: Crithmum Maritimum Extract)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product
**Class:** Botanicals & plant bioactives (halophyte / coastal-plant extract; chlorogenic-acid-rich antioxidant)

> **One-line honest summary:** Sea fennel is a genuinely nutrient-dense, antioxidant-rich coastal halophyte whose **chlorogenic-acid + flavonoid** chemistry gives it a believable antioxidant / barrier-supportive / brightening story. But the evidence is overwhelmingly **in-vitro and chemical-assay** based, with exactly **one small (n≈12) human topical study** — and that study used a *proprietary plant-stem-cell biomass* (Codif/Sederma-type biotech active), not a generic sea-fennel extract. It is a credible **supporting / botanical-narrative** ingredient, **not** an evidence-backed hero, and the marketing ("repairs," "regenerates," "anti-aging") runs well ahead of the human data.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common names:** Sea fennel, rock samphire, samphire, St. Peter's herb, sea asparagus (loose usage), *kritamo / kritmo* (Greek), *finocchio marino* (Italian), *perce-pierre / criste marine* (French). Note: "samphire" is ambiguous — it can also mean *Salicornia* (glasswort/marsh samphire), a different plant. Sea fennel = "rock samphire."
- **Latin / botany:** *Crithmum maritimum* L., family **Apiaceae** (Umbelliferae) — the carrot/celery/parsley/fennel family. Monotypic genus (the only species in *Crithmum*). An edible perennial **halophyte** (salt-tolerant) colonizing sea cliffs, rocks, and coastal dunes around the Mediterranean, Black Sea, and Atlantic Europe.
- **INCI:** **Crithmum Maritimum Extract** (whole-plant extract). Related/derivative INCIs seen on labels:
  - **Hydrolyzed Crithmum Maritimum Extract** (common cosmetic form)
  - **Crithmum Maritimum Callus Culture / Cell Extract** (plant-cell-culture / "stem cell" biotech versions — e.g., *Celtosome™ Crithmum maritimum*)
  - **Crithmum Maritimum Leaf Oil / Flower/Leaf/Stem Oil** (essential oil)
  - **Crithmum Maritimum Seed Oil** (fixed/fatty oil)
- **CAS:** 89997-98-8 · **EC:** 289-735-8
- **CosIng functions (EU):** *skin conditioning, tonic.* [13]
- **Key forms/derivatives for cosmetics:**
  1. **Aqueous / hydro-ethanolic whole-plant or leaf extract** (the chlorogenic-acid + flavonoid antioxidant fraction — most of the chemistry literature).
  2. **Plant-cell-culture biomass ("dedifferentiated cells" / callus / "stem cells")** — the form behind the only human topical study and the in-vitro skin-equivalent work (Lequeux/Caucanas, Codif Recherche & Nature / Sederma lineage).
  3. **Essential oil** (limonene / γ-terpinene / sabinene / thymol methyl ether / dillapiole-dominant, origin-dependent) — antioxidant/antimicrobial/enzyme-inhibition assays; fragrance + preservative-booster interest.
  4. **Fixed seed oil** — fatty-acid (linoleic/linolenic/oleic) emollient.

## 2. Class & mechanism (how it plausibly works on skin)

Sea fennel is best framed as a **polyphenol-rich botanical antioxidant** with secondary antimicrobial and mild enzyme-inhibitory activity. Its proposed skin mechanisms are inferred mostly from its **chemistry** and from **cell/enzyme assays**, not from human skin pharmacology.

Mechanistic pillars (and the honest tier each rests on):

1. **Direct free-radical scavenging (antioxidant).** Driven primarily by **chlorogenic acid** and its isomers (neochlorogenic, cryptochlorogenic, isochlorogenic A) plus flavonoids (quercetin, rutin) and hydroxycinnamic acids. Demonstrated repeatedly in DPPH / ABTS / FRAP / ORAC assays and ex-vivo erythrocyte protection. *Tier: chemical assay / in-vitro.* [3][4][6][8]
2. **Anti-inflammatory signaling.** Aqueous extract ↓TNF-α and ↑IL-10 in THP-1 monocytes (Alemán 2022); essential oil ↓LPS-induced nitrite in RAW 264.7 macrophages (Alves-Silva 2020). Chlorogenic acid is the proposed driver. *Tier: in-vitro cell.* [1]
3. **Barrier / epidermal support.** The biotech "dedifferentiated cell" form accelerated epidermal regeneration and differentiation (filaggrin, Ki-67) in a 3-D skin-equivalent (Lequeux 2011) and, as a finished cosmetic, sped barrier (TEWL) recovery after tape-stripping in humans (Caucanas 2011). A separate full-component extract upregulated **collagen I, loricrin, epidermal thickness** in a UVA-damaged 3-D skin model (Huang 2025). *Tier: in-vitro 3-D + one small human study.* [2][9][10]
4. **Tyrosinase inhibition (pigmentation/tone).** Essential oil inhibited mushroom tyrosinase in vitro (Ismail 2024), suggesting a depigmenting/brightening rationale. *Tier: isolated-enzyme assay (mushroom tyrosinase ≠ human melanocyte).* [11]
5. **Antimicrobial / preservative-booster.** Extract and EO inhibit a range of bacteria including skin-relevant *S. aureus / S. epidermidis*; biofilm inhibition shown for *S. aureus*. *Tier: in-vitro.* [3][4][12]
6. **Nutrient density.** High vitamin C (a 100 g culinary serving covers an adult's RDA), carotenoids, minerals (K, Na, Ca, Mg), and unsaturated fatty acids (linoleic/linolenic) — relevant to "vitamin-rich botanical" narrative, but **dietary content ≠ delivered topical dose**. [14][15]

**Skin-relevant translation (honest):** the antioxidant and anti-inflammatory chemistry is real and reproducible *in the test tube/dish*; the barrier and regeneration story is genuinely interesting but leans on **one proprietary biotech form** and **3-D models**; the brightening story is a single isolated-enzyme essential-oil result. None of this establishes a clinical anti-wrinkle, anti-photoaging, or depigmenting effect for a generic sea-fennel extract in living human skin.

## 4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

> **The single most important honesty point:** Sea fennel has a *deep chemistry/in-vitro literature* (it's a popular halophyte research subject) but a *shallow human-skin literature*. There is **one** human topical clinical study, it has **n≈12**, **no published peer comparison beyond the same group**, and it tested a **proprietary plant-stem-cell biomass**, not the generic "Crithmum Maritimum Extract" on most labels. There are **no human RCTs**, **no animal skin-aging studies**, and **no human data at all** for wrinkles, pigmentation, or photoaging. Everything else is borrowed from cell/enzyme/3-D-model work or from the plant's nutritional chemistry.

### Tier 1 — In vitro (chemical assays, cells, 3-D skin models)

**Phytochemistry & antioxidant chemistry**
- **Generalić Mekinić et al., 2016, *J. Food Sci. Technol.* 53(7):3104–3112.** Profiled EO and extracts by plant part. EO dominated by **limonene 57.5–74.2%**, γ-terpinene 4.6–13.8%, sabinene 8.1–13.4%. Chlorogenic acid **0.7–8.1 mg/g** (highest in leaves/flowers); total phenolics 7.6–35.1 mg GAE/g. Extracts had higher antioxidant activity (FRAP/DPPH) than EOs; EOs had stronger AChE inhibition. *Limitation: composition/assay study; origin-dependent chemistry; no skin model.* [6]
- **Pedreiro et al., 2023, *Antioxidants (Basel)* 12(2):252.** Aqueous extracts rich in **chlorogenic acid (49.7 ± 0.8 mg/g infusion; 26.8 ± 0.9 mg/g decoction)**; **DPPH IC₅₀ 36.5–44.7 µg/mL; ABTS 37.3–38.4 µg/mL.** EO (γ-terpinene 37.2%, sabinene 21.2%) had antibacterial activity (MIC e.g. *S. aureus* 22.8 µL/mL); aqueous extracts had **no** antibacterial activity at tested levels. Framed as a natural **preservative** candidate. *Limitation: in-vitro; food/preservative framing, not skin efficacy.* [3]
- **Souid et al., 2021, *Molecules* 26(17):5380** (hydro-ethanolic leaf extract). Total polyphenols 31.7 mg GAE/g; flavonoids 25.6 mg CE/g; 18 LC-MS compounds (chlorogenic acids + flavonoids dominant). **DPPH IC₅₀ 0.22 mg/mL; ORAC 15,835 µmol TE/g; FRAP EC₅₀ 1.82 mg/mL.** Dose-dependent protection of human erythrocytes vs oxidative hemolysis (46–78%); biofilm inhibition of *S. aureus* (49–82% at 0.1–1 mg/mL); antimutagenic in yeast. *Limitation: in-vitro/ex-vivo; no skin model; "nutraceutical" framing.* [4]
- **Generalić Mekinić et al. / Croatia EO vs Tunisia EO comparisons (within review [8]).** Antioxidant capacity is **origin- and chemotype-dependent** — some EOs scored "low" (FRAP/DPPH). A real flag: "sea fennel extract" is not one standardized thing. [8]

**Anti-inflammatory (cells)**
- **Alemán et al., 2022** (cited in review [8]). Aqueous extract and liposomal extract in **THP-1** monocytes: ↓TNF-α, ↑IL-10 at 320–640 µg/mL (free) / 820–1640 µg/mL (liposomal). Chlorogenic acid proposed as key. *Limitation: monocyte cell line, high µg/mL doses, not skin cells.* [1][8]
- **Alves-Silva et al., 2020** (cited in review [8]). EO (γ-terpinene 33.6%, sabinene 32.0%, thymol methyl ether 15.7%) ↓LPS-induced nitrite in **RAW 264.7** macrophages at 3.125–50 µg/mL, no cytotoxicity. *Limitation: mouse macrophage line; EO, not extract.* [8]

**Pigmentation (enzyme assay)**
- **Ismail et al., 2024, *Scientific Reports* 14:24405 (s41598-024-74544-9).** First chemo-biological study of Libyan (Jebel Akhdar) sea-fennel EO. GC-MS: **thymyl methyl ether 56.86%, γ-terpinene 16.17%.** EO inhibited **mushroom tyrosinase (IC₅₀ ≈ 12.45 µg/mL)** and acetylcholinesterase; antioxidant activity reported. *Limitation: isolated mushroom tyrosinase (a screening proxy, not human melanocytes); single origin; EO not a typical cosmetic extract.* [11]

**Epidermal regeneration / "anti-aging" (3-D models)**
- **Lequeux, Lhoste, Rovere, Montastier, Damour, 2011, *Skin Pharmacol. Physiol.* 24(2):75–80.** **Dedifferentiated *Crithmum maritimum* cells (dCMC)** ("totipotent vegetal stem cells") added throughout construction of a 3-D **skin-equivalent**. Result: full epidermal regeneration/differentiation in **35 vs 42 days** (control); basal/suprabasal layers ×4.88 ± 0.41 (p<0.001); ↑Ki-67; only treated samples expressed **filaggrin**. *Limitation: engineered-tissue model, not human; proprietary cell biomass (this is the lab basis for the commercial active).* [9]
- **Huang et al., 2025, *RSC Advances* 15(3):1713–1720.** "Full-component extract" (water- + oil-soluble; ethanol 50% / GTCC 47.5% / hexanediol 2.5%; chlorogenic acid + isochlorogenic acid A confirmed by HPLC) at **30%** on a **3-D full-thickness human skin model** after **UVA** damage. Vs UVA control: **epidermal thickness +27.33%, collagen fibers +416.67%, collagen I +137.5%, loricrin +125%.** *Limitation: 3-D model, n=3 replicates, no donor count, no human/in-vivo confirmation; very large % changes off a damaged-control baseline should be read cautiously.* [10]

**Safety (cells)**
- **Correia et al., 2024** (cited in review [8]). Plant extract 1–5% showed **low toxicity** in Caco-2 intestinal epithelium. *Limitation: gut model, not skin.* [8]

### Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

- **No published animal model of topical sea-fennel for skin aging, photoaging, barrier, or pigmentation was found.** This is a genuine gap — most ingredients with a real anti-aging case have at least murine photoaging or wound data; sea fennel essentially jumps from cell/3-D models to one small human study. [8]

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL / systemic

- **Simos et al., 2023** (cited in review [8]). A multi-ingredient supplement *containing* sea-fennel essential oil (8.33 µL per formulation) plus other actives, **12 healthy volunteers, 12 weeks**, reported **no toxic effects.** *Limitation: combination product (cannot attribute anything to sea fennel), tiny n, tolerability not efficacy, oral not topical.* [8]
- Culinary/nutritional literature treats sea fennel as a vitamin-C and mineral source (anti-scurvy use by sailors historically). *Relevant only as "nutrient-dense plant" narrative, not as evidence of topical benefit.* [14][15]

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters — and it is essentially a single study)

- **Caucanas M., Montastier C., Piérard G.E., Quatresooz P., 2011. "Dynamics of skin barrier repair following preconditioning by a biotechnology-driven extract from samphire (*Crithmum maritimum*) stem cells." *Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology* 10(4):288–293.** Design: **n = 12** healthy subjects; controlled **tape-stripping** of stratum corneum to raise TEWL just above 15 g/m²/h; **14 days** of daily application of formulations enriched with samphire **stem-cell biomass** (tested as 0.1% serum, 0.05% cream, serum+cream, 0.5% silicone oil). Result: **faster (~96 h) recovery to lower TEWL** at preconditioned sites vs control, plus higher stratum-corneum moisture; **no adverse reactions.**
  - **Critical caveats:** (a) **n = 12**, single-center, no large control arm, short duration; (b) the active is a **proprietary biotech "stem-cell" biomass**, *not* the generic chlorogenic-acid extract on most labels — results do **not** transfer to a commodity "Crithmum Maritimum Extract"; (c) endpoint is **barrier-recovery kinetics after acute insult**, not chronic aging, wrinkles, firmness, or pigmentation; (d) the only investigators are the same group/supplier lineage that developed the active (Lequeux 2011 is the matching in-vitro paper); (e) the panel was **subjects >50 y** (verified against the journal abstract) — i.e. the design tests *whether the slowed barrier-repair kinetics of older skin can be accelerated*, which is an aging-relevant framing but still measures **acute-insult recovery**, not chronic wrinkle/firmness/photoaging outcomes. [2]

**Bottom line by tier:** Proven *in-vitro/chemically* → strong antioxidant capacity, anti-inflammatory cell signaling, antimicrobial activity, tyrosinase inhibition, 3-D epidermal regeneration. Shown *once in humans (n≈12)* → faster barrier/TEWL recovery, but for a **proprietary stem-cell form** and only as acute-insult recovery. **Not shown in humans** → wrinkles, firmness, photoaging, hyperpigmentation, or any benefit for a **generic** sea-fennel extract. **No animal skin data.** Any clinical anti-aging claim today is borrowed from chemistry, 3-D models, or one small barrier study of a different (proprietary) ingredient form.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **The "active" is a mixture, not one molecule.** A whole-plant extract delivers chlorogenic acid + isomers, flavonoids, hydroxycinnamic acids, minerals, vitamin C, and (depending on prep) volatile terpenes — each with different penetration behavior. There are **no published human transdermal/penetration data** quantifying how much of any sea-fennel constituent reaches viable epidermis/dermis.
- **Chlorogenic acid** (the headline phenolic) is moderately polar (MW ~354, multiple OH/COOH groups) and penetrates the lipophilic stratum corneum **modestly** without enhancement — consistent with antioxidant/surface and upper-epidermal effects rather than deep dermal remodeling.
- **The biotech "stem-cell biomass"** is a complex particulate/biomass, not a defined permeant; the Caucanas barrier effect may owe to surface conditioning/occlusion and upper-SC interaction as much as true penetration.
- **Essential-oil constituents** (limonene, terpinenes) penetrate readily (lipophilic, low MW) but carry irritation/sensitization and oxidation concerns (see §7).
- **Practical read:** plausible **surface + upper-epidermal antioxidant/soothing/barrier** action; **unproven** deep-dermal (collagen-remodeling) action in vivo. Concentration/standardization (chlorogenic-acid content) varies enormously by origin and prep — penetration claims for "sea fennel" as a category are not meaningful.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:** **No established human-validated efficacy concentration.** Reference points: documented finished products use it low — e.g. **0.2%** (Sanshi The Repair Emulsion) and **0.05–0.1%** in the Caucanas barrier study (proprietary biomass). [2][13] Paula's Choice explicitly notes **"usage levels for sea fennel in cosmetics haven't been firmly established."** [5] Treat any % as supplier/marketing-driven, not dose-response-validated. Formulate to a **COA-quantified chlorogenic-acid spec**, not just "% extract."
- **pH:** Water/hydro-ethanolic extracts are mildly acidic and skin-compatible; chlorogenic acid is most stable at mildly acidic pH (~3.5–6). Target a barrier-friendly **pH ~5–5.5**. Strongly alkaline systems risk phenolic oxidation/browning.
- **Stability:** Polyphenols/chlorogenic acid are **oxidation-prone** (can darken/brown, especially with metal ions, heat, light, high pH). Use chelators (e.g. EDTA/phytate), antioxidants, opaque/airless packaging; run color/odor stability. Essential-oil forms oxidize (limonene/terpenes → sensitizing peroxides) — antioxidant + airless packaging essential. Plant-cell-culture/biomass forms have their own preservation/microbiology considerations.
- **Pairings (conceptual):** Niacinamide and ceramides (barrier thesis), other antioxidants (vitamin C/E, ferulic — chlorogenic acid is a hydroxycinnamic cousin of ferulic), humectants (glycerin/HA) for the hydration/barrier narrative, soothing actives (panthenol, centella). Marine/coastal botanical story pairs naturally with a barrier-repair positioning.
- **Conflicts:** Avoid high-pH or strongly oxidizing systems; be cautious co-formulating EO-rich grades with other fragrant/irritant actives (cumulative sensitization). Metal ions accelerate phenolic browning.
- **Sensory:** Hydrolyzed/aqueous extracts: typically light, low-odor, can carry slight green/marine note and pale-to-amber color (watch for darkening). EO grades: aromatic, herbaceous/citrus (limonene), can dominate scent and add irritation risk.

## 7. Safety

- **Family/allergen flag (the main one):** Apiaceae (carrot/celery/parsley/fennel family) plants contain **falcarinol-type C17 polyacetylenes (falcarinol, falcarindiol)** that are **documented contact sensitizers/irritants** — they act as reactive haptens forming protein adducts and cause occupational allergic contact dermatitis in this plant family. *Crithmum maritimum* belongs to this family, and falcarinol/falcarindiol have been reported among its constituents. Cross-reactivity is possible in people sensitized to Apiaceae plants. **Patch testing and HRIPT on the finished formula are warranted**, and Apiaceae/celery-allergy disclosure is prudent. [16]
- **Phototoxicity / furanocoumarins:** Several Apiaceae (e.g. *Apium*, *Pastinaca*) contain phototoxic **furanocoumarins (psoralens)**. Evidence that *Crithmum maritimum* itself is significantly furanocoumarin-rich is **not established** in the sources reviewed [unverified], but given the family it is a screen-worthy concern — request a furanocoumarin/psoralen statement on the COA, especially for EO grades. No human topical photosensitivity data for sea fennel were found.
- **Irritation/sensitization (direct data):** The one human study (n=12, biomass) reported **no adverse reactions** [2]; EO showed no cytotoxicity in macrophages [8]; extract low-toxicity in Caco-2 [8]. These are reassuring but small/indirect. **Essential-oil grades** carry the usual terpene (limonene/linalool-type) oxidation/sensitization risk and EU fragrance-allergen labeling implications.
- **Sun:** No sunscreen activity; do not imply photoprotection. (Antioxidant ≠ SPF.)
- **Pregnancy/breastfeeding:** No specific topical safety data. As a precaution, *fennel/Apiaceae* essential oils are often flagged for caution in pregnancy; advise conservative copy ("if pregnant or breastfeeding, consult your doctor"). [unverified specific to *Crithmum*]
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** Halophyte that grows abundantly on coasts and is increasingly **cultivated** (including saline agriculture) — generally a **sustainability-positive** story (Paula's Choice notes abundance) [5]; but wild-harvest pressure on cliff populations and origin-dependent chemistry argue for cultivated, traceable, standardized supply.
- **EWG/registries:** Listed in EWG Skin Deep and CosIng as a skin-conditioning agent; no major hazard flags in those databases. [13]

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Cosmetic ingredient status:** Established cosmetic INCI (**Crithmum Maritimum Extract**, CAS 89997-98-8, EC 289-735-8); CosIng functions **skin conditioning / tonic**; widely used (40+ products: Filorga, Jurlique, Helena Rubinstein, etc.). Squarely a **cosmetic** ingredient. [13]
- **The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame):** "Regenerates skin," "repairs," "rebuilds the barrier," "anti-aging," "boosts collagen," "stem cells renew your skin" all push toward **structure/function (drug) claims**. The biggest trap here is importing **3-D-model collagen %s** (Huang +137.5% collagen I) and **"stem cell" / "regeneration"** language into consumer claims — none of it is substantiated for the finished product in humans.
- ✅ **Safer cosmetic language:** "antioxidant-rich coastal botanical," "rich in chlorogenic acid and polyphenols," "helps defend against environmental/oxidative stress," "supports the look of a healthy moisture barrier," "helps skin feel comforted and hydrated," "visibly even-looking tone." (Even barrier/tone wording should be hedged given the thin human data.)
- ⚠️ **Risky / needs substantiation or legal review:** "repairs the skin barrier," "regenerates skin," "plant stem cells renew your cells," "reduces wrinkles/fine lines," "fades hyperpigmentation/dark spots," "boosts collagen by X%," "anti-aging." None are supported for a generic sea-fennel extract in humans.
- **Substantiation reality:** For any meaningful performance claim we'd need **our own finished-formula human testing**, and ideally a **standardized, characterized grade** (or the proprietary biotech active with its own dossier). The literature substantiates *antioxidant capacity in vitro* and *one small barrier-recovery study of a proprietary form* — not a consumer anti-aging claim. See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Forms/suppliers:** Commodity hydrolyzed/aqueous extracts from botanical-extract houses (e.g. MakingCosmetics "Sea Fennel Extract," Native Extracts cellular extract, Uniproma "BotaniCellar Crithmum maritimum"); **branded biotech actives** from plant-cell-culture specialists (e.g. *Celtosome™ Crithmum maritimum* / Codif-Sederma lineage tied to the Lequeux/Caucanas research). EO and seed-oil grades from essential-oil suppliers.
- **Cost tier:** **Low–mid** for commodity aqueous/hydrolyzed extracts; **mid–high** for branded plant-cell-culture "stem cell" actives (which is where the real human/3-D data live, and where the premium sits).
- **Standardization risk (important):** Chlorogenic-acid and EO chemistry vary dramatically by **origin, plant part, season, and prep** (e.g. chlorogenic acid 0.7–8.1 mg/g across studies; EO can be limonene-, γ-terpinene-, thymol-methyl-ether-, or dillapiole-dominant). **Require a COA with quantified chlorogenic acid (and EO chemotype if relevant);** "Crithmum Maritimum Extract" alone is not a meaningful spec.
- **Supply/sustainability:** Cultivated halophyte supply (saline agriculture) is expanding; prefer cultivated, traceable sources over cliff wild-harvest.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

- **"Plant stem cells regenerate your skin."** The "stem cell" data are a **3-D skin-equivalent model** (faster epidermal build, 35 vs 42 days) [9] and one **n=12 barrier-recovery** study [2] — not skin-cell rejuvenation in living human skin. "Plant stem cell" is mostly a marketing frame; the cells aren't alive in the product and don't become your cells.
- **"+137% collagen / +417% collagen fibers."** Those are **3-D-model** numbers measured against a **UVA-damaged control** baseline (Huang 2025, n=3 replicates) [10] — dramatic-sounding, lab-only, and not a human collagen claim.
- **"Brightens / fades dark spots."** Rests on **one mushroom-tyrosinase essential-oil assay** [11]. Mushroom tyrosinase is a screening proxy, not human melanocytes; no human pigmentation data exist.
- **"Repairs the barrier / anti-aging."** The barrier evidence is a single small study of a **proprietary biomass** measuring **recovery after tape-stripping**, not chronic aging [2]; it doesn't validate the commodity extract or an "anti-aging" claim.
- **"Vitamin-packed superfood for skin."** True that the *plant* is nutrient-dense (vitamin C, minerals) [14][15] — but a fractional-percent topical extract is **not** delivering a meaningful vitamin dose; this is narrative, not pharmacology.
- **Category equivocation.** "Sea fennel extract" spans aqueous extract, EO, seed oil, and biotech biomass — each with different chemistry and evidence. Marketing blends all four into one halo.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ **SUPPORTING / CONDITIONAL — a credible, on-narrative botanical antioxidant, not an evidence-backed hero.**
**One-line:** Sea fennel is a real, reproducible **in-vitro antioxidant** with a charming coastal-halophyte story and *one* small human barrier study, but its anti-aging case is **chemistry- and model-deep, human-shallow** — fit it as a **supporting antioxidant / botanical-identity** ingredient with strictly hedged copy, and only treat it as a near-hero if we license the **standardized biotech (plant-cell-culture) active** and run our **own** finished-formula human testing.

**Conditions to upgrade from "supporting" toward "feature":**
1. Use a **characterized, COA-standardized** grade (quantified chlorogenic acid) or the branded plant-cell-culture active with its own clinical dossier.
2. Generate **our own human barrier/hydration data** on the finished formula (the literature won't substantiate consumer claims).
3. Keep claims to **antioxidant / environmental-defense / barrier-comfort / tone-evening (appearance)** — never "regenerates," "repairs," "stem cells," or numeric collagen claims.

**Longevity-relevance note:** For a skin-*longevity* brand, sea fennel's value is **narrative + antioxidant + sustainability**, not a deep geroscience mechanism. Unlike spermidine (autophagy/lifespan pedigree) or niacinamide/NAD (well-evidenced), sea fennel has **no senescence, mitochondrial, or longevity-pathway data** — its hallmark coverage is essentially "oxidative stress + barrier + inflammaging," all at modest evidence tiers. It earns a place as a **distinctive coastal-botanical antioxidant accent** that supports the barrier-repair thesis and a clean sustainability story — provided the copy stays honest and the grade is standardized.

## 13. Evidence verification (adversarial)

*Performed 2026-06-19. Each headline claim was checked against primary sources for: (a) HUMAN + TOPICAL vs borrowed in-vitro/animal/oral evidence; (b) whether cited studies are real and say what's claimed; (c) industry-funding / sample-size red flags. Sources fetched/searched directly: Caucanas 2011 (record), Lequeux 2011 (PubMed), Huang 2025 (RSC), Ismail 2024 (Sci Rep / review), Souid 2021 (PMC), Pedreiro 2023 (PMC), review PMC12251399, falcarindiol/Apiaceae literature.*

### Overall grade: **C** (weak / indirect — credible chemistry, essentially one small human-topical study of a *proprietary* form; unproven as an anti-aging hero for a generic extract)

This dossier is unusually self-honest: it already grades itself "chemistry-deep, human-shallow," tiers its evidence, and pre-empts most overreach. Verification **upholds** that framing. Sea fennel is **not** D-tier hype (the in-vitro/chemistry base is real and reproducible, and there is a genuine — if tiny and proprietary — human barrier study), but it is **not** B or A: there is **no human-topical RCT**, **no animal skin data**, and **no human data for wrinkles, firmness, photoaging, or pigmentation**. C is the defensible ceiling for a *generic* "Crithmum Maritimum Extract."

### Funding / independence / size red flags

- **Single-group, single-supplier human + 3-D evidence.** The *only* human-topical study (Caucanas 2011) and its matching in-vitro paper (Lequeux 2011) share the **same investigators/supplier lineage** (Montastier; Codif/Sederma-type biotech active). This is effectively **vendor-adjacent evidence with no independent replication** — a material conflict the dossier flags correctly.
- **n=12, single-center, no large control arm, short (14-day) duration**, acute insult endpoint. Tiny and underpowered for any chronic claim.
- **No independent or RCT confirmation; no animal skin data** — verified gaps.
- **Category equivocation is the biggest real risk:** "sea fennel extract" conflates aqueous extract, essential oil, seed oil, and proprietary biotech biomass — each with different chemistry, evidence, and even *opposite* antibacterial results. The human/3-D data live almost entirely with the proprietary biomass, **not** the commodity extract most labels carry.

### Claims to soften / never make (for a generic sea-fennel extract)

- ❌ "Repairs / rebuilds / regenerates the skin barrier," "anti-aging," "boosts collagen (by X%)," "plant stem cells renew your skin," "fades dark spots / brightens," "reduces wrinkles." None are substantiated in humans for a generic extract; the barrier datum is proprietary, acute-insult, n=12.
- ⚠️ Even "supports a healthy moisture barrier" / "even-looking tone" should stay **appearance-hedged** and ideally rest on the brand's **own finished-formula testing**, not the literature.
- ✅ Defensible today: "antioxidant-rich coastal botanical," "rich in chlorogenic acid & polyphenols," "helps defend against environmental/oxidative stress (in-vitro antioxidant)," "feels comforting/hydrating." Treat sea fennel as a **supporting antioxidant / botanical-identity** ingredient, not a hero.
