# Ingredient: Ebselen (INCI: none assigned — not a recognized cosmetic ingredient)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ⬜ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ✅ rejected (for now) / ⬜ in a product
**Class:** Antioxidants (vitamin C/E, ferulic, CoQ10, idebenone, glutathione, etc.) — specifically a **synthetic glutathione-peroxidase (GPx) mimic / organoselenium catalytic antioxidant**

> **Bottom line up front:** Ebselen is a genuinely interesting, mechanistically elegant antioxidant — a catalytic selenium-based mimic of the body's own glutathione-peroxidase enzyme, with a real human safety record from *oral* drug trials. **But it is an investigational drug, not a cosmetic ingredient.** It has **no INCI name, no cosmetic regulatory standing, and essentially zero human topical anti-aging data.** Its only meaningful topical-skin evidence is a single 2012 lab/animal study on *depigmentation*, not wrinkles/firmness. For a credibility-first brand this is a **skip** today (see Verdict). It is logged here so the brand has a defensible, citable reason on file.

## 2) Class & mechanism

Ebselen is the textbook example of a **synthetic GPx mimic**. Natural glutathione peroxidase is a selenium-containing enzyme that detoxifies hydrogen peroxide and lipid hydroperoxides using glutathione (GSH) as the reducing fuel. Ebselen reproduces this catalytic cycle with a small molecule:

- **GPx-mimic / hydroperoxide reduction.** Ebselen reacts with thiols (e.g., GSH) to form a selenenyl sulfide, then catalytically reduces H₂O₂ and organic/lipid/phospholipid hydroperoxides back to water/alcohols — i.e., it neutralizes the reactive species that drive lipid peroxidation. [3][4][6]
- **Thioredoxin reductase (TrxR) substrate / peroxiredoxin-like activity.** In mammalian cells ebselen is a very efficient substrate for the thioredoxin system; TrxR reduces ebselen (Km ≈ 2.5 µM), and via Trx/TrxR it scavenges H₂O₂ and peroxynitrite — acting partly as a peroxiredoxin mimic, not only a GPx mimic. [5][7][8]
- **Peroxynitrite scavenging.** Ebselen reacts rapidly with peroxynitrite (ONOO⁻), a potent nitrosative oxidant implicated in UV/inflammatory tissue damage. [5][6]
- **Anti-inflammatory / enzyme modulation.** Reported to inhibit several pro-inflammatory and oxidant-generating enzymes (lipoxygenases, NADPH oxidase, NO synthase, certain protein kinase C activity) and to modulate NF-κB signaling. [3][6]
- **Catalytic, not stoichiometric.** Unlike vitamin C/E (consumed as they quench radicals), ebselen is *regenerated* by cellular thiols — in principle it keeps working as long as GSH/Trx are available. This is the genuinely attractive "longevity" angle (see §11).

Net: ebselen is a real, well-characterized redox catalyst. The mechanism is not hype. The question is whether any of it has been *shown* to do anything useful on intact human skin (it largely hasn't — see §4).

## 4) Evidence review by tier

Tiers run weakest→strongest *for our purpose* (topical human use). The headline: ebselen has **strong mechanistic and oral-human evidence**, but **no human topical RCT for anything**, and **no human topical anti-aging evidence at all**. Its best topical signal is depigmentation, at the preclinical tier.

### Tier 1 — In vitro / biochemical (abundant, strong, but mechanism-only)
- **Müller, Cadenas, Graf, Sies (1984)** — original description of ebselen (PZ 51) as a GPx-mimicking, membrane-protective antioxidant. Foundational. [6]
- **Maiorino, Roveri, Coassin, Ursini (1988)** and related — ebselen as a GPx mimic and scavenger of peroxynitrite; reduces phospholipid/cholesteryl-ester hydroperoxides. [4]
- **Zhao, Masayasu, Holmgren (2002, PNAS)** — ebselen is a fast substrate for human thioredoxin reductase, strongly stimulating hydroperoxide-reductase activity; clarifies the Trx/peroxiredoxin-mimic mechanism. [8]
- **Briviba, Kissner, Koppenol, Sies (1998)** — kinetics of ebselen + peroxynitrite (peroxynitrite reductase function via TrxR). [5][7]
- Numerous mechanistic/computational papers modeling the catalytic cycle. [3]
- **Limitation:** all of this is biochemistry/cell-free or cultured cells. None of it is skin-aging outcome data, and in vitro antioxidant performance is a notoriously poor predictor of cosmetic benefit.

### Tier 2 — Skin-cell / reconstructed-skin / animal (sparse; one real study)
- **Kasraee B, Nikolic DS, Salomon D, Carraux P, Fontao L, Piguet V, Omrani GR, Sorg O, Saurat JH. "Ebselen is a new skin depigmenting agent that inhibits melanin biosynthesis and melanosomal transfer." *Experimental Dermatology* 2012;21(1):19–24. DOI: 10.1111/j.1600-0625.2011.01394.x.** [2]
  - **Models:** murine B16 melanocytes; B16/BDVII keratinocyte co-cultures; reconstructed human epidermis; black guinea-pig ear (in vivo animal).
  - **Findings:** 25 µM ebselen inhibited melanogenesis and depolymerized actin filaments in B16 cells; melanocyte pretreatment strongly inhibited melanosome transfer to keratinocytes; **topical 0.5% ebselen** produced a ~**2-fold decrease in melanin** in reconstructed epidermis and reduced epidermal melanin in guinea-pig ear, **without reducing active melanocyte density**.
  - **Why it matters:** this is the single best-quality piece of topical-skin evidence for ebselen, and it points at **tone/pigmentation**, not wrinkles/firmness.
  - **Limitations:** preclinical only; small/animal; no controlled human efficacy or safety; no published replication in a human melasma/hyperpigmentation RCT (searches found none as of 2026-06). Depigmentation can be a *liability* (uneven lightening, post-treatment rebound, regulatory drug-claim risk) as much as a benefit.
- **No ebselen-specific UVB/photoaging, MMP/collagen, barrier, or senescence skin studies were found** despite targeted searches — the broad "antioxidant protects skin from UV" literature is about other molecules (vitamin C/E, ferulic, fisetin, luteolin, etc.), not ebselen.

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL (good safety data, wrong delivery route for us)
Ebselen's human evidence is almost entirely **oral systemic drug** work, which does **not** transfer to topical cosmetic claims but is reassuring on intrinsic toxicity:
- **Kil J, et al. "Safety and efficacy of ebselen for the prevention of noise-induced hearing loss: a randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled, phase 2 trial." *Lancet* 2017;390(10098):969–979.** [10][11]
  - n = 83 healthy adults; ebselen 200/400/600 mg vs placebo, twice daily, oral, 4 days around a calibrated noise challenge.
  - 400 mg dose: ~68% reduction in temporary threshold shift at 4 kHz (1.32 vs 4.07 dB; p=0.0025). 200/600 mg not significant.
  - **Safety: well tolerated across all doses; no significant differences in hematology, serum chemistry, or radiology vs placebo; no serious adverse events.**
- **Lynch & Kil (2009)** and the SPI-1005 development program (Sound Pharmaceuticals) — phase 2 work in Ménière's disease, tinnitus, and noise/aminoglycoside ototoxicity; consistently described as well tolerated. [9]
- **Japanese acute-stroke program (1990s–2000s)** — oral ebselen trialed as a neuroprotectant (reported up to phase 3); contributed to the human safety/pharmacokinetic base. (Sourced via review [3]; primary trial reports not individually verified here.)
- Also investigated orally for bipolar disorder and as a SARS-CoV-2 Mpro inhibitor (COVID-19) — again oral, again outside skin. [9]
- **Limitation for us:** every one of these is *oral, short-term, in patients/volunteers for non-skin indications.* It tells us ebselen is not acutely toxic when swallowed for days; it tells us **nothing** about topical efficacy, long-term topical safety, or cosmetic claims.

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (this is the gap)
- **No randomized controlled trials.** None found.
- **No lower-tier human topical studies** (no published human split-face, no human hyperpigmentation cohort) confirmed as of 2026-06-19.
- The 0.5% topical data is **reconstructed-epidermis + guinea-pig only** (Tier 2). [2]

**Verdict on evidence:** For *topical human anti-aging*, ebselen is **unproven** — borrowed entirely from biochemistry and oral drug trials. Its only topical-relevant outcome (depigmentation) is preclinical. This is the opposite of the evidence profile a credibility-first brand wants in a hero ingredient.

## 5) Topical bioavailability

- Ebselen is **lipophilic and very poorly water-soluble** [9][12], so in principle it can partition into the stratum corneum — but no peer-reviewed human percutaneous-absorption study of *ebselen* was found.
- Comparable selenium chemistry is route-dependent: seleno-L-methionine penetrates skin and reaches circulation, while inorganic selenite does **not** (Zheng et al., percutaneous absorption of selenium species). [13] We cannot assume ebselen behaves like either — its absorption profile is uncharacterized for skin.
- Its catalytic antioxidant action **depends on intracellular thiols (GSH) and the Trx/TrxR system** [8] to regenerate it; whether enough active compound reaches living epidermis/dermis at a usable, regenerating concentration is unknown.
- **Net:** topical bioavailability and the delivered, *catalytically active* dose in human skin are unestablished. This is a formulation unknown, not a known quantity.

---

## 6) Formulation notes

- **Concentration:** the only topical reference point is **0.5% in a preclinical model** [2]. No validated human cosmetic use level exists.
- **Solubility/vehicle:** lipophilic; would need an oil/solvent phase or solubilizer; not a simple water-phase add.
- **pH/stability:** the selenazolone ring and Se–N chemistry are reactive (that's the point) — ebselen's reactivity with thiols and oxidants means **stability in a finished emulsion is a real question**, and it would likely interact with other thiol-containing or strongly reducing/oxidizing actives. No published cosmetic stability data.
- **Pairings (theoretical only):** as a catalytic antioxidant it would conceptually complement chain-breaking antioxidants (vitamin E) — but this is untested for ebselen and not a reason to use an investigational drug.
- **Conflicts:** likely incompatible with high-thiol systems (could be consumed) and with actives it can oxidize/reduce; its antibacterial action could disturb preservative systems or skin flora.
- **Sensory:** pale-yellow solid; color/odor and potential staining would need assessment.
- **Bottom line:** formulating ebselen is an R&D project with no precedent, not a plug-in active.

---

## 7) Safety

- **Intrinsic toxicity (oral, human):** reassuring within short oral trials — well tolerated at up to 600 mg twice daily for days, no serious adverse events, no clin-chem/hematology signals. [10][11] A frequently cited advantage is that **the selenium is not released into the body's selenium metabolism** during biotransformation, lowering selenium-toxicity risk versus inorganic selenium. [9]
- **Topical safety: essentially unknown.** No human dermal irritation, sensitization, photo-irritation, or cumulative-irritation data for ebselen on skin were found. Selenium compounds as a class warrant caution; absence of data ≠ evidence of safety.
- **Sensitization:** not characterized for ebselen specifically. Cannot be assumed low.
- **Sun/photostability:** no photo-irritation/photosensitization data on skin. (It is being *given* to reduce oxidative damage, but its own behavior under UV in a topical film is untested.)
- **Pregnancy/lactation:** no topical safety data; as an investigational drug substance, default to **avoid** in copy and product targeting pregnancy.
- **Pigment effect cuts both ways:** a compound that measurably *reduces melanin* [2] is not "inert" on tone — uneven lightening and unpredictable results are plausible risks if used off-label in a cosmetic.
- **Sourcing/quality:** sold as a **research chemical / drug API** (suppliers: MedKoo, Glentham, BOC Sciences, Focus Biomolecules, etc.), typically ~98% purity, with **no cosmetic-grade specification or cosmetic safety dossier**. [1][12]

## 8) Regulatory & claims (cosmetic vs drug)

This is the decisive section per `CLAUDE.md`'s claims-discipline guardrail.

- **Not a recognized cosmetic ingredient.** No INCI name; not an EU CosIng-approved cosmetic ingredient; not a US OTC monograph or GRAS skincare ingredient. [1][12] Using it would likely be treated by regulators as **introducing an unapproved (drug) substance** into a cosmetic.
- **Drug-development pedigree raises the bar.** Ebselen is an **investigational new drug** (SPI-1005) studied for disease indications. Marketing a product containing it could invite classification as an **unapproved drug**, especially alongside any functional claim.
- **Pigmentation = drug-territory claim risk.** Its one real skin effect is depigmentation; in the US, skin-lightening/"treats hyperpigmentation/melasma" language pushes toward drug claims and dedicated regulatory scrutiny. [2]
- **What we could honestly say if (hypothetically) used:** almost nothing safe. "Antioxidant" is generic; any specific anti-aging/brightening claim would be **unsubstantiated for topical human use** and the ingredient itself is non-compliant.
- **Recommendation:** treat ebselen as **not usable** in a compliant cosmetic without (a) regulatory reclassification/approval and (b) human topical safety + efficacy data. Flag for legal/regulatory if ever reconsidered.

## 9) Sourcing & cost

- Available only from **research-chemical / API suppliers** (MedKoo, Glentham Life Sciences, BOC Sciences, Focus Biomolecules, Creative Enzymes, TargetMol), generally ~98% purity, milligram-to-gram research quantities. [1][12]
- **No cosmetic-grade supply chain, no cosmetic CoA/safety dossier, no documented bulk cosmetic pricing.** Research-grade selenoorganics are expensive per gram relative to commodity cosmetic antioxidants, and a cosmetic-qualified supply would have to be built from scratch.
- Supply, batch consistency, and cosmetic-grade qualification are all unsolved — another reason it is not deployable now.

## 10) Hype vs evidence

- **The legitimate part:** the *mechanism* is real and well-published — ebselen is a bona fide catalytic GPx/peroxiredoxin mimic and peroxynitrite scavenger, regenerated by cellular thiols, with a clean short-term oral human safety record. [3][6][8][10] If marketed as "the antioxidant that acts like your own enzyme," that story is mechanistically defensible.
- **The hype trap:** that story is **borrowed from biochemistry and oral drug trials and has not been demonstrated to do anything beneficial on intact human skin.** There is **no human topical anti-aging evidence**; the only topical-skin data is preclinical depigmentation. [2] Any "boosts skin's antioxidant defense / fights photoaging" topical claim today would be marketing ahead of evidence — exactly what this brand is built to avoid.
- **Reality check:** ebselen circulates in longevity/biohacking circles because of the elegant enzyme-mimic mechanism and the hearing-loss trial. None of that is topical skin-aging proof.

---

## 11) Verdict for the brand

**❌ Skip (for now).** Reasoning:

1. **Not a cosmetic ingredient** — no INCI, no cosmetic regulatory standing, drug-development pedigree → high regulatory/legal risk for a brand whose whole premise is credibility and claims discipline (§8).
2. **No human topical efficacy or safety data** — the entire human record is *oral* and for non-skin diseases; the only topical-skin study is preclinical depigmentation (§4). We would be the ones running the experiment.
3. **Best-documented skin effect is depigmentation, not anti-aging** — off-thesis for a barrier-repair / skin-longevity hero, and itself a drug-claim and uneven-results risk (§7, §8).
4. **Formulation, bioavailability, stability, sourcing all unsolved** (§5, §6, §9).

**Longevity note (why it's worth keeping on file, not deleting):** Ebselen is the most interesting *mechanistic* longevity-antioxidant in this class — a *catalytic, self-regenerating* GPx mimic rather than a sacrificial antioxidant, which is conceptually superior to vitamin C/E for sustained redox defense. If the field ever produces (a) a cosmetic-approved derivative or analog, (b) human topical safety data, and (c) a human topical anti-aging or even brightening RCT, it could be reconsidered as a differentiated **supporting** antioxidant. Until then it stays in the "scientifically cool, commercially unusable" bucket. For an *actually deployable* catalytic/longevity antioxidant in this class today, **idebenone** (and to a lesser extent established forms of vitamin C + ferulic + E) are the credible, INCI-listed choices to research instead.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

_Verified 2026-06-19 by independent re-check of the headline citations against PubMed/journal records._

**Overall grade: D (unproven topically / mechanism-and-borrowed-evidence only).**

This is the correct grade for the brand's purpose (topical human anti-aging), and — importantly — **the dossier already says so.** Unlike a hype sheet, this dossier leads with a "skip" verdict, grades topical anti-aging evidence as nonexistent, and cleanly separates in-vitro/animal/oral evidence from human-topical. The adversarial pass largely *confirms* the dossier rather than overturning it.

**Citation checks (a = route/species, b = real & accurate, c = funding/size):**
- **Kasraee 2012 (depigmentation) [2]** — REAL; PMID 22082249, *Exp Dermatol* 21(1):19–24. (a) Route is **topical**, but species is **preclinical** (B16 murine cells, reconstructed human epidermis, guinea-pig ear) — NOT human. (b) The dossier's specifics (25 µM in cells; 0.5% topical; ~2-fold melanin decrease; melanocyte density preserved) match the abstract exactly. (c) Academic (Geneva/Saurat group); small/preclinical by nature. Accurately represented and correctly tier-flagged as the only topical-relevant signal.
- **Kil 2017 Lancet (hearing loss) [10][11]** — REAL; PMID 28716314, *Lancet* 390(10098):969–979. (a) **Oral, systemic, non-skin** — does not transfer to topical claims (dossier states this). (b) n=83, 200/400/600 mg twice daily, TTS at 4 kHz, 400 mg effective (~68% reduction), well tolerated — all verified accurate. (c) Sponsored by **Sound Pharmaceuticals (SPI-1005 developer)** — industry-funded; small (n=83). Dossier cites it only for *intrinsic oral safety*, which is the defensible use; it should not be read as efficacy support for skin. **Note: the underlying conflict of interest (manufacturer-sponsored) is not flagged in the dossier — minor caveat.**
