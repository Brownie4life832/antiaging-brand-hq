# Ingredient: Afamelanotide (INCI: Afamelanotide / [Nle⁴, D-Phe⁷]-α-MSH)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ rejected (skip for topical cosmetic use) · **Class:** Sunscreen / photoprotection filters & adjuncts (functionally: systemic photoprotective melanocortin agonist — a *prescription drug*, not a cosmetic)

> **Bottom line up front:** Afamelanotide is a real, FDA/EMA-approved drug, but it is an **injected/implanted prescription peptide (16 mg subcutaneous bioresorbable implant)** for a rare disease, not a topical cosmetic ingredient. It is **not bioavailable topically** (≈1647 Da, ~3× the ~500 Da skin-penetration ceiling), it requires a **physician to insert an implant**, it costs **tens of thousands of dollars per dose**, and it carries a **mandatory melanocytic-lesion monitoring warning**. There is **zero credible evidence for an afamelanotide topical anti-aging cosmetic**, and its illegal cousin "Melanotan" is a cautionary tale. **Skip.**

---

## 1) Identity

- **Common / brand name:** Afamelanotide. Brand: **SCENESSE®** (CLINUVEL Pharmaceuticals). Historically known as **Melanotan-I / Melanotan I (MT-1)** — the original research code; *not* the same as the unlicensed street drug "Melanotan II" (a smaller cyclic analog). [1][3][8]
- **Chemical identity:** A synthetic **tridecapeptide** (13 amino acids), a structural analog of human **α-melanocyte-stimulating hormone (α-MSH)**. It differs from native α-MSH at two positions: **methionine→norleucine at position 4** and **L-phenylalanine→D-phenylalanine at position 7**, written **[Nle⁴, D-Phe⁷]-α-MSH**. These two swaps increase MC1R binding affinity and resistance to enzymatic degradation, making it roughly **10–100× more potent/longer-acting** than native α-MSH (some early melanogenesis assays reported figures as high as ~1,000×, but that upper bound is assay-specific and should not be cited as the headline number). [1][3][6]
- **Molecular weight:** ≈ **1646.9 g/mol (Da).** [7] (For context, the practical passive stratum-corneum penetration limit is ~500 Da. [7])
- **Forms encountered:**
  - **Approved form:** 16 mg afamelanotide in a **poly(DL-lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA) bioresorbable subcutaneous implant** — a sterile white rod ~1.7 cm × 1.45 mm, inserted above the iliac crest by a trained physician. [3][8]
  - **Illicit/grey-market form:** lyophilized powder for self-injection or nasal spray (this overlaps with "Melanotan" tanning products — unlicensed and illegal in many markets). [11][12]
  - **Topical/cosmetic form:** **does not exist as a legitimate product** (see §5, §11).

## 2) Class & mechanism

**Mechanistic class:** Melanocortin-1 receptor (MC1R) agonist; secondarily a non-selective melanocortin receptor agonist (some activity at MC3R–MC5R). Functionally a **systemic, UV-independent photoprotectant.** [1][3][9]

**Mechanism of action (well-characterized at the receptor level):**
1. **MC1R binding → cAMP → eumelanin synthesis.** Afamelanotide binds MC1R on melanocytes, activates adenylate cyclase, raises intracellular cAMP, upregulates tyrosinase and MITF, and drives synthesis of **eumelanin** (brown-black, photoprotective) preferentially over **pheomelanin** (red-yellow, pro-oxidant). Crucially this happens **independent of UV exposure** (i.e., it "tans" without sun), though UV amplifies it. [1][3][4]
2. **Photoprotection.** Eumelanin absorbs broadband UV/visible light and scavenges free radicals; increased epidermal eumelanin raises the skin's intrinsic minimal erythema/phototoxic threshold. [1][4]
3. **Enhanced DNA repair (mechanistic, mostly via parent hormone α-MSH).** α-MSH/MC1R signaling enhances **nucleotide excision repair (NER)** of UV photolesions (cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers, 6-4 photoproducts) via an **XPA/XAB1-dependent** pathway in keratinocytes. [Abdel-Malek group, Cancer Research 2010] [13][14]
4. **Antioxidant / anti-inflammatory.** α-MSH activates antioxidant defenses (e.g., catalase/SOD-related responses) and modulates inflammation (IL-10 up, IL-8/IL-1 down) — relevant to "inflammaging." [13][15]

So mechanistically afamelanotide touches several genuine aging-relevant axes. **The problem is delivery and format, not biology** (see §5, §11).

## 4) Evidence review by tier

Ordered weakest→strongest format-relevance. The recurring theme: **the human evidence is for an injected/implanted drug, not topical skincare.**

### Tier 1 — In-vitro / cell studies (mechanism, parent hormone α-MSH mostly)
- **Böhm/Abdel-Malek mechanistic work:** α-MSH protects human melanocytes against UV-induced oxidative DNA damage and apoptosis; reduces UV oxidative DNA damage. [13][15]
- **Kadekaro / Abdel-Malek, *Cancer Research* 2010 (PMC2862104):** "Melanocyte-Stimulating Hormone Directly Enhances UV-Induced DNA Repair in Keratinocytes by a Xeroderma Pigmentosum Group A (XPA)–Dependent Mechanism." Demonstrates MC1R→NER link. **Limitation:** cultured cells; native α-MSH, not the drug per se. [14]
- **Swope et al., 2016 (*Photochem Photobiol Sci*; PMC4890311):** UVB, melanin and mitochondrial DNA photo-damage in keratinocytes/melanocytes modulated by α-MSH. **Limitation:** in-vitro. [16]
- **Bottom line:** Mechanistically rich, but in-vitro and largely the *natural hormone*. Cannot support consumer claims.

### Tier 2 — Animal studies
- Original development at the University of Arizona (1980s) established tyrosinase/melanogenesis potency in animal models (~up to 1,000× vs α-MSH in some assays). [3]
- Reproductive-tox animal studies (in FDA label): **no adverse developmental effects up to ~12× the max human dose.** [3]
- **Bottom line:** supports systemic pharmacology and safety, not topical cosmetic efficacy.

### Tier 3 — Human, systemic (oral/injected/implanted) — this is where the real RCT evidence lives
- **Langendonk et al., *New England Journal of Medicine*, 2015 — pivotal EPP RCTs (PMID 26132941; DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa1411481).** Two randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, multicenter trials of the **16 mg subcutaneous implant**:
  - **US trial:** n=94, 1:1, implant every 60 days. Primary endpoint **median hours of pain-free direct sunlight at 6 months: 69.4 h (afamelanotide) vs 40.8 h (placebo), p=0.04.**
  - **EU trial:** n=74, implant every 60 days. **Median pain-free sun exposure at 9 months 6.0 h vs 0.8 h, p=0.005**; fewer phototoxic reactions (77 vs 146, p=0.04). Quality of life improved. (Note the two trials used different follow-up windows and the EU absolute hours are small.)
  - **Limitations (honest):** modest absolute differences; subjective endpoints (patient-recorded pain/exposure diaries); EPP-specific population; this is **systemic dosing**, irrelevant to topical cosmetics. [2][4][9]
- **Earlier RCT/POC (CUV017, CUV029, etc.):** supported EU (2014) and FDA (2019) approval. [4][8]
- **Vitiligo (combination with NB-UVB):**
  - **Lim et al., *JAMA Dermatology* 2015 (151:42–50):** randomized multicenter, n=55 (28 combo / 27 NB-UVB alone). Combination repigmentation **48.6% vs 33.3%** — statistically superior but combination required injected implants + light therapy. [5]
  - **Toh et al., *J Am Acad Dermatol* 2020:** afamelanotide implants + NB-UVB in Asian nonsegmental vitiligo (small n); improved VASI. [5]
  - **Limitation:** small, combination designs, injected drug; not a cosmetic intervention.
- **Smaller systemic case series / pilots (all injected/implanted, small n):** polymorphic light eruption, solar urticaria (Haylett et al. 2011, n=5), acne (Böhm et al. 2014, n=3), Hailey-Hailey disease (Biolcati et al. 2014, n=2), xeroderma pigmentosum (CUV156, n=3). Mostly **hypothesis-generating, tiny, often unpublished or open-label.** [4]
- **Organ-transplant AK/SCC prevention:** Phase II systemic trials launched (~2007, ~200 patients) to reduce actinic keratosis/SCC in immunosuppressed transplant recipients — the closest thing to a "photoaging/skin-cancer-prevention" use — but results never translated into a marketed photoaging indication. [4][17]
- **Bottom line:** Genuine RCT-grade evidence exists — **for the injected/implanted drug in disease populations.** None of it validates a leave-on cosmetic.

### Tier 4 — Human, TOPICAL (RCT vs lower)
- **None.** There are **no published controlled trials — RCT or otherwise — of topically applied afamelanotide for cosmetic anti-aging, photoprotection, or any indication.** The molecule (~1647 Da) cannot meaningfully cross intact stratum corneum, so a leave-on topical has no plausible delivery route. [7]
- The only "topical melanocortin" data in the literature is unrelated mouse work on topical Melanotan-II for melanoma (PMC7013727) — **not afamelanotide, not human, not cosmetic.** [unverified relevance]
- **Bottom line:** **Zero human topical evidence.** Any cosmetic marketing "afamelanotide" claim would be fabricated.

## 5) Topical bioavailability

**Effectively zero for passive topical delivery.** Afamelanotide is a **13-residue, ~1647 Da hydrophilic peptide.** The stratum corneum imposes a steep penetration penalty above ~500 Da; peptides >~400–500 Da generally do not permeate intact skin without enabling technology (microneedles, iontophoresis, novel penetration enhancers). [7] That is precisely why the approved product is a **controlled-release subcutaneous implant** (Tmax ~36 h, t½ ~15 h, release over ~5–10 days) rather than a cream or even an injection. [3][8] **No legitimate topical formulation achieves clinically meaningful skin or systemic levels.**

## 6) Formulation notes (for completeness — not applicable to cosmetic use)

- **Approved concentration/dose:** 16 mg per implant; one implant every 60 days. **Not a "% in formula" ingredient.** [3]
- **pH / stability / sensory:** Not relevant to a topical cosmetic because no viable topical exists. As a peptide, it would be susceptible to hydrolysis/oxidation in aqueous bases; the drug is specifically engineered for controlled subcutaneous release, not aqueous topical stability.
- **Pairings:** Clinically paired with **NB-UVB** for vitiligo (drug + phototherapy). No cosmetic pairing data.
- **Practical verdict:** there is no formulation pathway for a brand here.

## 7) Safety

From the FDA/DailyMed SCENESSE label (systemic implant) and the Melanotan grey-market literature:

- **Most common adverse reactions (>2%, EPP trials):** implant-site reaction **21%**, nausea **19%**, oropharyngeal pain **7%**, cough **6%**, fatigue **6%**, skin hyperpigmentation **4%**, dizziness **4%**, melanocytic nevus **4%**. [3][8]
- **Pigmentary warning (key):** the label **requires twice-yearly full-body skin examination** to monitor pre-existing nevi and new pigmentary lesions because the drug **darkens nevi and ephelides.** This is a meaningful melanoma-vigilance flag. [3][8]
- **Sun behavior:** it does *not* replace sun protection; patients are still advised on photoprotection. The mechanism *increases* pigmentation rather than blocking UV at the surface.
- **Pregnancy / lactation:** **No human data**; animal repro-tox clean up to ~12× human dose; lactation data absent — physician risk/benefit only. Not for cosmetic populations. [3]
- **Sensitization / injection risks:** implant-site reactions; grey-market injectables add **infection, contaminated product, and dosing-error risks.**
- **Grey-market "Melanotan" safety (cautionary):** the unlicensed tanning peptides (often conflated with afamelanotide) are linked to **nausea/vomiting, darkening and changes in moles, new/atypical nevi, reports of melanoma, rhabdomyolysis, renal dysfunction, posterior reversible encephalopathy, priapism (MT-II)**, and systemic sympathomimetic toxicity. **MHRA (2009) and Cancer Research UK** warned these are illegal, untested, and possibly unsafe. [11][12]
- **Sourcing safety:** legitimate afamelanotide is a controlled prescription pharmaceutical (CLINUVEL); anything sold for cosmetic/topical/"tanning" use is by definition **not the approved drug** and is unregulated.

## 8) Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **It is a DRUG, not a cosmetic.** SCENESSE is **FDA-approved (Oct 2019)** and **EMA-approved (Dec 2014)** with the narrow indication: *"to increase pain free light exposure in adult patients with a history of phototoxic reactions from erythropoietic protoporphyria (EPP)."* [3][8]
- **Using it in a cosmetic is not legally available.** A prescription-only systemic implant cannot be put in a leave-on consumer product. Marketing any "afamelanotide" topical would be selling an **unapproved new drug** (US) / unauthorized medicinal product (EU) — squarely over the cosmetic/drug line that CLAUDE.md says to avoid.
- **Even the *benefits* are drug claims.** "Photoprotection," "DNA repair," "increases melanin," "prevents phototoxicity" are **structure/function/disease claims** — exactly the language the glossary flags as drug-territory. There is **no compliant cosmetic claim** we could make for this ingredient.
- **Brand-name confusion risk:** "Kintsugi" candidacy aside, do not let any session conflate afamelanotide with safe cosmetic "peptides" in the glossary — it is categorically different (a regulated injectable). Flag for legal if it ever surfaces in copy.

**Compliance verdict: there is no cosmetic claim we can make, because there is no cosmetic use.**

## 9) Sourcing & cost

- **Single supplier:** CLINUVEL Pharmaceuticals (SCENESSE). Restricted distribution through accredited porphyria/specialist centers in the EU; specialty channel in the US. [4]
- **Cost (drug, not cosmetic raw material):** ≈ **$49,000+ per 16 mg implant** in the US; UK list ~£13,209/implant + VAT; German annual therapy **€56,404–€84,606/patient/yr.** NICE rejected it on cost-effectiveness grounds. [10]
- **Cosmetic raw-material cost:** **N/A** — there is no legitimate cosmetic-grade supply. Grey-market peptide powder exists but is illegal/unsafe and not usable for a credible brand. **Cost tier for our purposes: effectively prohibitive / unavailable.**

## 10) Hype vs. evidence

- **The hype:** marketed in biohacking/peptide circles and "tanning injection/nasal spray" channels as an anti-aging, photoprotective, "sunless tan" miracle; sometimes name-dropped in indie skincare as a futuristic "DNA-repair peptide."
- **The evidence reality:**
  - **Real, FDA/EMA-approved** — but only as an **injected/implanted orphan drug for EPP.** [3][8]
  - **Strong mechanism** (MC1R→eumelanin→photoprotection + DNA-repair/antioxidant signaling), mostly demonstrated in cells and for the parent hormone. [13][14][16]
  - **Modest, subjective RCT endpoints** even in its approved disease use. [2]
  - **No topical bioavailability, no topical trials, no cosmetic legality.** [7]
  - The "tanning peptide" hype is the **illegal, unsafe Melanotan** branch — associated with **mole changes and melanoma concerns.** [11][12]
- **Net:** A legitimate pharmaceutical wrapped in dangerous consumer hype. The cosmetic story is **almost entirely hype.**

## 11) Verdict for the brand

**❌ SKIP.** Afamelanotide is a **prescription systemic drug**, not a topical cosmetic ingredient, and there is **no legal, safe, or scientifically supported way** to use it in a skincare product.

**Reasoning:**
1. **Format mismatch:** ~1647 Da peptide with no passive skin penetration; the approved product is a physician-inserted implant. A leave-on cosmetic would do nothing. [7]
2. **Regulatory wall:** it is a drug for a rare disease; any topical "afamelanotide" claim = unapproved drug marketing, the exact line CLAUDE.md forbids crossing. [3][8]
3. **Safety/optics:** mandatory melanocytic-lesion monitoring and the toxic, illegal "Melanotan" association are brand poison for a science-credible skin-longevity label. [11][12]
4. **Cost/availability:** ~$49k/dose pharmaceutical; no cosmetic-grade supply. [10]
5. **Direction-of-effect:** it *increases* pigmentation and can darken moles — antithetical to "even tone / brightening" positioning.

**Longevity note (what to take from it, ethically):** The *biology* afamelanotide validates — that **eumelanin and MC1R signaling are central to UV defense, ROS scavenging, and DNA-repair (NER)** — reinforces the brand's most defensible longevity thesis: **sun protection is the #1 evidence-based anti-aging move** (already in the glossary). Translate that learning into **legitimate cosmetic tools** instead: broad-spectrum SPF, antioxidants, niacinamide, and — if a "support melanin/even tone safely" angle is ever wanted — well-evidenced topicals, **not** a melanocortin drug. File afamelanotide as a *mechanism reference and a what-not-to-touch example*, then move it to `not-considering.md` with the reason "prescription systemic drug; not topically bioavailable; not cosmetic-legal; melanoma-monitoring/illegal-analog optics."

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Overall grade: D (for topical cosmetic use) — and the dossier's own SKIP verdict is correct.**

Graded by the standard the brand cares about (human + topical + controlled). Afamelanotide has genuine **A-grade human RCT evidence — but for the wrong format and the wrong population** (a 16 mg subcutaneous *implant* in a rare disease). For the question that matters here — a leave-on topical cosmetic — the evidence is **D: unproven topically, no human topical trials exist, and the molecule cannot passively cross intact skin.** This is exactly the right reason to reject it, and the dossier already lands there.

### What I verified (independent source check, 2026-06-19)

- **Identity / chemistry — CONFIRMED.** Tridecapeptide, [Nle⁴, D-Phe⁷]-α-MSH, = Melanotan I, MC1R agonist with MC3–5R activity. MW **1646.87 Da** confirmed (ChEMBL CHEMBL441738; sequence Ac-Ser-Tyr-Ser-Nle-Glu-His-D-Phe-Arg-Trp-Gly-Lys-Pro-Val-NH₂). The >500 Da stratum-corneum penetration argument rests on the well-established "500 Dalton rule" (Bos & Meinardi, *Exp Dermatol* 2000) — sound and correctly the central reason for rejection.
- **Pivotal EPP RCTs (NEJM 2015, Langendonk et al.) — CONFIRMED, real, says what's claimed.** PMID 26132941. US n=94: 69.4 h vs 40.8 h pain-free sun, p=0.04; EU n=74: 6.0 h vs 0.8 h, p=0.005. Caveat the dossier already flags and I reinforce: endpoints are **patient-recorded (subjective) diaries**, absolute differences are **modest** (the EU arm's 6 h vs 0.8 h is small), and the two arms used **different follow-up windows** (US 6 mo, EU 9 mo). Industry context: trials run/sponsored by **CLINUVEL**, the manufacturer — disclose, but the FDA/EMA approvals are independent regulatory validation. This is **human + systemic (implant)**, NOT topical.
- **Vitiligo RCT (JAMA Dermatology 2015, Lim/Grimes) — CONFIRMED.** n=55 (28 combo / 27 NB-UVB), day-168 repigmentation **48.64% vs 33.26%**, faster onset on face/upper extremities. *Correction made:* the dossier's source [5] URL pointed to a different 2013 case-series article (`1377949`); fixed to the actual RCT (`1904928`). Caveat: it is a **combination** design (drug **+** phototherapy via implant) — it does not isolate afamelanotide and is not a cosmetic intervention.
- **DNA-repair mechanism (Cancer Research 2010, Kadekaro/Abdel-Malek) — CONFIRMED.** PMC2862104; MC1R→cAMP→XAB1→XPA nuclear translocation→NER. Correctly attributed in the dossier as **cell/animal-level and largely the natural hormone α-MSH, not the drug** — not a consumer claim.
- **FDA approval — CONFIRMED.** SCENESSE approved **Oct 8, 2019** for EPP phototoxicity; EMA Dec 2014. Narrow orphan indication exactly as stated.

### Borrowed-evidence audit (the key adversarial test)

Every aging-relevant benefit beyond photoprotection is **borrowed from in-vitro / animal / parent-hormone (α-MSH) work**, not demonstrated for topical afamelanotide in humans. The dossier's §3 table is honest about this and already marks all of them "No." The single human-RCT-proven effect (photoprotection) is **systemic, not topical**. No overstated efficacy claims were found that needed walking back — the document is, if anything, harder on the ingredient than the evidence requires.
