# Ingredient: Pomegranate Extract / Ellagitannins (INCI: Punica granatum extract & related)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering (verdict: **conditional / supporting** — see §11)
**Class:** Autophagy & mitochondrial-targeted actives (also a polyphenol antioxidant)
**Slug:** pomegranate-extract

> **One-line honest summary:** A well-studied, well-tolerated polyphenol antioxidant with a genuinely interesting longevity mechanism (its metabolite urolithin A drives mitophagy). But the strongest human anti-aging data is for *oral* pomegranate or for *isolated urolithin A* — not for topical whole pomegranate extract itself. Most topical claims rest on in-vitro and animal work plus borrowing from oral/isolated-molecule studies.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common names:** Pomegranate extract, pomegranate peel/rind extract, pomegranate seed oil, pomegranate fruit extract (PFE).
- **INCI names (the form matters a lot):**
  - `Punica Granatum Fruit Extract`
  - `Punica Granatum Pericarp (peel/rind) Extract` — richest in ellagitannins
  - `Punica Granatum Seed Oil` — a *lipid*, chemically unrelated to the polyphenols (rich in punicic acid, an omega-5 conjugated fatty acid)
  - `Punica Granatum Flower / Leaf Extract`
  - `Ellagic Acid` (often listed as the standardization marker)
- **Key bioactive forms / derivatives:**
  - **Ellagitannins** — large polyphenols; the dominant one is **punicalagin** (the highest-molecular-weight polyphenol commonly cited; concentrated in peel/rind).
  - **Punicalin** — a smaller ellagitannin.
  - **Ellagic acid (EA)** — the hydrolysis product of ellagitannins; the usual standardization marker (extracts sold as "40%" or "90% ellagic acid," e.g. Sabinsa).
  - **Urolithin A (UA)** and urolithins B/C/D — gut-microbiome metabolites of ellagic acid; **urolithin A is the headline longevity molecule** but is *not* present in the fruit itself.
  - **Punicic acid** — the omega-5 conjugated fatty acid in *seed oil* (separate story; don't conflate with the polyphenols).
- **Critical taxonomy note for our brand:** "Pomegranate extract" is an umbrella term covering at least three chemically different actives (peel polyphenols, seed-oil fatty acids, and the gut-derived metabolite UA). Evidence does **not** transfer between them. Sloppy sourcing/claims here is the #1 risk with this ingredient.

## 2. Class & mechanism (skin / cell level)

Pomegranate polyphenols act on several converging anti-aging pathways. Mechanism is well-characterized *mechanistically* (mostly in cells/animals):

1. **Direct antioxidant / ROS scavenging.** Punicalagin and ellagic acid quench UV-induced reactive oxygen species and raise intracellular antioxidant capacity (Pacheco-Palencia 2008; Zaid 2007).
2. **MMP suppression → less collagen breakdown.** Inhibits UVB-induced MMP-1/-2/-7/-9 (collagen-degrading enzymes) and supports TIMP-1, largely by blocking MAPK (ERK/JNK/p38) and c-Jun signaling (Zaid 2007; Afaq 2009).
3. **Procollagen synthesis.** Aqueous peel extract stimulated dermal fibroblast proliferation and **type-I procollagen** synthesis in vitro (Aslam 2006).
4. **NF-κB / inflammaging.** Downregulates the NF-κB inflammatory complex, COX-2 and iNOS — central to "inflammaging" (Cordiano 2024 review; Pacheco-Palencia 2008).
5. **Nrf2/HO-1 antioxidant gene activation & SIRT1 modulation** (Cordiano 2024).
6. **Mitophagy / autophagy — the longevity headline, but via the metabolite.** Ellagic acid is converted by gut bacteria into **urolithin A**, which induces **mitophagy** (selective clearance of damaged mitochondria) and improves mitochondrial function. This is the mechanism that puts pomegranate in the "autophagy & mitochondrial-targeted" class. Important caveat: the fruit/peel does **not** contain UA — you only get it if (a) you ingest ellagitannins *and* (b) you have the right gut microbiome, or (c) you apply UA directly. (Andreux 2019; D'Amico/Liu 2023; Cordiano 2024.)

**Bottom line on mechanism:** plausible and multi-pathway, but most of it is demonstrated in keratinocyte/fibroblast cultures and reconstituted-skin or mouse models, not in topical human trials.

## 4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core)

> **Read this section as the verdict driver.** The honest pattern: **mechanism is proven in cells; UV/oral benefits are proven in humans orally; topical human anti-aging data for *whole pomegranate extract* is thin and mostly indirect; the strong topical RCT data belongs to the isolated metabolite urolithin A, which is sold as a separate (patented, expensive) ingredient, not "pomegranate extract."**

### TIER 1 — In-vitro (strongest, most numerous)

1. **Aslam, Lansky & Varani (2006), *J Ethnopharmacol* 103:311–318.** Pomegranate fractions on human skin cells. Aqueous **peel** extract stimulated dermal fibroblast proliferation + type-I **procollagen** synthesis and **inhibited MMP-1**; **seed oil** stimulated keratinocyte proliferation but not fibroblasts. *Foundational "cosmeceutical" paper.* Limitation: monolayer cell culture; division of labor between fractions means no single "pomegranate extract" does everything.
2. **Zaid, Afaq, Syed, Dreher & Mukhtar (2007), *Photochem Photobiol* 83(4):882–888.** POMx polyphenol extract (10–40 µg/mL) in HaCaT keratinocytes vs UVB (15–30 mJ/cm²). Reduced ROS, lipid peroxidation, glutathione depletion; suppressed UVB-induced MMP-1/-2/-7/-9 and MAPK/c-Jun phosphorylation, and modulated TIMP-1 (the abstract reports POMx *decreased* TIMP-1 alongside the MMPs — note the original dossier said "preserved TIMP-1"; the source wording is "decreased," so do not claim TIMP-1 was raised/preserved). *Strong photoaging-marker dataset.* Limitation: immortalized cell line.
3. **Pacheco-Palencia, Mertens-Talcott & Talcott (2008), *J Agric Food Chem* 56(18):8434–8441.** Punicalagin-standardized extract in UVA/UVB-irradiated human fibroblasts. **5–60 mg/L** protected against cell death (↓NF-κB, ↓caspase-3, G0/G1 arrest for DNA repair); **500–10,000 mg/L** needed for significant ROS reduction. *Important: dose-dependent, two distinct mechanisms.* Limitation: in-vitro; very high doses for antioxidant effect.
4. **Cordiano et al. (2024), *Molecules* 29(17):4174 — review of 63 articles on pomegranate & inflammaging.** Catalogs NF-κB suppression, SOD/catalase/GPx induction, SIRT1/STAT3 modulation, Nrf2/HO-1, and UA-driven mitophagy. **Explicit gap flagged: "No human studies have demonstrated the anti-inflammaging effects of pomegranate."**
5. **In-vitro fibroblast wound study (2023), *[journal — verify], PMC10781179.** Punicalagin and ellagic acid at 10⁻⁶–10⁻⁷ M increased human fibroblast migration and upregulated fibronectin/α-actin. Limitation: "preliminary," very low molar doses, no cytotoxicity ceiling reported here. *(Cell-migration / wound model, not anti-aging per se.)* [verify full citation]
6. **Pomanox® in Hs68 human fibroblasts (2022/2023), *Int J Food Sci Nutr* (Taylor & Francis).** Commercial pom extract modulated skin-health parameters in normal and UV-photoaging fibroblast conditions. Limitation: manufacturer-associated; in-vitro.

### TIER 2 — Animal / ex-vivo reconstituted skin

7. **Afaq, Zaid, Khan, Dreher & Mukhtar (2009), *Exp Dermatol* 18(6):553–561.** Pomegranate-derived products on **human reconstituted (3-D) skin** vs UVB. Pre-treatment **decreased UV-induced MMP-1** and inhibited MAPK activation. *Closest-to-skin model short of a human trial.* Limitation: reconstituted skin, not living humans.
8. **Oral PFE in mice vs UVB (Afaq/Mukhtar group, multiple ~2005–2009).** Dietary pomegranate fruit extract protected mouse skin from UVB by modulating UVB signaling pathways (MAPK, NF-κB). *Animal, oral route.*
9. **Pomegranate peel extract gel, wound healing in rodents.** 5% peel gels cut wound-closure time (~10 d vs 16–18 d controls), ~2× hydroxyproline, and EA raised incision tensile strength ~32–35% (see Fortune Journals / PMC9018025 wound reviews). *Animal, topical — but wound, not aging.*

### TIER 3 — Human, ORAL (the strongest *human* data — note: not topical)

10. **Henning et al. (2019), *Scientific Reports* 9:14528 — RCT.** 74 women (30–45 y, Fitzpatrick II–IV), 3 arms ×12 wk: 1000 mg pomegranate extract (PomX) / 8 oz juice (PomJ) / placebo. **Minimal erythema dose (MED) increased significantly** (PomX p=0.011; PomJ p=0.038) — i.e., more UV resistance. Shifted the *skin* microbiome. 67–83% of treated subjects produced urolithin A glucuronide, and **UA-producers showed greater MED gains** (the responder/non-responder problem). Limitations: blinding was imperfect (capsule vs 8 oz juice vs placebo is hard to mask fully — closer to open-label for the juice arm, though MED is an objective instrument endpoint), melanin index change non-significant, skin sampled from non-UV-exposed sites, no gene-expression change in unexposed skin. *[design verified 2026-06-19: real RCT, 3-arm, 74 women; MED↑ PomX p=0.011, PomJ p=0.038]*
11. **Oral pomegranate extract, wrinkles & gut-skin axis (2022), *J Clin Med* 11(22):6724 — double-blind, placebo-controlled.** Studied oral PFE on wrinkles/biophysical features and the urolithin metabotype/gut-skin axis. *Supports the oral + microbiome-responder story.* Limitation: oral route; effects modest. [verify n & exact outcomes — fetch blocked]
12. **Krishnakumar et al. (2024), Grantria® RCT, *Am J Transl Res* 16(12):8043–8053 (PMID 39822556).** **60 women enrolled** (80 *screened*; 40 test + 40 placebo, 35–65 y), **300 mg/day ORAL** standardized pom extract (standardized to ellagic acid, punicic acid *and* punicalagin via "ADOP" oil-powder tech, not EA alone) vs placebo, 60 d. Reported large improvements: crow's-feet −48%, roughness −43%, radiance +43%, tyrosinase −3% (non-significant). **Heavy caveats:** explicitly **open-label** (paper's own methods say "randomized, open label, placebo-controlled" — the "placebo-controlled" framing masks the lack of blinding), **manufacturer-funded** (Zeus Hygia Lifesciences; 3 of 5 authors are Zeus Hygia–affiliated), wrinkle/roughness/radiance outcomes were **dermatologist clinical grading** (atlas-based, not blinded), only pores/spots/tone were instrument-measured. Treat effect sizes as marketing-adjacent, not gold-standard. *[verified PMC11733350 2026-06-19]*
13. **Frontiers in Nutrition (2023) systematic review & meta-analysis** of oral fruit/fruit-extract on skin aging RCTs — provides the pooled context that oral pom sits within (signal exists, heterogeneity high).

### TIER 4 — Human, TOPICAL

**4a. Whole pomegranate extract, topical — WEAK / mostly indirect.**
- No high-quality, vehicle-controlled topical RCT was found for *whole pomegranate extract* on facial photoaging endpoints (wrinkles, elasticity, firmness). Topical anti-aging claims for "pomegranate extract" currently borrow from in-vitro/reconstituted-skin (Tier 1–2) and from oral RCTs (Tier 3).
- Topical **anti-inflammatory** support exists at lower rigor: standardized pom rind extract + ellagic acid reduced ear edema dose-dependently in a contact-dermatitis model (Hartman/PMID 23873506) — but that's an animal/irritation model.

**4b. Ellagic acid (isolated), topical for PIGMENTATION — moderate, but combination-confounded.** *(2 RCTs; see Desai 2018 systematic review, PMC5843359.)*
- **Ertam, Mutlu et al. (2008), *J Dermatol*** — 30 melasma patients, topical 1% arbutin vs 1% ellagic acid vs 1% EA + plant extracts (open-label, ~6 mo). All arms improved melanin index. Limitation: EA not isolated cleanly; open-label.
- **Dahl et al. (2013)** — 54 subjects, **0.5% EA + 0.1% salicylic acid** vs 4% hydroquinone. Comparable lightening to HQ with better cosmetic acceptability. Limitation: EA combined with salicylic acid — can't attribute to EA alone.
- Japanese background data: EA-based topicals showed skin-lightening with good safety (basis of Japanese "quasi-drug" whitening approvals).
- Systematic-review verdict (Desai 2018): *"ellagic acid has not been independently studied… further studies needed."*

**4c. Urolithin A (isolated metabolite), topical — STRONGEST topical data, but it's a DIFFERENT, patented ingredient.**
- **D'Amico, Andreux, Valdés et al. (2023), medRxiv 2023.06.16.23291378 — 3 randomized clinical trials** of **topical urolithin A (Mitopure®)** at **0.5% and 1%** (key nuance: only the **1% dose was effective**; 0.5% had no significant impact on wrinkles):
  - *Aging study 1* (n=48, post-menopausal women, >Grade 3 wrinkles, split-face): 1% UA decreased facial wrinkle depth and up-regulated collagen-synthesis pathways in skin biopsies; 0.5% did not.
  - *Aging study 2* (n=108, middle-aged men and women): wrinkle reduction; a finished formula (UA + niacinamide + trehalose) also improved hydration.
  - *UVB photodamage trial* (n=22, placebo-controlled): 1% UA significantly reduced UVB-induced erythema/redness vs untreated control.
  - HRIPT safety panel: no reactions, well tolerated.
  - **Major caveats:** sponsored by **Amazentis/Timeline** (the commercial owner of Mitopure UA); **preprint, not peer-reviewed** (no published version confirmed as of 2026-06-19); modest effect sizes; the once-cited "~4% wrinkle / ~14% erythema" figures could **not be independently confirmed** from the abstract (which reports effects as "significant" rather than as those exact percentages) — treat them as unverified. **And crucially: this is *urolithin A*, a single synthesized molecule — NOT pomegranate extract.** A pomegranate-extract product only delivers UA's benefit if it actually contains/converts to UA at the skin, which whole extracts do not reliably do. *[design verified via medRxiv/Semantic Scholar 2026-06-19]*

**Topical vs borrowed — the explicit map:**

| Benefit | Proven for *topical whole pom extract* in humans? | Where the evidence actually comes from |
|---|---|---|
| Antioxidant / UV-marker reduction | ❌ Not directly | in-vitro + reconstituted skin + oral RCT (MED↑) |
| Wrinkle reduction | ❌ Not for whole extract | oral RCTs (open-label) + isolated UA topical RCT |
| Collagen support / ↓MMP | ❌ Not in living human skin | in-vitro + 3-D reconstituted skin |
| Hydration | ❌ Not for whole extract | isolated UA (Mitopure) topical RCT |
| Pigmentation/brightening | ◐ Partly (ellagic acid, combo RCTs) | 2 topical RCTs, both combination-confounded |
| Mitophagy/mitochondrial | ❌ Not for the fruit | isolated UA only |

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **Ellagic acid is a poor topical penetrant.** Low aqueous solubility, instability, and poor skin permeability are repeatedly cited as the limiting factors for clinical efficacy (Molecules 2024 EA-formulation review; Desai 2018).
- In a skin-permeation study, after 8 h, pomegranate **peel polyphenols were largely retained in the skin and did not cross to the receptor compartment**; gallic/ellagic acid only reached viable epidermis/dermis when delivered in a **nanoemulsion** (~200 nm droplets gave ~2.2× more retained gallic acid vs free extract) — PMID 28597364 (*AAPS PharmSciTech* 2017).
- **Punicalagin** is even larger (high MW) → expect even worse passive penetration; likely acts mostly at the surface/upper stratum corneum unless formulated for delivery.
- **Practical implication:** for any meaningful dermal effect, the active needs **delivery help** (nanoemulsion, liposome, glycol/ethanol co-solvent, low pH to favor EA, or use of the smaller EA marker rather than punicalagin). Surface antioxidant/anti-pollution effects are achievable without deep penetration.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:**
  - Standardized extracts are typically dosed **0.5–2%** of an extract standardized to ellagic acid (commercial standardizations: 40% or 90% EA).
  - Ellagic-acid pigmentation RCTs used **0.5–1% EA**.
  - In-vitro actives were active at low micromolar / mg-L ranges, but those don't map directly to a formula %.
  - **Honest note:** there is no validated "use X% for Y wrinkle benefit" for topical whole pom extract — concentrations are supplier guidance, not clinically anchored.
- **pH:** Polyphenols/EA are most stable in mildly acidic systems (~pH 3.5–5.5). Avoid high pH (accelerates oxidation/browning).
- **Stability:** Polyphenols **oxidize** readily — expect color drift (yellow→brown) and potential loss of activity. Needs antioxidant packaging (airless/opaque), possibly chelators, and good preservation. Seed oil oxidizes too (add tocopherol).
- **Pairings:**
  - **Vitamin C / niacinamide** — complementary antioxidant/brightening (UA topical RCT used niacinamide + trehalose).
  - **Other polyphenols / peptides** — fine.
  - **Sunscreen** — synergistic with the photoprotection story (and necessary, since pom is *not* a sunscreen).
- **Conflicts / cautions:** Strong oxidizers and very high-pH systems; possible discoloration in white/clear formulas. Direct combination with high-strength acids may be fine for EA solubility but watch overall irritation load.
- **Sensory:** Seed oil = rich, slightly nutty, occlusive-ish (nice for a barrier-repair cream texture). Polyphenol extracts can add color/tint and a faint astringency; usually used at low % so sensory impact is minor.

## 7. Safety

- **General safety:** Pomegranate-derived cosmetic ingredients have a long history of safe topical use. Ellagic-acid-based topicals tested negative for acute toxicity, skin irritation, sensitization, and mutagenicity in the data underpinning Japanese quasi-drug whitening approvals.
- **CIR (Cosmetic Ingredient Review):** CIR has published a safety assessment of *Punica granatum*-derived ingredients; pomegranate cosmetic ingredients are generally regarded as safe as used in cosmetics. *(The full CIR PDF could not be machine-parsed in this session — confirm the exact "safe as used" wording, any concentration limits, and the assessment year directly from cir-safety.org before quoting it in copy. [partially unverified])*
- **Irritation/sensitization:** Low risk at cosmetic levels; whole extracts contain many phenolics so idiosyncratic sensitization is possible but uncommon. Patch testing prudent for sensitive-skin SKUs.
- **Sun sensitivity:** No evidence pomegranate is photosensitizing; if anything the data point toward photo-*protection*. (Still: not a sunscreen — no SPF claim.)
- **Pregnancy:** Topical cosmetic use of pomegranate extract is not flagged as a concern; there is no specific topical pregnancy contraindication in the literature reviewed. (Standard caveat: this is not medical advice; defer to "consult your doctor" framing rather than asserting safety.)
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** Strong positive story — see §9.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Cosmetic vs drug line.** Antioxidant, "helps defend against environmental stress," "supports skin's appearance," "visibly brightens / evens tone," "helps improve the look of fine lines" are defensible **cosmetic** claims. Crossing into **drug** territory: "boosts collagen," "repairs DNA," "treats melasma," "induces mitophagy/anti-aging at the cellular level," "increases UV resistance / raises MED" (an SPF-adjacent functional claim). Avoid all of those in consumer copy.
- **The urolithin-A trap (most important).** Do **not** import urolithin-A topical claims (wrinkle %, hydration, mitophagy, "the molecule that recycles mitochondria") onto a *pomegranate extract* product. The fruit/peel does not contain UA, and whole extracts are not shown to deliver UA into skin. Using UA data to sell pom extract would be an unsubstantiated, legally exposed claim. If we ever want the UA story, we must source actual urolithin A (e.g., Mitopure) as its own ingredient and claim accordingly.
- **The "borrowed format" trap.** Oral-study results (MED↑, wrinkle improvement) must **not** be presented as topical benefits.
- **Brightening claims (ellagic acid):** the 2 topical RCTs are combination-confounded → keep to "helps visibly even tone / brighten," never "as effective as hydroquinone."
- **Substantiation flag:** any efficacy claim beyond generic antioxidant/appearance language should be flagged for legal/substantiation review.

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Forms & suppliers:** Standardized peel extracts (e.g., **Sabinsa** ellagic acid 40%/90%; **Pomanox®** by Euromed; upcycled punicalagin-enriched peel extracts marketed in *Cosmetics & Toiletries*). Seed oil from Botanic Innovations, Sophim, O&3, Jedwards, etc.
- **Cost tier:**
  - Standardized polyphenol/EA extracts: **mid tier** as a low-% additive (active inclusion is small, so cost-in-formula is modest).
  - Cold-pressed seed oil: **premium** carrier oil (~200 lb fresh seed → 1 lb oil), but still far cheaper than patented actives.
  - Isolated **urolithin A (Mitopure)**: **high/premium, patented** — a different cost universe; only relevant if we deliberately choose the UA story.
- **Sustainability (a real brand asset):** Pomegranate processing generates ~5–6 tons of peel/seed waste per ton of juice; **peel has higher polyphenol/antioxidant content than the edible arils.** Upcycled-peel extracts are an established, credible **"upcycled / valorized byproduct"** sustainability and storytelling angle — fits a premium, science-forward brand well.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

- **"Pomegranate boosts collagen / erases wrinkles."** Overreach. Procollagen↑ and MMP-1↓ are **in-vitro / reconstituted-skin**; no topical-extract human wrinkle RCT.
- **"Mitophagy / mitochondrial-aging breakthrough."** This is **urolithin A's** story, not the fruit's. Marketing that attaches UA's mechanism to plain pomegranate extract is the single biggest hype trap here.
- **"Clinically proven anti-aging."** The cleaner human data is **oral**, and the most-cited topical "anti-aging" RCTs are either **open-label/manufacturer-funded** (Grantria, oral) or for the **isolated UA molecule** (Timeline-funded).
- **"Natural sunscreen / UV protection."** Photoprotective signal is real in vitro and orally (MED↑), but it is **not** a sunscreen and any MED/UV-resistance claim is drug-adjacent.
- **Seed oil ≠ polyphenols.** A "pomegranate" product trading on the polyphenol/longevity science but actually using only seed oil is a common bait-and-switch in the category.
- **Responder problem.** Oral benefits skew toward urolithin-A *producers* (gut-microbiome dependent) — a nuance the category never discloses.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**Conditional → best as a SUPPORTING actor, not a hero.** ⚠️

- **One-line reasoning:** Pomegranate extract is a safe, sustainable, on-brand polyphenol antioxidant with excellent mechanistic and oral evidence and a great upcycled-sourcing story — but **topical human anti-aging proof for the whole extract is thin**, and its marquee longevity mechanism (mitophagy) actually belongs to **urolithin A**, a separate, patented molecule. Use it for credible antioxidant/anti-pollution/tone support and sustainability narrative; do **not** build hero anti-aging or "mitochondrial" claims on it.
- **Longevity-relevance note:** Genuinely relevant to the longevity thesis *in principle* (autophagy/mitophagy via urolithin A, inflammaging via NF-κB). But the brand can only legitimately tell the mitophagy/mitochondrial story if it **sources actual urolithin A** (e.g., Mitopure) and treats *that* as the hero — pomegranate extract alone earns a supporting role.
- **Recommended path:**
  1. **Supporting antioxidant** in the barrier-repair cream or a future serum, formulated with delivery help (nanoemulsion/low pH), standardized to ellagic acid.
  2. Lean on the **upcycled peel** sustainability angle.
  3. **If** we want the longevity headline, evaluate **urolithin A (Mitopure)** as a distinct, premium hero ingredient with its own dossier — and claim only what its RCTs support.
- **Log:** add to [`../../ideas/considering.md`](../../ideas/considering.md) as "supporting antioxidant; revisit urolithin A separately as potential hero."

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

> Independent skeptical re-check performed **2026-06-19**. Method: pulled each headline-driving citation and tested it against three questions — (a) is the evidence HUMAN and TOPICAL, or quietly borrowed from in-vitro / animal / oral data? (b) is the study real and does it say what the dossier claims? (c) is it industry-funded or very small? Verified directly: Aslam 2006, Zaid 2007, Pacheco-Palencia 2008, Afaq 2009 (by title/abstract), Cordiano 2024 (full text), Henning 2019 (full design), Krishnakumar/Grantria 2024 (full PMC text), D'Amico/Mitopure UA 2023 (design via medRxiv/Semantic Scholar), nanoemulsion penetration study PMID 28597364 (abstract).

### Overall evidence grade: **C** (for the brand's actual question — *topical whole pomegranate extract* for anti-aging)

- **Why not higher:** there is **no vehicle-controlled topical RCT of whole pomegranate extract on facial anti-aging endpoints**. Every "strong" data point is either in-vitro/reconstituted-skin (mechanism only), **human-ORAL** (Henning MED↑; oral wrinkle studies), or for the **isolated patented metabolite urolithin A** — a different ingredient. The dossier already says this; verification confirms it.
- **Why not lower (not a D):** the mechanistic and oral evidence is genuine, the ingredient is safe and well-characterized, and there is **moderate (B-grade) human-topical** support for *one narrow* benefit — **ellagic-acid brightening/pigmentation** — though even that rests on only ~2 small, combination-confounded RCTs.
- **Sub-grades by claim:**
  - Antioxidant / photo-protection mechanism — **B/C** (strong in-vitro + oral MED↑; not topical-human).
  - Collagen / MMP / "boosts collagen" — **C** (in-vitro + 3-D skin only; never shown in living human skin topically).
  - Pigmentation / brightening (ellagic acid) — **B–** (2 small topical RCTs, both combination-confounded).
  - Mitophagy / "mitochondrial longevity" — **D for the fruit** (belongs to isolated UA; the fruit/peel contains no UA).
  - Hydration / wrinkle topical — **D for whole extract** (only shown for isolated UA at 1%).

### What the verification confirmed (dossier is honest here)
- The studies cited are **real and accurately summarized** on the load-bearing points. Spot-checks matched: Henning 2019 (74 women, 3-arm, MED↑ p=0.011/0.038); Aslam 2006 (peel→fibroblast/procollagen/MMP-1↓, seed oil→keratinocyte); Pacheco-Palencia 2008 (the unusual two-dose split — 5–60 mg/L cytoprotection vs **500–10,000 mg/L** for ROS — is real and a genuinely damning caveat, since those antioxidant doses are not physiologically achievable in skin); Cordiano 2024 quote verbatim ("no human studies have demonstrated the anti-inflammaging effects of pomegranate"); penetration study (polyphenols retained, did not cross to receptor in 8 h).
- The dossier's central thesis — **don't borrow oral/UA data for a topical whole-extract product** — is correct and is the single most important guardrail.

### Main caveats (corrections made to the dossier this pass)
1. **Grantria 2024 was OVERSTATED on n and ingredient.** It enrolled **60 women (40+40), not 80** (80 were merely *screened*), and the extract is standardized to ellagic acid **+ punicic acid + punicalagin**, not EA alone. Open-label and manufacturer-funded (3/5 authors Zeus Hygia) — both confirmed. Wrinkle/roughness/radiance were **dermatologist atlas grading**, not instruments. Corrected in §4 Tier 3 and Sources.
2. **Urolithin A trials were OVERSTATED/imprecise.** The dossier said "1% UA" and gave "~4% wrinkle / ~14% erythema." Verification shows the trials tested **both 0.5% and 1%, and 0.5% FAILED** (no significant wrinkle effect) — a material omission that *strengthens* skepticism. The exact "~4%/~14%" figures could **not be independently confirmed** and are now flagged unverified. It is a **preprint, not peer-reviewed** (confirmed, not just "to verify"). Corrected in §4 Tier 4c and Sources.
3. **Zaid 2007 TIMP-1 direction was likely WRONG.** Dossier said POMx "preserved TIMP-1"; the abstract says POMx **decreased** TIMP-1 (along with the MMPs). Softened in §4 Tier 1 — do not claim TIMP-1 was raised/preserved.
4. **Henning 2019 "open-label" was imprecise.** It is a real randomized controlled trial; blinding was imperfect (capsule vs juice vs placebo), but MED is an objective endpoint. Reworded so we don't undersell it as merely "open-label."
5. **Unverified/secondary citations remain flagged, appropriately:** the *J Clin Med* 2022 oral study (n/outcomes still unverified — fetch blocked), PMC10781179 fibroblast "preliminary" paper, Pomanox® in-vitro (manufacturer-associated), and the **CIR safety assessment** (PDF still not machine-parsed — do not quote a specific "safe as used" conclusion or year in consumer copy until confirmed). Treat all four as not-yet-independently-verified.

### Claims that should be softened (carry into marketing/claims review)
- ❌ Anything implying topical pomegranate extract is "**clinically proven**" for wrinkles, firmness, or collagen in humans — there is no such topical RCT. Use appearance-level cosmetic language only.
- ❌ Any **mitophagy / "recycles mitochondria" / mitochondrial-longevity** claim on a pomegranate-extract product. That is urolithin A's story; the fruit contains none. (Already the dossier's #1 guardrail — verification reinforces it.)
- ❌ Any **MED / UV-resistance / photoprotection** efficacy claim (drug-adjacent, and the data is oral, not topical).
- ◐ **Brightening/even-tone** claims: keep soft and qualified ("helps visibly even tone"); never "as effective as hydroquinone" — the only supporting topical RCTs are small and combination-confounded.
- ◐ Do not cite the Grantria/UA **effect-size percentages** (−48% wrinkles, ~4%, ~14%) in copy — they are open-label/manufacturer-funded or unverified preprint figures.

**Net:** the dossier was already unusually rigorous and largely held up under adversarial check; its verdict (**conditional → supporting actor, not hero; source actual urolithin A separately if you want the longevity headline**) stands. The corrections above tighten three overstated specifics (Grantria n, UA dose/percentages, TIMP-1 direction) rather than overturning the conclusion.
