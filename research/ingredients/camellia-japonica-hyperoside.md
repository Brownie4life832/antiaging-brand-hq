# Ingredient: Camellia japonica Flower Extract + Hyperoside (INCI: Camellia Japonica Flower Extract; Hyperoside)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering (leaning ⚠️ supporting / conditional) · **Class:** Autophagy & mitochondrial-targeted actives (flavonoid mitophagy inducer)

> **One-line honest summary:** This is a botanical-flavonoid pairing with an unusually *on-thesis* longevity mechanism — restoring **mitophagy** (the recycling of damaged mitochondria, a canonical hallmark of aging) — and, for once, a published *in-vivo topical* study (in **mice**) showing visible photoaging benefit. But the headline 2025 anti-aging paper is **100% in-vitro**, the mouse study is mouse, and there is **no human topical RCT, no human skin data of any kind, and no clinical penetration data**. Hyperoside is also a large, hydrophilic **glycoside** that flavonoid skin-penetration literature says crosses the stratum corneum *worse* than its aglycone. Strong story, real and recent mechanistic science, but human topical proof is absent. Treat as a **science-forward supporting active**, not an evidence-backed hero.

---

## 1. Identity

This is a **two-part ingredient**: a whole botanical extract plus its named active marker compound. They are sold and studied together, and the relationship is genuine — hyperoside is a real, HPLC-confirmed major flavonoid of *Camellia japonica* flower, not a marketing afterthought.

### A. Camellia japonica flower extract (the botanical)
- **Common name:** Japanese camellia (tsubaki) flower extract. (Distinct from *Camellia sinensis* = tea, and from *Camellia japonica* **seed oil** / **leaf** extract — do not conflate these; different INCI, different chemistry.)
- **INCI:** **Camellia Japonica Flower Extract**
- **CAS:** 223748-13-8 (the shared CAS spans camellia japonica flower/leaf/seed extracts) [12]
- **Cosmetic functions (INCI listing):** emollient / skin-conditioning; vegetal origin. [12]
- **Botanical family:** Theaceae.
- **Key constituents (flower, 80% ethanol extract, HPLC):** rutin, **hyperoside**, isoquercitrin, chlorogenic acid, gallocatechin gallate, phillygenin; quercetin 3-O-galactoside (hyperoside) and quercetin 3-O-glucoside are among the highest-content flavonol glycosides. Also contains quercetin, quercitrin, kaempferol, gallic-acid derivatives, and (in the wider plant) palmitic and linoleic fatty acids. [8][11]

### B. Hyperoside (the active marker)
- **Common name:** Hyperoside (a.k.a. hyperin).
- **Chemical name:** **Quercetin-3-O-β-D-galactopyranoside** (i.e., the galactoside of quercetin). [2][8]
- **INCI:** Hyperoside (used as a purified cosmetic active / standardization marker).
- **CAS:** 482-36-0. [2]
- **Molecular formula / weight:** C₂₁H₂₀O₁₂ · **464.38 g/mol** — a *large, polar, glycosylated* molecule (this matters a great deal for topical penetration; see §5). [2]
- **Chemical class:** Flavonol glycoside (quercetin aglycone + galactose sugar).
- **Natural sources (broad):** *Hypericum* (St John's wort), *Crataegus* (hawthorn), Rosaceae, Ericaceae, plus *Camellia* spp. — and confirmed in *Camellia japonica* and *C. sasanqua* flowers. [2][11]

**Why the pairing makes sense (honest framing):** Unlike many "extract + isolated molecule" stories where the isolate isn't actually in the plant, hyperoside *is* a genuine, quantifiable constituent of *C. japonica* flower. The 2025 anti-aging study deliberately tested the **whole extract and the purified hyperoside side-by-side**, and they behaved similarly — supporting the claim that hyperoside is a real (though not necessarily the sole) driver of the extract's activity. [1]

## 2. Class & mechanism

The marketing-relevant headline mechanism is **induction of mitophagy** — selective autophagy of damaged mitochondria. This places the ingredient squarely on two canonical hallmarks of aging (mitochondrial dysfunction; loss of proteostasis/autophagy), which is why it slots into an "autophagy & mitochondrial-targeted actives" class for a longevity brand.

Mechanistic pillars (each tagged by the strongest evidence tier behind it):

1. **FUNDC1-mediated mitophagy induction (the marquee mechanism).** In the 2025 *Antioxidants* study, proteomics flagged **FUNDC1** (and BNIP3L) as the core regulatory node. CJF extract and hyperoside **upregulated FUNDC1**, enabling clearance of damaged mitochondria, restoring mitochondrial membrane potential and ATP, and lowering mitochondrial ROS in senescent human fibroblasts. *Tier: in-vitro only.* [1]
   - *Mechanistic context:* FUNDC1 is a well-characterized outer-mitochondrial-membrane mitophagy receptor with an LC3-interacting region; it is dephosphorylated (at Tyr18 by Src, and Ser13) under hypoxia/stress to trigger mitophagy, and is a recognized node in mitochondrial quality control and aging. The biology is real and well-supported in cardiac/neuro fields. [9][10]
2. **A second, independent mitophagy route — miR-361-5p/PI3K/Akt/mTOR.** A *separate* 2025 study found hyperoside **suppresses miR-361-5p**, relieving inhibition of the PI3K/Akt/mTOR axis and upregulating Beclin1/Parkin/PINK1/LC3 to drive mitophagy and reduce UVB-induced senescence. *Tier: in-vitro + in-vivo mouse.* [3]
   - *Note the tension:* mitophagy is induced here by *modulating* (not simply inhibiting) the Akt/mTOR pathway, whereas in cancer models hyperoside induces autophagy by *inhibiting* Akt/mTOR. The pathway story is context-dependent and not fully resolved — a caution against over-precise mechanistic copy. [2]
3. **Direct antioxidant / Nrf2-type enzyme induction.** Hyperoside scavenges ROS directly, reduces H₂O₂-induced lipid peroxidation, and *induces* endogenous antioxidant enzymes (SOD, catalase, GPx). The whole CJF flower extract does the same in keratinocytes. *Tier: in-vitro (well-replicated).* [2][6]
4. **MMP suppression / collagen preservation (photoaging & pollution).** CJF extract inhibits MMP-1 and preserves/increases collagen and the dermo-epidermal junction under pollutant stress; hyperoside reduces MMP-9 and restores dermal collagen under UVB. *Tier: in-vitro + ex-vivo + mouse.* [3][5][7]
5. **Anti-inflammatory.** Hyperoside inhibits TNF-α, IL-6, NO, iNOS in inflammation models. *Tier: in-vitro.* [2]

**Skin-relevant translation (honest):** The mitophagy mechanism is genuinely novel and on-thesis, and it is *demonstrated specifically in human skin cells* (not just borrowed from other tissues like spermidine or MitoQ). The antioxidant and MMP/collagen actions are well-replicated in skin cells and now in one mouse model. What is **not** shown: that a topical cosmetic dose reaches viable human dermis/epidermis in sufficient quantity to do any of this in a living person.

## 4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

> **The single most important honesty point:** The entire evidence base is **preclinical**. The flagship 2025 anti-aging paper is **in-vitro only (cultured cells)**. The best study — and a genuinely good one for a botanical — is a **topical mouse** photoaging study. There is **zero human data** for this ingredient in any format: no human topical RCT, no lower-tier human topical study, no human oral trial of hyperoside, and no published human skin-penetration data. Every "anti-aging / repairs DNA / restores mitochondria" claim a product could make today is extrapolated from cells in a dish and from mice.

### Tier 1 — In vitro (cell models) — the foundation

- **Gao H, Shi J, Li G, et al. 2025, *Antioxidants (Basel)* 14(8):968 (DOI 10.3390/antiox14080968) — THE flagship study.** Human skin fibroblasts (HSFs) + HaCaT keratinocytes in a **doxorubicin (0.2 µM)-induced senescence** model. CJF extract (tested 0.224–250 µg/mL; optimum cited ~15 µg/mL) and **purified hyperoside** (1.16–74.32 µg/mL; optimum ~9.3 µg/mL) both: ↓DNA damage (γH2AX foci, from ~315% down to ~149–215% of control), ↓SA-β-gal senescence, ↓mitochondrial ROS (mitoSOX 36.7%→25.3% CJF / 32.6% hyperoside), restored membrane potential (JC-1) and ATP (to ~130% CJF / ~102% hyperoside vs 73% in DOX), reduced S-phase arrest, ↑proliferation (EdU ~178% CJF / ~194% hyperoside, p<0.001), and **restored type III collagen and elastin (p<0.001)** and **Claudin-1** (barrier; p<0.05). Seahorse: hyperoside ↑basal respiration +37%, ATP-linked +36%, maximal +128%. Mechanism attributed to **FUNDC1-mediated mitophagy**. [1]
  - **Critical caveats (author-acknowledged):** *No human or animal work whatsoever.* FUNDC1's necessity was **not** proven by knockout/knock-in (the paper states "the core pivotal role of FUNDC1 has not been verified as necessary by gene editing (KO/KI)"). The DOX model is *induced* senescence, and the paper concedes "the mechanistic differences between the DOX model and natural aging have not been discussed" and that the pathway's behavior "in human aging skin … remain[s] to be verified." Causality between ATP restoration and DNA-repair/collagen enzymes lacked direct evidence. This is a mechanistically rich but entirely cell-dish study.
  - **⚠️ Conflict of interest (verified June 2026, not author-volunteered as a limitation):** Two authors are **employees of cosmetics/biotech firms** — Hongqi Gao and Guangtao Li at **Shanghai Forest Cabin Biological-Tech Co., Ltd.** (a Chinese skincare brand, "Forest Cabin"/林清轩) and Chanling Yuan at Guangzhou Ruby Biotechnology Co., Ltd. The study's government funding (NSFC etc.) is independent, but the flagship anti-aging paper is **partly industry-authored by a skincare company with a commercial interest in the result.** Weight the in-vitro efficacy claims accordingly.
- **Piao MJ, et al. 2011, *Int J Mol Sci* 12(4):2618–2630 (PMC3127137).** CJF ethanol extract in **HaCaT keratinocytes**: scavenged DPPH, intracellular ROS, superoxide and hydroxyl radicals (ESR confirmed), and **increased SOD, catalase, GPx** protein and activity. Identified quercetin, quercetin-3-O-glucoside, quercitrin, kaempferol as actives. *Limitation: keratinocyte antioxidant assay; not anti-aging endpoints; direct medium dosing.* [6]
- **Hwang HV, et al. 2018, *PLoS One* 13(1):e0190374 (DOI 10.1371/journal.pone.0190374) — an important NEGATIVE result.** Tested hyperoside (Q3G, up to 100 µM) and quercetin as **senolytics** in primary human coronary artery endothelial cells. **Hyperoside did NOT kill senescent cells at any concentration** ("Q3G did not affect cell death"); quercetin was non-selectively toxic. Authors speculate the sugar moiety prevents hyperoside from reaching β-gal-rich lysosomes. *Relevance: tempers any "senolytic" framing — hyperoside is at best senomorphic/senescence-preventive, not a senescent-cell killer; non-skin cells.* [4]
- **Broader hyperoside in-vitro pharmacology (context, non-skin):** Multiple studies show ROS scavenging, lipid-peroxidation protection (e.g., PC12 cells), SOD/GPx induction, anti-inflammatory cytokine suppression, and context-dependent autophagy (induces protective autophagy in some models; restrains excessive autophagy in others). [2]

**In-vitro verdict:** Consistent, mechanism-matched, and — unusually — generated *in human skin cells* for the headline anti-aging claim. But all of it is cells bathed in compound; nothing about topical delivery through skin, and the marquee study's central mechanism (FUNDC1) is unconfirmed by genetic knockout.

### Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo) — the strongest tier here

- **Yang D, et al. 2025, *Antioxidants (Basel)* 14(12):1401 (DOI 10.3390/antiox14121401) — the best study in the file, and the only in-vivo one.** HaCaT cells (1, 5 µM) **plus male ICR mice** (n=8/group, 6 groups), **TOPICAL** hyperoside to dorsal skin at 10/20/40 mg/mL (100 µL/application) against **UVB photoaging**. Results: high-dose visibly reduced wrinkles ("no obvious signs of photoaging"), reduced UVB-driven **epidermal thickening** (26.6 µm vs 53.4 µm model), **restored dermal collagen ~+51% vs UVB model** (which had ~−45% collagen), ↓MMP-9, ↓ROS, ↓p16/p21. Mechanism: **miR-361-5p ↓ → PI3K/Akt/mTOR → ↑mitophagy** (Beclin1/Parkin/PINK1/LC3). [3]
  - **Caveats (author-acknowledged):** acute UVB damage, not chronic aging; **mouse skin differs structurally from human**; the direct HY→miR-361-5p binding was **not** experimentally validated; **dermal absorption efficiency was not assessed**; long-term safety not studied. Still: this is a real *topical, in-vivo, dose-ranging* photoaging study with histology — substantially stronger than the in-vitro-only data behind many trendy actives (cf. spermidine, MitoQ, which lack any in-vivo topical aging study).
- **CJF extract pollution / DEJ studies (ex-vivo human skin + cells):**
  - **2016, *Journal of Dermatological Science* (S0923-1811(16)30596-5) abstract.** CJF extract prevented pollutant-induced (heavy metals + hydrocarbons) **detachment of the dermo-epidermal junction** and slightly increased collagen density vs pollutant-treated samples in skin models. *Conference/abstract-level; limited methods.* [5]
  - **2019, *BMC Complementary Medicine and Therapies* 19:30 (DOI 10.1186/s12906-018-2405-4).** CJF extract vs urban air pollutants in human fibroblasts/skin: ↓pollutant-induced MDA (lipid peroxidation), ↓**MMP-1**, ↑collagen synthesis, ↓ROS, ↓AhR transactivation and CYP1A1, ↓pyknotic nuclei and DEJ detachment. *Cell + ex-vivo; no human in-vivo testing; hyperoside not isolated as the sole active here.* [7]

**Animal verdict:** One solid topical mouse photoaging study (Yang 2025) plus supportive ex-vivo human-skin-model anti-pollution data. This is a *better* preclinical in-vivo position than most "longevity" topicals — but it is still mouse + ex-vivo, not living humans, and absorption was explicitly not measured.

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL

- **None for skin.** No human oral trial of hyperoside or CJF flower extract for skin endpoints was located.
- **Hyperoside human clinical trials generally: none identified.** A 2025 *Frontiers in Pharmacology* review states hyperoside research is "primarily confined to in vitro and animal studies" and that "clinical trials are necessary to assess its safety and effectiveness in human subjects." Oral pharmacokinetics exist only in **rats** (half-life ~4 h; absolute bioavailability ~26%). [2]

**Oral verdict:** No human data in any indication. This is unusual — many "longevity molecules" (spermidine, urolithin A) at least have human *oral* trials to borrow narrative from; hyperoside does not.

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters for a skincare brand)

- **Peer-reviewed RCT: none.** (Searched June 2026.)
- **Any human topical study (lower tier): none located.** No split-face, no open-label clinical, no instrumental (cutometer/TEWL) human study of CJF flower extract + hyperoside was found.
- **Human skin-penetration / PK: none published.** No data showing topical hyperoside reaches viable human epidermis/dermis (see §5).

**Topical verdict: entirely unproven in humans.** Every robust finding is in cells or mice. A "Camellia + hyperoside mitophagy serum" today rests *exclusively* on preclinical extrapolation. This is the decisive evidence gap and must govern all copy.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

This is the **biggest scientific liability**, and it is structural, not just "understudied."

- **Hyperoside is a large, hydrophilic glycoside (MW ~464, C₂₁H₂₀O₁₂).** Flavonoid skin-delivery research is consistent that **glycosides penetrate the stratum corneum *worse* than their aglycones**: the sugar moiety raises molecular weight and hydrophilicity, both of which impair crossing the lipophilic SC barrier. In the definitive head-to-head (Chuang et al., *Nutrients* 2017), "absorption was generally greater for topically applied aglycones than glycosides," and SC removal markedly increased glycoside deposition — i.e., **the stratum corneum is the gatekeeper that holds glycosides like hyperoside back.** [13]
- **Implication:** Of the two parts of this ingredient, the *aglycone-type* and lipophilic components of the whole CJF extract (quercetin, kaempferol, fatty acids) may actually penetrate *better* than the named star molecule hyperoside. The branding hero (hyperoside) is the harder-to-deliver part.
- **No ingredient-specific human penetration data.** Neither the flagship in-vitro study nor the mouse study measured dermal absorption (Yang 2025 explicitly lists "dermal absorption efficiency was not assessed" as a limitation). [1][3] So even the mouse efficacy doesn't tell us delivered dose.
- **Delivery-system dependence (inference, not from [13]):** The head-to-head paper [13] itself does *not* conclude that special carriers are required — its own recommendation is to favor the more-permeable *aglycones* (it nominates naringenin/kaempferol as best topical candidates). The "use nanostructured lipid carriers / liposomes / hydrogels for quercetin-type flavonoids" point comes from the *broader* flavonoid-delivery literature, not from [13]. Either way, the practical implication is the same: a plain aqueous serum of the hydrophilic glycoside hyperoside is a poor delivery choice, and an encapsulated form (or reliance on the extract's more-permeable minor constituents) is the more defensible path. [13]
- **Practical read:** Penetration of the headline molecule is **plausibly poor without an enabling delivery system**. The whole-extract approach (relying on the more permeable minor constituents) or an encapsulated hyperoside is the more defensible formulation path — but neither has human data.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:** **No human-validated topical concentration exists.** In-vitro actives were CJF ~15 µg/mL and hyperoside ~9 µg/mL (culture medium, not formulation guidance); the mouse used 10–40 mg/mL topical hyperoside (very high, on shaved mouse skin). Cosmetic products typically use CJF flower extract at low extract percentages and hyperoside at fractional-percent marker levels. **Formulate conservatively; do not imply a validated dose-response.** [1][3]
- **pH:** Quercetin-type flavonol glycosides are most stable at mildly **acidic pH (~4–6)**; alkaline pH accelerates oxidative degradation and browning of flavonoids. Target a skin-friendly acidic finish. (General flavonoid-stability principle; confirm on the specific raw material's COA.)
- **Stability:** Flavonoids are **oxidation- and light-sensitive** and can **discolor (yellow/brown)** over shelf life; the whole flower extract carries natural color/odor. Use antioxidants (it is itself one), chelators, low pH, and **opaque/airless packaging**; run real-time + accelerated stability and challenge testing. Plant-extract **batch-to-batch variability** in hyperoside content is a real concern — require a **standardized, HPLC-quantified** extract. [8][11]
- **Pairings (conceptual):** Niacinamide (barrier/NAD-renewal story), other autophagy/longevity actives (spermidine, urolithin A — shared "cellular housekeeping" narrative), ceramides/barrier lipids (supports the brand's barrier-repair thesis), vitamin C/E antioxidant network, and an SPF system (the photoaging data is UVB-driven — pairing with sun protection is both honest and synergistic). An **encapsulated/lipid-carrier** delivery form is arguably necessary to make the hero molecule meaningful (see §5).
- **Conflicts:** As a polyphenol, possible interactions with high metal-ion content and strong oxidizers; co-formulating with unstable, easily-oxidized actives raises the joint stability burden. Avoid alkaline systems.
- **Sensory:** Botanical extract may impart slight color/scent; at low marker levels the purified hyperoside is unlikely to dominate sensory. A premium clear serum risks visible yellowing — a real aesthetic consideration.

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation / sensitization (topical):** No specific human topical irritation or sensitization dataset for this pairing was located. *C. japonica* flower extract is an established cosmetic INCI (emollient/skin-conditioning) with a long use history (incl. prestige brands), suggesting low concern at cosmetic levels, but **no published HRIPT/patch-test data for the flower-extract-plus-hyperoside combination** were found — generate our own. The mouse study reported no overt skin toxicity at up to 40 mg/mL topical but did **not** do formal long-term safety. [3][12]
- **Quercetin-family caveat:** Quercetin aglycone showed *non-selective cytotoxicity* in the senolytic study [4]; this argues for the glycoside (gentler) and for conservative use levels.
- **Sun sensitivity:** No photosensitization flag; mechanistically the ingredient is *photo-protective* (anti-UVB in mice). Not a substitute for SPF. [3]
- **Pregnancy / breastfeeding:** No topical pregnancy data. For **oral hyperoside**, rat studies showed **reduced fetal growth at 1,000 mg/kg** (high dose) with minimal maternal effects [2] — a caution-by-default signal even though topical exposure would be far lower. Use conservative copy ("if pregnant or nursing, consult your doctor").
- **Systemic toxicology (oral, supportive context):** Hyperoside is generally regarded as low-toxicity — oral **LD50 in mice >5,000 mg/kg**, no genotoxicity (Ames, chromosomal aberration). But **long-term high oral dosing can cause reversible kidney effects**; kidneys are the main accumulation/excretion site. None of this is directly relevant to a low-dose topical, but it argues against any "take it internally too" crossover messaging. [2]
- **Sourcing / allergen:** Botanical extract — disclose source; standardize to limit variability. No common contact-allergen flags identified, but botanical polyphenol extracts can occasionally sensitize; patch-test the finished formula.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Cosmetic ingredient status:** *Camellia Japonica Flower Extract* is an established INCI (emollient/skin-conditioning; CAS 223748-13-8); hyperoside is used as a cosmetic active/marker. Confirm legality and any concentration limits per target market before formulating. [12]
- **The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame):** "Repairs DNA," "restores/repairs mitochondria," "induces autophagy/mitophagy in your skin," "reverses senescence," "anti-aging at the cellular level," and "boosts collagen by X%" all describe **structure/function effects** and drift firmly into **drug** territory. Importing the *in-vitro* "repairs DNA damage" and *mouse* "restores collagen" findings into human topical claims is the central legal trap here — and it would be doubly misleading because there is **no human data at all**.
- ✅ **Safer cosmetic language:** "inspired by mitophagy / cellular-renewal longevity science," "a flavonoid antioxidant naturally found in camellia flowers," "helps defend the look of skin against oxidative and environmental stress," "supports the appearance of firmer, more resilient skin," "helps improve the look of skin elasticity." Even these should be hedged given the absence of human data.
- ⚠️ **Risky / needs substantiation or legal review:** "repairs DNA," "restores your mitochondria," "activates autophagy in your skin," "reverses skin-cell aging," "clinically proven," "increases collagen by 51%" (that figure is a *mouse dermal collagen* result, not a human claim and not a finished-formula result).
- **Substantiation reality:** To make even modest topical performance claims we would need **our own finished-formula human testing**. The literature — strong as the mechanism is — cannot substantiate a human topical claim for this pairing. See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Forms / suppliers:** *Camellia japonica* flower extract is widely available as a cosmetic botanical (multiple suppliers; vegetal origin). Purified hyperoside is available as a fine chemical / phytochemical reference standard (e.g., Sigma-Aldrich/Merck, Santa Cruz, Cayman, and Chinese phytochemical manufacturers riding the broader hyperoside/quercetin supplement interest). [2][12]
- **Cost tier:** **Low–mid** for the whole flower extract; **mid–higher** for standardized-hyperoside extracts and for any **encapsulated/delivery-enhanced** form (which the penetration problem arguably requires). Cheaper and more commodity-like than patented single-source actives such as MitoQ — and therefore *more* compatible with the brand's "buy commodity bases, invest in the brand" principle.
- **Standardization risk:** Plant hyperoside content varies by cultivar, flower part, harvest, and extraction solvent (80% ethanol maximizes hyperoside/isoquercitrin). **Require a COA with HPLC-quantified hyperoside.** [8][11]
- **Sustainability/story:** A real botanical with East-Asian heritage (tsubaki) — a usable narrative asset if the brand wants a botanical angle, without being single-source/IP-locked.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

- **"Clinically proven mitophagy skincare."** There is **no clinical** (human) study at all. The strongest data is a topical *mouse* study. Any "clinically proven" claim would be false.
- **"Repairs DNA damage."** This is an *in-vitro* γH2AX result in a doxorubicin model [1] — a chemotherapy-induced damage assay in a dish, not human skin, and not "DNA repair" in the regulatory sense a consumer would understand.
- **"Restores your mitochondria / boosts cellular energy."** ATP and respiration improvements are real *in cultured cells* [1]; nothing shows this happens in living human skin after topical use.
- **The 51% collagen figure.** It's a **mouse dermal collagen** restoration vs a UVB-damaged mouse model [3] — not a human result and not a finished-product result. Using it on-pack would be misleading.
- **"Senolytic" framing.** Directly contradicted: hyperoside **failed** as a senolytic in human cells (didn't kill senescent cells) [4]. It is at most *senescence-preventive/senomorphic*. Don't call it a senolytic.
- **The delivery blind spot.** Marketing will tout hyperoside as the hero, but it's the **least skin-penetrant** part of the system (large hydrophilic glycoside) and no study measured delivery. [13]
- **What's genuinely better-than-average here (give credit, with one asterisk):** Unlike many longevity-topical trend ingredients, this one has (a) a mechanism demonstrated *in human skin cells specifically*, and (b) an *in-vivo topical* efficacy study with histology. That is a stronger preclinical package than spermidine or MitoQ have for skin — the honest gap is "no humans yet," not "no relevant biology." **Asterisk:** the flagship in-vitro paper is partly authored by a skincare company (see §4 COI), so independent replication would strengthen confidence in the headline anti-aging findings.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ **SUPPORTING (conditional) — strong, on-thesis mechanism and unusually good *preclinical* data, but zero human evidence and a real penetration problem.**

**One-line:** A credible, recent, *skin-specific* mitophagy story — arguably the best-fitting "autophagy/mitochondrial longevity" botanical for a skin-longevity brand on *mechanism* and *preclinical* grounds — but with **no human topical data**, a hero molecule that **penetrates skin poorly**, and therefore a hard ceiling on what we can honestly claim. Use as a science-forward supporting active with strictly mechanism-framed, human-data-honest copy; do not make it a proof-backed hero.

**Why not hero (today):** It fails the brand's evidence bar for a hero (no human study of any kind), and its flagship benefit rests on a molecule with a documented skin-delivery disadvantage. Building hero claims on in-vitro + mouse data would force exactly the drug-adjacent, unsubstantiated language CLAUDE.md tells us to avoid.

**Why not skip:** The mechanism (mitophagy / FUNDC1 / mitochondrial quality control) is *precisely* the kind of geroscience a skin-longevity brand is built to talk about, and — crucially — the supporting data was generated **in human skin cells and in vivo topically**, which is more than most fashionable longevity topicals can say. As an *education/differentiation and supporting* ingredient (especially paired with barrier lipids, niacinamide, and SPF), with honest copy, it is a genuine asset.

**Conditions to use it well:** (1) Source a **standardized, HPLC-quantified** extract; (2) seriously consider an **encapsulated/lipid-carrier** delivery form for the hyperoside, given the penetration data; (3) keep all copy in cosmetic/"inspired-by-science" register — **no** "repairs DNA / restores mitochondria / clinically proven / +51% collagen"; (4) if we want real performance claims, do **our own finished-formula human testing** (instrumental + ideally split-face).

**Longevity-relevance note:** On the "skin-longevity" thesis specifically, this scores high on *narrative fit* (mitophagy is front-line geroscience) and, unusually, *medium* on *preclinical evidence* (human-skin-cell mechanism + in-vivo topical efficacy) — but *zero* on human proof. It is a better-evidenced longevity-mechanism botanical than spermidine or MitoQ are for topical skin, while still being honestly pre-clinical. A strong candidate for the "longevity science" supporting cast, not the headline act.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Verified:** 2026-06-19 (independent re-check of primary sources via PMC/PLoS/journal pages).

### Overall grade: **C+ (weak/indirect for topical human use — but with an unusually strong *preclinical* sub-grade)**

On the brand's A–D rubric (A = strong human-topical RCT; B = moderate human-topical; C = weak/indirect; D = unproven topically/hype), this lands at **C+**. There is **zero human evidence of any kind** (no topical RCT, no lower-tier human topical study, no human PK, no human oral trial), which caps it below B regardless of mechanism quality. It earns the "+" over a bare C — and clears D — because, unlike most trendy "longevity topicals," it has (a) a mechanism shown *in human skin cells specifically* and (b) a real *in-vivo topical, dose-ranging, histology-backed* mouse photoaging study. The biology is real and on-thesis; the human topical proof is absent.

### What I checked and what held up

- **Citations are real and say what's claimed.** All four load-bearing studies were verified against primary sources:
  - **[1] Gao et al., *Antioxidants* 2025;14(8):968** — real; DOI/PMC (PMC12383142) correct; in-vitro only (HSF + HaCaT, doxorubicin-senescence); FUNDC1 mitophagy mechanism; FUNDC1 necessity *not* proven by KO/KI; DOX-vs-aging gap acknowledged. Dossier's characterization is accurate.
  - **[3] Yang et al., *Antioxidants* 2025;14(12):1401** — real; DOI/PMC (PMC12729947) correct. **Verified specifics:** TOPICAL to dorsal skin at 10/20/40 mg/mL (100 µL); male ICR mice, n=8/group, 6 groups; **51.31%** dermal collagen increase vs UVB model (dossier's "~+51%" is exact); authors explicitly state dermal absorption efficiency was NOT assessed and the HY→miR-361-5p direct binding was NOT experimentally confirmed. Dossier accurate.
  - **[4] Hwang et al., *PLoS One* 2018;13(1):e0190374** — real; DOI/PMC correct. Genuinely a NEGATIVE senolytic result for hyperoside/Q3G in human endothelial cells. Dossier correctly uses it to defeat any "senolytic" framing.
  - **[13] Chuang et al., *Nutrients* 2017;9(12):1304** — real; correctly supports the glycoside-penetrates-worse thesis and the "SC is the gatekeeper" point.
- **Evidence-tier honesty is excellent.** The dossier already, and correctly, labels the entire base as preclinical: flagship = in-vitro only; best study = topical *mouse*; human topical = none. No tier inflation found. The §10 "hype vs. evidence" section pre-empts the obvious overclaims ("clinically proven," "repairs DNA," "+51% collagen," "senolytic") accurately.

### Issues found and fixed (this pass)

1. **Undisclosed industry conflict on the flagship study [1].** Two authors are employees of cosmetics/biotech companies — **Shanghai Forest Cabin Biological-Tech Co., Ltd.** (a Chinese skincare brand) and Guangzhou Ruby Biotechnology Co., Ltd. The original dossier did not mention this. The headline anti-aging paper is therefore **partly industry-authored by a party with a commercial stake in the result.** (Funding itself is Chinese government grants — independent.) Added COI flags in §4, §10, and the source list.
2. **Mild miscitation in §5.** The claim that the literature "repeatedly concludes nanostructured lipid carriers/liposomes/hydrogels are *needed*" was attributed to [13]. [13] does **not** say that — its own recommendation is to choose permeable aglycones (naringenin/kaempferol). Reworded to attribute the delivery-vehicle point to the broader flavonoid literature, not [13]. (The practical conclusion is unchanged.)

### Main caveats (unchanged, all confirmed valid)

- **No human data whatsoever** — this is the decisive gap and correctly governs all copy.
- **Hero molecule penetrates skin poorly** — hyperoside is a large hydrophilic glycoside; the *named star* is the *least* skin-permeable part of the system. Confirmed.
- **Best efficacy study is mouse + topical, with absorption explicitly unmeasured** — so even the mouse efficacy doesn't establish a delivered dose, and mouse skin ≠ human skin.
- **Flagship efficacy data is partly industry-authored** (new) — wants independent replication.
- **"Senolytic" is contradicted by [4]** — it is at most senomorphic/senescence-preventive.

### Claims to keep softened / never make

- Do **not** say "clinically proven" anything (no clinical study exists).
- Do **not** say "repairs DNA," "restores/repairs mitochondria," "activates autophagy/mitophagy in your skin," "reverses senescence," or "+51% collagen" (mouse dermal figure, not human, not finished-product).
- Do **not** call it a "senolytic."
- Mechanism-/"inspired-by-science"-framed cosmetic language only, hedged for the absence of human data — exactly as §8 already prescribes.
