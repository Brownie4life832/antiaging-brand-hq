# Ingredient: Retinyl Ascorbate (INCI: Retinyl Ascorbate)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Class:** Retinoids & retinoid alternatives · **Status:** ⬜ considering (leaning ❌ skip as a standalone hero — see verdict)

> **One-line honest summary:** Retinyl ascorbate (RA-AsA) is a **vitamin A–vitamin C "co-drug"** — a single ester molecule designed so that, once it penetrates skin, esterases cleave it to release **both retinoic/retinyl acid and L-ascorbic acid** at the same site. The concept is elegant and the *pharmaceutics* are genuinely well-characterised by **one academic group (Abdulmajed & Heard, Cardiff University, 2004–2006)** in **ex-vivo human/porcine skin**: it is more lipophilic (log P ~2.2), markedly **more stable** than free retinol or free ascorbic acid, penetrates well, and delivers more of both vitamins to the viable epidermis than the corresponding palmitate esters. **BUT there is essentially zero stand-alone topical human efficacy evidence** — no published RCT, no clinical wrinkle/collagen/pigment trial of retinyl ascorbate on people. Every *benefit* claim is **borrowed** from the (strong) retinoid literature and the (moderate) topical vitamin C literature, on the *assumption* that in-skin hydrolysis liberates active forms. It is a **niche, custom-synthesised** ingredient (notable user: Paula's Choice) with no clean public CAS/PubChem record. Real, clever, mechanistically defensible — but evidentially a **delivery hypothesis, not a proven anti-aging active in humans.**

## 1. Identity

- **Common / trade names:** Retinyl ascorbate; **RA-AsA** (the abbreviation used throughout the primary literature); vitamin A–vitamin C ester / "co-drug"; sometimes marketed simply as a "vitamin A + C" conjugate.
- **INCI:** **Retinyl Ascorbate**. (Listed as an INCI name and indexed by ingredient databases / Paula's Choice ingredient dictionary, but with sparse regulatory documentation — see §8.) **[verified — name in use]; [unverified — no full INCI monograph located]**
- **Chemistry / structure:** An **ester (co-drug)** joining the **retinyl/retinoyl moiety of vitamin A** to a **hydroxyl of L-ascorbic acid (vitamin C)**.
  - In the **Abdulmajed & Heard synthesis** [1], the ester is formed from **all-*trans*-retinyl chloride + L-ascorbic acid** in ethanol at low temperature — i.e. the **retinoic-acid carboxyl** condenses with an **ascorbate hydroxyl** (retinoid is the acid, ascorbate the alcohol). [1]
  - In the later **Korean (The Face Shop) synthesis patent KR101002795B1** [9], the bond is placed specifically at the **C-2 hydroxyl of L-ascorbic acid** (leaving the reactive C-3 hydroxyl free), via a protected (5,6-isopropylidene-ascorbate) route with retinyl chloride. [9]
- **CAS / PubChem:** **No clean public record.** A direct PubChem compound search for "retinyl ascorbate" returns **zero compound records** (verified via NCBI E-utilities, 2026-06-19), and no consensus CAS number is published in the cosmetic INCI databases. This is consistent with RA-AsA being a **custom/specialty-synthesised molecule** rather than a standard catalogue chemical. **[verified that no clean CID/CAS exists in the standard databases under this name].** Treat any single quoted CAS with suspicion until confirmed with a supplier CoA.
- **Approximate molecular size:** retinoid (~C20) + ascorbate (~C6) ester ⇒ roughly **C26-class, MW ~440–460 g/mol** depending on exact bond/water of esterification. **[unverified — estimate; confirm against supplier spec].**
- **Key relatives / "siblings" to keep straight:**
  - The **two parent vitamins** it is built from: **retinol/retinoic acid** (see [`retinoids.md`](retinoids.md)) and **L-ascorbic acid + its derivatives** (see [`vitamin-c.md`](vitamin-c.md)).
  - Other **retinyl esters** it is conceptually grouped with: **retinyl palmitate** [`retinyl-palmitate.md`](retinyl-palmitate.md), **retinyl propionate** [`retinyl-propionate.md`](retinyl-propionate.md), **retinyl acetate** [`retinyl-acetate.md`](retinyl-acetate.md), **retinyl linoleate** [`retinyl-linoleate.md`](retinyl-linoleate.md), **retinyl retinoate** [`retinyl-retinoate.md`](retinyl-retinoate.md).
  - Other **vitamin-C esters** in the same "lipophilic conjugate" logic: ascorbyl palmitate (AsA-Pal), tetrahexyldecyl ascorbate (THD ascorbate), ascorbyl tetraisopalmitate.
  - **Do not confuse** with **"ascorbyl retinoate"** wording, or with **potassium ascorbyl tocopheryl phosphate (EPC)** [`ascorbyl-tocopheryl-phosphate.md`](ascorbyl-tocopheryl-phosphate.md), which is a *different* multi-vitamin conjugate.

## 2. Class & mechanism

**The co-drug concept (the whole point of this molecule).** A "co-drug" (or "mutual prodrug") chemically links **two actives into one molecule** so they (a) travel together, (b) gain shared physicochemical properties (here: lipophilicity + stability), and (c) are **released together at the target site** by enzymatic cleavage. Retinyl ascorbate is a textbook co-drug: it fuses **vitamin A** (a lipophilic, receptor-driven cell-renewal/collagen actor) with **vitamin C** (a hydrophilic antioxidant and collagen-synthesis cofactor) — two ingredients that are individually **unstable, hard to co-formulate, and have opposite solubility**. [1, 2]

**Why anyone bothered — the formulation problem it solves:**
- **Free L-ascorbic acid** is hydrophilic, **poorly skin-penetrating** unless at very low pH, and **highly unstable** (oxidises rapidly in water/light/air).
- **Free retinol** is lipophilic but **highly unstable** (oxidation, isomerisation, photodegradation).
- Co-formulating them is awkward (one wants low pH/anhydrous; both degrade). Esterifying the retinoid's acid to an ascorbate hydroxyl produces a molecule that is **more lipophilic (log P ~2.2)** — good for partitioning into the stratum-corneum lipids — and **much more stable than either parent**, comparable to the established palmitate esters. [1]

**Mechanism, step by step (and where the honesty lives):**
1. **Penetration.** The lipophilic ester partitions into the stratum-corneum lipid domains and penetrates into the viable epidermis (demonstrated ex vivo — §4). [1]
2. **Enzymatic hydrolysis.** **Skin/tissue esterases** (and, in the model, porcine liver esterase) cleave the ester bond, **liberating free retinoid + free L-ascorbic acid** in situ. [3]
3. **Then — and only then — the parent vitamins act by their own mechanisms:**
   - **Retinoid arm:** the liberated vitamin-A species must ultimately become **all-*trans* retinoic acid (RA)** to bind nuclear **RAR/RXR** receptors → ↑type-I procollagen, ↓matrix metalloproteinases (MMP-1/-3/-9 via AP-1 suppression), normalised keratinocyte turnover, ↑dermal glycosaminoglycans. *(Note an important ambiguity below.)* See [`retinoids.md`](retinoids.md) §2.
   - **Ascorbate arm:** liberated L-ascorbic acid acts as a **cofactor for prolyl/lysyl hydroxylases** (essential for stable collagen triple-helix formation), a **direct ROS scavenger**, a **regenerator of oxidised vitamin E**, and a **tyrosinase-pathway modulator** (brightening). See [`vitamin-c.md`](vitamin-c.md) §2.

**A crucial mechanistic ambiguity (be honest):** the literature is inconsistent about which vitamin-A species is released. Abdulmajed & Heard describe the synthesis from **retinyl chloride / "retinoic acid"** and repeatedly say hydrolysis yields **"retinoic acid" (RA) + ascorbic acid** [1, 3] — which, if literally true, would make RA-AsA a **retinoic-acid prodrug** (much more potent, and a regulatory drug concern, see §8). But the name "retin**yl** ascorbate" and the cosmetic framing imply a **retin*ol*/retinyl** release (a weak retinyl ester, cosmetic-legal). **This distinction is load-bearing** and is *not* cleanly resolved in the public sources. **Do not claim it "delivers retinoic acid"** in copy — that crosses into drug territory and is not reliably established. [1, 3]

**Net:** the mechanism is **entirely "prodrug-by-conversion"** — RA-AsA has **no intrinsic activity** of its own; everything depends on (a) penetrating, then (b) being hydrolysed into its two parent vitamins, which then do the work each is known for. Plausible and well-reasoned — but the *in-skin hydrolysis-to-active and downstream efficacy* has not been demonstrated **clinically in humans**.

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

| Hallmark | Plausibility / evidence for *retinyl ascorbate specifically* |
|---|---|
| **Oxidative stress** | The **strongest conceptual fit** — explicitly proposed as a "topical antioxidant for reducing UV-induced free-radical generation." [3] Vitamin C is a bona-fide ROS scavenger and the co-drug carries an ascorbate payload. But **molecule-specific** antioxidant efficacy in human skin is **not demonstrated**; it is borrowed from the ascorbate literature. **Moderate plausibility, weak direct proof.** |
| **Collagen / ECM loss** | Doubly plausible *if hydrolysed*: retinoid arm ↑procollagen/↓MMPs; ascorbate arm is a collagen-synthesis cofactor. **No retinyl-ascorbate-specific human collagen data.** Borrowed from both parents. **Weak (borrowed).** |
| **Photoaging (wrinkles, roughness)** | The aspirational class endpoint (both vitamins have anti-photoaging stories). **No stand-alone RA-AsA wrinkle/photoaging RCT exists.** Borrowed. **Weak.** |
| **Pigmentation / tone** | Vitamin C is a tyrosinase modulator; retinoids accelerate pigment turnover. Plausible brightening, **not measured for RA-AsA.** Paula's Choice lists "anti-blemish / pore-minimiser" framing. Borrowed. **Weak.** |
| **Barrier decline / hydration** | Indirect; lipophilic ester sits in SC lipids. No barrier-repair or TEWL data for RA-AsA. **Very weak.** |
| **Glycation/AGEs · cellular senescence · mitochondrial decline · inflammaging · microbiome** | **No evidence specific to retinyl ascorbate.** (Vitamin C has some anti-glycation/antioxidant framing; retinoids touch inflammaging via AP-1 — but none of this is shown for the co-drug.) **Do not claim.** |

**Net:** RA-AsA's hallmark coverage is **entirely inherited from its two parent vitamins, contingent on in-skin hydrolysis.** Its single best-fit, best-reasoned hallmark is **oxidative stress / environmental defense** (the framing its inventors used), but even that lacks molecule-specific human proof. There is **no hallmark for which retinyl ascorbate has its own stand-alone topical human efficacy data.**

## 4. Evidence review by tier (core of the dossier)

> **Overarching caveat — read before the tiers.** The retinyl-ascorbate evidence base is **unusually lopsided**: it has a **respectable, peer-reviewed PHARMACEUTICS package** (synthesis, log P, stability, ex-vivo human-skin penetration, tissue/keratin binding, hydrolysis kinetics) from **a single academic group at Cardiff University (K. Abdulmajed, C.M. Heard, and colleagues), 2004–2006** — and **almost nothing else.** There is **NO published stand-alone topical human RCT** (or even an uncontrolled human clinical study located) of retinyl ascorbate for skin aging, pigmentation, or photoprotection. Its efficacy story is **100% borrowed** from the retinoid and vitamin-C literatures, on the *assumption* that ex-vivo penetration + esterase hydrolysis translate to clinical benefit in living skin. The ingredient is also **commercially niche** — its most visible adopter is Paula's Choice, whose "Best/proven" rating leans on the *parent-vitamin* evidence and the Cardiff pharmaceutics, not on a RA-AsA clinical trial. [4, 8]

### Tier 1 — In vitro / pharmaceutics & ex-vivo human skin  *(this IS retinyl ascorbate's real, molecule-specific evidence)*

**(a) Synthesis, physicochemistry, penetration & permeation — the foundational paper:**
- **Abdulmajed K, Heard CM. "Topical delivery of retinyl ascorbate co-drug. 1. Synthesis, penetration into and permeation across human skin." *Int J Pharm.* 2004;280(1-2):113–124. doi:10.1016/j.ijpharm.2004.05.008. PMID 15265552.** [1]
  - **What was done:** Novel low-temperature synthesis of RA-AsA from all-*trans*-retinyl chloride + L-ascorbic acid; measured **log P, solubility, degradation constant**; measured **flux and permeability coefficient across heat-separated human skin membrane**; assessed skin penetration by **tape-stripping**.
  - **Key results (verified against the PubMed record):** RA-AsA showed **favourable log P = 2.2**; **stability much greater than free retinol (RA) and free ascorbic acid (AsA)**, and **similar to retinyl palmitate (Rol-Pal) and ascorbyl palmitate (AsA-Pal)** — i.e. on par with established commercial esters. RA-AsA had **higher skin retention** than the two comparator esters, and **delivered more retinoid and more ascorbate to the viable epidermis** than retinol-from-palmitate / ascorbic-acid-from-ascorbyl-palmitate, respectively. Authors conclude **potential value for treating UV-induced skin damage.** [1]
  - **Limitations:** ex-vivo human skin (not living skin → no biological response measured); single lab; "delivered more of both vitamins" is a *delivery* finding, **not** an efficacy finding; the retinoid-species-released ambiguity (RA vs retinol) is unresolved (§2).

**(b) Tissue and keratin binding — where the molecule ends up:**
- **Abdulmajed K, Heard CM, McGuigan C, Pugh WJ. "Topical delivery of retinyl ascorbate co-drug. 2. Comparative skin tissue and keratin binding studies." *Skin Pharmacol Physiol.* 2004;17(6):274–282. doi:10.1159/000081112. PMID 15528957.** [2]
  - **What was done:** Compared binding of RA-AsA vs the individual vitamins to **human callus, pig skin, and bovine horn keratin**, native vs delipidised.
  - **Key results:** Polar compounds (ascorbate) bound delipidised tissue strongly via **dipolar/H-bonding**; **human tissue bound more than pig/bovine**; in native (lipid-containing) tissue, **very lipophilic materials' binding increased with lipid content** while polar compounds' binding fell (lipids competed with protein interaction). Informs how the co-drug partitions/retains in skin. [2]
  - **Limitations:** binding/partitioning study, not efficacy; ex-vivo/animal keratin models.

**(c) Degradation, chemical & enzymatic hydrolysis — does it actually release the actives?:**
- **Abdulmajed K, McGuigan C, Heard CM. "Topical delivery of retinyl ascorbate co-drug. 3. In vitro degradation studies." *Skin Pharmacol Physiol.* 2006;19(5):248–258. doi:10.1159/000093980. PMID 16778457.** [3]
  - **What was done:** Studied **chemical and enzymatic hydrolysis** of RA-AsA; ester-hydrolysis activity in **crude cellular extracts from fresh porcine ear skin**; enzymatic hydrolysis with **porcine liver esterase**.
  - **Key results:** RA-AsA is **hydrolysed by esterases**, releasing the parent acids; it also undergoes **hydrolytic/oxidative reduction in which the ascorbate moiety disintegrates** in the presence of free radicals (an *expected* behaviour for a sacrificial antioxidant, but also a stability caveat). **Fresh skin had ~5× more soluble protein/active esterase than frozen.** Adding antioxidant **decelerated hydrolysis.** [3]
  - **Limitations:** porcine skin/liver esterase model (not human in vivo); shows hydrolysis *can* occur, not that it produces *clinically meaningful* active levels in living human skin; confirms the ascorbate arm is vulnerable to oxidative breakdown.

**(d) Supporting mechanistic context (parent-vitamin, NOT RA-AsA-specific):**
- Boehnlein et al. (1994) — **retinyl esters are hydrolysed to retinol by skin esterases** during percutaneous absorption (the palmitate model; ~44% of absorbed ester hydrolysed). Supports step 2 of the mechanism by analogy. PMID 7971717. [10]
- Pinnell/Phillips-lineage work — **topical L-ascorbic acid stimulates collagen biosynthesis** (hydroxylase cofactor + procollagen mRNA stabilisation) and penetrates best at low pH. Supports the ascorbate arm by analogy. [11]

> **Tier-1 verdict:** This is a **genuinely solid pharmaceutics dataset** for a niche ingredient — synthesis, stability, penetration, binding, and hydrolysis are all characterised in peer-reviewed journals. But it is **all delivery/chemistry, no biology** (no cells, no living skin response, single group), and it **stops at "the molecule penetrates and can be hydrolysed."**

### Tier 2 — Animal

- **No retinyl-ascorbate-specific in-vivo animal anti-aging or photoprotection study located.** The porcine material above is **ex-vivo tissue/enzyme**, not a live-animal efficacy model. Do not lean on an "animal tier" for this molecule. *(Honest gap.)*

### Tier 3 — Human, ORAL

- **Not applicable / none.** Retinyl ascorbate is **not** an oral nutrient form (unlike retinyl acetate/palmitate, which are food-fortification vitamin-A forms). There is **no oral human literature on the RA-AsA molecule.** (Its two *parents* — vitamin A and vitamin C — have vast oral literatures, but those are about the free vitamins, not the conjugate, and are irrelevant as topical claims.) *(Honest gap — there is simply no oral tier here.)*

### Tier 4 — Human, TOPICAL  *(the tier that matters for the brand — and it is empty)*

**There is NO published stand-alone topical human RCT — or any located human clinical study — of retinyl ascorbate for skin aging, wrinkles, collagen, pigmentation, or photoprotection.** This is the single most important fact in the dossier.

- **No RCT.** A targeted literature search (PubMed, journal databases, review articles) surfaces **only the three Cardiff ex-vivo pharmaceutics papers** [1, 2, 3] for the molecule by name — none is a clinical efficacy trial.
- **Not in the meta-analyses.** Retinyl ascorbate does **not appear as an intervention** in the major topical-photoaging network meta-analyses and retinoid systematic reviews (which rank tretinoin, retinol, isotretinoin, adapalene, etc.). [12] Its absence is itself evidence of how little human data exists.
- **Commercial "proven" ratings are borrowed.** Paula's Choice rates retinyl ascorbate "Best/proven" and lists anti-aging / anti-blemish / pore-minimiser benefits and "greater stability in skin than retinol or vitamin C alone." [4] On inspection, this rests on **(i)** the Cardiff pharmaceutics (stability/penetration) and **(ii)** the parent-vitamin efficacy literatures — **not** on a retinyl-ascorbate clinical trial. Marketing-grade support, not RCT-grade.

**What is PROVEN for topical human use vs borrowed:**
- **Proven for retinyl ascorbate itself (human/ex-vivo skin):** that it **penetrates human skin, is more stable than free retinol/ascorbic acid, retains well in tissue, and can be enzymatically hydrolysed** to its parent vitamins [1, 2, 3]. That is a **delivery/stability claim**, and it is legitimately the molecule's strongest selling point.
- **Borrowed (not shown for RA-AsA):** *every* downstream benefit — collagen, wrinkles, brightening, antioxidant protection in living skin — is inherited from the retinoid [`retinoids.md`] and vitamin-C [`vitamin-c.md`] literatures, **conditional on in-skin hydrolysis producing clinically relevant active levels**, which has **never been demonstrated in a human trial.**
- **vs gold standard:** no head-to-head of RA-AsA vs tretinoin, retinol, or a standard vitamin-C serum exists.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **This is RA-AsA's strongest, most defensible domain** — and the *only* domain with molecule-specific human-skin data. Ex vivo, RA-AsA's **log P ~2.2** gives it favourable partitioning into stratum-corneum lipids; it shows **higher skin retention** than retinyl palmitate or ascorbyl palmitate and **delivers more of each vitamin to the viable epidermis** than those comparator esters. [1] Tissue/keratin-binding work shows it partitions and retains in skin in a lipid-content-dependent way. [2]
- **The conversion chain is the catch.** Penetration is necessary but not sufficient. The benefit requires: penetrate → **be hydrolysed by skin esterases** (shown *in principle* with porcine esterase [3]) → release retinoid + ascorbate → the **retinoid must still be converted onward** (retinyl ester → retinol → retinal → retinoic acid, each step enzyme-limited and lossy; see [`retinoids.md`](retinoids.md) §2) → and the **ascorbate must survive** (the degradation study shows the ascorbate arm is **vulnerable to oxidative disintegration** under radical load [3]). Each link is plausible; **none of the chain has been shown to deliver clinically meaningful active levels in living human skin.**
- **No human in-vivo PK / penetration-to-effect data.** All penetration data are **ex vivo** (heat-separated human skin membranes, tape-stripping, excised tissue). There is **no in-vivo human study** confirming how much active retinoid or ascorbate is actually freed in living skin, or how deep. *(Honest gap.)*
- **Net:** "Better delivery on paper (and ex vivo)" is real and is the ingredient's genuine differentiator — but "better delivery" ≠ "better clinical outcome," and that final step is unproven.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:** **Not established.** There is **no dose–response anti-aging data** to define an effective %. Cosmetic use levels are not publicly standardised; expect **low single-digit % or less**, used as a "vitamin A+C" feature ingredient. Confirm with supplier guidance.
- **pH:** A genuine *advantage* of the conjugate is **escaping free ascorbic acid's low-pH requirement** — esterification means you don't need pH ~3 to drive penetration. Formulate near **skin-compatible pH (~5–6)**; avoid extreme pH that could prematurely hydrolyse the ester before application.
- **Stability:** **The headline benefit.** RA-AsA is **far more stable than free retinol or free L-ascorbic acid** and **comparable to retinyl/ascorbyl palmitate** [1] — esterification protects both labile parents. **Caveats:** (i) it is **still a vitamin-A + vitamin-C derivative** → apply standard retinoid/ascorbate hygiene (**opaque, airless/low-oxygen packaging, antioxidant system, cool storage**); (ii) the degradation study shows the **ascorbate moiety can be sacrificially consumed/disintegrate under free-radical load** [3] — meaning in a poorly protected formula the "C" half may be spent before reaching skin. Add chelators/co-antioxidants (vitamin E, ferulic) to protect it.
- **Pairings:** logically compatible with **niacinamide, peptides, ceramides, hyaluronic acid, vitamin E/ferulic** (which also protect the ascorbate arm). As a self-contained "A+C" unit it reduces the classic difficulty of co-formulating separate retinol + L-AA. Best used **PM** (retinoid component).
- **Conflicts / cautions:** standard retinoid caution — avoid same-routine stacking with strong AHAs/BHAs, benzoyl peroxide, or a *second* high-strength L-ascorbic acid (redundant + irritation/oxidation risk).
- **Sensory:** oil-soluble/lipophilic; the retinoid chromophore can impart a **yellow-orange tint**; both vitamin moieties are oxidation-prone, so expect packaging constraints.

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation:** Likely **low-to-moderate**, contingent on which retinoid species is released in skin. If it behaves as a **weak retinyl ester** (retinol-level or below), irritation should be **mild** (typical retinisation: transient dryness/flaking). If it genuinely liberates **retinoic acid** (the unresolved ambiguity, §2), irritation — and regulatory status — would be **higher**. **No dedicated human irritation/patch dataset for RA-AsA located.** *(Honest gap.)*
- **Sun sensitivity:** As a vitamin-A–yielding molecule, treat as **PM use + daily SPF** (the non-negotiable retinoid-routine standard). The ascorbate arm is antioxidant (daytime-friendly in principle), but the retinoid arm dictates the conservative PM/SPF rule.
- **Pregnancy / breastfeeding:** **Avoid as a precaution.** Any vitamin-A topical that could yield retinol/retinoic acid falls under the **general "avoid topical retinoids in pregnancy"** advice. Because the released retinoid species is *uncertain* (and could be RA), the precautionary case is **stronger**, not weaker. Include a **pregnancy caution + "consult your doctor."**
- **Sensitisation / allergens:** Synthetic; low fragrance-allergen risk. **Long-term sensitisation data are sparse** (small, single-group literature). No specific allergen flags located.
- **Sourcing / regulatory safety reviews:** **No dedicated CIR or SCCS safety assessment of "retinyl ascorbate" by name located** — a notable gap. (The CIR/SCCS reviews cover **retinol, retinyl acetate, retinyl palmitate** and **ascorbic acid + salts**, the parents — not the conjugate.) This means RA-AsA's safety is **inferred** from its parents, not independently assessed. **[verified — no standalone monograph found].**
- **Manufacturing note:** Custom synthesis (low-temperature esterification; protected-ascorbate route in the patent) → confirm **purity, residual-solvent, and isomeric profile** on the CoA; impurity/oxidation control matters more than for commodity esters.

## 8. Regulatory & claims

- **Cosmetic vs drug line (US FDA) — and a specific risk for THIS molecule:** As a leave-on cosmetic ingredient framed around appearance/antioxidant benefit, retinyl ascorbate is treated as a **cosmetic ingredient**. **BUT** the primary literature repeatedly describes hydrolysis releasing **"retinoic acid"** [1, 3]. **Retinoic acid (tretinoin) is a prescription drug.** Any copy that says or implies RA-AsA **"delivers/releases retinoic acid,"** "works like prescription retinoid," "boosts collagen," "increases cell turnover," or "treats" anything risks reclassification as an **unapproved drug**. This is a sharper line than for ordinary retinyl esters — **flag for legal review** and **do not make the "delivers retinoic acid" claim** even though a source says it.
- **EU vitamin-A caps — likely in-scope by spirit, status unconfirmed by name:** Commission **Regulation (EU) 2024/996** caps **retinol, retinyl acetate, and retinyl palmitate** at **0.05% RE (body lotion) / 0.3% RE (other)** with a **"Contains Vitamin A"** label. Retinyl ascorbate is **not explicitly named** in that list, but a vitamin-A-yielding ester would be expected to fall under **retinol-equivalent (RE)** accounting in spirit. **[unverified for this specific molecule — confirm RE treatment and labelling obligation with a regulatory consultant before any EU launch.]** Also confirm **EU CosIng listing** of the INCI name.
- **Safe cosmetic claim language (if used at all):** "contains a stabilised **vitamin A + vitamin C** complex," "designed to deliver vitamins A and C together into the skin," "helps defend the look of skin against environmental stress," "helps improve the appearance of skin texture/tone." Keep everything **appearance-based and soft**; lean on the **stability/delivery** story (which is genuinely supported) rather than efficacy magnitudes (which are not).
- **Risky / avoid or substantiate:** "delivers retinoic acid," "as effective as retinol/tretinoin," "clinically proven to reduce wrinkles," any **"+X% collagen/wrinkle"** figure, "boosts collagen." **None of these can be substantiated for retinyl ascorbate** — there is no human efficacy trial. See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md) and the claims guardrail in [`../../CLAUDE.md`](../../CLAUDE.md).

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Cost tier:** **Higher / specialty.** Unlike commodity retinyl esters (palmitate/acetate are cheap, mass-produced vitamin-A forms), retinyl ascorbate is a **custom-synthesised co-drug** (low-temperature esterification; protected-ascorbate route per the patent [9]). Expect **specialty pricing, limited suppliers, and MOQ/lead-time considerations.**
- **IP / origin:** A **Korean synthesis patent (KR101002795B1)** was filed by **The Face Shop (2008, granted 2010; ownership later moving to LG Household & Health Care)** for a C-2-linked retinyl ascorbate route. [9] The foundational *pharmaceutics* work is academic (Cardiff University). This combination — a single-origin patent route + a small academic evidence base — means supply may be **concentrated and proprietary**; verify a **non-infringing, documented source** with a current spec/CoA.
- **Supply consideration:** **Niche.** It is **not** a widely stocked catalogue active (most "vitamin A + C" products instead use *separate* retinol + a vitamin-C ester like THD ascorbate). Confirm a reliable supplier and regulatory dossier before designing a SKU around it.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

- **"Vitamin A and C in one stable molecule":** **Largely TRUE on the chemistry, overstated on the benefit.** The stability/co-delivery advantage is real and peer-reviewed [1]. But "stable + delivered" is **not** "clinically proven to do X" — and that leap is where marketing overreaches.
- **"Greater stability in skin than retinol or vitamin C alone":** **Supported** by the Cardiff data [1] and reasonable to say (it's a *delivery/stability* claim, the molecule's genuine strength).
- **"Delivers/releases retinoic acid for retinoid-level results":** **Do not say this.** Even though a source uses "retinoic acid," (a) the released species is **ambiguous**, (b) it is **unproven in living human skin**, and (c) it is a **drug-claim landmine** (§8).
- **"Clinically proven anti-aging / boosts collagen / smooths wrinkles":** **NO.** There is **no human efficacy trial** of retinyl ascorbate. Any such claim borrows from the parents and is unsubstantiated for this molecule.
- **"Best/proven active" (per some ingredient dictionaries):** **Borrowed rating.** The "proven" label rests on parent-vitamin evidence + ex-vivo pharmaceutics, **not** on a RA-AsA clinical trial. Don't repeat "clinically proven" as if RA-AsA itself were trialled.
- **vs the obvious alternative (separate retinol + vitamin-C ester):** the co-drug's *pitch* is elegance and stability — but a brand can get **far better-evidenced** outcomes from **encapsulated retinol/retinaldehyde** + a **well-studied vitamin-C ester (THD ascorbate, MAP/SAP)**, each with real human data, formulated to be stable. The co-drug trades a **proven dual-active routine** for a **single elegant molecule with no clinical proof.**

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**❌ SKIP as a hero/lead active for a science-led launch; ⚠️ at most a conditional, "interesting-delivery" supporting ingredient if a specific story calls for it — and only with strictly soft, stability-focused claims.**

**One-line reasoning:** Retinyl ascorbate is a **clever, genuinely well-characterised delivery/stability molecule** (the Cardiff co-drug pharmaceutics are real and creditable) **with essentially no human efficacy evidence** — no RCT, no clinical study, not even an oral tier — so for a credibility-first brand it offers an appealing "vitamin A + C in one stable molecule" *story* that **cannot be substantiated as anti-aging**, plus a **drug-claim hazard** (the "releases retinoic acid" framing) and **niche/specialty sourcing**. The brand can achieve **better-evidenced** A+C outcomes by formulating **encapsulated retinol/retinaldehyde** ([`retinoids.md`](retinoids.md)) alongside a **proven vitamin-C ester** ([`vitamin-c.md`](vitamin-c.md)) — two ingredients with real human trials — rather than betting a SKU on an untrialled conjugate.

**Longevity-relevance note:** Mechanistically it touches the right hallmarks **on paper** — **oxidative stress** (its best-reasoned fit), and, via its parents, **collagen/ECM and photoaging/tone** — but it has **zero senescence, mitochondrial, glycation, or long-term data**, and even its core hallmark coverage is contingent on unproven in-skin hydrolysis. It is **not** a longevity differentiator; it's a delivery experiment wearing two vitamins.

**Where it could *legitimately* appear:** as a **stability/elegance play in a "vitamin A + C" concept product**, *if* the brand (a) leads claims on the **co-delivery/stability** mechanism (supported) rather than efficacy magnitudes (not), (b) resolves the **retinoid-species / drug-claim** question with legal + supplier, (c) confirms **EU RE/labelling** treatment, and (d) secures a **documented, non-infringing specialty supply**. It should **never** be the sole substantiation basis for an anti-aging claim.

Log in [`../../ideas/not-considering.md`](../../ideas/not-considering.md) as a standalone hero (reason: no human efficacy evidence; drug-claim risk; niche sourcing; better-evidenced alternatives), with a note that it may reappear as a **conditional "novel A+C delivery"** supporting ingredient if a specific concept and the regulatory/sourcing questions are cleared. Cross-reference [`../../ideas/considering.md`](../../ideas/considering.md) only in that conditional case.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

_Verified 2026-06-19 by adversarial fact-check of each headline claim against external sources (PubMed, Karger, Int J Pharm, Google Patents, NCBI E-utilities, Paula's Choice, Antioxidants/Sci Rep reviews)._

### Overall evidence grade (for TOPICAL anti-aging use of retinyl ascorbate specifically): **D — well-characterised delivery/chemistry, but NO human efficacy proof.**

Rationale: Retinyl ascorbate has a **legitimate, peer-reviewed pharmaceutics package** (synthesis, log P 2.2, stability, ex-vivo human-skin penetration, tissue binding, enzymatic hydrolysis) from one academic group [1, 2, 3] — better molecule-specific *chemistry* characterisation than many fringe actives. But it has **no clinical efficacy evidence whatsoever**: no topical RCT, no uncontrolled human study, no oral tier, no animal efficacy model. Every benefit is **borrowed** from the retinoid and vitamin-C parents, contingent on an in-skin hydrolysis-to-active step that is shown only *in principle* (porcine esterase) and never quantified in living human skin. It earns **D, not F**, because the delivery/stability advantage is real and peer-reviewed and the mechanism is coherent — but **delivery ≠ efficacy**, and there is nothing to substantiate an anti-aging claim for the molecule itself.

### Citation integrity — checked

### Load-bearing caveats (must carry into copy/strategy)

1. **No human efficacy evidence — at all.** Not just "no RCT" — no clinical study, no oral tier, no animal efficacy. The Tier-3 and Tier-4 sections are genuinely empty. This is the single fact that governs the verdict.
2. **The "releases retinoic acid" claim is a trap.** A primary source says it, but the released species is ambiguous and unproven in vivo; repeating it risks an **unapproved-drug** classification. Never put it in copy.
3. **Delivery ≠ efficacy.** The molecule's real, creditable strength (stability + co-delivery, ex vivo) must not be inflated into clinical-benefit language.
4. **Niche, single-origin sourcing + no standalone safety review.** Specialty supply (patent-linked), no CIR/SCCS monograph by name, identity not in PubChem — all add execution risk.
5. **Better-evidenced alternatives exist.** Encapsulated retinol/retinaldehyde + a proven vitamin-C ester deliver an A+C outcome with real human trials behind each component.

### Claims that must be avoided/softened (hard guardrails)

- ❌ "Delivers retinoic acid" / "works like prescription retinoid" — **drug claim + unproven.**
- ❌ "Clinically proven anti-aging / boosts collagen / smooths wrinkles" / any "+X%" figure — **no human trial exists.**
- ❌ "As effective as retinol + vitamin C" — **never compared.**
- ⚠️ Acceptable, soft only: "a stabilised vitamin A + vitamin C complex designed to deliver both vitamins together," "helps defend the look of skin against environmental stress," "more stable than retinol or vitamin C used alone" (the last is the one genuinely supported claim) — none implying retinoid-tier proven results.

**Net for the brand:** The dossier's **❌ SKIP-as-hero / ⚠️ conditional-supporting-only** verdict is endorsed. Retinyl ascorbate is an **elegant, well-engineered delivery molecule with an empty clinical cupboard** — a delivery hypothesis, not a proven active. For a science-led brand it is not a credible hero and not a substantiation basis for anti-aging claims. Re-grade upward only if a stand-alone topical human RCT of retinyl ascorbate ever appears (none currently exists), and only after the retinoid-species/drug-claim and EU RE-cap questions are resolved.
