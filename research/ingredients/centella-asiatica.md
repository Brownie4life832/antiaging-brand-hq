**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ✅ shortlisted (SUPPORTING — soothing / barrier-adjacent / calm-skin active; conditional hero only for a sensitive-skin line)
**Class:** Botanicals & plant bioactives (pentacyclic triterpenoid saponins + their aglycone acids)

> Scope note: This is an exhaustive, citation-backed literature review for the brand's research base. Evidence is separated strictly by tier (in-vitro → animal → human ORAL → human TOPICAL). Where a benefit is borrowed from a non-topical format or from mechanistic data only, it is flagged. ## 1. Identity

- **Common names:** Centella asiatica, "Cica," gotu kola, Indian pennywort, tiger grass, *Brahmi* (note: "Brahmi" is ambiguous and also used for *Bacopa monnieri*), *Bua-bok* (Thai). [3][7]
- **Botanical:** *Centella asiatica* (L.) Urban — a small herbaceous perennial in the Apiaceae (parsley) family, native to wetland regions of Asia. Long history in Ayurvedic and Traditional Chinese Medicine for wounds and "longevity." [3][7]
- **The four bioactive triterpenoids (the part that matters for skin):**
  - **Asiaticoside** — a triterpene **glycoside (saponin)**; large, water-soluble; the most-studied collagen stimulant. [11][12]
  - **Madecassoside** — a triterpene **glycoside (saponin)**; the most abundant triterpene in most extracts; the most-studied anti-inflammatory / soothing constituent. [1][2]
  - **Asiatic acid** — the **aglycone** (sugar removed) of asiaticoside; more lipophilic; strong antioxidant / anti-glycation activity. [11][16]
  - **Madecassic acid** — the **aglycone** of madecassoside; anti-inflammatory (iNOS/COX downregulation). [2][4]
- **Key standardized forms / derivatives:**
  - **TECA / TTFCA** (Titrated Extract of Centella Asiatica / Total Triterpenic Fraction) — a **pharmaceutical-grade reconstituted mixture: ~40% asiaticoside, ~30% asiatic acid, ~30% madecassic acid** (some sources cite 30/30/40). Used as a *drug* in Europe for venous insufficiency and wound healing — this matters for the cosmetic-vs-drug line (§8). [5][8][11]
  - **Madecassoside-isolate** and **asiaticoside-isolate** — purified single-molecule actives used in premium cosmetics (e.g., La Roche-Posay's madecassoside line). [13][14]
  - **ECa 233** — a standardized Thai extract (asiaticoside + madecassoside ~80% w/w) used in several clinical trials. [10]
  - **Whole-leaf / "Madagascar Centella" extracts** — crude cosmetic extracts of variable, often undisclosed, triterpene content (the K-beauty "cica" mass market). Composition varies hugely by origin/genetics/season. [1][3]

> Identity caution: "Cica" on a label can mean anything from a pharmaceutical TECA fraction to a dilute crude leaf extract. **Triterpene content is the active variable and is frequently undisclosed.** [1][3]

## 2. Class & mechanism

Centella's skin activity comes from its triterpenoid saponins (and their acids) acting on **fibroblasts, keratinocytes, melanocytes and inflammatory cells**. The mechanisms are unusually well-mapped *in vitro* (this is a heavily studied plant), even though human topical confirmation lags (§4).

- **Collagen / ECM anabolism (the headline mechanism):**
  - **Asiaticoside** induces **Type I collagen** synthesis in human dermal fibroblasts via a **TGF-β receptor-I-kinase–INDEPENDENT Smad pathway** — it phosphorylates Smad2/3, drives Smad3/4 nuclear translocation, but does *not* require the canonical TβRI kinase (Lee et al. 2006). [11]
  - **TECA** raises collagen synthesis dose-dependently in fibroblast culture; **asiatic acid** is a key active component (Maquart et al. 1990). All three triterpenes raise intracellular free proline and stimulate **glycosaminoglycan** synthesis. [12]
  - In rat wound models, triterpenes increase **collagen and ECM accumulation** and remodeling (Maquart et al. 1999). [9]
- **Anti-inflammatory / soothing ("calm skin"):** Madecassoside and madecassic acid suppress **NF-κB**, **TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β**, **iNOS, COX-2, PGE2**, and modulate **macrophage polarization (M1→M2)**. This is the mechanistic basis of cica's "soothing/anti-redness" reputation. [1][2][4]
- **Antioxidant:** Restores **glutathione (GSH)**, lowers **malondialdehyde (MDA)** and ROS, supports antioxidant enzymes; protects mitochondrial/endothelial membranes from lipid peroxidation. [1][2]
- **Anti-glycation (AGE/RAGE):** **Asiatic acid** protects human keratinocytes (HaCaT) against AGE-induced injury — suppresses **RAGE** (↓14–40%), p38/JNK, ROS and NO, **preserves collagen I**, and reduces **MMP-1/-8/-9** under glycative stress (Wang 2014). [16]
- **Photoprotection:** TECA restored UVB-damaged fibroblast viability via miRNA-mediated pro-survival/anti-apoptotic gene regulation (An et al. 2012). Madecassoside attenuates UVB-induced **ferroptosis** (targets POR) and inhibits UV-induced melanogenesis (below). [6][1]
- **Pigmentation:** Madecassoside inhibits **UV-induced melanin synthesis and melanosome transfer**, working **indirectly via anti-inflammation** (blocking PAR-2 signaling / PGE2 in keratinocytes) rather than by direct tyrosinase inhibition (Jung et al. 2013). [15]
- **Barrier / hydration:** Associated with reduced **TEWL** and increased stratum-corneum hydration in human and in-vitro work; proposed to support ceramide synthesis and barrier lipids (mechanism less precisely characterized than for ceramides themselves). [19][20]
- **Scar modulation (double-edged):** In keloid fibroblasts, madecassoside/asiaticoside *suppress* abnormal migration (anti-keloid), yet in normal wounds triterpenes *promote* collagen — context-dependent ECM modulation, not a simple "more collagen" story. [1]

**Bottom line on mechanism:** A genuinely **multi-target** botanical — collagen/ECM, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, anti-glycation, mild photoprotection and pigment-via-inflammation. The mechanistic case is **broad and real in vitro**; the open question is how much survives topical delivery in a finished cosmetic at realistic concentrations (§4–§5).

## 4. Evidence review by tier  *(the core of this dossier)*

> Headline reading: Centella is **mechanistically rich and clinically thin-but-real**. The strongest human topical evidence is for **wound healing, soothing/erythema reduction, barrier/hydration, and scar/striae** — *not* for stand-alone anti-wrinkle efficacy. Most cosmetic anti-aging human data uses **combination products** (e.g., madecassoside + vitamin C) or **undisclosed crude extracts**, making the isolated contribution of Centella hard to quantify.

### Tier 1 — In-vitro (cell & reconstructed-skin models) — *abundant, fairly strong*

- **Maquart FX, et al. 1990** — *Connect Tissue Res* 24(2):107–120. Human foreskin fibroblasts. **TECA increased collagen synthesis dose-dependently**; asiatic acid the primary active; all three triterpenes ↑ intracellular free proline and ↑ GAG synthesis. *Limitation:* in-vitro; foreskin fibroblasts. [12]
- **Lee J, et al. 2006** — *Planta Med* 72(4):324–328. Human dermal fibroblasts. **Asiaticoside induces Type I collagen via TβRI-kinase–independent Smad2/3 signaling** (defined the molecular pathway). *Limitation:* mechanism only, monolayer culture. [11]
- **An IS, et al. 2012** — *Int J Mol Med* 30(5):1194–1198. Normal human dermal fibroblasts. **TECA (25–50 µg/mL) restored UVB-reduced viability** via miRNA-mediated pro-survival/anti-apoptotic regulation (40 miRNAs altered). *Limitation:* in-vitro only; viability not wrinkle endpoints. [6]
- **Jung E, et al. 2013** — *Molecules* 18(12):15724–15736. Keratinocyte–melanocyte co-culture + reconstructed epidermis. **Madecassoside inhibits UV-induced melanin synthesis & melanosome transfer** via blocking PAR-2/PGE2 inflammation (not tyrosinase). (Also had a small human arm — Tier 4.) [15]
- **Wang ZH 2014** — *Biomedicine (Taipei)* 4(3):19. HaCaT keratinocytes + AGE-BSA. **Asiatic acid (2–8 µM) anti-glycation:** ↓RAGE, ↓p38/JNK, ↓ROS/NO, **preserved collagen I**, ↓MMP-1/-8/-9, restored viability. *Limitation:* keratinocyte model; supraphysiologic glycation stress. [16]
- **Bandopadhyay S, et al. 2023** (review) — *J Cell Mol Med* 27(5):593–608. Compiles asiaticoside/madecassoside mechanisms: NF-κB inhibition, M1→M2 macrophage shift, GSH restoration, keratinocyte migration (FAK/Akt/MAPK), angiogenesis, anti-keloid signaling. [1]

**Verdict (Tier 1):** Consistent, multi-mechanism in-vitro support for **collagen/ECM, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, anti-glycation, photoprotection and pigment-via-inflammation**. This is one of the better-characterized botanicals at the cellular level. Caveat: concentrations used in vitro often exceed what a topical cosmetic delivers to living dermis (§5).

### Tier 2 — Animal — *solid for wound/ECM, oral-leaning for photoaging*

- **Maquart FX, et al. 1999** — *Eur J Dermatol* 9(4):289–296. Rat experimental wounds. **Topical triterpenes ↑ ECM accumulation, collagen and remodeling** in vivo. [9]
- Multiple rodent **incision/burn wound models** show C. asiatica accelerates re-epithelialization, granulation, neovascularization and tensile strength. [1][23]
- **Oral Centella, UVB-hairless-mouse photoaging (2025/recent):** oral C. asiatica extract **↓ wrinkle formation, ↓TEWL, ↓MDA, ↓pro-inflammatory cytokines; ↑ hydration, elasticity, antioxidant enzymes, collagen.** *Critical caveat:* **ORAL route** — relevant to mechanism/ingestible products, **not** transferable to a topical claim. [24]

**Verdict (Tier 2):** Good animal support for **topical wound/ECM/collagen**; the broad "photoaging reversal" animal data is largely **oral** and must not be borrowed for topical copy.

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL — *real for venous/wound use; mostly off-target for topical cosmetic claims*

- **TECA/TTFCA is an oral & topical drug in parts of Europe** (chronic venous insufficiency, venous leg ulcers, wound healing) with decades of use — the **TECA "status paper"** (Cesarone et al. 2011, *Minerva Cardioangiol*) reviews microcirculatory/vascular applications. [8]
- **Paocharoen V 2010** — *J Med Assoc Thai* 93(Suppl 7):S166–70. Randomized, placebo-controlled, **n=200 diabetic *wound* patients** (general diabetic wounds, not specifically foot ulcers): oral C. asiatica (50 mg asiaticoside ×3/day = 300 mg/day) reported faster wound healing. *Limitation:* **ORAL route — systemic/drug context, not transferable to a topical claim.** [21]
- Oral C. asiatica supplements (e.g., Centellicum) show **postpartum striae** improvement (skin thickness, collagen, elasticity). [25]

**Verdict (Tier 3):** Oral Centella has genuine **wound/venous/striae** support, but this is a **systemic/drug context.** **Do not borrow oral results into topical cosmetic claims.** Oral use also has its own safety profile (hepatotoxicity signals with prolonged high-dose oral use — see §7).

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL  *(the only tier that supports our product claims)*

**4a. Randomized / controlled (the strongest topical data):**

- **Haftek M, et al. 2008** — *Exp Dermatol* 17(11):946–952. **Randomized, double-blind**, **n=20** women, **6 months**, **5% vitamin C + 0.1% madecassoside** on photoaged skin. Significant ↓ deep & superficial wrinkles; ↑ suppleness, firmness, ↓ roughness, ↑ hydration; corroborated by elasticity measurement and **histological re-appearance of a normal elastic-fibre network** in ~2/3 of subjects. *Limitations:* **small n (20)**, female-only, **combination with vitamin C (can't isolate madecassoside)**, no explicit vehicle arm reported. **The single most-cited topical anti-aging study — and it's a combo.** [14]
- **Jenwitheesuk K, et al. 2018** — *Evid Based Complement Alternat Med* 2018:9525624. Prospective randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, **n=30 enrolled / 23 completed**, **7% C. asiatica cream** on **split-thickness skin-graft donor-site scars**: significant improvement was driven mainly by the **pigmentation** sub-parameter of the Vancouver Scar Scale (and total VSS at weeks 4–12); not a striae or facial-wrinkle endpoint. *Limitations:* small completer n; crude extract; surgical-scar (not aging/wrinkle) context; benefit narrower than "scar improvement" implies. [19]
- **Damkerngsuntorn W, et al. 2020** — split-face, double-blind, randomized: **ECa 233 gel 0.05% (4×/day, 3 months)** post-fractional-laser: **erythema subsided by day 7 vs day 28** for placebo; significant improvements days 2–7 (p=0.009–0.044). *Limitation:* procedure-recovery context, not chronic aging. [19]
- **Indonesian batik-workers trial (2021)** — double-blind, **n=30**, **C. asiatica + ceramide**: improved **TEWL, SC hydration, skin pH** (barrier protection vs occupational irritant exposure). *Limitation:* small n; combination with ceramide. [20]
- **Changsan N, et al.** — multicenter randomized controlled, **n=60**: C. asiatica polymeric spray-film **sped wound recovery** and ↓ exudate/PUSH scores. [19]

**4b. Lower-tier human topical (open-label / observational / combination / undisclosed-dose):**

- **García Hernández JA, et al. 2013** — *Int J Cosmet Sci.* Randomized double-blind on **striae gravidarum prevention** with a cream containing hydroxyprolisilane-C, rosehip oil, **Centella triterpenes** and vitamin E. (Centella is one of several actives.) [26]
- **Trofolastin striae trial (Mallol J, et al. 1991)** — double-blind, **n=80** pregnant women: cream with **C. asiatica + α-tocopherol + collagen-elastin hydrolysates**: **34% developed striae vs 56% placebo**; strongest effect in women with a puberty-striae history (89% prevented). *Limitation:* combination product; Centella's isolated role unproven. [27]
- **Jung et al. 2013 human arm** — topical **madecassoside on tanned skin, 8 weeks**: **melanin index significantly ↓** vs control. *Limitation:* volunteer count/concentration not fully specified in abstract. [15]
- **Su et al. 2025** — *J Cosmet Dermatol.* Prospective observational study of a **Centella + Ceramide NP + Panthenol** product in **sensitive skin**: improved tolerability/hydration. *Limitation:* observational, combination, manufacturer-adjacent. [22] *(full text gated; abstract/listing accessed)*
- A large body of **K-beauty "cica" finished-product** testing exists but is mostly **manufacturer-run, open-label, undisclosed triterpene content** — low evidentiary weight. [17][18]

**4c. Honest synthesis of human topical evidence:**

The topical human literature is **broad but methodologically weak for *anti-aging*** specifically: small samples (n=20–60), heavy reliance on **combination products** (vitamin C, ceramide, vitamin E, tocopherol), **undisclosed/variable extract strengths**, and several manufacturer-linked or open-label designs. The **wound-healing, post-procedure soothing, barrier/TEWL and scar/striae** endpoints are the **best-supported topical uses**. Stand-alone topical **anti-wrinkle efficacy of isolated Centella** rests essentially on the Haftek combo study and mechanistic extrapolation.

#### What is PROVEN for TOPICAL human use vs. borrowed

- **Proven / well-supported (topical human):** soothing & **erythema/redness reduction**, post-procedure recovery, **barrier support (↓TEWL, ↑hydration)**, **scar & striae improvement**, good tolerability on sensitive/compromised skin. [14][19][20][22][27]
- **Supported in combination only (can't isolate Centella):** photoaging wrinkle/firmness/elasticity improvement (madecassoside **+ vitamin C**); striae prevention (Centella **+ vitamin E/collagen hydrolysates**). [14][26][27]
- **Borrowed from in-vitro (NOT proven topical clinical endpoints):** "boosts collagen," anti-glycation/AGE protection, antioxidant capacity, anti-pigment effect (only a small human melanin-index signal). [11][12][16][15]
- **Borrowed from ORAL / drug context (do NOT use for topical copy):** venous insufficiency, diabetic-ulcer healing, systemic striae repair. [8][21][25]

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

This is Centella's **central formulation weakness** and is often glossed over in marketing.

- **The saponin glycosides (asiaticoside, madecassoside) penetrate skin POORLY.** They are **large, highly water-soluble molecules with a glycoside (sugar) chain on a pentacyclic triterpene** — they "scarcely permeate through the skin" in conventional vehicles. [28][29]
- **The aglycone acids (asiatic acid, madecassic acid) are more lipophilic** and penetrate better, but are less abundant in many crude extracts. [16][30]
- Consequence: a lot of in-vitro collagen/anti-glycation activity is demonstrated **at the cell** — getting enough active **to living dermis** topically is non-trivial. Much may stay in the stratum corneum (which still supports surface soothing/barrier effects, but undercuts deep dermal "collagen-boosting" claims).
- **Delivery enhancement is an active research field:** nanoemulsions (asiaticoside permeation reportedly ~13.6× conventional), **liposomes/phytosomes** for madecassoside, **NLCs/transfersomes** for asiatic acid, and microneedles all markedly improve cutaneous delivery in vitro/in vivo. [28][29][30] For a **premium** product, an encapsulated/optimized-delivery Centella is a credible differentiator over commodity "cica."

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:**
  - **Crude extract:** commonly **0.1–7%** in clinical studies; CIR reports cosmetic use up to ~**0.5%** (extract) typical, with leave-on products commonly 0.1–1%. [19][31]
  - **Isolated madecassoside:** **0.05–0.1%** is the clinically used range (0.1% in Haftek; 0.05% in pigment/laser work). [14][15]
  - **TECA:** pharmaceutical use ~0.5–1%; in cosmetics treat as a potent standardized fraction. [8]
- **pH:** Triterpene glycosides are relatively pH-tolerant (broad latitude ~4–7). Note that **TECA's component acids are more soluble/stable at slightly higher pH**, while pairing with **L-ascorbic acid** (as in Haftek) forces a **low pH (~3.5)** environment — formulate the vitamin C constraints first. [14]
- **Stability:** Triterpene saponins are reasonably **heat- and light-stable** vs. labile actives like ascorbic acid/retinol — an advantage. Crude extracts can carry color/odor and batch variability; oxidation of co-extracted phenolics over time is the main concern. Standardized/isolated grades are more stable and reproducible. [1][31]
- **Pairings (synergistic):** **Ceramides / barrier lipids** (clinically combined; on-thesis), **vitamin C** (clinical anti-aging combo), **niacinamide, panthenol, hyaluronic acid, madecassoside + retinoid** (cica is widely used to *buffer retinoid irritation*). Excellent companion to actives that irritate. [14][20][22]
- **Conflicts:** Few hard incompatibilities. Main risks are (a) **vehicle/penetration** (see §5) and (b) very low pH or high-electrolyte systems can affect some extract solubility. No major actives are "off-limits."
- **Sensory:** Crude extracts can impart a green/herbal tint and odor; isolated madecassoside/asiaticoside are easier to keep elegant. Generally lightweight, plays well in serums, emulsions, creams, balms.

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation:** **Low** — Centella's whole reputation is as a *soothing* active; used on compromised, post-procedure and sensitive skin. CIR notes minimal irritation at cosmetic use levels. [22][31]
- **Sensitization (the one real flag):** Centella and its triterpenes (asiatic acid, asiaticoside, madecassic acid) are **weak/very weak contact sensitizers** — documented **allergic contact dermatitis** case reports exist (Gomes et al. 1996; Eun & Lee; Turkish 2024 cases), especially with **medicinal/occlusive use on broken skin.** HRIPT data: a cream at **0.045%** extract (n=110) and **0.018%** (n=52) did **not** sensitize; 1% and 5% extract patch tests in petrolatum (n=20) were negative. **Net: low but non-zero ACD risk** — declare the extract clearly; consider patch-test guidance for very sensitive users. [31][32][33]
- **Photosensitivity:** No evidence that topical Centella increases photosensitivity (unlike retinoids). Daily SPF remains routine advice. [1]
- **Pregnancy / breastfeeding:** **Topical** Centella appears in many **stretch-mark creams marketed for pregnancy** and the topical safety signal is reassuring — **but** **oral/high-dose Centella has hepatotoxicity case reports** and is generally **advised against in pregnancy as a supplement.** For a brand: topical use is widely considered acceptable, **but do not guarantee "pregnancy-safe"; use "consult your doctor" framing**, and never imply the *oral* safety/efficacy literature applies. [25][34]
- **Systemic/oral toxicity:** Prolonged high-dose **oral** Centella has **case reports of hepatotoxicity** and the EMA/herbal monographs flag liver-related cautions — irrelevant to topical exposure but a reason to keep all messaging strictly topical. [34]
- **CIR conclusion (2023):** The Cosmetic Ingredient Review Expert Panel concluded the Centella asiatica–derived ingredients (Extract, Leaf Extract, Leaf Water, callus/meristem cultures, Root Extract, etc.) are **"safe in the present practices of use and concentration in cosmetics… when formulated to be non-sensitizing."** (Johnson, Bergfeld, et al. 2023.) [31]
- **Sourcing/sustainability & contaminants:** As a leafy wetland crop, crude Centella can carry **pesticide residues, heavy metals (esp. lead/arsenic from contaminated soils/water) and microbial load** — demand COA, heavy-metal and pesticide testing, and standardized triterpene assay. Wild-harvest vs cultivated and Madagascar/Korea/India origin all affect quality. [3][31]

## 8. Regulatory & claims

- **Cosmetic vs drug line — IMPORTANT for Centella specifically:** Because **TECA is an approved *drug* (wound healing, venous insufficiency) in some European markets**, Centella sits unusually close to the drug border. In a **cosmetic**, you may make **appearance/cosmetic** claims (look of calm/soothed skin, comfort, hydration, smoothness, "supports skin's natural recovery look"). Claiming it **heals wounds, treats eczema/atopic dermatitis, treats venous insufficiency, or "repairs/regenerates" tissue** drifts into **drug** territory (US FDA framing per [`../../CLAUDE.md`](../../CLAUDE.md)) and needs substantiation/legal review. The wound-healing and atopic-dermatitis literature is *real* but is **drug-adjacent — do not import it into cosmetic copy.**
- **EU:** Centella asiatica extract and isolates are **permitted in cosmetics** (CosIng-listed) with no general restriction; no SCCS restriction analogous to retinol's cap. *(Verify current CosIng entry before label claims — status can change.)* [unverified — confirm with regulatory counsel]
- **US:** OTC cosmetic ingredient; "cica" itself has no monograph. Avoid drug-style verbs.
- **Safe cosmetic language:** "helps visibly calm and soothe," "supports a comfortable, less-reactive look," "helps reduce the appearance of redness," "supports the skin barrier feel," "comforts stressed or sensitive skin," "a soothing companion to active ingredients." For the combo evidence: "in a 6-month study, a madecassoside + vitamin C formula improved the look of firmness and fine lines" (attribute to the *formula*, not Centella alone).
- **Risky / overreaching:** "heals," "repairs skin," "treats eczema/redness," "boosts collagen by X%," "anti-glycation protection" (in-vitro only), "tiger grass regenerates skin," "as proven as [drug] for wounds," "pregnancy-safe" (don't guarantee).

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Cost tier:**
  - **Crude Centella/cica extract: LOW–moderate** — cheap, ubiquitous, the K-beauty commodity. Easy to greenwash; quality (triterpene %) varies wildly.
  - **Isolated madecassoside / asiaticoside / TECA: moderate–HIGH** — standardized, assayed, premium; fits premium positioning and lets you state an actual % of a defined active.
- **Suppliers:** Widely available — specialty botanical houses, standardized-extract suppliers (TECA/ECa 233 grades), and isolate manufacturers. **Demand:** standardized **triterpene content (assay for madecassoside/asiaticoside %)**, **heavy-metal & pesticide COA**, **origin traceability** (Madagascar/Korea/India), microbial spec. [3][31]
- **Market context:** The Centella cosmetics market is ~**$750M (2024) → ~$1.2–1.5B by 2030–2035** (~6.5% CAGR); cica is a **crowded, viral, commoditized** category — differentiation must come from **standardized/encapsulated actives + honest claims**, not from "we use cica." [17][18]

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

- **"Tiger grass heals/regenerates your skin":** Folkloric ("tigers roll in it") + drug-context wound data dressed up as a cosmetic miracle. Topical *cosmetic* evidence supports **soothing/barrier/comfort**, not "regeneration." [1][8]
- **"Boosts collagen" / "anti-aging powerhouse":** In-vitro and combination-product data — **not** isolated-topical-clinical proof. The one real topical anti-aging study is **madecassoside + vitamin C** (can't separate them) at **n=20.** [11][12][14]
- **"Cica = the active":** Often a **dilute, undisclosed crude extract.** Triterpene content (the thing that actually works) is rarely stated. A "cica cream" and a 0.1% standardized-madecassoside serum are not the same product. [1][3]
- **"Penetrates deep to rebuild the dermis":** The key saponins **barely penetrate** in conventional vehicles — deep dermal claims overreach unless an enhanced delivery system is used and substantiated. [28][29]
- **"Anti-glycation / fights AGEs":** Real *in vitro* (asiatic acid), **no human topical endpoint.** Don't claim it. [16]
- **"Pregnancy-safe miracle for stretch marks":** Striae data is from **combination creams**; Centella's isolated contribution is unproven, and the *oral* literature carries liver cautions. Soften and add medical-consult framing. [27][34]
- **Social proof ≠ evidence:** >2.8B TikTok views drive demand, not efficacy. The brand's edge is to be the honest, standardized version in a hype-saturated category. [17][18]

## 11. Verdict for the brand

✅ **SUPPORTING active — the "calm/soothe + barrier-buffer" pillar; conditional hero only in a dedicated sensitive-skin product.** Centella's *best-evidenced, claim-safe* value is **soothing, redness-appearance reduction, post-active/post-procedure comfort, and barrier/hydration support** — which pairs naturally with the brand's **barrier-repair thesis** and lets harder actives (retinoids, acids, vitamin C) be tolerated. Use a **standardized/isolated grade (madecassoside 0.05–0.1% and/or assayed triterpene extract)**, ideally with **enhanced delivery**, so you can state a real % and out-position commodity "cica."

**One-line reasoning:** A genuinely multi-mechanism, very-well-tolerated botanical whose human *topical* proof is strongest for **soothing/barrier/scar** (not stand-alone anti-wrinkle) — perfect as a credibility-rich **supporting/buffering** active, not as the brand's lead anti-aging claim.

**Longevity-relevance note:** Touches several skin-aging hallmarks (inflammaging, ECM/collagen, oxidative & glycation stress, barrier), but the *durable, human-proven topical* payoff is **inflammaging/comfort + barrier**, which is **directly on-thesis** for barrier-repair and for making an aggressive longevity routine *tolerable long-term*. Treat it as a **tolerability-and-calm enabler** of the real anti-aging actives, with honest, drug-line-respecting claims. Log in [`../../ideas/considering.md`](../../ideas/considering.md).

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Verified:** 2026-06-19. Method: independent re-checking of the load-bearing citations against PubMed/PMC/publisher abstracts (Haftek 2008 [14]; Lee 2006 [11]; Maquart 1990 [12] & 1999 [9]; An 2012 [6]; Jung 2013 [15]; Wang 2014 [16]; Paocharoen 2010 [21]; Jenwitheesuk 2018 [19]; Damkerngsuntorn 2020 [19]; CIR/Johnson-Bergfeld 2023 [31]; nanoemulsion permeation [29]; oral hepatotoxicity [34]). Goal: catch evidence quietly borrowed across tiers, citations that don't say what's claimed, and small/industry-funded studies dressed up as proof.

### Overall evidence grade

**For stand-alone TOPICAL ANTI-AGING (wrinkles/firmness): C** — weak/indirect. The entire anti-aging-appearance case rests on **one** small (n=20), female-only, **combination** RCT (madecassoside **+ 5% vitamin C**, Haftek 2008) plus in-vitro mechanism; the active cannot be isolated and there is no monotherapy topical wrinkle RCT.

**For TOPICAL SOOTHING / POST-PROCEDURE / BARRIER / SCAR (the brand's actual intended use): B (low-to-mid B)** — moderate human-topical. Several small but genuinely controlled topical RCTs (Damkerngsuntorn post-laser erythema n=30; Jenwitheesuk scar n=23 completers; batik-worker barrier n=30; wound spray-film n=60) support soothing, post-procedure erythema reduction, barrier/TEWL and surgical-scar appearance — all **small (n=20–60)** and several **combination** (ceramide) or manufacturer-adjacent, which is what keeps this out of A.

**Net grade for how the brand intends to use it (supporting calm/barrier active): B−.** For anything it would be tempted to *headline* (collagen, anti-wrinkle, anti-glycation): **C**.

### What checked out (credit where due)

This dossier is unusually disciplined and largely self-audits. Confirmed accurate against source:
- **Every primary citation I checked is real and is represented accurately**, including correct sample sizes, routes, and the honest "combination / can't isolate" flags. No fabricated studies, no misquoted conclusions.
- The **tier separation (in-vitro → animal → oral → topical) is rigorous and honest** — oral and animal data are explicitly fenced off from topical claims (§4 Tiers 2–3, §10), which is exactly the failure mode this verification was hunting for and mostly did not find.
- The **§5 penetration weakness** (saponin glycosides barely permeate; MW ~959; aglycones better) is correct and is the single most important honest caveat — confirmed against the delivery-systems literature; nanoemulsion **~13.65×** figure is accurate ([29], Li et al. 2020).
- **CIR 2023 "safe when formulated to be non-sensitizing"** [31] and the **oral hepatotoxicity** signal [34] both confirmed.

### Main caveats (the real weaknesses)

1. **The anti-aging headline is one n=20 combo study.** Haftek 2008 is real and well-conducted (6 mo, double-blind, histology) but is **0.1% madecassoside + 5% vitamin C**, female-only, n=20, and (per the abstract) **does not report a clean vehicle-only arm isolating madecassoside**. Vitamin C alone produces much of this effect. Treat as *formula* evidence, never as madecassoside/Centella anti-wrinkle proof.
2. **"Boosts collagen" is in-vitro/animal only.** Lee 2006 [11] and Maquart 1990 [12] are monolayer fibroblast cultures (foreskin/dermal); Maquart 1999 [9] is rat. Real mechanism, **zero** human-topical collagen/wrinkle endpoint for the isolated active. Combined with §5 poor penetration, deep-dermal collagen claims are not defensible.
3. **Anti-glycation is a single in-vitro keratinocyte paper.** Wang 2014 [16] is HaCaT + AGE-BSA at 1–8 µM and the author explicitly calls for in-vivo confirmation. No human, no topical. Do not claim "anti-glycation / fights AGEs."
4. **The strongest controlled topical signal is post-laser recovery, not chronic redness/inflammaging.** Damkerngsuntorn 2020 is a clean split-face RCT but the effect size is small (erythema-index coefficient −0.03; p=0.046) and the context is **ablative-laser wound recovery** — don't generalize to rosacea/redness-prone "calm skin" without saying so.
5. **Small n + combination + manufacturer-adjacency throughout topical tier.** Every supportive topical RCT is n≤60; several are combinations (vitamin C, ceramide, tocopherol/collagen hydrolysates) or observational/manufacturer-linked (Su 2025 [22] is observational + combination). None is independently replicated at scale. No clear industry-funding red flag was found in the academic RCTs checked, but funding/COI was not stated in the abstracts and should be assumed possible.
6. **Borrowed-tier temptations the dossier already resists — keep resisting.** Oral diabetic-ulcer (Paocharoen n=200 [21]), oral striae (Centellicum [25]), oral UVB-mouse photoaging [24], and TECA-as-a-drug venous data [8] are all **oral/systemic or animal** and must never migrate into topical cosmetic copy.

### Claims that were softened in this pass (edits made to the dossier above)

- **§3 table, Collagen/ECM cell:** removed citation [21] (Paocharoen, **oral** diabetic wounds) from a **human-topical** evidence cell — it was cross-contaminating tiers — and re-labeled the human-topical firmness/elasticity evidence as "combination only (n=20)."
- **§3 table, Inflammaging/redness cell:** changed "Yes (strongest)" → "strongest *topical case*" and noted the controlled erythema data is **post-procedure**, not chronic-redness/inflammaging.
- **§4 Jenwitheesuk entry:** corrected to **n=30 enrolled / 23 completed**, **split-thickness skin-graft donor-site scars** (not striae/wrinkles), and noted the significant effect was driven mainly by the **pigmentation** VSS sub-parameter — narrower than "scar improvement."
- **§4 Paocharoen entry:** corrected "diabetic foot ulcers" → "diabetic **wound** patients," added dose (300 mg/day) and an explicit **ORAL — not topical** flag.
- **§12 ref [34]:** added the verified hepatotoxicity primary sources (LiverTox NBK603561; Jorge 2005 PMID:15801887).

### Claims that should still be softened / never made (for marketing & formulation to honor)

- ❌ "Boosts collagen," "rebuilds the dermis," "anti-aging powerhouse" — in-vitro/combo only; **C-grade**.
- ❌ "Anti-glycation / fights AGEs" — one in-vitro paper; no human topical.
- ❌ "Heals / repairs / regenerates," anything importing the wound-healing or venous-insufficiency **drug** literature — crosses the cosmetic/drug line (§8).
- ❌ "Proven to reduce redness/calm inflammation" stated flatly — the controlled human evidence is **post-procedure erythema**; phrase as "helps visibly soothe and reduce the look of redness after [actives/procedures]."
- ⚠️ Striae/stretch-mark claims — combination creams only; Centella's isolated contribution unproven; add medical-consult framing and never imply the oral data.
- ✅ Defensible as written: visibly soothing/comforting, post-active and post-procedure comfort, barrier-feel / hydration support, good tolerability on sensitive/compromised skin, "a soothing companion to stronger actives" — and, attributed to the *formula* not the molecule, the Haftek combo result.

**Bottom line:** The dossier's own verdict (§11) — Centella as a **B-grade supporting calm/barrier/tolerability active, not a headline anti-ager** — survives adversarial checking. The science is real but thin and small-n on the topical side, mechanism-heavy on everything else, and the genuine differentiator is **standardized/encapsulated actives + honest, drug-line-respecting claims**, not efficacy superiority.
