# Ingredient: Soy / Soybean Extract & Isoflavones (INCI: Glycine Soja (Soybean) Seed Extract; Soy Isoflavones; Genistein; Daidzein)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering (verdict: **conditional / supporting** — see §11)
**Class:** Botanicals & plant bioactives (phytoestrogen isoflavones + a serine-protease-inhibitor protein fraction)
**Slug:** soy-extract

> **One-line honest summary:** "Soy" is not one ingredient — it is at least three chemically distinct actives sharing a bean: (1) **isoflavones** (genistein/daidzein, phytoestrogens), (2) a **serine-protease-inhibitor protein fraction** (STI + Bowman-Birk inhibitor) that blocks the PAR-2 melanosome-transfer pathway, and (3) **soybean oil** (a bland emollient). The single best human-topical evidence soy has is for **even tone / hyperpigmentation** (the protease-inhibitor fraction — one solid 12-week vehicle-controlled RCT, n=65). For wrinkles/firmness the cleaner human data is mostly **oral**, and the topical "anti-aging" claims lean on in-vitro/animal work plus postmenopausal estrogen-comparison trials where **isoflavones consistently lost to estradiol** and showed only modest absolute effects. Add an awkward regulatory twist: the EU caps cosmetic **genistein at 0.007%** — far below the 4% used in the topical research — so the strongest topical isoflavone studies aren't legal to replicate as an EU cosmetic.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common names:** Soy extract, soybean extract, soy isoflavones, soy phytoestrogens, "non-denatured soy," soy protein, soymilk (in older derm literature).
- **INCI names (the form matters enormously — these are not interchangeable):**
  - `Glycine Soja (Soybean) Seed Extract` — the umbrella cosmetic extract; composition varies wildly by supplier (can be mostly protein/peptides, mostly isoflavones, or mostly carbohydrate).
  - `Soy Isoflavones` — the phytoestrogen fraction (genistein + daidzein + glycitein, in glycoside or aglycone form).
  - `Genistein` — the most-studied single isoflavone aglycone.
  - `Daidzein` — second isoflavone; precursor to **equol** (made by gut bacteria in only some people).
  - `Glycine Soja Protein` / non-denatured soy protein — carries the **STI (soybean trypsin inhibitor)** and **BBI (Bowman-Birk inhibitor)** serine-protease inhibitors. This is a *different* active from the isoflavones.
  - `Glycine Soja Oil` (Soybean Oil) — emollient lipid; chemically unrelated to the isoflavones or the protease inhibitors. Do not conflate.
  - `Hydrolyzed Soy Protein` — film-former/conditioning peptides; another distinct fraction.
- **Key bioactive forms / derivatives:**
  - **Isoflavone glycosides** (genistin, daidzin, glycitin) — the forms naturally abundant in soy; **less bioactive** than the aglycones until deconjugated.
  - **Isoflavone aglycones** (genistein, daidzein, glycitein) — the active forms; more skin-active and the forms used in most efficacy research.
  - **Equol** — a gut-microbiome metabolite of daidzein; a stronger, more stable estrogen-receptor-β ligand than its parent. Only **~30–50% of people are "equol producers"** → a built-in responder/non-responder problem (see Vijayakumar 2025, §4).
  - **STI / BBI** — the **non-denatured** soy protein protease inhibitors responsible for the depigmentation effect. **Heat/denaturation destroys this activity** — so processed soy and most soybean oil have no PAR-2 effect.
- **Critical taxonomy note for our brand:** Evidence does **not** transfer between the isoflavone fraction, the protease-inhibitor protein fraction, and soybean oil. The depigmentation RCT used a *non-denatured protein* moisturizer; the wrinkle research used *isoflavones/genistein*; a "soybean oil" product trades on neither. Sloppy "soy extract" sourcing and claim-swapping is the #1 risk with this ingredient.

## 2. Class & mechanism (skin / cell level)

Soy works through three largely independent mechanisms, mapped to its three fractions:

**A. Isoflavones (genistein/daidzein) — phytoestrogen + kinase/antioxidant signaling**
1. **Estrogen-receptor (ER-β-preferential) agonism.** Isoflavones weakly bind estrogen receptors. Skin is estrogen-responsive (ERs on keratinocytes/fibroblasts), and estrogen decline at menopause drives collagen loss, dryness and thinning — the rationale for using phytoestrogens on estrogen-deficient skin. Binding affinity is **weak** vs estradiol, which is why isoflavones underperform estrogen in head-to-head trials (Moraes 2009; Silva 2017).
2. **TGF-β/Smad activation → collagen.** Daidzein and glycitin upregulate type I/III/IV collagen, elastin and fibrillin-1 mRNA and inhibit MMP-1/-2 via TGF-β/phospho-Smad2/3 signaling in dermal fibroblasts (Zhao et al.; Mi-Sun et al. — in-vitro).
3. **Antioxidant / anti-inflammatory kinase modulation.** Genistein is a tyrosine-kinase inhibitor; it dampens UVB-driven MAPK (ERK/JNK/p38), NF-κB, STAT3 and COX-2, reduces ROS, and supports SOD/CAT/GPx — reducing the oxidative/inflammatory cascade of photoaging (Iovine, Chiu, Wang, Smolińska — in-vitro/animal).
4. **DNA-repair support.** Genistein preserved PCNA and reduced UV pyrimidine-dimer formation in reconstituted skin (Moore et al.).

**B. Protease-inhibitor protein fraction (STI + BBI) — the pigmentation mechanism**
5. **PAR-2 blockade → less melanosome transfer.** Keratinocytes (not melanocytes) express **protease-activated receptor-2 (PAR-2)**, which drives phagocytosis of melanosomes from melanocytes. STI/BBI inhibit PAR-2 cleavage, reducing melanosome uptake and thereby **visible pigmentation** — without killing melanocytes or inhibiting tyrosinase. This is a genuinely distinct, mechanistically elegant depigmentation route (Paine/Seiberg 2001). **Requires non-denatured soy** — heat destroys it.

**C. Soybean oil + soy carbohydrates** — emolliency, occlusion, mild humectancy. Bland, supportive only.

**Bottom line on mechanism:** plausible and multi-pathway, but the dermal/collagen mechanisms are mostly demonstrated in cell culture and rodents. The PAR-2 pigmentation mechanism is the most cleanly characterized and the one that actually carries through to a human topical RCT.

## 4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core)

> **Read this as the verdict driver.** The honest pattern: **(a) the pigmentation/even-tone benefit is the one with a real human topical RCT (protease-inhibitor fraction); (b) the wrinkle/firmness story is strongest ORALLY and, where tested topically, isoflavones consistently underperformed estradiol; (c) the antioxidant/anti-inflammatory/collagen mechanisms are robust in cells/animals but largely unproven in living human skin topically; (d) the responder problem (equol producers) and the EU 0.007% genistein cap further complicate the topical isoflavone story.**

### TIER 1 — In-vitro (mechanistically rich; many studies)

*(Comprehensively catalogued in Smith/Michalak-Tomczyk et al. 2024 review, *Int J Mol Sci*, PMC11643064.)*

1. **Paine, Sharlow, Liebel, Eisinger, Shapiro & Seiberg (2001), *J Invest Dermatol* 116(4):587–595.** The foundational pigmentation paper. Soymilk and purified **STI/BBI** inhibited **PAR-2 cleavage**, reduced keratinocyte phagocytosis of melanosomes, and depigmented in cultured cells. *Defines the mechanism.* Limitation: in-vitro core; activity depends on non-denatured protein.
2. **Zhao et al. (in-vitro, NHDFs).** Daidzein (0.5–50 µg/mL) increased collagen synthesis, inhibited MMP-1/-2, raised TGF-β and phospho-Smad2/3. *Best mechanistic collagen dataset.* Limitation: monolayer fibroblasts.
3. **Iovine et al. (2 studies, UVB fibroblasts).** Genistein + daidzein (2–10 µM) synergistically enhanced **Gadd45 (DNA repair)** and suppressed **COX-2**; glycosides/aglycone extracts outperformed single aglycones. Limitation: in-vitro; synergy-dependent.
4. **Moore et al. (reconstituted skin).** Genistein (10–50 µM) preserved PCNA and reduced UV **pyrimidine-dimer** formation. *Closest-to-skin in-vitro model.*
5. **Chiu et al. / Chiang et al. (HaCaT + mouse).** Isoflavone fractions (0.5–1%; 43.8 µg/g) protected keratinocytes from UVB (40–50 mJ/cm²) by inhibiting JNK/ERK/p38 phosphorylation and preserving catalase.
6. **Wang et al.; Smolińska et al.; Li et al. (anti-inflammatory).** Genistein (5–100 µM) suppressed IL-1β/IL-6/IL-8/IL-23/TNF-α and NF-κB/STAT3/MAPK signaling in stimulated keratinocytes. *Strong inflammaging mechanism in cells.*
7. **Sienkiewicz et al. (dose-dependent caution).** Genistein was **protective at 1 µM but inhibitory of collagen at 10–100 µM** under oxidative stress — an important reminder that high concentrations can backfire (genistein also reduces collagen/fibroblast proliferation in hypertrophic-scar tissue at 50–100 µM).

### TIER 2 — Animal / ex-vivo

8. **Polito et al. (2012), *Br J Pharmacol* 165(4):994–1005.** Ovariectomized rats, **subcutaneous** genistein (1 mg/kg/day, 12 wk) vs estradiol vs raloxifene. Genistein restored collagen, modulated TGF-β1/VEGF/MMP-2/MMP-9, and produced **greater skin breaking strength than all other treatments (P<0.001)**. *Strong estrogen-deficient-skin model.* Limitations: rats; **injected, not topical**; supraphysiologic context.
9. **Chiu et al.; Huang et al.; Wei et al.; Brand & Jendrzejewski (topical, mice).** Topical soy isoflavone fractions before UVB reduced epidermal thickening, erythema, TEWL, desquamation, COX-2/PCNA, sunburn cells and leukocyte infiltration; genistein cut UVB-induced H₂O₂ >50% and MDA 30–50% in SKH-1 mice. *Consistent topical photoprotection signal — in mice.*
10. **Wang et al.; Li et al. (topical genistein, psoriasis-like mouse models).** Genistein cream (0.5–2%) reduced epidermal thickness and inflammatory cytokines. *Anti-inflammatory, but disease model, not aging.*
11. **Topical 2% soy extract emulsion (human pilot, n=21, double-blind).** Increased dermal-papillae number / rejuvenated the dermal-epidermal junction — small, often cited as the lone double-blind topical-extract anti-aging signal. **[primary citation not located in verification — no author/journal/year was traceable; treat as unverified secondary-source claim until a primary reference is found.]**

### TIER 3 — Human, ORAL (the cleaner *human* anti-aging data — note: not topical)

12. **Izumi, Saito, Obata, Arii, Yamaguchi, Matsuyama (2007), *J Nutr Sci Vitaminol* — double-blind, placebo-controlled RCT.** 26 Japanese women (late 30s–40s), **40 mg isoflavone aglycones/day, 12 wk.** Significant improvement in **fine wrinkles at the lateral eye (wk 12, p<0.05)** and **malar elasticity (wk 8, p<0.05)**; no adverse events. Limitations: **n=26**, short, single ethnicity.
13. **Rizzo, Sivamani et al. (2023), *Nutrients* 15(19):4113 — prospective, randomized, double-blind, casein-controlled RCT.** 44 postmenopausal women, **30 g/day soy protein isolate + 50 mg isoflavones, 24 wk.** Wrinkle severity −7.1% (wk 24); **skin hydration +39–68%**; pigment intensity −2.4%; no sebum change. Limitations: small; Fitzpatrick I–III only; heavy dietary restrictions; oral. **Industry-funded: United Soybean Board + Soy Nutrition Institute Global (soy-industry bodies); sponsor stated no role in conduct/analysis, but lead author Sivamani carries extensive industry consulting ties. Treat hydration headline with caution.**
14. **Takuathung et al. (2023), *J Clin Med* — randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled.** *(Note: title indexed as "nutraceutical product" but this trial is actually a **topical** cream of 4% genistein + 1% vit E + 1% B3 + 0.2% ceramide; see Tier 4.)* n=50, 6 wk.
15. **Vijayakumar et al. (2025), *Front Nutr* — triple-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled pilot.** 66 postmenopausal women, **oral 80 mg soy isoflavones (glycosides)/day, 12 wk.** Crow's-feet roughness improved 5.6% vs −1.6% placebo (**NS**); benefit concentrated in **S-equol producers** (only ~47% of the soy arm) and older women. *Cleanest demonstration of the equol responder problem.* Limitations: underpowered pilot; **NS primary endpoint** (the responder/older-women findings are *exploratory post-hoc* subgroups); low baseline soy intake. **Heavily conflicted: fully funded by ADM, all eight authors are ADM employees, the product is ADM's NovaSoy®400, and ADM was involved in conceptualization, analysis and writing. This is the single most COI-laden study cited here — the equol-responder narrative rests on an industry post-hoc analysis with a null primary result.**
16. **"Oral soy isoflavone" wrinkle literature (Wiley *Phytother Res* 2019 review, ptr.6538)** — pooled context: oral phytoestrogens show modest, fairly consistent gains in collagen/water content/oxidative markers, with high heterogeneity.

### TIER 4 — Human, TOPICAL

**4a. Pigmentation / photoaging — non-denatured soy protein (STI/BBI): the standout topical evidence.**
- **Wallo, Nebus & Leyden (2007), *J Drugs Dermatol* — double-blind, vehicle-controlled RCT.** **65 women**, moderate facial photodamage, **12 wk**, soy moisturizer with **non-denatured STI + BBI**. Significantly better than vehicle for **mottled hyperpigmentation, blotchiness, dullness, fine lines, overall texture, tone and appearance**; improvements from wk 2. *The single best human topical soy trial.* Limitations: vehicle-controlled (not vs an active comparator like niacinamide/HQ); finished-product (can't isolate STI/BBI contribution from the base); industry (Johnson & Johnson/Aveeno) authorship.
- **Leyden, Wallo (2011), *Int J Dermatol* 50(4):470–477 — review.** Summarizes the soy/STI-BBI mechanism and clinical hyperpigmentation benefit; concludes soy is a safe, effective option for hyperpigmentation/even tone.

**4b. Wrinkles / firmness — topical isoflavones/genistein: real but modest, and beaten by estrogen.**
- **Moraes et al. (2009), *Eur J Obstet Gynecol Reprod Biol* — randomized, double-blind, estrogen-controlled.** 36 postmenopausal women; **topical isoflavones 40% (genistein 4%)** vs **0.01% estradiol**, 24 wk, with biopsies. Both improved, but **estradiol >> isoflavones**: epidermal thickness +75% vs +20%; dermal papillae +125% (estrogen) vs no significant isoflavone gain; fibroblasts +123% vs no significant isoflavone gain; vessels +77% vs +36%. *Topical genistein "works" but is clearly the weaker partner.*
- **Silva et al. (2017), *Gynecol Endocrinol* 33(11):845–848 — randomized, double-blind.** 30 postmenopausal women; topical estradiol vs topical genistein, 24 wk; pre-auricular biopsies. **Both raised type I/III collagen; estradiol superior (p<0.001).** Authors flagged the open question of **systemic absorption** of topical phytoestrogens.
- **Takuathung et al. (2023), *J Clin Med* — double-blind, placebo-controlled.** **Topical** cream of **4% genistein** + vit E + B3 + ceramide, n=50 postmenopausal women, twice daily 6 wk. Mixed result: **skin redness increased** vs placebo; pores/area decreased; wrinkle reduction only in a **post-hoc subgroup** (≥56 y, compliant). Limitations: short; combination product (can't credit genistein alone); subgroup-driven positives; redness signal.
- **"Novadiol" / topical isoflavone cream (open European multicenter study).** Frequently cited "**−22% wrinkles, −24% [trans-epidermal water] loss vs untreated**" figures. **Open-label, not vehicle-controlled, manufacturer (Vichy/L'Oréal) cosmetic study** — treat the headline numbers as marketing-grade, not RCT-grade. [verify primary citation — appears only in secondary/marketing sources]

**Topical vs borrowed — the explicit map:**

| Benefit | Proven for *topical human soy* in a controlled trial? | Where the evidence actually comes from |
|---|---|---|
| Even tone / hyperpigmentation | ✅ Yes (vehicle-controlled RCT, n=65) | Wallo 2007 — non-denatured STI/BBI protein fraction |
| Texture / blotchiness / overall appearance | ✅ Yes (same RCT) | Wallo 2007 (finished product) |
| Wrinkle reduction | ◐ Partial / modest | Topical isoflavone trials (all lost to estradiol; one needed a subgroup) + oral RCTs |

| Elasticity | ◐ Mostly oral | Izumi 2007 (oral) |

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **Isoflavones penetrate modestly; aglycones > glycosides.** Genistein/daidzein aglycones are small (~270 Da), moderately lipophilic, and do reach viable epidermis/dermis to a degree (supported by the biopsy-level changes in topical genistein trials). The **glycosides** (genistin/daidzin) are larger and more hydrophilic → poorer penetration and lower activity; this is why suppliers favor aglycone-enriched ("non-denatured" / fermented) fractions and why **delivery systems (nanoemulsions, liposomes, nanoemulsion-hydrogels) are an active formulation focus** to raise skin permeability.
- **The protease inhibitors (STI/BBI) are large proteins (~8–20 kDa).** These do **not** meaningfully penetrate to the dermis and don't need to — their target (PAR-2 on keratinocytes / melanosome transfer) is in the **upper epidermis**, exactly where a topical protein can act. This is part of why the pigmentation effect translates to topical use better than the dermal collagen effect.
- **Practical implication:** for tone/pigmentation, surface-to-upper-epidermis activity (non-denatured protein) is sufficient and well-matched to topical delivery. For dermal/anti-wrinkle isoflavone effects, expect to need **aglycone-enriched actives + a penetration-enhancing vehicle**, and even then effects are modest vs estrogen.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:**
  - **Soybean seed extract** (cosmetic): commonly **0.1–2%** of product (CIR/usage data); up to ~2% in face products.
  - **Non-denatured soy / STI-BBI moisturizers** (pigmentation): commercial finished products (e.g., Aveeno Positively Radiant line) — exact STI/BBI % proprietary; efficacy demonstrated at finished-product level (Wallo 2007).
  - **Genistein (research-grade topical):** efficacy studies used **up to 4%** — but see regulatory cap below; **4% is not a legal EU cosmetic level.**
  - **Honest note:** there is no validated "use X% genistein for Y% wrinkle reduction" cosmetic dose; the strong-% studies are pharmaceutical/estrogen-comparison contexts.
- **pH:** Isoflavones are reasonably stable across mildly acidic to neutral pH (~4–7). The **protease-inhibitor proteins are pH- and heat-sensitive** — keep formulation gentle (avoid high temperature processing, extremes of pH) to preserve the "non-denatured" activity that the pigmentation benefit depends on.
- **Stability:** Isoflavones are fairly robust antioxidants but extracts can **discolor** (yellow/tan) and oxidize; protect with opaque/airless packaging. **Soybean oil oxidizes** (rancidity) — needs tocopherol. The protein fraction is the fragile one — heat-pasteurized or over-processed soy loses the depigmenting effect.
- **Pairings:**
  - **Niacinamide** — complementary even-tone mechanism (niacinamide also blocks melanosome transfer); natural pairing for a brightening/tone story.
  - **Vitamin C, ferulic acid, vitamin E** — antioxidant/brightening synergy (the Takuathung topical used vit E + B3 + ceramide).
  - **Sunscreen** — essential to any pigmentation/photoaging claim; soy is not a sunscreen.
  - **Soy + retinoids/AHAs** — fine, but watch cumulative irritation; soy is often positioned as the *gentle* alternative.
- **Conflicts / cautions:** Strong oxidizers and high-pH/high-heat systems (denature the protein). Possible tint in white/clear bases.
- **Sensory:** Soybean oil = light-medium emollient (nice slip, low odor). Extracts at low % have minimal sensory impact; some can add faint color/odor.

## 7. Safety

- **General safety:** Soy-derived cosmetic ingredients have a long, broad history of safe topical use. **CIR (Cosmetic Ingredient Review)** assessed **28 soy-derived ingredients** (Burnett et al., 2024, *Int J Toxicol*; PMID 38342963) and found them **safe as used in cosmetics** in the present practices of use and concentration (functions: antioxidant, skin protectant, skin/hair conditioning). **EWG** rates soy isoflavones / Glycine soja extract low hazard (~1).
- **Allergen / sensitization:** **Soy is a recognized food allergen.** Topical cosmetic reactions are uncommon but possible, especially in soy-allergic individuals; idiosyncratic contact sensitization can occur. Patch-test prudent for sensitive-skin SKUs; a "contains soy" advisory is appropriate.
- **Irritation:** Low at cosmetic levels. Note the **Takuathung 2023 topical 4%-genistein trial saw increased redness** vs placebo — high-% genistein may not be as benign as low-% extract.
- **Estrogenicity / endocrine concern (the headline caution):**
  - Isoflavones are **phytoestrogens** with **weak** ER binding; in-vivo endocrine effects appear only at high doses (animal data). **Dietary** soy is regarded as safe — major bodies (ACS, AICR) state soy is safe even for breast-cancer survivors, and meta-analyses do not link soy intake to increased breast-cancer risk.
  - **EU SCCS treated genistein & daidzein as priority potential endocrine disruptors** and set cosmetic limits (§8). The **systemic absorption of topical phytoestrogens is an explicitly flagged open question** (Silva 2017). For a brand, the prudent posture is **low cosmetic concentrations + conservative claims**, and caution in messaging around pregnancy/hormone-sensitive consumers.
- **Sun sensitivity:** No evidence soy is photosensitizing; data point toward photo-*protection*. Still not a sunscreen.
- **Pregnancy:** No specific topical contraindication in the literature reviewed, but given the phytoestrogen + unresolved systemic-absorption questions, **defer to "consult your doctor"** framing rather than asserting safety. (Not medical advice.)
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** Soy is abundant and cheap, but carries **deforestation / land-use baggage** (esp. non-certified South American soy). For a premium brand, source **certified-sustainable / non-GMO / identity-preserved** soy and be ready to substantiate it (see §9).

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Cosmetic vs drug line.** Defensible **cosmetic** language: "helps visibly even skin tone," "helps reduce the look of dark spots / blotchiness," "antioxidant," "helps improve the appearance of fine lines," "for mature/menopausal skin," "soothing." Crossing into **drug** territory: "treats melasma/hyperpigmentation," "boosts collagen," "replaces estrogen," "hormone therapy for skin," "repairs DNA," "prevents skin cancer." Avoid all of those in consumer copy.
- **The EU genistein/daidzein cap (critical, brand-specific).** The **SCCS** (Opinion SCCS/o_263, *final* 2022) concluded **genistein is safe only up to 0.007%** and **daidzein up to 0.02%** in cosmetics, citing endocrine-disruption potential. *(Correction from an earlier draft of this dossier: the "daidzein inconclusive" wording was the SCCS **preliminary** opinion; the **final** opinion gave daidzein a definitive 0.02% safe level — verified 2026-06-19 against the SCCS opinion and trade summaries. Both molecules are capped far below research levels, which if anything strengthens the regulatory argument below.)* This means the **4% genistein** used in the strongest topical studies is **not a permissible EU cosmetic concentration** — we cannot legally formulate (or imply the results of) those studies in an EU cosmetic. If we sell into the EU/UK, soy isoflavone actives must be dosed at these tiny caps (which undercuts the dermal anti-wrinkle rationale) or we lean on the **protease-inhibitor (STI/BBI) protein** route, which is not the capped molecule.
- **The "fraction-swap" trap.** Don't sell an *isoflavone* wrinkle product on the *protease-inhibitor* pigmentation RCT (Wallo 2007), or vice versa. They are different actives with different evidence.
- **The "borrowed format" trap.** Oral-study results (Izumi elasticity, Rizzo hydration +39–68%) must **not** be presented as topical benefits.
- **The "beat estrogen" trap.** Never imply topical soy equals/replaces estrogen — the head-to-head trials show the opposite, and any "estrogen for skin" framing is drug-adjacent.
- **Equol responder caveat:** any "clinically shown" wrinkle claim derived from oral isoflavone trials should acknowledge the effect is responder-dependent (equol producers) — relevant if we ever do an ingestible.
- **Substantiation flag:** any efficacy claim beyond generic antioxidant/even-tone/appearance language should be flagged for legal/substantiation review.

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Forms & suppliers:** Standardized isoflavone/aglycone actives and non-denatured soy proteins are widely available (e.g., **Aveeno/Kenvue** hold the STI/BBI cosmetic IP; NovaSoy® (ADM) and similar standardized isoflavone extracts; numerous botanical-extract houses supply Glycine soja seed extract; soybean oil is a commodity).
- **Cost tier:** **Low** for soybean oil and generic soy extract; **low-to-mid** for standardized isoflavone/aglycone actives; the **non-denatured STI/BBI protein** is the most differentiated/IP-encumbered route (mid). Overall a **value ingredient**, not a premium-priced exotic — which is both a margin opportunity and a *brand-prestige* mismatch risk for a premium positioning.
- **Sustainability (handle with care):** Soy's sustainability story can be a **liability** (deforestation, GMO perceptions, intensive monoculture) unless deliberately sourced. Mitigations: certified-sustainable (RTRS/ProTerra), **non-GMO/identity-preserved**, or **upcycled soy byproduct** (okara/soy whey) — the upcycled angle is the most on-brand if we want a sustainability narrative.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

- **"Soy = natural estrogen / hormone-balancing skincare."** Overreach + claim risk. ER binding is weak, topical soy lost to estradiol in every head-to-head, and "estrogen for skin" is drug-adjacent.
- **"Clinically proven to reduce wrinkles 22%."** That figure traces to an **open-label manufacturer cosmetic study** (Novadiol), not a vehicle-controlled RCT. The RCT-grade topical win is **even tone**, not wrinkles.
- **"Boosts collagen."** Collagen↑ is in-vitro/rat for isoflavones; the one human topical collagen trial showed isoflavones **inferior to estrogen** — not a "boost" headline.
- **Fraction conflation.** The single biggest hype trap: marketing a soybean-oil or generic-extract product with the **pigmentation RCT** (which required *non-denatured STI/BBI protein*) or the **oral-supplement** wrinkle/hydration data. Evidence doesn't transfer across fractions or formats.
- **"Antioxidant powerhouse."** Genistein/daidzein are only **weak direct radical scavengers**; the antioxidant benefit is mostly indirect (enzyme/kinase modulation) and shown in cells/mice, not human skin.
- **Equol silence.** Oral isoflavone benefits *may* skew toward the ~30–50% who are equol producers — a responder nuance the category never discloses. Caveat on our own evidence: the cleanest "responder" demonstration (Vijayakumar 2025) had a **null primary endpoint** and is an **ADM-funded, ADM-authored post-hoc** subgroup analysis, so the responder effect is biologically plausible but not yet established on independent data.
- **Sustainability halo.** "Natural soy = green" ignores deforestation/GMO realities unless specifically sourced.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**Conditional → best as a SUPPORTING actor, with a specific narrow lane.** ⚠️

- **One-line reasoning:** Soy has **one genuinely RCT-backed topical benefit — visibly evening tone / reducing hyperpigmentation via the non-denatured STI/BBI protease-inhibitor fraction** — and that's where it earns a place; its anti-wrinkle/collagen story is real but modest, mostly oral or in-vitro, consistently beaten by estrogen, and (for the isoflavones) hemmed in by an EU 0.007%-genistein cap and phytoestrogen-claim risk.
- **Longevity-relevance note:** Tangential to a hardcore "longevity/senescence" thesis. Soy's mechanisms (TGF-β/Smad collagen support, NF-κB/STAT3 inflammaging dampening, ER-β signaling on estrogen-deficient skin) are aging-relevant *in principle*, but the human-topical proof is concentrated in pigmentation, not in any cellular-aging hallmark. It is a **tone/photoaging-appearance supporting active**, not a longevity hero.
- **Recommended path:**
  1. **If we pursue an even-tone / "menopausal & mature skin" angle:** use the **non-denatured soy protein (STI/BBI)** route, pair with **niacinamide + SPF**, and claim only "visibly evens tone / reduces the look of dark spots." This is the defensible, evidence-matched use.
  2. **Avoid building hero anti-aging/"collagen"/"estrogen for skin" claims** on soy — the evidence and EU regulation don't support it, and it conflicts with the brand's claims-discipline guardrail.
  3. **If a premium positioning is chosen,** weigh the commodity/cost and sustainability-perception mismatch; a non-GMO/upcycled sourcing story would be needed to make soy feel premium.

## 13. Evidence verification (adversarial)

_Independent skeptical re-check performed 2026-06-19. Method: each headline efficacy claim was tested against primary/abstract sources via web search and direct fetch — (a) is the evidence HUMAN and TOPICAL, or quietly borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral data; (b) are the cited studies real and do they say what is claimed; (c) are key studies industry-funded or very small. Studies independently confirmed: Paine/Seiberg 2001, Wallo 2007, Izumi 2007, Moraes 2009, Silva 2017, Rizzo 2023, Takuathung 2023, Vijayakumar 2025, Polito 2012, SCCS o_263, CIR/Burnett 2024._

### Overall evidence grade: **B− (for the ONE narrow topical claim) / C overall**

- **For "visibly evens tone / reduces hyperpigmentation" via the non-denatured STI/BBI protein fraction:** **B−** (moderate human-topical). One real, well-conducted 12-week double-blind **vehicle-controlled** RCT (Wallo 2007, n=65) plus a coherent, cleanly characterized PAR-2 mechanism. Held below "B/B+" because it is a single trial, on a **finished product** (can't isolate the STI/BBI contribution from the base), **vehicle- not active-controlled**, and authored by the product owner (J&J/Aveeno). Independent replication is essentially absent.
- **For everything else soy is marketed on (wrinkles, collagen/"firming," antioxidant, anti-inflammaging):** **C to D**. Wrinkle/collagen human-topical data exist but are modest and **consistently lost to estradiol** in every head-to-head (Moraes 2009, Silva 2017); the firmer wrinkle/hydration/elasticity wins are **ORAL** (Izumi, Rizzo) and **industry-funded**; antioxidant/anti-inflammaging benefits are **in-vitro/animal only (D for human-topical)**.

The dossier's own §4/§11 framing already lands close to this; the grade is **not** a downgrade of the document — it is a confirmation that the honest, narrow read is the correct one.

### Verification result on the existing dossier

**The dossier is substantially accurate and unusually disciplined.** It does the hard things right: it splits the three chemically distinct fractions, refuses to let evidence transfer between them, separates topical from oral from in-vitro, hedges the Novadiol marketing numbers, and already flags Wallo's industry authorship. Every named study with a citation is **real** and its headline numbers, sample sizes and designs **match the sources** (e.g., Wallo n=65 from wk 2; Moraes epidermal +75% vs +20%, no significant isoflavone gain in papillae/fibroblasts; Izumi n=26 / 40 mg / wrinkle wk 12 & elasticity wk 8; Silva n=30 both collagens up, estradiol superior; Rizzo hydration +39–68%; Takuathung redness↑ with subgroup-only wrinkle benefit; Vijayakumar NS primary + equol subgroup). No fabricated or misquoted study was found.

### Main caveats (what a buyer of this ingredient must keep front-of-mind)

1. **Only one human-topical RCT does real work**, and it is a finished-product, vehicle-controlled, manufacturer-authored study. The entire defensible topical case rests on Wallo 2007. There is no independent topical replication of the pigmentation benefit.
2. **The positive *human* evidence is overwhelmingly industry-linked.** Wallo 2007 = J&J/Aveeno. Rizzo 2023 = United Soybean Board + Soy Nutrition Institute Global. Vijayakumar 2025 = ADM, all authors ADM employees, ADM's own NovaSoy product. The independent (non-industry) human-topical evidence is the estrogen-comparison trials — and those are the ones where **soy lost**.
3. **The equol-responder story is weaker than it reads.** Its cleanest "demonstration" (Vijayakumar 2025) had a **null primary endpoint**; the responder effect is an **exploratory post-hoc subgroup in an ADM-run trial**. Plausible, not established.
4. **Topical anti-wrinkle/collagen evidence is real but inferior to estrogen and partly borrowed from oral/animal data.** Never present oral results (Izumi elasticity, Rizzo hydration) as topical.
5. **Antioxidant / anti-inflammaging / DNA-repair claims are in-vitro and rodent only** — zero human-topical RCT support. (The dossier already marks these ❌ in §4; keep them out of consumer copy entirely.)
6. **Regulatory ceiling is real and the dossier's argument is, if anything, now *stronger*:** SCCS final opinion caps genistein at 0.007% **and** daidzein at 0.02% — the earlier "daidzein inconclusive" line was the superseded preliminary opinion and has been corrected in §8/§12. The 4% genistein of the research literature is not an EU-legal cosmetic dose.

### Claims corrected/softened in this pass

- **§8 & §12 — "daidzein safety inconclusive" → corrected.** SCCS *final* opinion sets daidzein at a definitive safe 0.02%; "inconclusive" was the preliminary opinion (factual error fixed).
- **Tier 3 Rizzo 2023 — added undisclosed industry funding** (United Soybean Board / SNI Global) and softened the hydration headline.
- **Tier 3 Vijayakumar 2025 — added the full ADM conflict** (funder + all-author employment + own product), labelled the responder/older-women findings as exploratory post-hoc, and emphasized the null primary endpoint.
- **§10 equol bullet — softened** from an asserted responder fact to "may skew," with the COI/null-primary caveat attached.
- **Tier 2 "n=21 topical pilot" — flagged as unverified** (no traceable author/journal/year located).

### Claims that remain well-supported (no change needed)

- The pigmentation/even-tone benefit as a **moderate, RCT-backed, mechanism-matched** topical use (Wallo 2007 + Paine 2001).
- The "isoflavones lose to estradiol topically" conclusion (Moraes 2009, Silva 2017 — both independent of soy industry).
- The fraction-non-transfer thesis, the EU cap argument, and the §11 verdict that soy is a **supporting even-tone actor, not an anti-aging hero**. The adversarial read affirms this verdict.
