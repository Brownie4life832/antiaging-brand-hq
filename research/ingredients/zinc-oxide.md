# Ingredient: Zinc Oxide, incl. nano (INCI: Zinc Oxide / Zinc Oxide [nano])

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ✅ shortlisted (as a photoprotection filter — see verdict)
**Class:** Sunscreen / photoprotection filters & adjuncts
**Slug:** zinc-oxide

> **One-line honest summary:** Zinc oxide is one of only two UV filters the US FDA proposes as *generally recognized as safe and effective* (GRASE), and it is the single best **broad-spectrum, photostable, well-tolerated mineral filter** available. Daily broad-spectrum sunscreen use is the **most evidence-backed "anti-aging" intervention that exists** (one good RCT). But almost none of that anti-aging RCT evidence is for **zinc oxide specifically** — it is for *broad-spectrum sunscreen as a category*. ZnO's own direct topical benefits beyond UV filtering (anti-inflammatory, barrier, acne) are real but **weakly evidenced**. Treat it as a workhorse filter and skin protectant, not a hero "active."

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common name:** Zinc oxide (ZnO). Historically "Chinese white," "flowers of zinc," "calamine" (calamine = ZnO + small amount of iron oxide).
- **INCI:** `Zinc Oxide`; nano grade declared as `Zinc Oxide (nano)` in the EU.
- **CAS:** 1314-13-2. **Chemical:** inorganic metal oxide, a wide-band-gap (~3.2–3.3 eV) semiconductor.
- **Key forms / grades used in skincare:**
  - **Non-nano** (particle size >100 nm; USP grades commonly ~200–350 nm). Higher white cast, favored for "reef-safe"/non-nano marketing.
  - **Nano / micronized** (typically ~30–200 nm primary particle size; the EU nano definition is <100 nm). More cosmetically transparent, less white cast, but raises (largely resolved) penetration/inhalation questions — see §5, §7.
  - **Coated grades** — surface-treated with silica, alumina, dimethicone/silicone, stearic acid, or triethoxycaprylylsilane to suppress photocatalytic ROS, improve dispersion, reduce agglomeration, and add water resistance (e.g., dimethicone-coated "Zcote HP-1"). Coating is a real formulation lever, not a cosmetic afterthought.
  - **Pre-dispersions** — ZnO pre-dispersed in esters/silicones at the supplier to avoid clumping at the bench.
- **Pharmacopeia status:** USP/Ph.Eur. monographed; pharmaceutical grade is widely available.

## 2. Class & mechanism (how it works at the skin/cell level)

Zinc oxide is an **inorganic ("physical"/"mineral") UV filter**. It is also a topical skin protectant/astringent in its own right.

**Photoprotection mechanism — correct the common myth:**
The popular description that mineral filters "sit on top of the skin and physically reflect/scatter UV like tiny mirrors" is **largely wrong**. Cole, Silverman & Bonitatibus (2016) measured the reflectance of ZnO and TiO₂ across the UV range at only **~4–5% (less than SPF 2)**; the overwhelming majority of protection (~95%) comes from **semiconductor band-gap absorption** of UV photons, which are then dissipated as heat — the same end-state as organic filters [1]. Reflection/scattering only becomes dominant at *long-UVA/visible* wavelengths above the band gap (which is what causes white cast). So ZnO is mostly an *absorber*, like the chemical filters, but as a stable solid that stays on the surface.

- **Spectral coverage:** Broad spectrum, including **long-wave UVA (UVA1, >360–370 nm)**. ZnO is one of the few filters (with avobenzone) that meaningfully attenuates UVA >360 nm; TiO₂ tails off below ~360 nm (ZnO's absorption peak sits ~360 nm vs TiO₂'s ~290 nm) [2]. *(Verification note: the qualitative UVA1 advantage of ZnO/avobenzone over TiO₂ is well-supported; an earlier draft cited a precise "~42% more long-UVA" figure that could not be traced to a verifiable source, so that number has been removed — see Evidence verification, §13.)*
- **Photostability:** ZnO does not photodegrade in normal use — a major advantage over photolabile organic filters (e.g., avobenzone) that lose potency and can form irritating breakdown products [1, 12].
- **Photocatalytic ROS (the catch):** As a semiconductor, UV-excited ZnO can generate reactive oxygen species (superoxide, hydroxyl radicals) at the particle surface. Uncoated nano ZnO can produce substantial ROS under UVA. This is why cosmetic grades are **surface-coated** (silica/alumina/silicone) to passivate the surface — though coatings *reduce but do not fully eliminate* photocatalytic activity [9, 10].
- **Direct skin actions (non-UV):** Zinc is an essential trace element and cofactor for >300 enzymes (including matrix metalloproteinases and antioxidant enzymes). Solubilized Zn²⁺ at the surface contributes mild astringent, antimicrobial, and anti-inflammatory effects and supports keratinocyte migration/re-epithelialization in wound models — mechanistically plausible but clinically modest topically (see §4) [6, 14].

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

| Hallmark | Relevance of ZnO | Strength (for *topical human* use) |
|---|---|---|
| **Photoaging** | **Primary.** Broad-spectrum + UVA1 filtering prevents the single largest driver of extrinsic aging (collagen breakdown, elastosis, dyspigmentation). | **Strong — but as a UV filter / category, not ZnO-specific** |
| **Collagen/ECM loss** | Indirect — by blocking UVA/UVB that upregulate MMPs and degrade collagen. | Moderate (mechanistic + sunscreen-category RCT) |
| **Pigmentation/tone** | Indirect — UVA drives persistent pigment darkening and melasma; ZnO's UVA1 coverage helps. | Moderate (mechanistic + photoprotection literature) |
| **Oxidative stress** | Mixed/double-edged — *prevents* UV-induced ROS in skin by filtering UV; but *uncoated* ZnO can itself *generate* ROS photocatalytically. Net depends on coating/formulation. | Weak/conditional |
| **Inflammaging** | Plausible — zinc modulates cytokines; soothes irritation. | Weak (mostly diaper/wound/eczema context) |
| **Barrier decline / water loss** | Plausible — occlusive/protectant film, barrier-soothing. | Weak (anecdotal + diaper-dermatitis use) |
| Cellular senescence | Not a direct target. | None topical |
| Mitochondrial decline | Not a direct target. | None topical |
| Glycation / AGEs | Not a direct target. | None topical |
| Microbiome | Mild antimicrobial (Zn²⁺); could shift surface flora. | Weak/unclear |

**Bottom line on hallmarks:** ZnO's anti-aging value is almost entirely *upstream prevention via photoprotection*, not direct repair.

## 4. Evidence review by tier (the core of this dossier)

Honest framing throughout: **what is proven for topical human use vs. borrowed from another format (oral supplements, wound models, in-vitro, animal).**

### Tier A — In-vitro
- **Cole, Shyr & Ou-Yang 2016** (*Photodermatol Photoimmunol Photomed* 32(1):5–10): spectroscopic measurement showing metal-oxide filters protect ~95% by **absorption**, ~4–5% by reflection/scatter [1]. *Mechanistic, not efficacy.*
- **Smijs & Pavel 2011** (Nanotechnology, Science and Applications): review documenting that uncoated nano TiO₂/ZnO generate superoxide and hydroxyl radicals under UV (cyto-/genotoxic in cell culture); silica coatings reduce but don't eliminate this; deeper penetration mainly in *impaired/diseased* skin in vitro [10].
- **Acne systematic review (Crainic et al. 2025, Biomedicines):** of 8 studies, **5 were in-vitro** — HA-coated ZnO NPs showed ~16-fold selective kill of *Cutibacterium/Staph*; plant nanogels gave inhibition zones ~11% wider than benzoyl peroxide [8]. *In-vitro antibacterial, not clinical.*
- **Limitation:** in-vitro ROS/antibacterial findings do not establish topical clinical benefit and can overstate risk (cells lack the stratum corneum barrier).

### Tier B — Animal
- In the same acne review, **2 animal studies**: zero zebrafish mortality at 100 µg/mL (safety signal), but **20 nm sunscreen-grade ZnO delayed rat wound closure by ~38%** — a caution that small-particle ZnO is not automatically pro-healing [8].
- Classic rodent/wound-healing literature (reviewed in Lansdown; MDPI *Nutrients* 2018) supports zinc's role in re-epithelialization but is largely animal/mechanistic [14].

### Tier C — Human ORAL (borrowed format — do NOT claim for topical)
- Oral zinc has a real evidence base for *acne* (anti-inflammatory, modest efficacy, inferior to antibiotics) and for *wound healing in the zinc-deficient*. **These are oral data and must not be transferred to a topical ZnO cosmetic claim.** Flagged here only to prevent contamination of topical messaging.

### Tier D — Human TOPICAL

**D1 — Photoprotection / anti-aging (the strongest, most relevant evidence — but mostly category-level):**
- **Hughes et al. 2013, *Annals of Internal Medicine*** (Nambour Skin Cancer Prevention Trial): RCT-derived, n=903 adults <55; **daily broad-spectrum sunscreen use produced ~24% less skin aging** (skin-surface microtopography) over 4.5 years vs discretionary use (odds 0.76, 95% CI 0.59–0.98) [3]. **This is the landmark "sunscreen slows visible aging" human trial.** *Critical honesty point:* the trial used a **broad-spectrum SPF sunscreen, NOT a zinc-oxide-specific product.* It validates *the category/behavior*, and ZnO is an excellent way to deliver broad-spectrum SPF — but it is **not** direct proof that ZnO specifically slows aging.
- The Nambour trial also showed daily sunscreen **reduced squamous cell carcinoma and (in follow-up) melanoma** incidence — again, category-level, broad-spectrum.

**D2 — Penetration/safety in humans (directly about ZnO, strong):**
- **Mohammed et al. 2019, *J Invest Dermatol*** 139(2):308–315: 5 volunteers, ZnO-NP sunscreen applied hourly ×6 then daily ×5 days; multiphoton tomography + FLIM showed NPs **stayed in the stratum corneum/furrows, did not enter viable epidermis, no cellular toxicity**; small rise in soluble Zn²⁺ in epidermis with no harmful effect [4]. (Companion risk-assessment paper Gulson/Roberts group, same JID issue, reached the same allaying conclusion [4a].)
- **Holmes/Mohammed et al. (Int J Mol Sci 2021, MDPI):** confirmed measurable epidermal **zinc-ion** increase after topical ZnO-NP but intact NPs not penetrating — i.e., the particles stay put; only dissolved ions move minimally [5].
- *Limitation:* small n, healthy intact skin. Impaired-barrier/diseased skin may behave differently (Smijs & Pavel) [10].

**D3 — Wound healing / barrier / anti-inflammatory (weak, often null):**
- **Ågren et al. 2021, *Acta Derm Venereol*** 101:adv00465 — randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, **n=30**, bilateral axillary lancet/laser wounds, 1.1% ZnO emulsion: **no significant acceleration of re-epithelialization**; only "slightly attenuated" erythema around lancet wounds (p<0.05), no effect on laser wounds [6]. A well-run RCT that is essentially **negative** for ZnO speeding healing. This is the most rigorous topical-ZnO RCT and it should temper any "repair" messaging.
- Older **double-blind RCT** evidence supports ZnO-impregnated dressings for **chronic leg ulcers** (a medical context, not cosmetic) [14].
- **Diaper dermatitis:** ZnO is a guideline-mentioned barrier protectant — widespread clinical *use*, but the evidence is consensus/experience-based, not high-quality RCT [13].
- **Atopic dermatitis/eczema:** soothing/barrier use is common, but evidence that ZnO controls flares is **limited and inconsistent**; not first-line [13].

**D4 — Acne (topical ZnO, weak):**
- **Crainic et al. 2025 systematic review (Biomedicines):** only **1 exploratory human study** (twice-daily 0.5% ZnO nanogel → ~58% drop in inflammatory lesions at week 4), but it lacked vehicle control, randomization, and blinding. Total human n <30. Authors' verdict: clinical evidence **WEAK**; "clinical validation lags behind technological innovation" [8].

**What's proven topical for humans vs. borrowed:**
| Claim | Verdict |
|---|---|
| Broad-spectrum + UVA1 UV protection | **Proven (ZnO-specific, instrumental/in-vivo SPF/PFA data)** [1,2] |
| Sunscreen *behavior* slows visible photoaging | **Proven for the category/RCT — not ZnO-specifically** [3] |
| Doesn't meaningfully penetrate; low systemic risk | **Proven (ZnO-specific human studies)** [4,5] |
| Speeds wound healing / "repairs" skin (topical) | **Not proven — best RCT is null** [6] |
| Soothes/anti-inflammatory (topical) | **Weak / context-specific (diaper, mild erythema)** [6,13] |
| Treats acne (topical) | **Weak (one uncontrolled human study)** [8] |
| Antioxidant for skin | **Double-edged; uncoated ZnO can generate ROS** [9,10] |

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- The functional point of a UV filter is to **stay on the surface** — and ZnO does. Multiple human in-use studies show intact ZnO nanoparticles **remain in the stratum corneum and skin furrows**, not the viable epidermis [4, 5].
- A small amount of ZnO **dissolves to Zn²⁺** at the sunscreen–skin interface; trace zinc ions can be measured in the epidermis, but the increment is tiny relative to the body's endogenous zinc pool and showed no toxicity in human studies [4, 5]. The SCCS judged any absorbed solubilized zinc "small compared with the amount of zinc normally in the body" [11].
- **Caveats:** penetration can increase in **broken/diseased/barrier-impaired skin** (in-vitro evidence) [10]; this argues for caution on compromised skin and for coated grades.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:** Commercial sunscreens use roughly **10–25%** ZnO (FDA caps at **25%**). Rough mapping: ~10–15% → daily SPF 15–20; 15–20% → SPF 25–35; 20–25% → SPF 40–50+ [15]. ZnO alone is a comparatively *weak* SPF-per-percent filter, so high loads and/or co-filters are needed for high SPF (see pairings).
- **pH:** Keep the finished emulsion roughly **~6.5–7.5**. Below ~7, ZnO dissolves to Zn²⁺, migrating into the water phase, destabilizing the emulsion and promoting agglomeration [16]. This is a real conflict with **low-pH actives** (see below).
- **Stability:** ZnO itself is extremely photostable and shelf-stable; the *formulation* challenges are **agglomeration, viscosity drift, and salt/ion interactions**, mitigated by coated grades, pre-dispersions, and chelation control [16, 17].
- **Pairings (synergy):**
  - With **iron oxides / tints** → reduces white cast and adds visible-light/blue-light protection (relevant for pigmentation).
  - With **avobenzone or other organic UVA/UVB filters** (in chemical/hybrid markets) → higher SPF at lower ZnO load.
  - With **antioxidants (vitamin C/E, etc.)** as adjuncts to mop up any residual UV/photocatalytic ROS (e.g., silica-coated ZnO + vitamin C composites studied) [9].
  - With **silicones/esters** for dispersion and elegant feel.
- **Conflicts:**
  - **Low-pH actives** — L-ascorbic acid vitamin C, AHAs/BHAs, and (to a lesser degree) niacinamide-with-acid systems can drop pH, dissolve ZnO, and destabilize. Best separated (different products/steps) or formulated with coated ZnO and careful buffering.
  - **Cationic/ionic incompatibilities** and certain chelators/electrolytes can flocculate dispersions.
- **Sensory:** The classic problem is **white cast and heavy/draggy feel**, worst on deep skin tones and at high loads. Nano grades, coated/dispersed grades, particle-shape engineering, and tinting all help. 2024–2026 work (PLOS One white-cast scoring; UCLA particle-shape research) is actively closing this gap [7, 7a].

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation/sensitization:** Among the **lowest-risk** cosmetic ingredients; effectively non-sensitizing, non-comedogenic at reasonable loads; preferred for sensitive, post-procedure, rosacea-prone, and pediatric skin. Allergic contact dermatitis to ZnO is very rare.
- **Sun sensitivity:** None — it is a sunscreen.
- **Penetration/systemic:** Negligible systemic absorption in intact skin (see §5); **SCCS and FDA both treat ZnO as low-risk topically** [4, 5, 11].
- **Nano-specific — inhalation is the real flag:** ZnO (and TiO₂) nanoparticles can cause lung inflammation if inhaled. The **EU prohibits nano ZnO in applications causing significant inhalation exposure (sprays/loose powders)**; SCCS deemed nano ZnO safe up to 25% in **non-sprayable** leave-on/sunscreen products [11]. **Practical rule for the brand: no nano-ZnO aerosol sprays or loose powders.**
- **Photocatalytic ROS:** Use **coated** grades; uncoated nano ZnO can generate skin- and (notably) environment-damaging ROS under UV [9, 10].
- **Pregnancy:** Mineral sunscreens (ZnO/TiO₂) are widely regarded as the **preferred sunscreen choice during pregnancy** precisely because of negligible systemic absorption (in contrast to some organic filters under FDA absorption review). (No medical advice in copy — see claims.)
- **Sourcing/sustainability — environmental flag:** **ZnO is NOT cleanly "reef-safe."** Uncoated/nano ZnO is **toxic to corals and marine microalgae/phytoplankton**, causing coral bleaching via dissolved Zn²⁺ and photocatalytic ROS; some data suggest ZnO can be *more* harmful to certain marine organisms than coated TiO₂ [18]. It is *less* studied-as-harmful than oxybenzone/octinoxate (the filters actually banned in Hawai'i/Key West/Palau), so "reef-friendlier than oxybenzone" is defensible — but a blanket "reef-safe" claim is **not scientifically supported** and is legally risky (no regulated definition).

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Sunscreen = a DRUG in the US (and most markets), not a cosmetic.** Any SPF/"sunscreen"/"broad spectrum"/"protects against sun" product is an **OTC drug** under the FDA OTC Monograph (M020). It must use a monograph-approved filter at approved levels, carry a Drug Facts panel, follow approved SPF/broad-spectrum testing, and meet manufacturing (cGMP/facility registration) requirements. **This is a different, heavier compliance burden than a cosmetic moisturizer.**
- **GRASE status:** In the FDA's 2019 proposed rule and the 2021 deemed final order framework, **ZnO and TiO₂ are the ONLY two filters proposed GRASE (Category I), up to 25%.** PABA and trolamine salicylate are non-GRASE; ~12 organic filters await more safety data (driven by FDA systemic-absorption findings) [12, 19]. This regulatory tailwind is a genuine strategic asset for a mineral-forward brand.
- **EU:** ZnO and ZnO (nano) are listed UV filters under Annex VI of the Cosmetics Regulation, **max 25%**, with the **no-inhalation (no spray/powder)** restriction for the nano form [11].
- **What we CAN say** (cosmetic-style, still substantiated): "broad-spectrum mineral UV filter," "helps protect skin from UVA/UVB," "photostable," "gentle/suitable for sensitive skin," "non-nano" (if true), "helps defend against sun-driven visible aging" *(only on an actual tested SPF drug product with the data)*.
- **What we CANNOT / should flag for legal review:**
  - Marketing SPF/sun-protection language on a product **not registered/tested as an OTC sunscreen drug.**
  - "Reef-safe" (no legal definition; environmentally unsupported for ZnO — §7).
  - "Reverses/repairs sun damage," "boosts collagen," "treats acne/eczema," "anti-aging cream" tied to ZnO's *intrinsic* action — these cross into drug/disease claims and are not supported topically (§4).
  - "Reflects 95% of UV rays" / "physical block / mirror" — **scientifically false** per Cole 2016; avoid (§10).

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Cost tier: LOW (commodity).** Pharma/USP-grade non-nano ZnO is inexpensive in bulk (low single-digit USD/kg territory at scale; retail DIY ~$10–20/lb). Coated, surface-treated, and pre-dispersed cosmetic grades cost more (the coating/dispersion and the SPF testing are where real money goes, not the raw oxide).
- **Suppliers:** Broadly available — e.g., Jedwards/Bulk Natural Oils (non-nano USP), New Directions Aromatics, MSE Supplies (nano 30/50/90 nm), and major actives houses for coated/dispersed cosmetic grades (silica/alumina/dimethicone-coated, pre-dispersions).
- **Supply considerations:** Robust, multi-source, low geopolitical risk. The cost and complexity of a *sunscreen* are dominated by **OTC-drug formulation, SPF/broad-spectrum testing, and regulatory/manufacturing**, not the ZnO itself — consistent with the brand's "buy commodity bases, invest in the brand" thesis (the raw filter is commodity; the tested, elegant, compliant formula is the value).

## 10. Hype vs. evidence (marketing overreach to avoid)

- **"Reflects/blocks UV like a mirror; sits on top and bounces rays."** False/oversimplified — ZnO protects ~95% by **absorption**, ~4–5% by reflection [1]. The "physical block" framing is marketing folklore.
- **"Reflects 95% of UVB / 98% of UVA"** (seen on supplier blogs) — **directly contradicts** the peer-reviewed reflectance data; do not repeat.
- **"Reef-safe mineral sunscreen."** Not supported — uncoated/nano ZnO is demonstrably toxic to corals/marine life [18]. At best "free from oxybenzone/octinoxate."
- **"Heals/repairs/soothes skin" as a ZnO active claim.** The best topical RCT is **null** for healing [6]; soothing evidence is weak/context-bound. Fine as gentle barrier-protectant framing; not as a repair "active."
- **"Zinc fights acne" (topical).** Borrowed from oral data + uncontrolled studies; topical evidence is weak [8].
- **"Nano ZnO is dangerous / penetrates your bloodstream."** Overblown — robust human in-use data show it stays in the stratum corneum with no toxicity [4, 5, 11]. The legitimate nano caution is **inhalation (sprays/powders)**, not skin penetration.
- **"Non-nano is safer for you."** For *skin* safety the difference is negligible; non-nano's honest selling points are *no inhalation worry in powders/sprays* and *marginally lower marine concern* — at the cost of more white cast.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**⚠️→✅ CONDITIONAL HERO of a sunscreen SKU; SUPPORTING/SKIP elsewhere.**

- **As the core filter of a dedicated broad-spectrum mineral SPF product → strong "supporting-to-hero" pick.** It is the best-tolerated, most photostable, broadest-spectrum (incl. UVA1) filter, with the strongest regulatory standing (one of only two FDA-GRASE filters) and the best safety profile for sensitive/longevity-minded consumers. And SPF is the **#1 evidence-based anti-aging step** (Hughes 2013) — perfectly on-thesis for a skin-*longevity* brand.
- **But it commits the brand to OTC-drug regulation** (Drug Facts, SPF testing, cGMP). That is a strategic decision, not a formulation footnote — it conflicts with the lean "commodity base + invest in brand" model unless the brand consciously wants a hero SPF.
- **As a generic "active" in a barrier-repair cream → SKIP/supporting only.** Its non-UV topical benefits (repair, anti-inflammatory, acne) are weakly evidenced or null; better hallmarks-of-aging actives exist for a non-SPF cream.

**Longevity relevance:** **Highest tier — by category.** Photoprotection is the most proven extrinsic-aging lever in dermatology, and ZnO is a premier way to deliver it. The honest caveat for our research base: the anti-aging RCT evidence belongs to *broad-spectrum sunscreen behavior*, not to zinc oxide as a molecule. We can stand behind "mineral broad-spectrum protection helps defend against sun-driven visible aging"; we cannot claim ZnO itself rejuvenates skin.

**Recommended next step:** log under "considering" as a *potential future SPF SKU filter*, flagged with the OTC-drug regulatory commitment; do not bank on it as a moisturizer "active." (See [`../../ideas/considering.md`](../../ideas/considering.md).)

### Main caveats (the things a skeptic should hold onto)

1. **The anti-aging proof is borrowed from the category, not the molecule.** Hughes 2013 used a generic broad-spectrum SPF, not a ZnO product, and the abstract never mentions zinc oxide. ZnO is an *excellent vehicle* for delivering broad-spectrum SPF, so the inference is reasonable — but "zinc oxide slows aging" is a category-to-molecule extrapolation, not a direct finding. The dossier states this plainly; do not let marketing collapse the distinction.
2. **The safety/penetration evidence is reassuring but thin.** The flagship human studies (Mohammed/Gulson) are very small (single-digit/low volunteer counts), use healthy intact skin, and were explicitly designed to *allay* safety concerns. They are credible but should be cited as "no penetration detected in small in-use studies of intact skin," not as a blanket "proven safe / negligible risk." Penetration on broken/diseased skin remains an open question (Smijs & Pavel).
3. **ZnO's *intrinsic* (beyond-UV-filter) skin benefits are weak-to-null.** The single best topical RCT (Ågren 2021) is negative for healing; acne rests on one uncontrolled study; "antioxidant" is double-edged because uncoated ZnO generates ROS. Nothing here justifies a "repair/soothe/treat" active claim.
4. **One unverifiable figure remained in the dossier.** The "~42% more long-UVA than 5% TiO₂" stat (old ref [2]) had no traceable source; I removed the number and downgraded the citation. The *qualitative* UVA1 advantage of ZnO stands.
5. **Industry funding is present but not distorting.** Cole 2016 (the absorption-mechanism paper) is industry-affiliated, but its finding *deflates* a marketing myth, so the bias direction is benign. The pivotal efficacy study (Hughes 2013) is independent academic. No instance of an industry-funded efficacy study being passed off as neutral.
