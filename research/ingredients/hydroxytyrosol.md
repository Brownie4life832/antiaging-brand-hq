# Ingredient: Hydroxytyrosol (INCI: Hydroxytyrosol)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering (shortlist as a supporting antioxidant; not yet hero-grade for topical) · **Class:** Antioxidants

> **Bottom line up front:** Hydroxytyrosol (HT) is the most potent antioxidant polyphenol in olive, with genuinely strong mechanistic and *in-vitro* skin data and a clean oral-safety dossier (EFSA). But the human evidence that matters most for us — **topical** randomized trials showing visible anti-aging benefit — is essentially **absent**. Almost every "anti-aging" claim circulating in marketing is borrowed from cell culture, mouse skin, or *oral* supplement trials. Treat it as a credible **supporting antioxidant**, not a proven topical hero.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common name:** Hydroxytyrosol (HT, 3-HT, sometimes "2-hydroxytyrosol"/"3-hydroxytyrosol" in older papers; also "DOPET" — dihydroxyphenylethanol).
- **INCI:** `Hydroxytyrosol`. Listed INCI functions: **skin conditioning** and **bleaching/brightening** (per SpecialChem & INCIDecoder INCI databases) [1][2].
- **CAS:** 10597-60-1.
- **Chemical:** 2-(3,4-dihydroxyphenyl)ethanol; IUPAC 4-(2-hydroxyethyl)benzene-1,2-diol. Formula C₈H₁₀O₃, MW ~154.2 g/mol. A small, water-soluble (hydrophilic) ortho-diphenol (catechol) — the catechol ring is the source of its radical-scavenging power.
- **Natural source:** olives and olive products. In olive oil/leaf it occurs free, as its **acetate ester**, and bound inside larger molecules — **oleuropein**, **oleacein**, and **verbascoside** — which hydrolyze to release HT. Commercial cosmetic/nutraceutical HT is sourced from olive fruit/leaf extract or olive-mill wastewater (upcycled), or made synthetically [1][3].
- **Key forms / derivatives used in formulation:**
  - **Free hydroxytyrosol** (the active itself).
  - **Hydroxytyrosol acetate (HTyAc)** — more lipophilic, studied for stability/penetration and Phase-II enzyme induction [4].
  - **Standardized olive extracts** (e.g., Hytolive by Alvinesa, standardized to ≥10% or ≥25% HT; Olive Active Hidroxitirosol LD) — most cosmetic use is via these extracts rather than the pure molecule [5].

---

## 2. Class & mechanism

Class: **polyphenol / catechol antioxidant** (Antioxidants taxonomy class). HT works at the skin/cell level through several complementary routes documented in cell and animal models:

1. **Direct free-radical scavenging.** The ortho-dihydroxy (catechol) structure donates hydrogen atoms to quench ROS/RNS (superoxide, peroxyl, hydroxyl, peroxynitrite) and to break lipid-peroxidation chains in membranes. This is its best-established property.
2. **Nrf2/ARE activation (indirect antioxidant).** HT promotes nuclear translocation of **Nrf2** via ERK and PI3K/Akt signaling, upregulating endogenous antioxidant/detoxifying ("Phase II") enzymes — glutathione peroxidase, glutathione reductase, glutathione-S-transferase, SOD, catalase, heme oxygenase-1. This "switching on the cell's own defenses" effect is more durable than simple scavenging [6][4][7].
3. **Anti-inflammatory signaling.** Suppresses NF-κB and MAPK activation, lowering pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-8) and inflammatory enzymes in stressed skin cells [8][9].
4. **MMP modulation / ECM protection.** In UV-stressed skin models, downregulates matrix metalloproteinases (MMP-1, MMP-9) that degrade collagen, while supporting type I/III collagen expression [8][9].
5. **Mitochondrial support.** Modulates mitochondrial function and biogenesis pathways (PPARGC1α/PGC-1α), relevant to the "mitochondrial decline" hallmark — strongest in oral/systemic models [7][10].
6. **Biphasic (hormetic) behavior — important caveat.** At low micromolar concentrations HT is antioxidant and even pro-proliferative/pro-migratory for keratinocytes; at high concentrations olive polyphenols can be **pro-oxidant and mildly cytotoxic**. This dose-dependence matters for formulation [11][12].

## 4. Evidence review by tier (core of the dossier)

> **Reading guide:** Quality of evidence is ranked in-vitro < animal < human ORAL < human TOPICAL (RCT highest). For a *topical* skincare brand, the human-topical row is what justifies on-pack claims. **For HT that row is nearly empty.** Most benefits below are mechanistic or borrowed from oral/systemic dosing.

### Tier 1 — In-vitro (cell culture). Strong and consistent, but lowest translational weight.

- **Sun, Park et al. 2018, *Biomedical Dermatology* — human dermal fibroblasts (HDFs).** HT reduced UVA-induced senescence (SA-β-galactosidase ↓ dose-dependently), lowered oxidative stress and inflammatory markers, and modulated collagen/MMP gene expression by qRT-PCR. First dedicated skin-biology study of HT. **Limitation:** in-vitro only, immortalized/primary cells, gene-expression endpoints, small replicate counts; authors themselves frame it as a *future* cosmeceutical candidate needing clinical work [8].
- **Wang et al. 2025, *Scientific Reports* — HDFs + HaCaT keratinocytes, transwell co-culture, UVB.** HT tested alone (20 µM) and in a 1:1:1 oleuropein/HT/verbascoside combination (10 µM each). The combination suppressed TNF-α/IL-1β/IL-6/IL-8 (MAPK/NF-κB), reduced MMP-1/MMP-9, restored Col1a/Col3a, cut SA-β-gal and p53/p21/p16, lowered ROS, upregulated Nrf2, and reduced apoptosis. **Key honesty flag:** the *combination* outperformed HT alone — much of the headline effect is not attributable to HT by itself [9].
- **Abate et al. 2021, *Int. J. Mol. Sci.* — HaCaT keratinocytes.** HT at low micromolar (5–10 µM) induced proliferation (CDK2/CDK6 ↑) and migration (MMP-9, ERK/PI3K/Akt), and protected against H₂O₂ cytotoxicity (blocked ATM/Chk1/Chk2/p53), reducing apoptosis — supports a wound-healing/barrier-recovery role. **Limitation:** in-vitro, immortalized line, 24 h [12].
- **Lecci et al. 2021, *Molecules* — human epidermal keratinocytes (HEKa), UVA, olive-mill wastewater polyphenol fractions.** ROS-quenching at all tested doses; **biphasic** — a weak but significant cytotoxic effect at the highest concentration (75 µg GAE/mL). Demonstrates antioxidant *and* pro-oxidant capacity depending on dose. **Limitation:** mixed polyphenol fractions, not pure HT [11].
- **Tyrosinase / pigmentation (in-vitro):** "2-hydroxytyrosol" inhibited mushroom tyrosinase (IC₅₀ ≈ 13 µmol/L, comparable to kojic acid) and reduced α-MSH-stimulated melanin in B16 cells (IC₅₀ ≈ 32.5 µmol/L) — structurally a substrate analog of L-DOPA. Other work shows olive extract/HT lowers melanin synthesis without inhibiting tyrosinase activity, implying additional mechanisms [13]. **Limitation:** mushroom-tyrosinase and murine-melanoma models notoriously over-predict human brightening.
- **DNA-damage protection (in-vitro):** HT protected human cells against oxidative DNA damage (8-OHdG) [14] and protected HaCaT against UVB-induced DNA damage [referenced; abstract only].

**In-vitro verdict:** Mechanism is real, reproducible, and broad. But cell-culture potency does not establish topical efficacy in living skin.

### Tier 2 — Animal (mostly mouse). Adds living-tissue context; mostly *oral/systemic*, some topical-delivery.

- **Fan et al. 2025, *Nutrients* (17:2810) — C57BL/6J mice, oral, AGE/glycation model.** 48 aged (8-mo) male mice on a high-AGE diet ± HT 25 or 50 mg/kg/day for 16 weeks (n=12/group). HT50 raised skin moisture (+5.74%), hydroxyproline (+42.5%, a collagen proxy), and epidermal/dermal thickness; cut IL-6/IL-1β/TNF-α; raised SOD (+44.6%) and GSH-Px (~2×); lowered MDA (−46.6%); and preserved gut-barrier proteins (ZO-1, occludin) — a **gut–skin axis** mechanism. **Limitations (authors' own):** murine only, oral (not topical) dosing, physical-barrier focus; needs human validation [10].
- **Microneedle photoaging study 2026, *Int. J. Mol. Sci.* (27:1005) — HT-loaded dissolving microneedles, UVA/UVB mouse model + UVA fibroblasts.** Microneedle tips 10% HT + 5% hyaluronic acid; >200 µm penetration. In vitro: ↑ viability, ↓ ROS, ↓ SA-β-gal. In vivo: ↑ skin hydration & elasticity, ↑ collagen density, ↓ MDA, ↑ GSH/catalase/GSH-Px. **Limitations:** mouse skin; delivery via microneedles (not a topical cream — bypasses the stratum corneum), small groups [15].

**Animal verdict:** Supports oxidative-stress, inflammation, AGE, and photoaging mechanisms in living skin — but via *oral* dosing or *device-assisted* delivery, not the leave-on topical route a cream would use.

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL (supplement) trials. The most rigorous HT-in-humans data — but not a topical readout.

- **Moratilla-Rivera et al. 2025, *Clinical Nutrition* (52:17–26) — RCT, CSIC (Spain).** Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled; 52 overweight prediabetic adults (40–70 y), 49 completed; **15 mg/day HT (Hytolive) for 16 weeks.** Significant reductions in oxidized LDL, protein carbonyls, and **8-OHdG (systemic oxidative DNA-damage marker)**; ↑ antioxidant capacity/GPx; ↓ IL-6. No change in CRP or TNF-α. **Relevance/limits:** proves HT lowers *systemic* oxidative-stress and one inflammatory marker — outcomes plausibly relevant to "inflammaging," but **measured no skin endpoint** (no wrinkles, elasticity, hydration, pigmentation) [16].
- Broader HT oral literature (cardiovascular/metabolic; EFSA-recognized olive-polyphenol claim for LDL protection) is robust but off-target for skin.
- A 2026 review (Tabanelli et al., *J. Funct. Foods*/related, "Does hydroxytyrosol deserve a health claim?") surveys HT supplementation across aging mechanisms; conclusion (from search summaries) emphasizes promising preclinical signals but heterogeneous, still-maturing human evidence [17][unverified — full text not accessed].

**Human-oral verdict:** Good RCT evidence that oral HT reduces systemic oxidative stress and IL-6. None of it measures the skin.

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL. **Sparse to absent for cosmetic anti-aging.**

- **Atopic-dermatitis pilot (Cabello-Verrugio / Spanish group, *Drug Delivery & Transl. Res.* 2017) — split-arm, 10 subjects, 28 days.** A cream with chitosan nanoparticles co-loading **hydrocortisone + HT** vs vehicle; measured TEWL, erythema, irritation, plus bloods/biopsy. Designed to test **tolerability/safety**, and HT was a **co-ingredient with a drug (hydrocortisone)** — so it cannot isolate a cosmetic anti-aging effect of HT. Tiny n [18].
- **Reconstructed human epidermis (RHE) model, *DARU J. Pharm. Sci.* 2019.** An HT-based formulation reduced inflammation/barrier impairment markers in a 3D epidermis model — *ex vivo* tissue, **not a living-human trial** [19].
- **Wound-healing context:** an HT EMT/wound-healing human-skin study exists but is a healing/biology readout, not an anti-aging cosmetic trial [20][unverified specifics].
- **No published, peer-reviewed, placebo-controlled human RCT** was found testing a leave-on topical hydroxytyrosol product for wrinkles, elasticity, firmness, pigmentation, or hydration as primary endpoints (search through June 2026). Industry "clinical" support is typically supplier-driven and not independently published.

**Human-topical verdict:** Effectively no independent RCT evidence for topical cosmetic anti-aging. This is the decisive gap.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **Favorable physicochemistry:** low MW (~154), small, and amphiphilic-leaning-hydrophilic with a catechol head — these properties support *some* passive stratum-corneum penetration, and HT readily crosses cell membranes in vitro [3][21].
- **But hydrophilicity cuts both ways:** very water-soluble small phenols can permeate but also clear/oxidize quickly; **rigorous human skin-permeation/retention data specific to HT in a finished cosmetic are thin.** Most penetration claims ("rapidly absorbed into the dermis") trace to supplier marketing, not controlled Franz-cell or in-vivo human studies [21][unverified for finished products].
- The fact that researchers reach for **microneedles** to deliver HT into skin [15] is itself a tell: passive topical delivery of a useful dose is not a solved problem.
- **Stability is the bigger practical limit** than penetration: as a catechol, HT oxidizes readily (air, light, alkaline pH, metals), which can both reduce delivered active and cause discoloration.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration (topical):** No evidence-based human target exists. In-vitro actives sit at single-digit to low-tens micromolar; cosmetic products typically use standardized olive extracts delivering a fraction of a percent to low single-digit % HT. **Honest position: there is no validated "use X%" number for visible benefit.** Dose by extract spec, not by an efficacy claim.
- **pH:** Favor mildly acidic (~4.5–6). Catechols are more stable acidic; alkaline pH accelerates oxidation/browning.
- **Stability:** The key challenge. Protect with antioxidants, chelators (to bind catalytic metals), low oxygen headspace, opaque/airless packaging, and possibly the **acetate derivative** for better stability/lipophilicity [4]. Discoloration to brown/pink on oxidation is a real risk.
- **Pairings (rational, evidence-light):**
  - **Vitamin E / ferulic acid** — antioxidant network regeneration, mutual stabilization.
  - **Niacinamide, hyaluronic acid** — well-tolerated barrier/hydration partners (HA also used as the microneedle carrier) [15].
  - **Other olive polyphenols (oleuropein, verbascoside)** — combination outperformed HT alone in vitro [9], supporting a "full-spectrum olive" angle.
- **Conflicts/cautions:** avoid high pH systems; avoid uncomplexed metal ions; expect interaction with strong oxidizers. Combining with other catechols increases total antioxidant load but also total oxidation/discoloration risk.
- **Sensory:** the pure molecule has a faint olive/phenolic note; at cosmetic levels in extracts it is generally neutral. Color drift is the main sensory liability.

## 7. Safety

- **Oral safety (well-characterized):** EFSA (Turck et al., *EFSA Journal* 2017;15(3):4728) judged synthetic HT a safe novel food. **NOAEL 50 mg/kg bw/day** (90-day rat study); margin of exposure ≥200 for adults; **no genotoxicity concern** (in-vitro clastogenicity not expressed in vivo at 2,000 mg/kg). **Target population excluded children <36 months, pregnant, and breastfeeding women** — a conservative exclusion, not evidence of harm, but worth noting [22].
- **Topical irritation/sensitization:** Generally regarded as well-tolerated; the small atopic-dermatitis cream pilot tracked irritation/erythema without flagging problems [18]. **But there is no dedicated published HRIPT/CIR monograph establishing a safe topical concentration for HT specifically** — tolerability data are sparse and often supplier-held. As a catechol/polyphenol, theoretical sensitization potential exists but is not documented as a real-world signal at cosmetic levels.
- **Pregnancy/breastfeeding:** EFSA excluded these groups from the oral novel-food assessment for lack of data. Prudent cosmetic stance: do not market to pregnant/nursing users without specific guidance; this is a precaution, not proof of risk.
- **Sun sensitivity:** No evidence HT is photosensitizing; mechanistically it is photo-*protective*. (Standard "antioxidants are not a sunscreen" caveat still applies.)
- **Pro-oxidant at high dose:** the biphasic effect [11] means "more is better" is wrong; over-dosing could be counterproductive or irritating.
- **Sourcing/sustainability — a genuine strength:** leading commercial HT is **upcycled from olive fruit/olive-mill wastewater using water-only extraction** (e.g., Hytolive), a real circular-economy story [5].

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Cosmetic vs. drug line:** HT is a permitted **cosmetic ingredient**; positioning it as an antioxidant/skin-conditioning/brightening active is within cosmetic scope. Claiming it **"repairs," "boosts collagen," "treats" photoaging/AGEs/senescence, or "prevents" wrinkles** drifts into drug/structure-function territory and would need substantiation/legal review (US FDA; analogous EU rules). See glossary claim cheat-sheet.
- **What the evidence will (and won't) support for COPY:**
  - ✅ Defensible (cosmetic, appearance-based, hedged): "olive-derived antioxidant," "helps defend against environmental/oxidative stress," "supports a healthy-looking, even-toned complexion," "soothing." Even these should be framed as *supports/helps the appearance of* — and the antioxidant/oxidative-stress framing is on firmer ground than any anti-wrinkle claim.
  - ⚠️ Risky / **needs substantiation or avoid**: "boosts collagen," "reverses/erases wrinkles," "anti-aging," "repairs the barrier," "reduces AGEs/glycation," "kills senescent cells," "X× more powerful than vitamin C." The "3× vitamin C bioavailability" line and "penetrates to the dermis" claims are **supplier/marketing assertions** not validated for topical cosmetic use — do not repeat as fact.
  - ❌ Do not borrow oral-trial outcomes (8-OHdG, IL-6, oxidized LDL) as if they were proven *topical* skin benefits. They were measured in blood after swallowing 15 mg/day.
- **EFSA olive-polyphenol claim** ("protection of blood lipids from oxidative stress") is an **oral food claim** and cannot be transferred to a topical cosmetic.

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Suppliers:** Alvinesa (**Hytolive**, ≥10%/≥25% HT, upcycled, water-extracted) and PLT Health distribute the premium standardized grade; DKSH (Olive Active Hidroxitirosol LD); numerous Chinese suppliers (Shaanxi cluster) offer 10–99% purity powder/liquid with ISO 22000/HALAL/GMP options [5].
- **Cost tier:** mid-to-premium for the branded standardized extracts; commodity Chinese material is cheaper but variable in purity/quality. **Exact per-kg pricing is quote-based** — published list pricing was not available; budget for sampling/COA verification [5][unverified pricing].
- **Supply considerations:** olive-derived = vintage/harvest variability; standardized extracts mitigate this. Catechol oxidation means storage/handling (cool, dark, sealed) materially affects delivered active — build COA and stability checks into procurement.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

| Marketing claim seen in the wild | Reality |
|---|---|
| "Most powerful natural antioxidant / 10× green tea, 3× vitamin C" | Comparative potency varies by assay; "3× bioavailability vs vitamin C" is an **oral-supplement** talking point, not a topical fact [21]. |
| "Penetrates rapidly to the dermis" | Plausible physicochemically, but **no controlled human skin-permeation data** for finished cosmetics; researchers use microneedles precisely because passive delivery is hard [15][21]. |
| "Clinically proven anti-aging" | **No independent human topical RCT** exists. Cell/mouse/oral data only. |
| "Boosts collagen / reverses photoaging" | Collagen/MMP effects are **in-vitro and mouse**; not demonstrated in human skin topically. Drug-like claim. |
| "Reduces AGEs / fights glycation" | One **mouse oral** study [10]. Not shown for human topical use. |
| "Brightens / fades pigmentation" | Tyrosinase inhibition is **mushroom-enzyme + melanoma-cell** data; human brightening unproven [13]. |

**Net:** HT is a legitimately strong *antioxidant molecule* with an unusually deep mechanistic and preclinical file — and an unusually thin *human topical* file. The marketing routinely overstates the topical case by importing oral and cell data.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**⚠️ Supporting / conditional — not a topical hero (yet).**

One-liner: *A credible, well-tolerated, sustainably-sourced olive antioxidant with strong mechanism but no human topical RCT — use it as a supporting "environmental-defense" antioxidant with carefully hedged claims, not as the headline anti-aging actor.*

- **Fit with the barrier-repair thesis:** Indirect. Keratinocyte-migration and anti-inflammatory data hint at barrier/recovery support, but ceramides/niacinamide carry the barrier story with far better evidence. HT is a complement, not the core.
- **Best role:** secondary antioxidant in an antioxidant-network formula (with vitamin E/ferulic, niacinamide, HA), and a strong **brand-story asset** (olive-derived, upcycled, circular sourcing) — provided claims stay appearance- and antioxidant-focused.
- **Longevity relevance:** Mechanistically on-theme for skin longevity (Nrf2, oxidative stress, inflammaging, senescence, AGEs, mitochondria) — arguably one of the more "longevity-biology-aligned" cosmetic antioxidants. But for our claims-disciplined brand, the longevity framing must be presented as *mechanism/ingredient story*, explicitly not as a proven topical anti-aging outcome.
- **Conditions to upgrade toward hero:** (a) a stable, color-stable cosmetic delivery; (b) ideally our own or a credible independent human topical study; (c) substantiation file before any structure-function language.
