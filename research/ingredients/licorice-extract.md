# Ingredient: Licorice Root + Actives — Glabridin, Dipotassium Glycyrrhizate, Licochalcone A (INCI: Glycyrrhiza Glabra (Licorice) Root Extract; Glabridin; Dipotassium Glycyrrhizate; Glycyrrhiza Inflata Root Extract / Licochalcone A)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ⬜ considering (SUPPORTING — strong for tone/redness *appearance*, weak for structural anti-aging)
**Class:** Botanicals & plant bioactives (functionally a multi-active complex: tyrosinase inhibitor + antioxidant + anti-inflammatory)
**Evidence verdict at a glance:** "Licorice" is not one ingredient — it is a **family of distinct molecules from at least two species**, each with its own evidence base. The honest read: (1) **Tone/pigmentation** is the best-evidenced topical benefit, but the human RCTs are mostly on **liquiritin** (a different licorice flavonoid) and **whole-extract creams**, *not* on isolated **glabridin** — glabridin's famous "16× hydroquinone" stat is **in-vitro only**, and there is essentially **no controlled monotherapy trial of glabridin** in humans. (2) **Redness/soothing** (licochalcone A, glycyrrhetinic acid, dipotassium glycyrrhizate) has decent human support, but almost always in **multi-ingredient formulas** (typically paired with 4-t-butylcyclohexanol), so the licorice active can't be cleanly isolated. (3) Classic **anti-aging endpoints — wrinkles, firmness, collagen** — are **in-vitro / animal only.** (4) The scary systemic licorice risk (**pseudoaldosteronism / hypertension**) is an **ORAL** problem and does **not** transfer to topical cosmetic use. Net: a legitimate, safe, well-liked **supporting** brightening-and-calming botanical — not a structural anti-aging hero.

---

## 1. Identity

Licorice/liquorice in skincare is a **bundle of related but chemically distinct actives** drawn from the roots of *Glycyrrhiza* species. Treating them as one ingredient is the single biggest source of marketing confusion. The four that matter:

- **Glycyrrhiza Glabra (Licorice) Root Extract** — INCI *Glycyrrhiza Glabra Root Extract*. The whole-root extract from European/Middle-Eastern licorice; a mixture containing glabridin, liquiritin, isoliquiritin, liquiritigenin, isoliquiritigenin, glycyrrhizic acid, and 18β-glycyrrhetinic acid. Composition (and therefore activity) varies wildly by genotype, geography, and extraction. [1][5]
- **Glabridin** — a prenylated **isoflavonoid** and the **hydrophobic (lipophilic) fraction** of *G. glabra*. The hero "skin-brightening" molecule in marketing. C₂₀H₂₀O₄, MW ~324.4; **poorly water-soluble, lipophilic, and physicochemically unstable** — which dominates its formulation difficulty (§5/§6). Typically the headline active in premium "glabridin X%" products. [2][12]
- **Dipotassium Glycyrrhizate (DPG)** — INCI *Dipotassium Glycyrrhizate* (a.k.a. dipotassium glycyrrhizinate). A **water-soluble di-potassium salt of glycyrrhizic acid** (the saponin glycoside; aglycone = 18β-glycyrrhetinic acid). This is the workhorse **soothing/anti-inflammatory** licorice active in cosmetics — cheap, soluble, elegant, widely used. Related: **Glycyrrhetinic Acid / Stearyl Glycyrrhetinate / Glycyrrhetinic Acid (enoxolone)** — the lipophilic aglycone, also used for soothing. [4][11]
- **Licochalcone A (Lico-A)** — a **retrochalcone** extracted specifically from ***Glycyrrhiza inflata*** (a *different species* — "Chinese licorice"), **not** from *G. glabra*. The signature anti-redness/anti-inflammatory/antioxidant active, most associated with **Eucerin/Beiersdorf** (which holds patents and most of the clinical literature). [3][10]

**Key takeaway on identity:** "licorice extract" on an INCI list could be any (or none) of these, at any concentration, from any species. For a science-led brand, **name the specific active and species** rather than "licorice."

## 2. Class & mechanism

Each active has its own mechanism; together they explain licorice's reputation as a "brightening + calming" botanical. (Most mechanistic detail is in-vitro/ex-vivo with clinical correlates — see §4.)

**Glabridin — pigmentation + antioxidant + anti-inflammatory:**
- **Tyrosinase inhibition.** Inhibits tyrosinase (the rate-limiting melanogenic enzyme) at 0.1–1.0 µg/mL in vitro **without cytotoxicity**, and selectively reduces the activity of specific tyrosinase isozymes — i.e., it blocks melanin production rather than killing melanocytes (a gentleness advantage over hydroquinone). [6][7]
- **Upstream melanogenesis pathway.** Network-pharmacology and cell work indicate glabridin downregulates **MITF** (the master melanogenic transcription factor) via reduced **CREB phosphorylation / CRTC1 nuclear translocation / SOX10**, cutting tyrosinase and TRP gene expression. [1]
- **Antioxidant / anti-inflammatory.** ROS scavenger; inhibits **superoxide-anion production** and **cyclooxygenase** activity — the basis of its "calming" reputation and its anti-photoaging hypothesis. [6][7]

**Liquiritin / isoliquiritin (flavonoids):** act less by enzyme inhibition and more by **dispersing/breaking up existing melanin** and reducing UVB-induced erythema — a different, complementary tone mechanism. [1][13]

**Dipotassium glycyrrhizate / glycyrrhetinic acid — anti-inflammatory/barrier:**
- Inhibits pro-inflammatory cytokine secretion (**IL-6, TNF-α**), suppresses epidermal hyperplasia, and helps **restore tight junctions / barrier function** in inflamed-skin models. [4][11]
- 18β-glycyrrhetinic acid has mild **cortisol-sparing / corticoid-like** anti-inflammatory activity (via 11β-HSD modulation) — the same enzyme axis that causes systemic trouble when *eaten* (§7), but locally this reads as "soothing." [9]

**Licochalcone A — anti-inflammatory + antioxidant (the deepest mechanistic file):**
- **Anti-inflammatory:** suppresses **TNF-α-induced NF-κB activation** and PGE2 secretion (active from ~1.5 µM in vitro); inhibits the **NLRP3 inflammasome**. [3][10]
- **Antioxidant (and the most "longevity-shaped" mechanism here):** activates **Nrf2 nuclear translocation**, upregulating **HO-1 and GCLM** to raise endogenous antioxidant defense; shown to blunt **visible-light- and UV-induced ROS** in human fibroblasts/keratinocytes. [3][14]
- **Antimicrobial:** activity against *Cutibacterium acnes* and various fungi (ergosterol-synthesis inhibition) — basis of the acne use. [3]

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

| Hallmark | Targeted? | Strength (for TOPICAL human use unless noted) |
|---|---|---|
| Pigmentation / uneven tone | ✅ Primary | **Moderate–strong** — RCTs exist, but mostly on **liquiritin / whole extract**, not isolated glabridin |
| Inflammaging / redness | ✅ Primary | **Moderate** (licochalcone A, glycyrrhetinic acid) — human data, but in **combination formulas** |
| Oxidative stress | ✅ | **Moderate** mechanistically (Nrf2/HO-1 for Lico-A; ROS scavenging for glabridin); human endpoint data thin |
| Photoaging (UV defense, MMPs) | ⚠️ Indirect | **Weak–moderate** — strong in-vitro/animal (MMP-1↓, COX-2↓, Nrf2↑); **no human anti-wrinkle RCT** |
| Barrier decline / hydration | ⚠️ | **Weak–moderate** — DPG/Lico-A support barrier in AD models & emollient trials; not a dedicated barrier hero |
| Collagen / ECM loss (firmness) | ⚠️ | **Weak** — in-vitro procollagen/MMP signals only; **not** a demonstrated topical firming benefit |
| Cellular senescence | ⚠️ | **Weak** — preclinical anti-senescence signals (esp. Lico-A); not human-proven |
| Microbiome (acne, *C. acnes*) | ✅ | **Moderate** for Lico-A in acne (combination formulas) |
| Mitochondrial decline | ❔ | **Insufficient** topical evidence — do not claim |
| Glycation / AGEs | ❔ | **No meaningful topical evidence** — do not claim |
| Hydration / TEWL | ⚠️ | **Weak** (emollient-formula data, confounded) |

**Bottom line on hallmarks:** The defensible, consumer-felt wins are **even tone / brightness** and **calm, less-red skin** — both of which read as "younger, healthier" without being structural anti-aging. Collagen, firmness, wrinkle, senescence, mitochondrial, and glycation claims are **mechanistic or absent** for topical human use and must not be stated as proven.

## 4. Evidence review by tier

> **Reading guide.** Quality climbs in-vitro → animal → human ORAL → human TOPICAL; within topical, RCT/vehicle- or active-controlled > open-label/observational. **Two defining caveats for licorice:** (a) the impressive human topical data are largely on **liquiritin and whole extract** and on **licochalcone-A combination products** — *not* on isolated glabridin; (b) the famous oral licorice literature is about **safety harms (pseudoaldosteronism)**, not skin benefits, and does **not** support eating licorice for skin.

### 4A. In-vitro (cell culture / ex-vivo) — mechanism only
- **Glabridin, tyrosinase/pigment:** Yokota et al., *Pigment Cell Res* 1998 — foundational paper. Glabridin inhibited tyrosinase at **0.1–1.0 µg/mL** without cytotoxicity and inhibited superoxide-anion production and cyclooxygenase (anti-inflammatory). The widely-repeated **"16× the skin-lightening effect of hydroquinone"** figure traces to this in-vitro lineage. [6][7]
- **Glabridin, upstream pathway:** *G. glabra* extract / glabridin downregulates **MITF via CREB/CRTC1/SOX10**; liquiritin, isoliquiritigenin and glabridin identified as the primary melanin-inhibitory components of the whole extract. [1]
- **Licochalcone A, anti-inflammatory/antioxidant:** suppresses TNF-α-induced NF-κB and PGE2 (Song et al. 2015, HaCaT, 1.25–10 µM); induces **Nrf2/HO-1/GCLM** (Kühnl et al. 2015; Mann et al. 2020, human fibroblasts, 0.25–16 µM) — blunts UV/visible-light ROS. [14][3]
- **Lico-A, pigment:** Hong et al. 2018 (B16 melanoma) — ↓melanin via ERK phosphorylation, MITF downregulation, tyrosinase↓. [3]
- **DPG, anti-inflammatory:** dipotassium glycyrrhizate ↓IL-6/TNF-α, suppressed epidermal hyperplasia and **recovered tight junctions** in an IL-4/IL-13-induced AD-like skin-equivalent (Cavinato/Jung-type models). [4][11]
- **Limitation:** in-vitro µg/mL–µM concentrations and isolated-cell endpoints don't translate linearly to a leave-on cosmetic; mechanism ≠ clinical endpoint.

### 4B. Animal — preclinical support, not load-bearing
- **Glabridin, UVB pigmentation:** topical **0.5% glabridin** inhibited **UVB-induced pigmentation and erythema** in guinea pig skin (Yokota 1998) — the key in-vivo signal behind the brightening claim, but it is animal, not human. [6][7]
- **Glabridin + bakuchiol, photoaging:** Peng et al., *J Cosmet Dermatol* 2024 — glabridin (± bakuchiol) reduced UVB-induced TNF-α/IL-1β in mouse fibroblasts/skin (anti-photoaging mechanism). In-vitro + animal; hypothesis-generating. [8]
- **Lico-A, acne/inflammation:** mouse *P. acnes* models — Lico-A inhibits NLRP3 inflammasome and shows direct antibacterial activity (comparable to benzoyl peroxide at 10–20 µM in vitro). [3]
- Reproductive/systemic licorice tox work exists but pertains to **oral** glycyrrhizin (§7), not topical efficacy.

### 4C. Human — ORAL
**Not a skincare route.** Oral licorice/glycyrrhizin is studied mainly for its **harms** (pseudoaldosteronism — see §7) and for unrelated indications (e.g., PCOS metabolic trials). **There is no credible oral-licorice-for-skin-aging evidence to borrow.** Any "ingestible licorice for radiant skin" framing would be unsupported and arguably reckless given the blood-pressure risk.

### 4D. Human — TOPICAL (the real, but caveated, evidence base)

**Pigmentation / melasma — the strongest topical file, but mostly NOT glabridin:**
- **Amer & Metwalli 2000** (*Int J Dermatol* 39(4):299–301; PMID 10809983) — split-face, vehicle-controlled, **n=20** women with epidermal melasma; **liquiritin 2% cream** vs vehicle, BID × 4 wk. **~80% "excellent" response** (pigmentation indistinguishable from surrounding skin) on the liquiritin side. *Liquiritin, not glabridin; small; short.* [13][15]
- **Zubair et al.** (*J Pak Assoc Dermatol*) — RCT comparing **2% vs 4% liquiritin** and liquiritin vs **4% hydroquinone**; 4% liquiritin significantly effective for melasma. [15][16]
- **Nahidi/Sahebkar et al. 2016** (*J Dermatol Cosmet*; the "44-women trial") — randomized, double-blind, **placebo-controlled**, **4% licorice extract** cream (solid-lipid-nanoparticle vehicle), n=40 analyzed, 12 wk, Modified MASI. MMASI fell **11.03→1.41 (active)** vs **11.25→2.37 (placebo)**; both improved (placebo vehicle was emollient + presumably sun-avoidance), active superior, p<0.001. **Whole extract, not isolated glabridin; nanoparticle vehicle.** [17]
- **Makino et al.** — double-blind, **n=18**, a **glabridin-containing** hydroquinone-free brightener vs 4% HQ; significant pigment reduction with the glabridin product (graded evidence level IIA in the systematic review). One of the *very few* human datapoints featuring glabridin — but it is a **finished product**, not isolated glabridin. [15]
- **Costa et al.** — RCT, cream with belides + emblica + licorice vs 2% HQ; both depigmented, **no statistical difference** (so licorice contribution not isolable). [15]
- **Systematic reviews' verdict:** the JCAD 2018 natural-ingredients review (Hollinger, Angra & Halder — *not* Sarkar; see citation-correction note in §12) and the JDD East-Asia botanicals review conclude licorice components "show promise" for melasma/UV pigmentation, but flag **small samples, combination formulas, short durations, and — explicitly — that no controlled clinical trial has tested glabridin as a stand-alone bleaching agent.** [15][18]

**Redness / rosacea / sensitive skin (licochalcone A + glycyrrhetinic acid) — human, but combination formulas:**
- **Schoelermann et al. 2016** (*JEADV*) — **licochalcone A + 4-t-butylcyclohexanol** skincare regimen in **n=32** rosacea subtype I, 8 wk: significantly **reduced erythema** and improved tactile roughness; good tolerability. *Can't separate Lico-A from the co-active.* [10]
- **Jovanovic et al. 2017** — large real-world cohort (**n=1,221**) sensitive-skin/rosacea, trans-4-t-butylcyclohexanol + Lico-A, 4 wk: significant efficacy, excellent tolerability (observational). [3]
- **Sulzberger et al. 2016** (n=38) — Lico-A + 4-t-butylcyclohexanol rapidly relieved neurosensory (stinging/burning) symptoms. [3]

**Atopic dermatitis / barrier (DPG + Lico-A) — human:**
- **Dipotassium glycyrrhizin emollient RCT** (*Pak J Med Sci* 2025) — randomized parallel-controlled; regular DPG-emollient use reduced SCORAD and flare frequency in mild–moderate adult AD, no notable AEs. [4]
- **Saeedi et al. 2003** (*J Dermatolog Treat*; PMID 14522625) — **1%/2% whole licorice extract gel** (standardized to ~20% glycyrrhizinic acid, *not* a purified glycyrrhetinic-acid gel) improved erythema/edema/itching in atopic dermatitis over 2 wk (2% > 1%; n=30/group). One of the cleaner licorice-monotherapy topical signals, but still small, short, and disease-population. [19]
- **Wananukul et al. 2013** (PMID 24163988) — randomized double-blind **split-side** study, Lico-A moisturizer vs **1% hydrocortisone** (the weakest OTC-strength steroid, *not* hydrocortisone butyrate) in childhood AD (n=55); comparable SCORAD improvement on both sides. Note: a same-side/split comparison against the mildest steroid, not a formal non-inferiority trial against a mid-potency steroid. [3]
- **Angelova-Fischer et al. 2018** — Lico-A + ceramide/fatty-acid emulsion significantly cut AD relapse vs vehicle (~60% reported; exact figure **[unverified]** in this pass) and preserved barrier (n=26, 12 wk, vehicle-controlled left-right). [3]

**Acne (licochalcone A) — human, combination formulas:**
- **Dall'Oglio et al. 2019** (*Clin Cosmet Investig Dermatol*; PMID 32099436) — multicenter prospective, **n=91** mild acne, **Lico-A + salicylic acid + L-carnitine** fluid + Lico-A/hydroxy-complex cream, 8 wk: comedones **−64%**, papules **−71%**, sebum **−52%**. *Industry-funded (Beiersdorf); observational; multi-active.* [20]
- **Kulthanan et al. 2020** (PMC7603542) — double-blind, randomized, vehicle-controlled, **n=110**, Lico-A/decanediol/L-carnitine/salicylic-acid moisturizer reduced relapse in maintenance. [3]

**Honest summary of the topical tier:**
- **Tone/brightening** = **moderate–strong human evidence**, but carried by **liquiritin and whole extract**, in **small/short trials** — *not* by isolated glabridin (glabridin's human file is thin and product-level).
- **Redness/soothing & barrier** = **moderate human evidence** for **licochalcone A and glycyrrhetinic-acid/DPG**, almost always in **multi-ingredient formulas** (can't isolate the licorice active) and several **industry-funded**.
- **Anti-wrinkle / firming / collagen / senescence** = **not demonstrated** in human topical trials (in-vitro/animal only).

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **Glabridin is the problem child.** **Poor water solubility, low physicochemical stability, and inefficient percutaneous penetration** are repeatedly named as the main barriers to its dermo-cosmetic use. Raw glabridin penetrates the stratum corneum poorly and degrades. [2][12]
- **Delivery systems materially help.** Liposome-encapsulated glabridin showed **~3.8× higher dermal penetration** than unencapsulated glabridin; nanostructured lipid carriers (NLC) and a **glabridin nanosuspension** (Wang et al., *Pharmazie* 2016, ~149 nm particles) significantly raised skin permeation flux vs coarse suspension/physical mixture, with acceptable stability. **Most are lab/supplier-stage**, and they add cost/complexity. [2][12]
- **Dipotassium glycyrrhizate is fully water-soluble** and easy to deliver — its main practical advantage (cheap, stable, elegant), at the cost of being the "soothing" rather than "hero brightening" active.
- **Licochalcone A** is lipophilic but is the active in numerous shipped Eucerin products, so delivery is solved at industrial scale (typically emulsions).
- **Implication:** a "glabridin X%" claim is **only as good as the delivery system** — nominal % on the label can be misleading if the molecule isn't solubilized/stabilized/penetrating.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration (guidance, not a hard rule):**
  - Whole *G. glabra* extract / "licorice root extract": commonly **~0.5–4%** in finished products (the 4% melasma trials used 4%). [17]
  - **Glabridin** (purified): typically used at low % (often **~0.1–0.5%**) given cost and solubility; needs a delivery system to matter. [2]
  - **Dipotassium glycyrrhizate:** commonly **~0.1–2%** as a soothing active.
  - **Licochalcone A:** used at fractions of a percent in commercial anti-redness products (exact % usually proprietary).
- **pH:** Glabridin/licochalcone (flavonoid/chalcone) actives are reasonably tolerant across mildly acidic-to-neutral; DPG is a salt and prefers near-neutral. No narrow pH window like a free acid, but extreme pH and prolonged light/heat degrade the flavonoids.
- **Stability:** Glabridin is **light/oxidation-sensitive and prone to discoloration**; whole extracts can darken and vary batch-to-batch. Favor opaque/airless packaging, antioxidants, and standardized (assayed) raw materials. Watch for **glabrene impurity** in glabridin raw material (a quality/safety flag). [21]
- **Sensory:** DPG and licochalcone formulas are typically elegant/lightweight; purified glabridin can carry color (yellow/amber) that must be managed.
- **Pairs well with:** niacinamide, vitamin C, tranexamic acid, alpha-arbutin (tone synergy); ceramides/fatty acids and centella/allantoin (soothing/barrier); **sunscreen is mandatory** for any tone product. Generally non-irritating, so it pairs comfortably with retinoids/acids as a **buffering soothing/brightening partner**.
- **Conflicts:** few true incompatibilities; main risks are **discoloration/degradation** (light, oxidizers) and **batch variability** of crude extracts.

## 7. Safety

- **Topical irritation/sensitization: low.** Topical licorice actives are widely used and **generally well tolerated**; licorice root extract rarely sensitizes on its own (allergy more often arises from co-ingredients). Long-term use of 2% 18β-glycyrrhetinic acid ointment reported no sensitization. [5][19]
- **Glabridin impurity caveat:** purified glabridin can carry **glabrene** and other impurities; impurity-laden material has raised toxicity/allergenicity flags — a **raw-material quality issue**, argues for assayed, high-purity sourcing. [21]
- **Sun sensitivity:** Not a photosensitizer; the actives are antioxidant/anti-inflammatory. (Daily SPF still required for tone results to hold.)
- **Pregnancy/lactation — the key nuance:** **Topical** cosmetic licorice is generally considered low-risk (minimal systemic absorption). The well-known danger is **ORAL** licorice/glycyrrhizin, which is linked to **pseudoaldosteronism (hypertension, hypokalemia, edema), miscarriage/preterm-birth signals, and is specifically cautioned in pregnancy.** This is a **systemic, ingestion-route** risk — it does **not** transfer to a leave-on cream — but copy must never blur the two. **We educate, we do not give medical advice**: frame as "topically well-tolerated; do not confuse with eating licorice; consult your doctor if pregnant." [9][22]
- **Systemic (oral) toxicity, for clarity:** glycyrrhizic acid → glycyrrhetinic acid inhibits **11β-HSD2**, raising cortisol's mineralocorticoid effect → **hypertension/hypokalemia.** Multiple case reports from licorice teas, candy, cough syrup. **Irrelevant to topical dose**, but the brand should know it so it never makes an "ingest for skin" claim. [9][22][23]
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** *Glycyrrhiza glabra* wild populations face **overharvesting/genetic erosion** (notably Iran); IUCN currently "least concern" but monitored. **Cultivated** licorice (Turkey, China, Italy supply >80% of ~30,000 t/yr) is the responsible route; **adventitious-root / cell-culture production** is emerging. Prefer **cultivated, traceable, assayed** raw material — it also supports a clean sustainability story. [24][25]

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

Licorice actives sit comfortably on the **cosmetic** side **if claims stay appearance-based** — but the temptation to import drug-adjacent language (rosacea, acne, atopic dermatitis, "treats hyperpigmentation") is strong because much of the *evidence* comes from disease-state studies.

- ✅ **Safe cosmetic language:** "helps visibly even skin tone," "for a brighter, more radiant-looking complexion," "helps reduce the look of dark spots / post-blemish marks," "soothes the look of redness-prone, sensitive skin," "comforting/calming," "antioxidant support against environmental stress."
- ⚠️ **Risky / drug-like — avoid or flag for legal/substantiation:** "treats melasma / rosacea / atopic dermatitis / acne," "16× more effective than hydroquinone" (in-vitro stat presented as consumer benefit), "anti-inflammatory" (drug-ish — prefer "soothing/comforting"), "depigments," "boosts/repairs collagen," any borrowing of **disease-population RCT** results into general cosmetic copy.
- ⚠️ **Never** suggest ingesting licorice for skin (blood-pressure risk).
- **Substantiation note:** because the strong tone data are on **liquiritin/whole extract** and the redness data are on **combination formulas**, a claim built specifically around **"glabridin"** as the efficacious agent is **weakly substantiated** — flag any glabridin-hero efficacy claim for review. See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Cost tier: low for whole extract / DPG; moderate–high for purified glabridin or licochalcone A.** Crude *G. glabra* extract and dipotassium glycyrrhizate are inexpensive commodities. **Purified glabridin** (high % assay, low impurity) is markedly more expensive and the cost rises further with a delivery system (liposome/NLC/nanosuspension). **Licochalcone A** is effectively a **Beiersdorf-associated** active (patents/trade literature), which constrains independent sourcing/positioning. [2][3]
- **Quality is the spend:** demand a **standardized assay** (e.g., % glabridin or % glycyrrhizic acid), low **glabrene** impurity, and species clarity (*glabra* vs *inflata*). Crude extracts vary enormously by genotype/region. [5][21]
- **Supply risk: low–moderate.** Whole licorice is abundantly cultivated; purified single actives are more specialized. Prefer **cultivated, traceable** supply over wild-harvested for both sustainability and consistency. [24][25]

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

- **"Glabridin is 16× more effective than hydroquinone."** ⚠️ **In-vitro tyrosinase/lightening figure** repeated as if it were a clinical result. There is **no controlled human monotherapy trial of glabridin** for melasma. Do not present this as a consumer benefit. [6][15]
- **"Licorice clears melasma / is a natural hydroquinone."** ⚠️ Half-true: the *human* tone data are real but sit on **liquiritin and whole-extract** creams in **small, short** trials, often nanoparticle vehicles — and "clears/treats melasma" is **drug language.** [13][17][15]
- **"Anti-inflammatory powerhouse for rosacea/eczema."** ⚠️ Genuine soothing mechanism and human data — but mostly in **combination products** and disease populations; saying it "treats rosacea/eczema" crosses into drug claims. [10][4]
- **"Anti-aging / collagen-boosting / anti-wrinkle."** ❌ **Not demonstrated** in humans topically — collagen/MMP/photoaging support is **in-vitro/animal only.** [8]
- **"Longevity active."** ⚠️ The most defensible longevity-shaped mechanism is **licochalcone A's Nrf2/HO-1 antioxidant induction**, but that's **preclinical**; do not claim cellular-longevity benefits. [3][14]
- **"All-natural so it's safe — and you can take it internally for glowing skin."** ❌ Topically fine; **orally dangerous** (pseudoaldosteronism). Never conflate. [9][22]

## 11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ **SUPPORTING.** Licorice actives are a **safe, well-tolerated, well-liked brightening-and-soothing botanical** that genuinely support the "even, calm, radiant = healthier-looking skin" dimension of skin-longevity — but they are **not** a structural anti-aging hero. **One-line:** include as a **supporting tone-evening / redness-calming / antioxidant** active (best as **standardized whole extract or DPG for soothing**, and **liquiritin or assayed glabridin-in-a-delivery-system for brightening**), with **appearance-only claims** and no borrowing of the in-vitro "16× hydroquinone" or disease-state RCT halos. **Conditional within "supporting":** worth it **only if** the raw material is assayed/low-impurity and (for glabridin) properly delivered; **skip glabridin specifically** if it can't be stabilized/penetrated, and lean on liquiritin/whole-extract + niacinamide/vitamin C instead. Licochalcone A is effective but **Beiersdorf-entangled** — fine as a soothing component, awkward as a differentiator.

**Longevity relevance:** Moderate and *appearance-led*. Even tone, reduced redness, and antioxidant (Nrf2) support are visible, credible markers of "healthy, youthful" skin and give a legitimate inflammaging/oxidative-stress narrative. The deeper hallmarks — collagen, senescence, mitochondria, glycation — are **not** where licorice earns its keep. Use it for the **calm, even-radiance** story, paired with better-evidenced structural actives (retinoids, sunscreen, barrier lipids) that do the heavy anti-aging lifting.

## 13. Evidence verification (adversarial)

_Independent adversarial fact-check performed 2026-06-19. Each headline efficacy claim was tested for: (a) is the evidence HUMAN + TOPICAL or quietly borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral data; (b) are the cited studies real and do they say what is claimed; (c) are key studies industry-funded or very small. Twelve of the dossier's load-bearing citations were re-located against PubMed/PMC/journal records._

### Overall evidence grade: **B− (low end of moderate), claim-by-claim**

There is **no single grade** for "licorice" because it is not one ingredient. Broken out by the actual benefit claimed:

| Claim | Grade | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Tone / brightening (as a licorice *family*, mostly liquiritin + whole extract)** | **B** | Multiple real human topical RCTs (Amer 2000 n=20; Nahidi/Sahebkar n=44; Zubair; Costa n=56), but small, short, often combination/nanoparticle vehicles |
| **Tone / brightening attributed specifically to *glabridin*** | **D** | No controlled human monotherapy trial exists; human signal is one finished-product study (Makino). The famous efficacy stat is **in-vitro only** |
| **Redness / soothing (licochalcone A, glycyrrhetinic acid, DPG)** | **B−/C+** | Real human data (Schoelermann n=32; large observational cohorts) but almost always **combination formulas** (Lico-A + 4-t-butylcyclohexanol) and several **Beiersdorf-funded** |
| **Anti-wrinkle / firming / collagen / photoaging structure** | **D** | In-vitro / animal only. No human topical anti-wrinkle RCT exists |
| **"Longevity" (Nrf2/HO-1) cellular benefit** | **D** | Preclinical mechanism only |
| **Systemic oral-licorice risk transferring to topical** | n/a (safety) | Correctly flagged as ORAL-only; does not transfer to leave-on cosmetic |

**Net:** the dossier's own headline framing — strong-ish for tone/redness *appearance*, weak/absent for structural anti-aging — **survives adversarial scrutiny.** This is an unusually honest dossier; it pre-empts most of the attacks an adversary would make (the glabridin-vs-liquiritin bait-and-switch, the in-vitro "16×" stat, combination-formula confounding, industry funding). The grade is held at B− rather than higher only because the *best human evidence does not belong to the molecule (glabridin) the category is marketed on*, and the cleanest-sounding human results are in disease populations and/or industry-funded.

### Citations verified as REAL and accurately represented
Yokota 1998 (in-vitro + guinea-pig, correctly labelled animal); Amer & Metwalli 2000 (n=20 split-face, liquiritin — correctly *not* glabridin); Nahidi/Sahebkar 2016 (numbers reproduced exactly: MMASI 11.03→1.41 active vs 11.25→2.37 placebo, p<0.001; whole extract in SLN vehicle); Schoelermann 2016 (Lico-A + 4-t-butylcyclohexanol combo); Dall'Oglio 2019 (**confirmed Beiersdorf-funded**, observational, multi-active — flag is accurate); Costa et al. (7% botanical combo vs 2% HQ, no significant difference); Kulthanan 2020 (n=110 vehicle-controlled maintenance); Angelova-Fischer 2018 (vehicle-controlled emollient); Saeedi 2003 (whole extract gel). The dossier's "16× hydroquinone is in-vitro only" caveat is **correct** and well-policed throughout.

### Errors found and CORRECTED in the dossier body
1. **Wananukul 2013 comparator overstated (§4D).** The dossier said Lico-A was "non-inferior to **hydrocortisone butyrate 0.1%**." The actual study (PMID 24163988) is a split-side comparison against **plain 1% hydrocortisone — the *weakest* OTC steroid**, not the mid-potency butyrate ester, and it is not framed as a formal non-inferiority trial. Beating the mildest possible steroid is a far weaker result than beating a mid-potency one. **Corrected.**
2. **Reference [15] misattributed author (§4D, §12).** The key systematic review ("Are natural ingredients effective in the management of hyperpigmentation?", JCAD 2018, PMC5843359) was cited as **"Sarkar R, et al."** It is actually authored by **Hollinger, Angra & Halder.** Plausible-but-wrong (Rashmi Sarkar is a real, prolific pigmentation author). **Corrected in body and source list.**
3. **Saeedi 2003 mischaracterized (§4D).** Called a "glycyrrhetinic-acid gel"; it is a **whole licorice extract standardized to ~20% glycyrrhizinic acid**, not a purified-aglycone gel. **Corrected.**

### Caveats the brand must carry forward
- **The category's best human evidence is on liquiritin and whole extract, not glabridin.** Any "glabridin hero" efficacy claim is **D-grade** and would be weakly substantiated — exactly as §8/§10 already warn. If the brand wants defensible brightening copy, lean the *evidence* on liquiritin/whole-extract, not glabridin %.
- **Redness/soothing human data cannot isolate the licorice active** — it rides inside Lico-A + 4-t-butylcyclohexanol formulas, several Beiersdorf-funded. Treat "licochalcone A calms redness" as **formula-level, partly industry-sourced** evidence.
- **All structural anti-aging (collagen, wrinkles, firmness, senescence, mitochondria, glycation) is in-vitro/animal only.** Do not let any of it migrate into human-benefit copy.
- **Reference [14] (Lico-A Nrf2 primary papers) remains [unverified]** at the per-paper level — unchanged from the dossier's own honesty note.

### Claims that should be softened (beyond the edits already applied)
- §3 hallmark table and §11 verdict label barrier/AD support as "moderate." Given it is disease-population + combination-formula + mild-steroid-comparator, **"weak–moderate, disease-derived"** is more defensible than "moderate."
- Any standalone use of the word "**non-inferior**" for Lico-A vs steroids should be dropped brand-wide; the evidence supports "comparable in a split-side study vs the mildest steroid," not regulatory non-inferiority.
- The "~60% relapse reduction" figure for Angelova-Fischer (§4D) could not be independently confirmed to that exact number in this pass; keep the *direction* (significant reduction vs vehicle) and treat the precise percentage as **approximate/[unverified]**.
