# Ingredient: Plant / Protein Peptides — Soy, Silk (Sericin/Fibroin), Rice, Hydrolyzed Collagen (INCI: see below)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering (as a **supporting / sensory** ingredient family, not a hero) · **Class:** Peptides (protein hydrolysates)

> Scope note: This is a *family* dossier covering four loosely related "protein peptide" inputs that the supply chain and marketing lump together. They are NOT mechanistically equivalent. Hydrolyzed collagen is an animal protein; soy and rice are plant proteins; silk (sericin/fibroin) is an insect-derived protein. They share one honest, well-supported function applied topically — **humectant / film-forming surface conditioning** — and a lot of *borrowed* hype from the very different **oral** collagen literature. The single most important finding in this dossier: **almost all the strong "anti-aging" data for these proteins is from ORAL supplementation or in-vitro fibroblast assays, not from topical human use.** Keep that wall up everywhere in copy.

---

## 1. Identity

| Input | Common name(s) | Representative INCI | Source | Typical MW |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Hydrolyzed collagen** | Collagen peptides, marine/bovine collagen | Hydrolyzed Collagen; Soluble Collagen; Collagen Amino Acids | Bovine, porcine, fish (marine) | ~0.3–8 kDa (avg 2–3 kDa); intact collagen ~300 kDa |
| **Soy peptides** | Soy protein, soy peptide | Hydrolyzed Soy Protein; Glycine Soja (Soybean) Protein; Soy Amino Acids | *Glycine max* | sub-kDa to ~few kDa (acid/enzyme hydrolysate) |
| **Silk peptides** | Sericin; fibroin; silk amino acids | Sericin; Hydrolyzed Sericin; Hydrolyzed Silk; Fibroin; Silk Amino Acids | *Bombyx mori* silkworm | Sericin native 10–300 kDa; hydrolysates much smaller |
| **Rice peptides** | Hydrolyzed rice protein | Hydrolyzed Rice Protein; Oryza Sativa (Rice) Bran Protein | *Oryza sativa* | low-MW hydrolysate |

**Key forms/derivatives that matter:** generic "hydrolyzed [X] protein" (humectant/conditioner), vs. **lipidated signal peptides** built on a similar logic but chemically distinct — e.g. **palmitoyl pentapeptide-4 (pal-KTTKS, "Matrixyl")** and palmitoyl tripeptides. Those lipidated synthetics are a *separate ingredient category* (covered in the brand's dedicated peptides taxonomy entry) and have their own — better — topical evidence. Do not let the "peptide" label blur the two.

## 2. Class & mechanism

All four are **protein hydrolysates**: large parent proteins enzymatically or acid-broken into shorter peptides + free amino acids. At the skin surface, the *evidenced* mechanism for the unmodified hydrolysates is physical/colligative, not signaling:

1. **Humectancy** — short peptides and amino acids (serine, glycine, proline, glutamic/aspartic acid) are hygroscopic; they overlap with the skin's own **Natural Moisturizing Factor (NMF)** amino-acid pool and bind water in the stratum corneum.
2. **Film formation** — larger fragments lay down a thin, flexible, moisture-retentive film, giving an immediate "tightening"/smoothing and reduced transepidermal water loss (TEWL). This is the basis of the "instant firming" sensory effect — it is **cosmetic and temporary**, not dermal remodeling.
3. **Antioxidant scavenging (in vitro)** — many of these hydrolysates show free-radical scavenging in tube assays. Relevance to live skin at cosmetic doses is unproven.
4. **Putative signaling (mostly in vitro / borrowed):** Soy and collagen peptides have been shown *in cultured fibroblasts* to upregulate type-I collagen gene expression; soy isoflavones (a co-traveler in soy, not a peptide) act on estrogen receptor-β; soy serine-protease inhibitors (STI/BBI — again not the peptide fraction) block the **PAR-2** keratinocyte pathway and reduce melanosome transfer (pigment). These are real but are **different molecules riding in the same botanical**, and the signaling has not been cleanly demonstrated for the topical *peptide* fraction in humans.

The honest one-line mechanism for copy purposes: **these are NMF-mimetic humectant/film-forming surface conditioners.**

## 4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core)

> **Read this as a wall of separation.** Tiers are ordered weakest-context → most-relevant for a *topical* product: in-vitro → animal → human ORAL → human TOPICAL. The brand sells **topical** product, so only the last tier directly substantiates on-pack claims.

### Tier 1 — IN VITRO (cell/enzyme assays)

- **Tokudome, Nakamura, Kage, Todo, Sugibayashi, Hashimoto (2012),** *Int J Food Sci Nutr.* "Effects of soybean peptide and collagen peptide on collagen synthesis in normal human dermal fibroblasts." Cultured human dermal fibroblasts. Both soybean peptide (SP) and collagen peptide (CP) raised **type-I collagen (COL1A1)** gene expression at 4/8/24 h (with a vitamin-C derivative). **Caveat:** SP *alone* raised MMP-1 (collagen-degrading); only CP and SP+CP combos reduced MMP-1. Peptide MW ~500 Da. **Limitation:** cell culture ≠ skin; says nothing about whether topically applied peptide reaches living fibroblasts. [1]
- **Zhang et al. (2021),** *J Cosmet Dermatol.* Collagen and soy peptides reduced ROS and increased collagen-related ECM gene expression in tert-butyl-hydroperoxide- and UVA-stressed dermal fibroblasts (flow cytometry, qPCR, 3-D collagen lattice). **Limitation:** in vitro only; protective effect on a *contraction* model, not human outcomes. [2]
- **Chen et al. (2021),** *Molecules.* "Evaluating the Antioxidants, Whitening and Antiaging Properties of Rice Protein Hydrolysates." Enzyme/cell assays: DPPH IC50 ~42.6 mg/mL, ABTS IC50 ~2.1 mg/mL; tyrosinase ("whitening") IC50 **107.6 mg/mL** — i.e. >1000× weaker than ascorbic acid (IC50 ~0.098 mg/mL); hyaluronidase IC50 ~8.9 mg/mL. Did **not** test collagenase/elastase. **Limitation:** in vitro only; the active concentrations are far above what a leave-on could plausibly deliver to skin. This is the honest ceiling on "rice peptide brightens." [3]
- **Paine, Sharlow, Liebel, Eisinger, Shapiro, Seiberg (2001),** *J Invest Dermatol.* "An alternative approach to depigmentation by soybean extracts via inhibition of the PAR-2 pathway." Soybean serine-protease inhibitors (STI) inhibit PAR-2, reduce keratinocyte phagocytosis of melanosomes (~50% melanin reduction in vitro; depigmentation in vivo in dark swine). **Note: this is the STI/BBI protein fraction, NOT generic soy peptides.** [4]
- **Biotransformed soybean cream — collagen stimulation in vitro and ex vivo (2023),** *PMC10591485.* Increased collagen-I in fibroblasts and in human skin explants. **Limitation:** ex-vivo explant, not a controlled in-vivo human topical endpoint. [5]

### Tier 2 — ANIMAL

- **Aramwit et al. and others — sericin wound-healing models (rodent).** Sericin shows mitogenic/cytoprotective effects on keratinocytes & fibroblasts, antibacterial/antioxidant/anti-inflammatory and pro-angiogenic activity; accelerates excision-wound contraction in mice. Reviewed in **Mazurek et al. (2024),** *Macromol Biosci*, "Silk Sericin and Its Effect on Skin Wound Healing: A State of the Art." **Limitation:** wound-healing ≠ cosmetic anti-aging; animal skin/dosing differs. [6]
- **Soy protein/peptide skin-repair in rats (2020),** *J Funct Foods* (ScienceDirect S1756464620304552). Nutritional (oral) soy peptide supplementation enhanced skin repair in rats — **oral route, animal.** [7]
- **Genistein (soy isoflavone) in ovariectomized rats (PMC3312494).** Topical/oral genistein improved estrogen-deficiency skin changes. **Again isoflavone, not peptide.** [8]

### Tier 3 — HUMAN, ORAL (strong literature — but wrong format for a topical brand)

- **Pu et al. (2023),** *Nutrients.* Systematic review & meta-analysis of **26 RCTs, 1,721 participants**: oral hydrolyzed collagen significantly improved **hydration** (pooled SMD 0.63; 95% CI 0.38–0.88) and **elasticity** (SMD 0.72; 95% CI 0.40–1.03) vs placebo. **Limitations the authors flag:** heterogeneous formulations, several trials small (<40), many products **blended collagen with vitamins/other actives** (confounds attribution), and the field has heavy **industry sponsorship**. [9]
- **de Miranda et al. / others — meta-analyses converge** on the same direction; a 2025 *Am J Med* meta-analysis of RCTs reached similar conclusions. [10]
- **Oral soy isoflavone (Izumi; and Accorsi-Neto et al.)** improved aged-skin parameters / dermal markers in adult/postmenopausal women. **Isoflavone supplement, oral.** [11]
- **Oral soy protein + isoflavones RCT (PMC10574417, 2023),** 44 postmenopausal women, 6 months: improved facial wrinkle severity, pigmentation, hydration vs placebo. **Oral; the active is isoflavone-bearing soy protein, not a topical peptide.** [12]

> **Translation rule for copy:** None of Tier 3 substantiates a topical claim. "Clinically shown to improve elasticity" is true for *swallowing* collagen, and must NEVER be implied on a topical SKU. This is the #1 hype trap in the category.

### Tier 4 — HUMAN, TOPICAL (the only tier that backs on-pack claims)

**RCT / controlled:**
- **Wallo, Nebus, Leyden (2007),** *J Drugs Dermatol.* Double-blind, **vehicle-controlled, 12-week**, n=65 women w/ moderate facial photodamage. A **soy moisturizer containing non-denatured STI + BBI** beat vehicle on mottled pigmentation, blotchiness, dullness, overall tone & texture (significant from wk 2–12). **Critical caveat:** this tested a **finished formula**, and the active is the **protease-inhibitor fraction (PAR-2)**, not generic "soy peptide." Strong evidence — for *that formula's pigment/tone benefit*, not for soy peptides broadly. [13]
- **Moraes et al. (2017),** *Gynecol Endocrinol.* Double-blind RCT, n=30 postmenopausal women, 24 wks, pre-auricular biopsies: **topical 4% genistein** increased facial type-I & III collagen and dermal thickness (less than estradiol, but real). **Caveat: genistein is a soy ISOFLAVONE, not a peptide.** Best human topical evidence in the "soy" orbit — but it doesn't transfer to hydrolyzed soy protein. [14]
- **Aramwit et al. (2012),** *BMC Nephrol.* Randomized, double-blind, **placebo-controlled** within-subject trial, **8% sericin cream**, n=50 (47 completed) hemodialysis patients, 6 wks twice daily: significantly increased skin **hydration** (arms 28.7→33.6, p=0.047; legs 25.1→29.1, p=0.025) and reduced **pruritus** (itch 7.05→2.23, p=0.008). **Limitations:** within-subject design, special population (uremic pruritus), small/short. Real human topical signal for sericin = **hydration + soothing**, not anti-aging. [15]

**Lower-tier topical (open-label / small / finished-product / review-level):**
- **Aguirre-Cruz et al. (2020),** *Antioxidants (Basel).* Review of collagen hydrolysates oral + topical. On **topical**: only ~8% of 5–13 kDa hydrolyzed collagen penetrates in models; topical studies are small (n≈11–40), short, and show **slight stratum-corneum hydration / microtextural smoothing / TEWL reduction** — i.e. surface conditioning, not dermal collagen synthesis. The "stronger" topical dataset cited (fish-collagen peptide, 30 women, 30 days) still measured surface moisture/elasticity, not biopsy collagen. [16]
- Rice/soy hydrolyzed proteins in finished cosmetics: supplier/INCI documentation supports **humectant + film-forming + skin-conditioning** function; independent peer-reviewed *topical human* anti-aging RCTs for isolated **hydrolyzed rice protein** or generic **hydrolyzed soy protein** are **essentially absent.** Treat their leave-on benefit as "feel/hydration," nothing more. [17]

**Bottom line of Tier 4:** Defensible topical human claims are narrow — (a) **hydration / smoother feel** (all four, weak-moderate), (b) **soothing** (sericin), (c) **tone/pigment** for a *soy-protease-inhibitor* formula specifically. Everything else is in-vitro or oral.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- The stratum corneum is largely impermeable to hydrophilic molecules **> ~500 Da**. Hydrolyzed collagen averages ~2–3 kDa; only ~8% of 5–13 kDa material penetrates even in enhanced models — and "penetrate the SC" is not the same as "reach fibroblasts and signal." [16][18]
- Practical consequence: unmodified protein hydrolysates **act at/near the surface** (humectant, film). Claims of "delivering building blocks to rebuild collagen" topically are **not supported**.
- The route that *does* penetrate is **lipidation** (e.g. pal-KTTKS / Matrixyl): attaching a fatty acid raises percutaneous absorption while keeping bioactivity. That is a different ingredient class and should be evaluated separately. [18]
- Free amino acids released by hydrolysis are small enough to contribute to surface NMF but won't meaningfully reconstruct dermal matrix.

## 6. Formulation notes

- **Typical use levels:** generally **1–5%** of a supplied solution (suppliers often sell ~ low-% protein liquids; finished-product concentrations are modest). They are conditioners/humectants, not dose-dependent actives. Sericin cream evidence used **8%**; topical genistein RCT used **4%** (isoflavone, not peptide).
- **pH:** broadly compatible with skincare pH (~4.5–6.5). Acid-hydrolyzed proteins are pH-tolerant; avoid extremes.
- **Stability:** proteins/peptides are **microbially attractive** (good nitrogen source) — robust **preservation is mandatory.** Can discolor/odor over time; watch for hydrolysis/oxidation. Sericin gels are processable in water.
- **Pairings:** play well with glycerin, panthenol, HA, niacinamide, ceramides (barrier/hydration stack). Soy moisturizer benefit pairs logically with the brand's barrier-repair thesis. The fibroblast data used a **vitamin-C derivative** alongside the peptides.
- **Conflicts:** strong oxidizers / very low pH / high heat can degrade proteins. Cationic/anionic charge interactions can affect texture in surfactant systems.
- **Sensory:** prized for an immediate **smoothing, "soft-focus," skin-tightening film** and slip — genuine formulation value as a *sensory/marketing* layer.

## 7. Safety

- **General tolerance:** good for most users at cosmetic levels; long history of use.
- **Sensitization — the serious caveat: hydrolyzed proteins can sensitize, and the precedent is hydrolyzed WHEAT protein.** The **"Cha no Shizuku" facial-soap outbreak in Japan** sensitized **>1,900 people** to acid-hydrolyzed wheat protein (Glupearl 19S), causing contact allergy AND **wheat-dependent exercise-induced anaphylaxis** — i.e. a *cosmetic* drove a *food* allergy. (Fukutomi et al., *Allergy* 2014; outbreak reports in *J Allergy Clin Immunol*.) **Lesson:** acid-hydrolyzed plant/grain proteins carry a real, documented sensitization risk; favor better-characterized enzymatic hydrolysates, lower MW distributions associated with lower allergenicity where data exist, and route claims through legal/tox review. [19][20]
- **Allergen cross-reactivity:** **soy** is a major food allergen; **silk/sericin** can be allergenic in textile/occupational contexts; collagen is animal-derived (BSE/kosher/halal/vegan considerations). Patch-testing and allergen labeling matter.
- **Sun sensitivity:** none expected (not photosensitizers).
- **Pregnancy:** no specific topical red flag for the peptide fraction at cosmetic levels; note that **soy isoflavones are phytoestrogens** — for the isoflavone-bearing soy actives, the conservative line is to flag hormonal-activity questions for review, not for the hydrolyzed-protein fraction.
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** collagen = animal byproduct (not vegan; marine has lower BSE concern, fishy odor risk); soy/rice = plant (vegan-friendly, watch GMO/deforestation narratives for soy); silk = insect-derived (**not vegan/vegetarian for many**, animal-welfare scrutiny re: silkworm processing — material for a values-based brand to weigh).

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Cosmetic vs. drug line:** as **surface conditioners/humectants**, these sit safely in cosmetic territory **only if claims stay appearance/feel-based.** Claims like "rebuilds collagen," "boosts collagen X%," "repairs the dermis," "treats wrinkles" would imply a structure/function change → **drug territory (US FDA)** and require substantiation few of these ingredients can meet topically.
- **The borrowed-evidence trap (flag for legal):** Do not import the **oral** collagen meta-analysis or **oral** soy-isoflavone trials onto a **topical** SKU. Saying "collagen peptides are clinically proven to improve elasticity" next to a topical product is misleading-by-context even if each word is "true" of the oral format. **Hard stop.**
- **The "soy peptide" vs "soy isoflavone/protease-inhibitor" trap:** the best soy human data (Wallo 2007; Moraes 2017) are from **isoflavone/STI-BBI** fractions, not generic hydrolyzed soy *protein*. Don't cite those to substantiate a "soy peptide" claim.
- **Safer cosmetic language (per glossary):** "helps **visibly** smooth," "**supports** hydration," "leaves skin feeling firmer/softer," "conditions the surface," "**helps improve the appearance of** tone" (soy-protease-inhibitor formula only, with the specific actives present).
- **Avoid:** "boosts collagen," "rebuilds," "repairs," "anti-aging" as a structure claim, any "% increase" without an on-formula human study. Flag all of these for substantiation/legal review. (See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).)

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Cost tier: low to mid.** Generic hydrolyzed soy/rice/wheat proteins are **inexpensive commodity** conditioners. Hydrolyzed collagen is cheap–moderate (marine costs more). Sericin/silk peptides run **mid**; well-characterized cosmetic-grade sericin and lipidated signal peptides cost more.
- **Suppliers:** broadly available through standard cosmetic-ingredient distributors (multiple INCI-listed grades); easy to source, easy to formulate.
- **Supply considerations:** commodity, low supply risk. The real "cost" is **reputational/claims risk**, not raw-material price.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

- **Hype:** "Topical collagen/peptides rebuild your skin's collagen." **Reality:** unmodified hydrolysates barely penetrate; topical benefit is **surface hydration + film**, not dermal remodeling. [16][18]
- **Hype:** "Clinically proven to improve elasticity & hydration." **Reality:** true for **oral** collagen (Pu 2023 meta-analysis) — **not** for topical, and often in industry-funded, blended-formula trials. [9]
- **Hype:** "Soy peptides boost collagen." **Reality:** in vitro only (and soy peptide *alone* raised the collagen-degrading enzyme MMP-1 in Tokudome 2012). [1]
- **Hype:** "Rice peptides brighten." **Reality:** tyrosinase inhibition is ~1000× weaker than vitamin C and at implausibly high concentrations. [3]
- **Hype:** "Silk protein = luxury anti-aging." **Reality:** best human topical data is **hydration + anti-itch** in a special population; the rest is wound-healing/animal. [6][15]
- **Honest core:** a genuinely nice **sensory + humectant + tone-supporting (soy)** family — undersold as "feel-good," oversold as "anti-aging."

---

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**SUPPORTING / CONDITIONAL — not a hero.** One line: *These protein hydrolysates earn a place as honest humectant/film-forming sensory and hydration aids (and, for a soy-protease-inhibitor formula specifically, tone) — but the headline "collagen/elasticity" evidence is oral or in-vitro, and unmodified peptides don't penetrate, so they cannot carry an anti-aging hero claim.*

- **Use as:** secondary humectant/skin-feel layer in the barrier-repair cream; soy (isoflavone/STI-BBI form) as a candidate **tone/pigment** supporting active **if** the brand wants that benefit and accepts the phytoestrogen-review burden.
- **Skip as a hero / as a "topical collagen" story** — that's exactly the kind of borrowed-evidence overreach this brand's credibility positioning should avoid.
- **If a true topical collagen-signaling claim is wanted,** evaluate **lipidated signal peptides (pal-KTTKS / Matrixyl, palmitoyl tripeptides)** in their own dossier — different, better-penetrating, better-evidenced category.
- **Longevity-relevance note:** Low. These map mainly to **hydration / barrier feel** (and surface tone for soy). They do **not** credibly touch the deeper longevity hallmarks (senescence, mitochondrial decline, glycation, ECM remodeling) when applied topically. The strongest longevity-flavored data (oral collagen elasticity; oral soy isoflavone dermal markers) belongs to the **ingestible** format, which is out of scope for a topical SKU.
