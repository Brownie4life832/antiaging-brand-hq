# Ingredient: Thymoquinone (Nigella sativa / black cumin / black seed)

**Slug:** thymoquinone · **Class:** Anti-glycation & related (antioxidant / anti-inflammatory polyphenol-adjacent quinone)
**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ⬜ considering / ☑️ shortlisted (conditional — see Verdict) / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product

> **One-line honest summary:** A genuinely interesting multi-target antioxidant/anti-inflammatory/anti-glycation molecule from black-seed oil with one *real* (if small, single-center, bias-prone) topical human RCT in acne and several small topical human trials of the parent oil — but almost all the anti-aging-specific data (collagen, glycation, photoaging, pigment) is in-vitro or animal, and the molecule is chemically fragile (light- and alkaline-unstable, poorly water-soluble). Promising supporting actor, not a proven anti-aging hero.

## 2. Class & mechanism

TQ is filed here under **anti-glycation & related** because one of its better-characterized biochemical actions is **inhibiting advanced glycation end-product (AGE) formation** — but mechanistically it is a **promiscuous redox-active small molecule**, not a single-pathway actor. Documented mechanisms (mostly in-vitro/animal):

- **Direct antioxidant / radical scavenging** and induction of endogenous antioxidant defenses via **Nrf2/ARE → HO-1, NQO-1, SOD, glutathione**. [6]
- **Anti-glycation:** inhibits Amadori-product and AGE formation, protects proteins (albumin, hemoglobin, myoglobin, SOD) from glycation; downstream it blunts AGE-induced NF-κB activation. [10][12]
- **Anti-inflammatory:** suppresses NF-κB, COX-2, and pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6). [5][6]
- **Pro-repair signaling:** in wound models it is associated with ↑ fibroblast proliferation, ↑ collagen deposition, and ↑ angiogenic signaling (VEGF, PDGF). [1][4]
- **Anti-melanogenic** (in cells): downregulates MITF → tyrosinase via the **Wnt/β-catenin** pathway. [7][8]
- **Antimicrobial** (incl. *S. aureus*, *Cutibacterium acnes*-relevant antibacterial activity supporting the acne data). [3]

Note the **internal tension**: TQ *reduces* pigment in mouse melanoma cells (anti-melanogenic), yet *N. sativa* whole extract/TQ has been reported to *stimulate* pigment cells (melanogenic) in lizard melanophores and is used clinically for **vitiligo re-pigmentation**. The likely explanation is dose-, model-, and matrix-dependence (isolated TQ vs whole oil; tumor melanoma cells vs normal melanocytes/melanophores). This is a genuine unresolved contradiction, not a settled "brightening" story.

## 4. Evidence review by tier (most → least removed from real topical human use)

### Tier A — In-vitro / biochemical (abundant, generally positive, low translational weight)

- **Bordoni et al. 2019 — "Antioxidant and Anti-Inflammatory Properties of *Nigella sativa* Oil in Human Pre-Adipocytes"** and the broader TQ literature establish baseline antioxidant/anti-inflammatory activity (context for mechanism). *(supportive background)*
- **TQ anti-glycation (Losso et al. / Awad et al. line of work):** at **20 µM**, TQ inhibited hemoglobin glycation ~39%, formation of Amadori products ~82%, methylglyoxal-driven albumin glycation ~68%, and late glycation products ~78%. [10] Structural studies confirm TQ protects **SOD** and **human myoglobin** from glycation. [12] *Limitation: pure-protein/biochemical assays, supraphysiologic in some cases, no skin matrix.*
- **Thymocid® (standardized black-cumin seed extract) in B16F10 melanoma cells — Bhattacharjee/Akhtar et al., 2020, *Antioxidants* (MDPI), PMC7400895:** the **extract** inhibited AGE formation **16.7–70.7%**, collagen cross-linking **45.1–93.3%**, collagenase **10.4–92.4%**, plus elastase inhibition and reduced melanogenesis. [1] *Limitation: a melanoma cell line + cell-free enzyme assays; this is the **whole extract**, not isolated TQ; dose-response wide; explicitly framed by authors as "cosmeceutical potential," i.e. hypothesis-generating.*
- **Anti-melanogenesis — Kim et al., 2019/2020, *Int. J. Oncology*, PMID 31789395:** TQ 10–20 µM dose-dependently ↓ MITF, tyrosinase, and β-catenin in B16F10 cells (Wnt/β-catenin pathway). [7][8] *Limitation: melanoma cells, µM doses; opposite direction to the vitiligo clinical use.*
- **UVA photoprotection — (2021), *Toxicol. Appl. Pharmacol.* / ScienceDirect S0161589021000985:** TQ protected **cultured human keratinocytes** against UVA-induced oxidative stress, inflammation, COX-2, and mitochondrial apoptosis via Nrf2/ARE. [6] *Limitation: cell culture; UVA only; no human skin or photoaging endpoint.*
- **Senescence/longevity — TQ-loaded chitosan nanoparticles, 2025, PMC11858917:** ↓ oxidative aging via SIRT1/FOXO3a — but in a **testicular aging** model, not skin. [9] *Limitation: off-target tissue; included only as a longevity-mechanism signal.*

### Tier B — Animal (mostly wound healing / burns; consistent but indication ≠ anti-aging)

- **Comprehensive review of in-vivo wound healing — Sallehuddin et al., 2020, *Int. J. Environ. Res. Public Health* 17(11):4160; PMC7312523; doi:10.3390/ijerph17114160:** pools rodent studies showing TQ/N. sativa accelerate wound closure, ↑ fibroblast proliferation, ↑ collagen density, ↑ angiogenesis, with anti-inflammatory/antioxidant/antibacterial mechanisms. [4]
- **Rat burn model — Bayrak/Yıldız et al. (TQ in burns), *Burns*-type study, PMID 23816998:** topical and topical+systemic TQ gave the smallest necrotic areas (~6.1–6.7 cm vs ~11.2 cm control). [2] *Limitation: burn injury, not chronological/photo-aging.*
- **TQ nanoemulgel wound healing in Wistar rats — Khan et al., 2021, *AAPS PharmSciTech* / PMID 34202733; PMC8270244:** TQ nanoemulgel healed faster than conventional hydrogel and comparably to 1% silver sulfadiazine. [13] *Limitation: efficacy is partly a delivery-system story.*
- **TQ films/hydrogels — (2020), *Biomedicines* 8(10):386; PMC7600314:** controlled *S. aureus* in infected mouse wounds; in a keratinocyte scratch assay 100 ng/mL TQ gave 85% closure at day 6 vs 50% vehicle. [3]
- **Random-pattern skin-flap viability — (2024), PMC11487308:** TQ improved flap survival (anti-ischemic/antioxidant). *(supportive)*

### Tier C — Human, ORAL (extensive for *N. sativa*; safety-relevant, weak for skin appearance)

- **Phase I safety RCT of TQ-rich black-cumin oil (BCO-5 / BlaQmax®) — Thomas et al., 2022, *Toxicology Reports* 9:851–862; PMC9742867; doi:10.1016/j.toxrep.2022.04.020:** randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, **n=70**, **200 mg/day oral for 90 days** of a 5%-TQ oil. Conclusion: **safe** at that dose, no significant adverse biochemical/hematologic changes. [14] *Use: establishes oral-systemic safety of a TQ-standardized oil; NOT a skin-efficacy or topical study.*
- Numerous oral *N. sativa* RCTs exist for allergy/atopic disease, metabolic and inflammatory markers (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis, Behçet's) showing ↓ IgE/eosinophils and ↓ inflammatory/oxidative markers. Relevant only as **systemic plausibility**, not topical proof.

### Tier D — Human, TOPICAL (the tier that actually matters — small, mixed quality)

> Mostly the **whole oil**; only the acne hydrogel is explicitly **standardized to thymoquinone**. None target chronological/photo-aging endpoints (wrinkles, elasticity, AGEs in skin).

- **Acne — TQ-standardized hydrogel RCT — Soleymani et al., 2020, *Phytotherapy Research* 34(11):3052–3062; doi:10.1002/ptr.6739:** randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled, **n=60** (30/30), N. sativa hydrogel **standardized on thymoquinone** vs placebo, twice daily, **60 days**. Reported **~78% mean reduction in IGA** vs **~3.3%** for vehicle. [15] **The single best topical, TQ-standardized human RCT** — but single-center, modest n, acne (not aging), industry-adjacent herbal formulation. **Treat the ~78% figure with caution:** an effect of that magnitude over vehicle is unusually large for a topical botanical acne trial, the near-zero (~3.3%) vehicle response is atypical, and this trial type (small, single-center, from a narrow phytotherapy research community) is associated with optimistic effect estimates and high risk of bias. Verified to the primary record (PMID 32548864); the *number* is reported accurately by us, but its real-world generalizability is weak.
- **Acne — N. sativa + vinegar vs benzoyl peroxide 5% RCT — (2025):** n=40, 28 days, topical N. sativa powder+vinegar comparable-ish to BPO. [16] *(oil/powder, not isolated TQ.)*
- **Acne — 10% N. sativa oil lotion, 8 weeks:** reported safe/well-tolerated with efficacy. [17] *(oil.)*
- **Hand eczema RCT — Yousefi et al., 2013, *J. Dermatolog. Treat.*:** topical N. sativa vs betamethasone vs Eucerin, twice daily 4 weeks (HECSI/DLQI); N. sativa comparable to betamethasone on quality-of-life-type measures. [18] *(oil; small.)*
- **Vitiligo — Ghorbani-Birgani et al., 2014, *Iran. Red Crescent Med. J.*:** N. sativa oil vs fish oil, **n=52 (26/26)**, topical; **greater re-pigmentation with N. sativa** on hands/face/genitals; no adverse effects. [19]
- **Vitiligo — Sarac et al., 2019, *Dermatologic Therapy* 32(4):e12949; doi:10.1111/dth.12949:** N. sativa oil improved VASI / re-pigmentation in a small comparative study. [20] *(oil; small; opposite pigment direction vs the in-vitro anti-melanogenic data — reinforces "do not make a tone claim.")*
- **Systematic review & meta-analysis of N. sativa in skin disease — Nasiri et al., 2022, *Evid. Based Complement. Alternat. Med.* 2022:7993579; PMC9744621; doi:10.1155/2022/7993579:** 14 RCTs across atopic dermatitis, acne, psoriasis, vitiligo, eczema, keratosis, leishmaniasis, warts; pooled **OR 4.59 (95% CI 2.02–10.39)** favoring N. sativa "lotion." [21] *Honest caveats: heterogeneous indications and formulations, mostly small single-center trials, several from a narrow research community, high risk of bias, and **none are anti-aging endpoints**.*

**Bottom line by tier:** mechanistic anti-aging story is in-vitro/animal; the *human topical* evidence is real but is (a) mostly the **whole oil**, (b) for **disease indications** (acne, eczema, vitiligo) not cosmetic aging, and (c) small/biased. There is **no human topical RCT demonstrating wrinkle, firmness, elasticity, or skin-AGE improvement** for TQ.

## 5. Topical bioavailability

- **Poorly water-soluble + chemically fragile.** TQ is lipophilic; raw aqueous solubility is limited and, critically, **stability-limited**: TQ degrades rapidly in aqueous solution, is **very light-sensitive** (short light exposure → severe degradation, independent of pH/solvent) and **unstable at alkaline pH** (Salmani et al., 2014, *Molecules* 19(5):5925; doi:10.3390/molecules19055925). [11] Dark/anhydrous or ethanolic systems preserve it best.
- **Skin penetration is workable with help.** A permeation/skin-deposition study (Alam et al., 2018, *Drug Delivery* 25(1); doi:10.1080/10717544.2018.1523256) found **Azone, oleic acid, and Transcutol P at ~5% v/v** give measurable transdermal flux. [22] Lipid nanoparticles / nanoemulgels report dermal flux on the order of ~5–6 µg/cm²/h and good skin retention. [23]
- **Implication:** delivered as black-seed *oil*, TQ already sits in a lipophilic, somewhat occlusive vehicle that aids skin partitioning. For a serum, expect to need an anhydrous or oil/ester phase, an antioxidant-protected system, opaque/airless packaging, and possibly a penetration enhancer or nanocarrier to get reproducible delivery.

## 6. Formulation notes

| Parameter | Guidance |
|---|---|
| **Typical use level** | As **N. sativa seed oil**: commonly 1–10% in leave-ons (acne lotion data at 10% oil; hydrogels standardized to TQ). As **isolated/standardized TQ**: target marker levels are sub-percent of TQ; published cell/animal actives are µM–ng/mL — there is **no established topical "effective %" for aging**. |
| **pH** | Keep **mildly acidic to neutral (≈4.5–6.5)**. **Avoid alkaline** systems — TQ degrades fastest at alkaline pH. [11] |
| **Stability** | The hard part. **Light-sensitive and oxidation-prone.** Use opaque/airless packaging, minimize water phase, add co-antioxidants (e.g., tocopherol), avoid high heat, consider encapsulation/nanocarrier. Anhydrous oil or ester serums and balms are the most stability-friendly formats. |
| **Pairings (rational)** | Other lipophilic antioxidants (**tocopherol, bakuchiol, squalane, niacinamide** in low-water systems); barrier lipids (**ceramides** — fits the brand's barrier-repair thesis). Anti-inflammatory + barrier framing is the safest, most defensible pairing. |
| **Conflicts / cautions** | Avoid high-pH actives and strong oxidizers in the same anhydrous phase; do not co-formulate where the system must run alkaline. Black-seed oil's color/odor (warm, pungent, slightly bitter, yellow-brown) can challenge sensory and can darken/oxidize a formula. |
| **Sensory** | The oil has a **distinctive savory/peppery aroma and amber color** that fragrance and color systems must manage. Standardized/refined extracts reduce this. |

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation / sensitization:** Generally well-tolerated topically in the small trials. **But documented allergic contact dermatitis (ACD) risk** with black-seed oil: classic case report **Zedlitz et al., 2002, *Contact Dermatitis* 46(3):188; PMID 12000337** (ACD from N. sativa-oil ointment, positive patch test). [24] Severe and even systemic reactions are reported: a **DRESS syndrome** case after topical N. sativa oil (2022, PMC9545020) [25] and a severe bullous/systemic contact-dermatitis case (JCAD, PMC11694734) [26]. **tert-Butylhydroquinone (TBHQ)** has been flagged as a possible sensitization marker. Rare overall, but real — **patch testing and a conservative sensitizer-aware claim posture are warranted.**
- **Phototoxicity / sun:** No specific phototoxicity signal in the literature found; TQ is studied as a *photo-protective* antioxidant in vitro. It is **not** a sunscreen and must not be positioned as sun protection.
- **Pregnancy/lactation:** **Caution.** *N. sativa* taken **orally** in large/medicinal amounts has traditional and some experimental associations with uterine effects; oral high-dose use in pregnancy is generally discouraged. Topical cosmetic exposure is far lower, but absent dedicated topical pregnancy data, the prudent stance is **"consult a clinician; avoid medicinal oral doses."** Do not make pregnancy-safety claims.
- **Sourcing/quality:** TQ content of black-seed oil **varies widely** by cultivar, origin, and (especially) processing — cold-pressed vs solvent vs CO2, and heat/light exposure. Adulteration and oxidation are real market issues. Standardized, TQ-assayed, oxidation-controlled raw material is essential for any quantitative marker claim.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (cosmetic vs drug — per CLAUDE.md)

- **Status:** In the US/EU, **Nigella sativa seed oil/extract is a cosmetic ingredient** (no positive-list restriction known to this review; *not* a US OTC-monograph drug active). It is **not** an approved drug.
- **The trap:** the strongest TQ data are **disease indications** — acne, eczema, vitiligo, wound healing, "antibacterial," "anti-inflammatory." Phrasing the brand around those crosses into **drug claims** (treat/cure/prevent disease; affect structure/function). Per CLAUDE.md and the glossary, those require substantiation/legal review and likely make it a drug.
- **Safe cosmetic framing (✅):** "antioxidant-rich black-seed oil," "helps defend against environmental/oxidative stress (in lab studies)," "soothing/comforting," "supports a healthy-looking barrier," "nourishes." Use **"helps protect the look of," "visibly," "supports."**
- **Avoid (⚠️ drug-like / unsubstantiated):** "treats acne," "clears breakouts," "repigments," "anti-inflammatory," "kills bacteria," "boosts collagen," "reverses glycation/AGEs," "anti-wrinkle." The anti-glycation and collagen data are **in-vitro** — any glycation/collagen claim would be unsubstantiated for topical human use and should be flagged for legal.

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Form to buy:** standardized, TQ-assayed black-seed oil or extract (e.g., branded CO2 oils standardized to ~3–5% TQ) for marker consistency; isolated TQ for nanocarrier R&D.
- **Cost tier:** **Low–moderate** as commodity cold-pressed black-seed oil; **moderate–higher** for standardized/branded TQ-rich oils and **higher** for isolated/encapsulated TQ. Aligns well with the brand's "buy commodity bases, invest in the brand" logic — but the **stability engineering** (packaging, antioxidants, possibly encapsulation) is where real cost/complexity sits.
- **Supply:** widely available (large traditional-medicine and nutraceutical market); main risks are **quality/standardization and oxidation**, not scarcity.

## 10. Hype vs evidence

- **Hype:** marketed as a near-panacea ("miracle of black seed," "cure for everything but death"), with skincare copy implying proven anti-aging, collagen-boosting, brightening, and "anti-glycation" benefits.
- **Evidence reality:** (1) Genuinely multi-target antioxidant/anti-inflammatory mechanism with consistent in-vitro/animal support. (2) **One** decent topical, TQ-*standardized* human RCT — in **acne**, not aging. (3) Several small topical human trials of the **oil** in disease states. (4) **Zero** human topical anti-aging (wrinkle/elasticity/skin-AGE) endpoints. (5) Self-contradictory pigment story. (6) Chemically fragile. The "anti-glycation anti-aging" headline is **mechanistically plausible but clinically unproven for skin**.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**⚠️ CONDITIONAL — supporting actor, not a hero.**

- **Why not a hero:** the brand's credibility rests on real science (CLAUDE.md). There is **no human topical anti-aging proof** for TQ; the marquee anti-glycation/collagen data are in-vitro, the pigment story contradicts itself, and the molecule is hard to keep stable. Building a hero claim on it would force either drug-adjacent (acne) language or unsubstantiated aging claims — both off-limits.
- **Why not skip:** it's a legitimately interesting, **lipophilic, barrier-friendly antioxidant** with a plausible inflammaging + oxidative-stress + anti-glycation triple mechanism that **complements the barrier-repair thesis** (pairs naturally with ceramides/tocopherol in an anhydrous or low-water system). It also offers a differentiated, story-rich botanical (black seed / *habbat al-barakah*) if the brand later wants a hero botanical — though name/story are OPEN, so don't lean on metaphor.
- **Use it as:** a **supporting antioxidant/soothing actor** in a barrier or facial-oil format, framed only with safe cosmetic antioxidant/soothing language, standardized and oxidation-protected, patch-test-aware.
- **Conditions to clear before use:** (1) standardized, TQ-assayed, low-oxidation raw material; (2) anhydrous/opaque/airless, mildly acidic, antioxidant-protected formulation; (3) sensitizer-aware safety posture (patch testing); (4) claims limited to cosmetic antioxidant/soothing; (5) ideally a small in-house consumer/use test before any "visible" claim.
- **Longevity note:** The SIRT1/FOXO3a and Nrf2 mechanistic hooks make it an attractive "skin-longevity" *story* ingredient, but those data are non-skin / in-vitro. Treat the longevity angle as a **mechanistic narrative to be honest about**, not a substantiated outcome.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

*Independent fact-check, 2026-06-19. Each headline claim re-examined for: (a) HUMAN + TOPICAL vs borrowed in-vitro/animal/oral evidence; (b) whether cited studies are real and say what's claimed; (c) industry-funded / tiny / biased.*

**Overall grade: D for the anti-aging headline (unproven topically); C for the broader "supporting antioxidant/soothing actor" use.**
The molecule is real and multi-target, but the *anti-aging-specific* evidence — collagen, glycation, photoaging, pigment — is entirely in-vitro or animal. The only credible human-topical signal is a single small acne RCT in the wrong indication. The dossier's own Verdict already reaches the right conclusion; this grade simply makes the tier explicit. **Funding / independence flags:** the Thomas oral-safety trial is on a **branded ingredient (BlaQmax/BCO-5)** and is effectively industry-supplier evidence; the Thymocid extract study is on a **proprietary branded extract**. Both are correctly labeled as branded in the dossier. Several human-topical trials originate from a **narrow Iranian phytotherapy research community** (Soleymani, Nasiri pool), which the dossier already flags.

**Main caveats (the load-bearing ones):**
1. **No human topical RCT exists for any anti-aging endpoint** (wrinkle, firmness, elasticity, skin-AGE). The dossier states this; it is correct and is the single most important fact.
2. **The marquee anti-glycation / collagen numbers are in-vitro / cell-free / whole-extract / mouse-melanoma** — they do not transfer to topical human skin.
3. **The strongest human-topical data are disease indications** (acne, eczema, vitiligo) → drug-claim territory, not cosmetic anti-aging.
4. **Pigment direction is genuinely contradictory** (anti-melanogenic in vitro vs pro-pigment / vitiligo clinically) → no tone/brightening claim is defensible.
5. The eye-catching **~78% acne figure is real but bias-prone**; do not let it anchor expectations.

**Claims softened in this pass:** (a) one-line summary now qualifies the acne RCT as "small, single-center, bias-prone"; (b) Tier D entry adds an explicit caution that the ~78% magnitude is atypically large and the trial type runs optimistic. No other claim required softening — the dossier was already conservative and did not overstate.

**Net:** the dossier is accurate, well-cited, and appropriately cautious. The CONDITIONAL "supporting actor" verdict stands. Anti-glycation/anti-aging is **mechanistically plausible but clinically unproven for topical skin** — keep it as honest narrative, never as a substantiated claim.
