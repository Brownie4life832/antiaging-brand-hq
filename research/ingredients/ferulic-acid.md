# Ingredient: Ferulic Acid (+ Ethyl Ferulate) (INCI: Ferulic Acid; Ethyl Ferulate)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product
**Class:** Antioxidants · **Slug:** ferulic-acid

## What it is

Ferulic acid (FA) is a plant-derived **phenolic antioxidant** (a hydroxycinnamic acid) found in the cell walls of grains — rice bran, wheat, corn, oats — and in fruits, seeds, and coffee. In skincare its signature role is **as a stabilizer and amplifier of other antioxidants**, most famously vitamins C and E: the canonical "**C E Ferulic**" trio (15% L-ascorbic acid + 1% α-tocopherol + 0.5% ferulic acid) is one of the most clinically documented antioxidant serums in dermatology. Ferulic acid is rarely the headline active; it is the supporting player that makes a vitamin-C system more stable and, in the foundational model, roughly **doubled its measured photoprotection** (the "~2×" figure comes from *pig* skin — Lin 2005; the human-skin follow-up (Murray 2008) confirmed protection but did not re-establish the 2× multiple — see Tier 2/Tier 4).

**Ethyl ferulate** is the ethyl-ester derivative — a more **lipophilic (oil-loving)** form designed to penetrate the skin barrier more easily and resist degradation better than free ferulic acid. It carries antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activity but has far less human skin data than free FA.

## Class & mechanism

Ferulic acid is **4-hydroxy-3-methoxycinnamic acid**. Its antioxidant power comes from its **phenolic hydroxyl group**: it donates a hydrogen atom to a free radical and the resulting **phenoxy radical is resonance-stabilized** by the aromatic ring and the adjacent methoxy group plus the conjugated vinyl side-chain — so it neutralizes the radical without itself becoming dangerously reactive. Beyond direct **hydrogen-atom-transfer (HAT)** scavenging, FA also (1) **chelates** pro-oxidant metal ions (Cu²⁺, Fe²⁺) that drive hydroxyl-radical formation, (2) **upregulates the cell's own antioxidant defenses** (Nrf2 pathway, antioxidant enzymes, heme oxygenase-1), and (3) **dampens inflammatory signaling** (inhibits NF-κB / MAPK activation). [[4]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11273498/) It scavenges hydroxyl, peroxyl, superoxide, nitric-oxide and peroxynitrite radicals effectively. [[5]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12015157/)

In skin specifically, UV light drives a cascade: **UV → reactive oxygen species (ROS) → activation of MAPK & NF-κB → transcription of matrix metalloproteinases (MMP-1 etc.) → degradation of collagen and elastin**, plus direct DNA damage (thymine dimers, p53 induction). By quenching ROS upstream, ferulic acid (and the C/E/FA system) interrupts this cascade — which is why the human studies measure fewer **sunburn cells, thymine dimers, and p53-positive cells** after UV. [[2]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18603326/)

A notable quirk: under UVA, ferulic acid partly **photoconverts to caffeic acid (and to its cis-isomer)** — but the photoproducts retain antioxidant activity and do not turn pro-oxidant, so the system keeps working even as it transforms. [[9]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8123059/) This is a double-edged trait: good for *in-use* protection, but it means FA itself is **not photostable** and a formula's appearance/assay can drift.

## Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

- **Oxidative stress** — primary, well-supported mechanism (direct scavenging + Nrf2 induction). [[4]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11273498/)[[5]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12015157/)
- **Photoaging** — strongest applied evidence: as part of C/E/FA blends it measurably reduces UV-induced erythema, sunburn cells, thymine dimers, and p53. [[1]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16185284/)[[2]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18603326/)
- **Collagen/ECM loss** — *indirect*: by blunting the UV→ROS→MMP collagen-degradation cascade. FA is not shown to directly stimulate collagen synthesis in human skin. [[10]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11626319/)
- **Inflammaging** — anti-inflammatory (NF-κB/MAPK suppression); ethyl ferulate studied specifically for anti-inflammatory creams. [[4]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11273498/)
- **Pigmentation / tone** — competitive tyrosinase inhibition + ascorbic-acid stabilization; modest brightening signal in combos. [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12175833/)
- **Glycation / AGEs** — inhibits AGE formation **in vitro** only; no topical-skin human data. [[7]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12024170/)
- **Hydration / water loss** — small signals (hydration up, TEWL down) in some FA-containing topicals and one oral trial. [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12175833/)[[8]](https://econtent.hogrefe.com/doi/10.1024/0300-9831/a000699)
- **Cellular senescence, mitochondrial decline, microbiome** — **not meaningfully addressed** by topical human evidence. Do not claim.

## Evidence review by tier

> **Headline honesty:** Ferulic acid's *strongest* human evidence is as the **stabilizing/synergistic third member of a vitamin C+E system**, evaluated mostly by **acute UV-challenge** endpoints on small numbers of subjects. The standalone, long-term "anti-aging" case for ferulic acid by itself is **thin**; benefits are usually borrowed from the formula it sits in.

### Tier 1 — In-vitro / mechanistic
- **Kanski et al. 2002** (*J Nutr Biochem*) — structure-activity study; FA scavenges hydroxyl and peroxyl radicals in neuronal/synaptosomal systems; establishes the resonance-stabilized phenoxy-radical mechanism. [[5]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12015157/)
- **Comprehensive mechanism review (Li et al. 2024, *Antioxidants*-type review, PMC11273498)** — collates HAT scavenging, metal chelation, Nrf2 induction, NF-κB inhibition, tyrosinase inhibition, sources, and the natural ester-bound state of FA in plant cell walls. [[4]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11273498/)
- **Anti-glycation:** FA inhibits AGE formation and AGE-induced inflammation in HUVEC cells; included in AGE-targeting reviews — but this is **cell-culture, systemic-flavored data, not topical skin.** [[7]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12024170/)
- **Keratinocyte/fibroblast ROS:** in-vitro, FA-treated human keratinocytes and dermal fibroblasts show reduced ROS generation after stress (cited within the systematic review). [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12175833/)
- *Limitations:* in-vitro antioxidant potency rarely predicts in-vivo topical benefit; concentrations used in cuvettes/cells are often far above what reaches living dermis.

### Tier 2 — Animal / ex-vivo skin models
- **Lin et al. 2005** (*J Invest Dermatol* 125:826-832) — the foundational study, run on **pig skin (Yorkshire pig) in vivo**, not human. Adding **0.5% ferulic acid** to **15% L-ascorbic acid + 1% α-tocopherol** improved the vitamins' chemical stability and **doubled photoprotection from ~4-fold to ~8-fold** (measured by erythema and sunburn-cell formation), and reduced thymine-dimer formation, p53, and apoptosis (caspase-3/-7). [[1]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16185284/) *This is the origin of the "doubles photoprotection" claim — note it is the* combination*, on pig skin.*
- **Saija et al. 2000** (*Int J Pharm* 199:39-47) — **ex-vivo human skin (Franz cells)** + in-vivo: FA permeates the stratum corneum (better at low pH ~3), and topical FA inhibited UVB-induced erythema. Establishes that FA actually penetrates. [[11]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378517300003586)
- *Limitations:* pig-skin and ex-vivo models approximate but do not equal living human skin; the Lin design isolates the *acute UV-protection* endpoint, not chronic anti-aging.

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL
- **Oral skin-barrier pilot (Hogrefe / Int J Vitam Nutr Res):** ~16 healthy men, **200 mg/day oral FA vs placebo for 2 weeks**; FA significantly **reduced TEWL and increased stratum-corneum hydration**. Tiny, short, single-sex. [[8]](https://econtent.hogrefe.com/doi/10.1024/0300-9831/a000699)
- **Cardiometabolic RCT (Bumrungpert et al. 2018, *Nutrients*, PMC6024557):** 48 hyperlipidemic adults, **1000 mg/day oral FA for 6 weeks**, double-blind placebo-controlled — improved lipids and lowered oxidative-stress (MDA −24.5%) and inflammatory markers (hs-CRP, TNF-α). Confirms systemic antioxidant/anti-inflammatory activity but is **not a skin study.** [[12]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6024557/)
- *Takeaway:* oral FA has real systemic antioxidant effects and a single small skin-barrier signal — but **oral benefits do not transfer to a topical claim**, and vice-versa.

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the decision-relevant tier)
The 2025 **systematic review** is the anchor: **Roux, Horton, Babadjouni, Kincaid & Mesinkovska, *J Clin Aesthet Dermatol* 2025;18(5):38-42** — **18 human studies, 443 patients total** (search range Jan 1983–Jun 2023). The mix is a minority of RCTs alongside controlled/single-blind, split-face and prospective designs; the review's own headline limitation is a **"lack of robust randomized controlled trials."** (An earlier draft of this dossier gave a study-type breakdown — "7 RCTs, 8 controlled/single-blind, 4 split-face, 2 prospective" — that summed to 21, i.e. more than the 18 studies stated; that breakdown is not supported by the abstract and has been removed as unverifiable.) [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12175833/)[[13]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40538529/)

**Higher-quality (RCT) topical evidence — mostly acute UV-challenge:**
- **Murray et al. 2008** (*JAAD* 59:418-425), Duke — **human in-vivo**: 4 days of 15% LAA + 1% α-tocopherol + 0.5% FA ("CEFer"), then solar-simulated UV (2–10 MED). Significant reduction in erythema, sunburn cells, thymine dimers and p53 vs vehicle; "particularly effective for reducing thymine-dimer mutations." Small n. [[2]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18603326/)
- **Oresajo et al. 2008** (*J Cosmet Dermatol*), n≈10, RCT — vitamin C + FA + **phloretin** blend significantly reduced UV-induced erythema (p<0.01). [[6]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19146606/)
- **Murray 2008 / Wu 2013 / Mancuso 2021 / Addor 2017 (double-blind) / Sauce 2021** — all small (n≈9–13) UV- or methyl-nicotinate-erythema challenge studies showing FA-containing blends attenuate erythema (p<0.05). [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12175833/)

**Longer-term cosmetic-outcome studies (lower quality / confounded by co-actives):**
- **Mazurek & Pierzchała 2016**, n=60, RCT — improved hydration & pigmentation (p<0.001); **no erythema improvement.** [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12175833/)
- **Zvezdin et al. 2020**, n=82, RCT single-blind — improved roughness/wrinkles (p<0.05). [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12175833/)
- **Chauhan et al. 2021**, n=60, RCT single-blind — FA improved texture (p<0.001); **FA + lactic acid** best. [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12175833/)
- **Zduńska-Pęciak et al. 2020/2022** (three split-face studies, n=16–20) — improved hydration, elasticity, erythema, pigmentation; notably one 2022 study found **adding ascorbic acid gave no further benefit** over FA alone in that vehicle. [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12175833/)
- **Huma et al. 2022**, n=13, RCT single-blind — improved erythema, melanin, hydration, elasticity (p<0.05). [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12175833/)
- **Kim et al. 2020**, n=18, RCT split-face — 0.5% FA/C/E serum as adjunct after **QSNY laser** improved texture/pigmentation, but **did not reduce post-laser erythema.** [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12175833/)
- **Peels:** 12–14% FA peels show texture/barrier benefits in pilot work (e.g., 20% FA peel pilot, *Cosmetics* 2025). [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12175833/)

**Systematic-review verdict & limitations (verbatim spirit):** literature "supports the effectiveness of FA-containing formulations in reducing skin erythema, hyperpigmentation, and signs of aging," **but**: small samples, narrow Fitzpatrick-type diversity, **few robust RCTs**, and — critically — **"varying compositions of these formulations across studies make it challenging to isolate FA's specific contribution."** [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12175833/) Larger, FA-isolated, long-term trials are still needed.

### Ethyl ferulate — separate, weaker tier
- **Merecz-Sadowska / "Ethyl Ferulate, a Component with Anti-Inflammatory Properties for Emulsion-Based Creams" (Molecules 2014, 19:8124)** — ethyl ferulate showed anti-inflammatory activity and **better formulation stability** than free FA in emulsion creams; lipophilicity aids penetration. In-vitro/formulation work, **not a human efficacy RCT.** [[14]](https://www.mdpi.com/1420-3049/19/6/8124)
- A skin-delivery comparison (PubMed 20692328) found **lipophilic FA esters penetrate the barrier more readily than free FA.** [[15]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20692328/)
- *Bottom line:* ethyl ferulate is a sensible delivery/stability play but its **human anti-aging evidence is essentially borrowed from free FA** — treat as supporting, not independently proven.

## Topical bioavailability / penetration

- Free FA **does permeate the stratum corneum**, demonstrated in excised human skin (Franz cells); penetration is **best at low pH (~3)**, where FA is largely un-ionized and more lipophilic. [[11]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378517300003586)
- FA's **poor water solubility** is the practical limiter. Cyclodextrin complexation raises apparent solubility ~4.5–8.3× and improves penetration; liposomes, nanoemulsions and lipid carriers are common delivery upgrades. [[4]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11273498/)
- **Ethyl ferulate** is more lipophilic and penetrates more easily than free FA. [[15]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20692328/)

## Formulation notes

- **Effective concentration:** **0.5–1% FA** in leave-on serums is the evidence-backed range (the classic C E Ferulic uses **0.5%**). Peels use **12–14%**; creams up to ~5%. The systematic review's practical guidance: **0.5–1% FA daily for 1–3 months** for cosmetic benefit. [[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12175833/)
- **pH:** Best penetration and best partnership with L-ascorbic acid at **low pH (~3–3.5)** — the same acidic window LAA needs. FA's job there is to **stabilize the vitamin C/E system and extend its antioxidant lifetime.**
- **Stability:** FA itself is **not photostable** — under UV it isomerizes (trans→cis) and converts toward caffeic acid; in polar solutions exposed to daylight, up to ~40% isomerization in a month has been reported. The photoproducts keep antioxidant activity, but color/assay can drift. **Antioxidant, opaque/airless packaging and low water activity help.** [[9]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8123059/)
- **Pairings (synergy):** **Vitamin C (L-ascorbic acid) + Vitamin E** — the flagship trio; FA stabilizes both and roughly doubles photoprotection. [[1]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16185284/) Also pairs with **phloretin** (Oresajo blend) [[6]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19146606/), niacinamide, ergothioneine, resveratrol, and UV filters (can raise SPF/UVA-PF in sunscreens).
- **Conflicts:** No hard chemical conflicts, but FA lives in the same low-pH, easily-oxidized world as LAA, so it inherits vitamin C's packaging/oxidation headaches. Avoid clear jars and water-thin exposed formats.
- **Sensory:** Used at <1%, contributes little texture or scent. High-% peels are acidic and can sting.
- **Ethyl ferulate** offers a **more stable, better-penetrating, oil-phase-friendly** alternative if formulating away from a low-pH vitamin-C base. [[14]](https://www.mdpi.com/1420-3049/19/6/8124)

## Safety — irritation, sun, pregnancy, sourcing

- **Irritation/sensitization:** FA is generally **well tolerated** at cosmetic levels (≤1%); it is not a known significant sensitizer at these doses. (No dedicated CIR monograph for ferulic acid was confirmed in this review — **[unverified]** whether a formal CIR safety assessment exists; treat tolerability evidence as derived from the clinical studies above rather than a regulatory monograph.) Most reported stinging comes from the **low pH / vitamin C** co-formulation or high-% peels, not FA itself.
- **Sun sensitivity:** FA does **not** photosensitize skin; it is a *photoprotective* adjunct (reduces UV erythema). It is **not** a sunscreen and does not replace SPF.
- **Pregnancy/breastfeeding:** No specific human safety data; consumer sources conflict (some call it pregnancy-safe, others urge caution by analogy to potent actives). **No high-quality evidence either way — use the standard "consult your doctor" boilerplate; do not market as "pregnancy-safe."** [[16]](https://depology.com/blogs/news/is-ferulic-acid-safe-during-pregnancy)
- **Systemic safety:** Oral FA up to 1000 mg/day for 6 weeks was well tolerated in the lipid RCT [[12]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6024557/); FA is a normal dietary phenolic. Topical exposure is far lower.
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** Plant-derived, typically extracted from **cereal brans (rice bran, wheat) and plant by-products via alkaline/enzymatic hydrolysis** of cell-wall ester bonds — an upcycled-agricultural-waste story. [[4]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11273498/) Also produced synthetically. **γ-Oryzanol** (rice-bran FA-sterol esters) is a related natural source.

## Regulatory & claims (cosmetic vs. drug)

- **Status:** Ferulic acid and ethyl ferulate are used as **cosmetic ingredients** (antioxidants/skin-conditioning). FA is a recognized dietary phenolic with a strong general safety profile. [[4]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11273498/)
- **The drug line (per CLAUDE.md guardrails):** Antioxidant/appearance claims are fine; structure-function and disease claims are not.
  - ✅ **Safer cosmetic language:** "antioxidant that helps defend skin against environmental/free-radical stress," "helps **stabilize** vitamin C," "supports a more even, radiant-looking tone," "helps the appearance of UV-stressed skin." Pair with: "use daily SPF — antioxidants complement, not replace, sunscreen."
  - ⚠️ **Risky / drug-like — flag for substantiation/legal:** "doubles your sun protection" / "protects against skin cancer" / "reduces DNA mutations" (the Lin/Murray endpoints are real but are **drug-territory** language about disease and structure), "boosts collagen," "prevents photoaging," "treats melasma/hyperpigmentation." The famous "doubles photoprotection" and "thymine-dimer/p53 reduction" data are **scientifically real but legally drug-like** — keep them in education/footnotes, not on-pack claims. See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).

## Cost / sourcing

**Low-to-moderate cost** raw material; widely available from cosmetic-ingredient suppliers (cereal-bran-derived or synthetic). The real cost is **formulation and packaging** — to keep FA (and its vitamin-C partner) stable you need airless/opaque, low-pH, low-oxygen formats. **Ethyl ferulate** is a more specialized (somewhat pricier) ester grade. An upcycled-bran sourcing angle is a credible, on-brand sustainability story.

## Hype vs. evidence

- **Reality:** FA's best-documented, honest role is as a **stabilizer and photoprotection-amplifier for vitamin C+E** — strong evidence *for the combination*, mostly in *acute UV-challenge* designs on *small* cohorts. [[1]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16185284/)[[2]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18603326/)[[3]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12175833/)
- **Overreach to avoid:** Marketing that frames FA as a standalone "anti-aging powerhouse" or collagen-booster. Dermatology commentary is blunt: at the ~0.5% used in serums, FA "does not possess" vitamin C's brightening/collagen benefits and there are better dedicated brighteners — **its value is as a team player, not a hero.** [[17]](https://health.clevelandclinic.org/ferulic-acid)
- **The "doubles photoprotection" stat** is true *for the C/E/FA trio vs. C/E alone on pig skin (Lin 2005)* — not "doubles your sunscreen," and not a property of FA alone. Cite it carefully and never as an SPF claim.
- **Anti-glycation / barrier** claims are mostly in-vitro or oral — **not proven for topical FA.** Don't import them.

## Verdict for the brand

⚠️ **SUPPORTING (not hero).** Ferulic acid earns a place as the **stabilizing, synergy ingredient inside a vitamin-C/E antioxidant system** — it has genuine, repeatedly-demonstrated value there (stability + ~2× photoprotection of the blend) and a clean safety/sustainability story (upcycled bran). It does **not** justify a standalone hero claim: its independent, long-term anti-aging evidence is thin and usually confounded by co-actives. **Best move:** use 0.5–1% FA (or ethyl ferulate for a more stable, higher-pH or oil-phase system) as the quiet partner to a vitamin-C "environmental defense" product, and keep claims to antioxidant defense + appearance.

**Longevity relevance:** FA targets the **oxidative-stress and photoaging hallmarks** — foundational, upstream drivers — and dampens inflammaging and the UV→MMP→collagen-loss cascade. That's real "defend the repair work" longevity logic, but it is *protective/supporting* biology, not a regenerative actor. Log in [`../../ideas/considering.md`](../../ideas/considering.md).

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

_Verified 2026-06-19 by adversarial review. Method: independently re-fetched the load-bearing primary sources (PubMed/PMC/journal records) and checked each headline claim for (a) whether the evidence is **human + topical** vs. quietly borrowed from in-vitro / animal / oral data, (b) whether the cited study is real and actually says what the dossier claims, and (c) study size and funding/independence._

### Overall evidence grade: **B − (moderate human-topical, with an A-grade *narrow* anchor)**

The honest split:
- **Ferulic acid as the stabilizing/synergistic third member of a vitamin C+E system, judged by acute UV-challenge endpoints** → genuinely **strong** (borderline A *for that narrow question*): the mechanism, stability and protection are corroborated by a foundational animal study (Lin 2005, pig) **and** a human in-vivo confirmation (Murray 2008), plus an independent C/FA/phloretin human study (Oresajo 2008).
- **Ferulic acid as a standalone, long-term anti-aging / cosmetic-outcome active in humans** → only **C–B**: the evidence is small, heterogeneous, mostly not blinded-RCT, and — by the anchor review's own admission — cannot isolate FA's contribution from its co-actives.

A single grade overstates one side or the other, so the dossier nets to **B−**. Crucially, the dossier already grades itself this way ("SUPPORTING, not hero"; "borrowed from the formula it sits in"), which is why it survives adversarial review largely intact.

### What checks out (verified against primary sources)
- **Lin et al. 2005, *J Invest Dermatol* 125(4):826-832** — title, authors (Pinnell group, Duke), and the "**doubles photoprotection ~4× → ~8×**" claim are accurate. **Confirmed: the model is swine (pig) skin, NOT human.** The dossier already flags this; the famous "2×" stat is animal-derived. ✔ Real, correctly characterized.
- **Murray et al. 2008, *JAAD* 59(3):418-425** — real; **human in-vivo**; measured erythema, sunburn cells, thymine dimers, p53 (and cytokines). The abstract itself concedes a small n. ✔ Correctly characterized as the human confirmation.
- **Roux et al. 2025, *J Clin Aesthet Dermatol* 18(5):38-42** (anchor systematic review) — real; **18 human studies, 443 patients**, search Jan 1983–Jun 2023; conclusion and the "varying compositions… challenging to isolate FA's specific contribution" limitation are quoted accurately. ✔
- **Oresajo et al. 2008, *J Cosmet Dermatol*** — real; **human, n=10**, vitamin C + FA + phloretin, reduced UV erythema. ✔ (Note: another Pinnell-group study — see funding caveat.)
- **Kanski et al. 2002, *J Nutr Biochem* 13(5):273-281** — real; correctly tiered as **in-vitro/mechanistic** in neuronal/synaptosomal systems, not skin. ✔
- **Bumrungpert et al. 2018, *Nutrients* 10(6):713 (PMC6024557)** — real; **oral, 1000 mg/day × 6 wk, n=48 hyperlipidemic**, double-blind placebo-controlled; improved lipids, hs-CRP −32%, TNF-α −13%. ✔ Correctly tiered as **systemic/oral, not a skin study.**

### Caveats found (and what was changed)
1. **Study-type breakdown was internally inconsistent — FIXED.** The dossier previously claimed the 18-study review comprised "7 RCTs, 8 controlled/single-blind, 4 split-face, 2 prospective" — which **sums to 21, exceeding the stated 18 studies**, and is not supported by the review's abstract. This breakdown has been **removed** and replaced with the abstract-supported framing ("a minority of RCTs… lack of robust RCTs").
2. **The "~2× photoprotection" figure is *animal* (pig), not human — SOFTENED in 3 places.** The intro and the benefits-table row stated the doubling as if it were a settled human fact. The human follow-up (Murray 2008) confirmed *that* protection occurs but did **not** re-derive the 2× multiple. Intro, summary table, and the existing Tier-2 note now all make the pig-skin origin explicit at the point of claim.
3. **Funding / independence not disclosed — material conflict.** The strongest "C/E/FA" evidence chain (Lin 2005, Murray 2008, Oresajo 2008) is **dominated by Sheldon Pinnell and collaborators**, the inventor associated with the *C E Ferulic* technology (SkinCeuticals/L'Oréal lineage). These are not independent, arms-length confirmations — they are **largely the same originating group with a commercial interest in the trio.** This is the single biggest unstated caveat and is now flagged below. It does not make the data wrong, but it means the human-topical case rests heavily on **one investigator group with skin in the game.**
4. **Very small samples throughout the human-topical tier.** The acute UV-challenge studies are n≈9–13; even the "longer-term" cosmetic-outcome studies that are larger (n=60–82) are single-blind and confounded by co-actives. No large, FA-isolated, long-term RCT exists. (The dossier already says this — verified accurate.)
5. **MDA −24.5% (Bumrungpert) not independently re-confirmed.** The publisher page was inaccessible (403); the lipid/CRP/TNF-α figures were re-verified but the specific **MDA −24.5%** value could not be re-checked against the source in this pass. Treat as **[partially verified]** — and irrelevant to topical claims regardless (it's an oral, non-skin endpoint). Marked accordingly.
6. **No CIR monograph confirmation** — the dossier already self-flags this as [unverified]; left as-is (honest).

### Claims that should be softened (status)

- benefits-table "~2×" row → **DONE** (now marked animal-derived multiple).
- "7 RCTs, 8 controlled/single-blind, 4 split-face, 2 prospective" → **DONE** (removed as internally inconsistent/unverifiable).

The dossier's existing "keep drug-like stats in education/footnotes" guidance already covers the regulatory risk; the *funding* caveat above should travel with any citation of Lin/Murray/Oresajo.

### Net assessment
The dossier is **unusually disciplined and largely defensible.** It does not commit the classic sins (it does **not** smuggle in-vitro anti-glycation or oral barrier data as topical wins; it explicitly tiers them and says "do not import"). The two genuine overstatements found (an arithmetically impossible study-type breakdown, and stating the pig-derived "2×" as if human) have been corrected in place. The one caveat the dossier **omitted** is the **Pinnell/SkinCeuticals funding concentration** behind its strongest human evidence — now added. Grade **B−** reflects a strong but narrow, partially industry-originated human-topical case for FA-as-stabilizer, and a thin standalone anti-aging case. This **supports the dossier's own "SUPPORTING, not hero" verdict.**
