# Ingredient: Betaine (Trimethylglycine) (INCI: Betaine)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product
**Class:** Humectants & hydration actives (organic/compatible osmolyte; quaternary-ammonium zwitterion)

> **One-line honest summary:** Betaine is a cheap, exceptionally well-tolerated, genuinely *natural-to-skin* humectant + osmolyte with **solid mechanistic and in-vitro/ex-vivo barrier science** (tight-junction support, osmoprotection, anti-irritancy) and **real human in-vivo anti-irritant data** — but **no published, betaine-isolated human topical RCT for facial hydration or anti-aging**. It is a reliable **supporting** workhorse and a credible "soothing/barrier-friendly hydration" story, not an evidence-backed anti-aging hero.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common name:** Betaine; trimethylglycine (TMG); glycine betaine; oxyneurine; "betaine anhydrous" (pharma).
- **INCI:** **Betaine** (cosmetic-grade is typically *anhydrous* trimethylglycine, plant-derived from sugar beet molasses).
- **CAS:** 107-43-7
- **Molecular formula / weight:** C₅H₁₁NO₂ · **117.15 g/mol** — a *very small* molecule (relevant to penetration; see §5). [1][2]
- **Chemical class:** **Quaternary-ammonium zwitterion** — the N,N,N-trimethyl inner salt of glycine: (CH₃)₃N⁺–CH₂–COO⁻. Carries a permanent positive (trimethylammonium) and negative (carboxylate) charge simultaneously, with **net zero charge**. This zwitterionic, highly polar character is the crux of both its water-binding strength and its poor stratum-corneum diffusion. [1][2]
- **Physical/chemistry notes:** White deliquescent crystalline solid; **freely water-soluble** (also soluble in methanol/ethanol; insoluble in ether); sweet taste; mp ~293 °C. Stable across a wide pH range; **not a meaningful pH shifter** in formulation (unlike, e.g., spermidine free base). [1][2]
- **Biological identity:** A **ubiquitous endogenous "compatible/organic osmolyte"** in animals, plants and microbes — used by cells to defend volume against osmotic stress without disrupting protein function — and a **methyl donor** (betaine-homocysteine methyltransferase converts homocysteine → methionine). It is **natural to human skin**: betaine is present in epidermis (reported higher in epidermis than dermis) and functions as part of the cell's osmolyte pool. [1][3][4]
- **Key forms / not-to-confuse:**
  - **Betaine (trimethylglycine)** — the humectant/osmolyte active this dossier covers.
  - **Cocamidopropyl betaine (CAPB)** and other **alkyl/amido betaines** — *surfactants*, NOT this ingredient; chemically related (zwitterionic) but **a known contact allergen** (the impurities aminoamide/DMAPA drive sensitization). Do **not** conflate CAPB's allergen profile with trimethylglycine. [5][6]
  - **Betaine salicylate** — a salicylic-acid (BHA) derivative; see the separate `salicylic-acid-bha` dossier. Different molecule, different purpose.
  - **Betaine anhydrous (Cystadane®)** — the *oral drug* for homocystinuria (same molecule, medical/methyl-donor use). [7][8]

## 2. Class & mechanism (how it works at the skin/cell level)

Betaine is best understood as a **dual-action small-molecule humectant + compatible osmolyte** with secondary barrier/anti-irritant effects.

1. **Humectancy (water binding).** As a small, highly polar zwitterion it hydrogen-bonds water strongly, drawing and holding moisture in the stratum corneum like glycerol/HA but with its own osmolyte twist. It is positioned by suppliers as part of the "next-generation osmolyte" hydration class. [9][10]
2. **Osmoprotection / cell-volume defense.** This is betaine's distinctive mechanism: as a *compatible* osmolyte, intracellular betaine lets cells counter osmotic stress (dehydration, hypertonicity) and stabilize volume **without** perturbing protein folding or enzyme activity. Demonstrated repeatedly in human corneal epithelial cells under hyperosmotic stress (preserved cell volume, viability, mitochondrial activity, reduced apoptosis). [11][12]
3. **Tight-junction (TJ) / permeability-barrier support.** Betaine (and taurine), taken up via osmolyte transporters, raise TJ protein levels (claudin-1, claudin-4, occludin) in human skin and improve barrier function (↑ TEER, ↓ paracellular permeability) in keratinocyte models. Effect is **transporter-dependent** (abolished by BGT-1/TAUT inhibitors). [13]
4. **Keratinocyte differentiation signaling.** In UVB-irradiated 3D rat-keratinocyte cultures, betaine most strongly upregulated **keratin 2** (a differentiation marker) and modulated proliferation/differentiation and DNA-replication genes — consistent with supporting orderly cornification. [14]
5. **Anti-irritancy.** Betaine measurably blunts surfactant (SLS/CAPB) irritation in human skin and oral mucosa in vivo — likely via osmolyte-mediated cell-volume protection and reduced surfactant-induced damage. [15][16][17]
6. **Anti-glycation/photoaging (indirect, non-topical).** Oral betaine in mice suppressed UVB-induced MMP-9 via MEK/ERK inhibition, reducing wrinkle/collagen damage — a *systemic methyl-donor / antioxidant* route, not a demonstrated topical mechanism. [18]

**Skin-relevant honesty:** mechanisms 1–5 are well-supported *biologically* and most are shown in **human cells / human ex-vivo skin / human in-vivo irritation models** — stronger than many trendy actives. The gap is that none of this has been packaged into a **betaine-isolated human topical facial RCT** measuring hydration/wrinkles over time. Mechanism 6 is animal + oral.

## 4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

> **Most important honesty point:** Betaine's evidence is unusually *credible at the in-vitro and human-ex-vivo/in-vivo-irritation level* — it is **not** a "borrowed from oral supplements" story the way many longevity actives are. But there is a specific, important hole: **no published randomized human topical trial isolating betaine for facial hydration, wrinkles, or elasticity.** The widely-repeated "2–5% betaine gives 20–30% hydration improvement at 28 days" figure traces to vendor/marketing copy, not a retrievable peer-reviewed RCT — treat it as **[unverified]**.

### Tier 1 — In vitro (cell & 3D / ex-vivo human-skin models) — *the strongest tier here*

- **El-Chami C., Foster A.R., Johnson C., et al. (2021), *British Journal of Dermatology* 184(3):482–494 (DOI 10.1111/bjd.19162).** Organ-cultured **human skin** (8 healthy donors, 72 h) + primary human epidermal keratinocytes on permeable supports. Betaine (35 mmol/L in skin; 5 mmol/L in keratinocytes) and taurine **significantly increased claudin-1, claudin-4 and occludin** protein in skin; improved barrier function in keratinocytes (**↑ TEER at 48–72 h, ↓ paracellular permeability to 4-kDa FITC-dextran**); effect **abolished by osmolyte-transporter inhibition** (mechanistically clean). *Limitations: only 8 donors; keratinocyte monolayers are a simplified model vs stratified epidermis; betaine appeared to stabilize existing TJ protein rather than raise mRNA (taurine raised both — full-text detail, not in abstract); not a finished-product or hydration-over-time study.* [13]
- **El-Chami C. et al. (2014), *Experimental Dermatology* — osmolyte uptake/barrier (same group's earlier work).** Establishes keratinocyte osmolyte transporters and that compatible osmolytes (incl. betaine) influence barrier homeostasis — mechanistic backbone for the 2021 paper. *In vitro.* [20] *[citation detail partially unverified]*
- **Rauhala L., Hämäläinen L., Dunlop T.W., et al. (2015), *Toxicology in Vitro* 30(1 Pt B):462–475 (DOI 10.1016/j.tiv.2015.09.015; PMID 26391144).** Genome-wide expression in **organotypic 3D rat epidermal keratinocyte** cultures ± betaine (10 mM) ± UVB (30 mJ/cm²). Among 89 betaine-influenced genes, **keratin 2 was the top upregulated** (confirmed at protein level); modulated DNA-replication and proliferation/differentiation genes; authors note betaine's wide cosmetic use has "occurred despite the lack of comprehensive studies addressing its specific effects in skin." *Limitations: rat keratinocytes, culture doses, no clinical endpoint.* [14]
- **Hua X., Su Z., Deng R., et al. (2013/2014), *Experimental Eye Research* — betaine + human corneal epithelial cells, hyperosmotic stress (DOI 10.1016/j.exer.2012.10.013).** Betaine (5–10 mM) under hyperosmotic stress (500 mOsm): **ameliorated cell-volume loss (27% → 11% shrinkage), ↑ mitochondrial activity ~17%, ↑ viable cells ~12%, reduced apoptosis.** Cross-tissue proof of the osmoprotection mechanism. *Limitation: corneal (not skin) epithelium; in vitro.* [11]
- **Corneal osmoprotectant anti-inflammatory / anti-MMP work (Deng/Chen et al., 2014–2016, *Cornea* / PMC4793974; Hua et al. 2015, PubMed 25352733).** Betaine (with L-carnitine/erythritol) **suppressed hyperosmolarity-induced pro-inflammatory cytokines and MMP production/activity** in primary human corneal epithelial cells. *Cross-tissue support for "soothing/osmoprotective"; not skin.* [12][19]

### Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

- **Im A-R., Lee B., Youn S.-J., Hyun J.-W., Chae S. (2015/2016), "Orally administered betaine reduces photodamage caused by UVB irradiation through the regulation of MMP-9 activity in hairless mice," *Molecular Medicine Reports* 13(1):440–446 (DOI 10.3892/mmr.2015.4613).** **Oral** betaine (100 mg/kg/day) in UVB-irradiated SKH-1 hairless mice **suppressed wrinkle formation** (length, depth, number), reduced epidermal thickening and collagen damage, and **inhibited UVB-induced MEK→ERK→MMP-9**. *Limitations: **oral, not topical**; mouse; mechanistic photoaging model, not a topical-betaine test. This is the single most-cited "anti-aging betaine" paper and it is the wrong route for a topical claim.* [18]

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL (relevant to safety & systemic biology, not topical skin)

- **Cystadane® (betaine anhydrous) FDA label + RoCH registry (2019), *Orphanet J. Rare Dis.* 14:62 (PMC6419445).** Oral betaine **6 g/day** (adults) lowers homocysteine to ~20–30% of baseline in homocystinuria; in a 125-patient registry only **3 of 277 adverse events** were drug-related (bad taste, headache, one interstitial lung disease). Establishes a **very benign systemic safety profile** at gram-level chronic oral doses (the main caution being cerebral edema risk only in hypermethioninemic CBS patients). *Not skin efficacy; strong supportive safety context.* [7][8]
- **Oral betaine in sports/metabolism literature** — generally well tolerated at ~1.25–6 g/day; not skin-relevant beyond safety reassurance. [21] *[supportive, partially unverified]*

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters for a skincare brand)

- **Nicander I., Rantanen I., Rozell B.L., Söderling E., Ollmar S. (2003), "The ability of betaine to reduce the irritating effects of detergents assessed visually, histologically and by bioengineering methods," *Skin Research and Technology* 9(1):50–58 (DOI 10.1034/j.1600-0846.2003.00367.x; PMID 12535285).** **Human in-vivo**, n=21, 24-h forearm patch tests of SLS and cocamidopropyl betaine ± betaine, ± betaine alone, vs controls; outcomes by visual score, **electrical impedance (31 frequencies), TEWL, and histology.** **Adding betaine reduced the irritant reaction for both detergents.** *This is real human topical data — but the endpoint is anti-irritancy, not hydration/anti-aging; small n; acute patch model; betaine concentration not stated in abstract.* [15]
- **Nicander I., Rozell B.L., et al. (2003), companion soap study, *Skin Research and Technology* (PMID 12535284): "The use of different concentrations of betaine as a reducing irritation agent in soaps monitored visually and non-invasively."** Human in-vivo; **dose-dependent reduction of soap-induced irritation** with betaine. *Same caveats; anti-irritancy endpoint.* [16]
- **Rantanen I., Nicander I., et al. — betaine reduces SLS irritation of human oral mucosa in vivo (Söderling group, *Acta Odontol Scand* / related).** Human in-vivo (oral mucosa, the betaine-toothpaste line of research): **betaine reduced SLS-induced irritation.** *Mucosa, not facial skin; supports the anti-irritant mechanism.* [17]
- **Facial hydration / anti-aging RCT isolating betaine:** **NONE found in the peer-reviewed literature.** The recurrent "2–5% topical betaine → 20–30% hydration improvement at 28 days; upregulates filaggrin" claim appears only in **vendor/brand/editorial copy**, with no retrievable primary RCT. **[unverified] — do not cite as proven.** [22][9]

**Bottom line by tier:** Proven for betaine specifically → osmoprotection & TJ/barrier support (**human cells + human ex-vivo skin**), differentiation gene effects (rat 3D), **anti-irritancy (human in-vivo)**, and oral photoprotection (mouse). **NOT proven:** a quantified human **topical** hydration/anti-aging benefit from betaine as an isolated active. The evidence base is *more credible than most* humectant adjuncts but is anti-irritant/barrier-mechanistic, not facial-anti-aging-clinical.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **Size:** Tiny (117 g/mol) — far below the ~500-Da rule-of-thumb ceiling; on size alone, good prospects.
- **Against:** It is a **permanently charged zwitterion** — highly hydrophilic and poorly partitioning into the lipophilic stratum corneum. Like glycerol/other small humectants, much of its action is reasonably understood to be **at/near the surface and upper SC** (water-binding) rather than deep dermal delivery.
- **Transporter angle (important):** The barrier/TJ effects in El-Chami 2021 were **transporter-dependent** — betaine had to be *taken up by viable keratinocytes via BGT-1* to raise TJ proteins. So the *functional* (non-humectant) benefits presuppose betaine reaching **viable epidermis** and being actively imported, which in intact skin with a competent barrier is uncertain and undocumented in vivo for a cosmetic dose.
- **Practical read:** As a **humectant**, betaine works at the surface/SC without needing deep penetration — that benefit is mechanically sound. As a **barrier-remodeling / osmoprotective active**, the in-vivo human penetration-and-uptake step is **plausible but unproven**. No published human transdermal/penetration quantification for cosmetic betaine was found.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:** Commonly used **~0.5–5%** as a humectant/co-humectant (often 1–4%); supplier guidance (e.g., GENENCARE OSMS BA) positions it in this range. There is **no clinically-validated facial dose-response**, so formulate within the customary range and **avoid implying a proven dose effect**. [9][22]
- **pH:** Very forgiving — stable and effective across the typical cosmetic range (**~3–9**); zwitterion does not appreciably shift formula pH. Compatible with low-pH actives (vitamin C, AHA/BHA) and high-pH systems alike. [1][2]
- **Stability:** Excellent — chemically robust, heat-stable (can be added to the water phase, tolerates standard processing), long shelf life; not prone to oxidation/discoloration. A genuinely low-maintenance raw material.
- **Solubility/use:** Water-soluble; add to the aqueous phase. Highly hygroscopic raw powder — store dry/sealed.
- **Pairings (rational):**
  - **Glycerin / HA / panthenol** — layered/complementary humectant systems; betaine adds osmolyte + anti-irritant character.
  - **Ceramides / cholesterol / fatty acids** — pairs with the brand's barrier-repair thesis (humectant + TJ support alongside lipid replenishment).
  - **Niacinamide** — barrier/NMF synergy story.
  - **Surfactant systems (cleansers)** — betaine's standout evidence-based use: **anti-irritant in cleansers/wash-off**, mitigating SLS/CAPB harshness. [15][16]
  - **Exfoliating acids / retinoids** — useful soothing co-ingredient to offset irritation.
- **Conflicts:** No notable incompatibilities at cosmetic levels (it's inert, endogenous, pH-flexible). Distinguish clearly on the label from **cocamidopropyl betaine** to avoid allergen confusion (§7).
- **Sensory:** At use levels, contributes a **smooth, non-tacky, "cushioned" skin feel**; can modestly improve thickener efficiency/viscosity build; essentially odorless; low/no color. A pleasant texturizer as well as an active.

## 7. Safety

- **Overall:** Among the **safest** cosmetic humectants — endogenous to human cells, an approved oral drug at gram doses, food-grade in many uses. CIR reviewed the **alkyl betaine** family (the zwitterionic class incl. glycine betaine context) and concluded the betaines are **safe as used when formulated to be non-irritating/non-sensitizing**. [5]
- **Irritation:** Non-irritating; **actively anti-irritant** — it *reduces* surfactant irritation in human skin/mucosa. [15][16][17]
- **Sensitization:** Trimethylglycine itself has **no meaningful sensitization signal**. **Key distinction:** the contact-allergy reputation belongs to **cocamidopropyl betaine (CAPB)** — a different (surfactant) molecule whose allergenicity comes from manufacturing impurities (3-dimethylaminopropylamine, amidoamine), **not** from trimethylglycine. Don't let CAPB's allergen status be misattributed to betaine humectant. [5][6]
- **Sun sensitivity:** None; no photosensitization. (Animal **oral** data are mildly photo-*protective* via MMP-9 suppression.) [18]
- **Pregnancy/breastfeeding:** No topical concern expected (endogenous, also a normal dietary/oral compound). Standard "consult your doctor" hedge for marketing prudence; the gram-dose oral drug is used clinically, but that doesn't equal a topical-pregnancy claim.
- **Eyes/sensitive areas:** Well tolerated; studied as an **osmoprotectant in dry-eye tear substitutes** — supports use near the eye/in sensitive formulas. [12][23]
- **Comedogenicity:** None expected (small hydrophilic solute).
- **Sourcing/allergen labeling:** Plant-derived (sugar beet); generally non-allergenic. No wheat/gluten issue (it's a small molecule, not a protein), though source the COA from reputable suppliers to confirm purity/identity.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Cosmetic status:** INCI **Betaine**, CAS 107-43-7; established cosmetic ingredient (humectant / skin-conditioning / viscosity-related), CIR-reviewed class, no global restriction known for trimethylglycine in leave-on cosmetics (confirm per target market). [1][5]
- **The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame):** Because betaine is also a **drug (Cystadane)** for homocystinuria, keep all *oral/systemic* and *disease* connotations out of cosmetic copy. Topical anti-aging claims that import the **mouse oral photoaging / MMP-9** data, or that claim it "repairs the barrier," "boosts filaggrin," or "reduces wrinkles," veer into **structure/function (drug)** territory and (worse) lack human topical substantiation.
- ✅ **Safer cosmetic language:** "humectant that helps **draw and hold moisture**," "a **naturally skin-identical osmolyte**," "helps skin **stay hydrated and balanced**," "**helps soothe** and reduce the look of irritation," "supports a **comfortable, resilient feel**," "**gentle enough for sensitive skin**." (Anti-irritant/soothing wording is the **best-substantiated** claim we have — human in-vivo.)
- ⚠️ **Risky / needs substantiation or legal review:** "**boosts filaggrin / NMF**," "**repairs the skin barrier**," "**strengthens tight junctions**," "**reduces wrinkles**," "**improves hydration by 20–30%**," "**anti-aging**." The "20–30%/filaggrin" numbers are **vendor-sourced and unverified** — do **not** put a figure in copy without our own finished-formula testing.
- **Substantiation reality:** Soothing/hydration *appearance* claims are defensible; any **quantified** hydration or **barrier/anti-aging** claim should rest on **our own corneometry/TEWL study** of the finished product, since the literature won't substantiate a betaine-isolated facial claim. See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Source:** Predominantly **sugar-beet molasses** (the original natural source) via established suppliers — e.g., **IFF/Danisco GENENCARE OSMS BA** (natural betaine), Nutreco/feed-grade lines, plus generic fine-chemical grades (Sigma-Aldrich, Glentham, Santa Cruz). [9][22]
- **Cost tier:** **Low (commodity).** Betaine is produced at large industrial scale for feed, food, supplements, and cosmetics; it is one of the **cheapest credible humectants/actives** available. Excellent margin-and-story ratio for a supporting active.
- **Supply/standardization:** Robust, multi-source supply; specify **cosmetic/anhydrous grade** with COA (identity, assay, heavy-metals, microbial). Distinguish on the PO/spec sheet from CAPB and betaine salicylate to avoid mix-ups. Aligns with the brand's "buy commodity bases, invest in the brand" logic.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

- **"Clinically proven to boost hydration 20–30%."** The figure recurs across blogs/vendor copy but I could **not** trace it to a peer-reviewed, betaine-isolated human RCT. **[unverified].** Betaine is a *good* humectant; the *number* is marketing.
- **"Boosts filaggrin / NMF."** Repeated in editorial copy; the closest real data are **TJ proteins and keratin 2** (barrier/differentiation), and **filaggrin upregulation by betaine specifically is not well-substantiated** in the primary literature I found. Treat as **[unverified]**.
- **"Anti-aging / reduces wrinkles."** The wrinkle/MMP-9 data are **oral, in mice** [18] — wrong route, wrong species for a topical claim.
- **"Repairs the barrier in your skin."** Mechanistically attractive (TJ + osmoprotection) and shown in **human ex-vivo skin/keratinocytes** [13], but **not** demonstrated as a barrier-repair outcome in an in-vivo human topical trial — and it requires transporter-mediated uptake that's unproven for a cosmetic dose in intact skin.
- **The CAPB allergen mix-up (in the other direction).** Some sources *over-warn* about "betaine" allergy by conflating it with cocamidopropyl betaine. Trimethylglycine humectant is **not** the allergen — but we should still label precisely. [5][6]
- **What's genuinely under-sold:** betaine's **anti-irritant** evidence is **real human in-vivo** [15][16][17] and its **osmoprotection** is mechanistically clean across human cell systems [11][13] — the honest, differentiated story is "**gentle, skin-identical osmolyte that soothes and helps skin hold water**," not "anti-aging."

## 11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ **SUPPORTING — reliable, cheap, well-tolerated hydration + soothing workhorse; not an anti-aging hero.**

**One-line:** Betaine is a low-cost, low-risk, skin-identical humectant/osmolyte with **better-than-average mechanistic and human-ex-vivo/in-vivo (anti-irritant) evidence** but **no betaine-isolated human topical facial RCT** — perfect as a **supporting** hydration + barrier-comfort ingredient (especially in cleansers and barrier-repair creams), framed honestly around *soothing and moisture*, never *anti-aging*.

**Why supporting, not hero:** (1) The brand's hero slots should go to actives with demonstrable human topical anti-aging outcomes (retinoids, niacinamide, etc.); betaine's strongest *human* data are anti-irritancy and osmoprotection, not facial anti-aging. (2) Its best benefits (humectancy, soothing) are *table-stakes-plus*, ideal to elevate a formula's tolerability and feel. (3) Cost and stability make it a no-brainer inclusion wherever hydration/comfort matters.

**Fit with the barrier-repair thesis:** Strong. Betaine slots neatly beside ceramides/cholesterol in the flagship **barrier-repair cream** — humectant water-binding + osmoprotection + tight-junction support (mechanistically) + anti-irritancy directly reinforce "barrier repair / comfort." Also an obvious add to any **gentle cleanser** to cut surfactant harshness (its single best-evidenced use).

**Longevity-relevance note:** Betaine has a *modest* longevity pedigree compared with marquee geroprotectors — it's an **endogenous osmolyte and methyl donor** (homocysteine metabolism, an aging-relevant pathway systemically), and oral betaine shows photoaging benefit in mice. But for a **topical skin-longevity** product, its contribution is **resilience/homeostasis** (helping skin cells defend volume and barrier under environmental/osmotic stress) rather than collagen/senescence/mitochondrial rejuvenation. A credible *supporting* "skin resilience" narrative — not a centerpiece longevity claim.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Verified:** 2026-06-19 (independent citation + claim check against primary literature).

**Overall grade: C** (with a B-grade island for one specific claim).
The grade is **split by claim**, because betaine's evidence is genuinely uneven:

- **Anti-irritancy (soothing) → B (moderate human-topical).** Real human in-vivo data exist (Nicander 2003 ×2: n=21 forearm patch with SLS + CAPB, dose-response soap study; plus oral-mucosa work). These are small, acute patch/mucosa models with an *anti-irritant* endpoint — not facial hydration or anti-aging, and not RCTs of a finished facial product — so they fall short of A but are real human topical evidence. This is the **best-substantiated claim in the dossier** and the one the brand should lean on.
- **Barrier / tight-junction support → C (indirect; human ex-vivo + in-vitro, no in-vivo topical outcome).** El-Chami 2021 (BJD) is real and well-represented (human organ-cultured skin + keratinocytes; ↑claudin-1/-4, occludin; transporter-dependent). But it is ex-vivo/in-vitro, the effect requires active transporter uptake that is **unproven for a cosmetic dose on intact in-vivo skin**, and there is no human topical barrier-repair outcome trial. Mechanistically clean, clinically unproven.
- **Hydration (its headline cosmetic job) → C (mechanistically sound, no betaine-isolated human topical RCT).** Humectancy is plausible from physical chemistry and class behaviour, but there is **no retrievable peer-reviewed RCT isolating betaine for facial hydration**. The widely-quoted "**2–5% betaine → 20–30% hydration at 28 days; upregulates filaggrin**" figure traces only to vendor/editorial copy — independently confirmed **[unverified]**; a targeted search returned no published RCT matching those specs. Do not put a number or "filaggrin" in copy.
- **Photoaging / wrinkle / MMP-9 / collagen → D for topical.** The single "anti-aging betaine" paper (Im 2016, *Mol Med Rep*) is real but **oral, in hairless mice** — wrong route and wrong species for any topical claim. Correctly flagged in-dossier.
- **Osmoprotection / cell-volume defense → C (real but cross-tissue).** Hua 2014 (corneal) is real and the numbers (27%→11% shrinkage, +17% mitochondrial, +12% viable cells) match the source exactly — but it is **corneal epithelium, in vitro**, not skin.

**Citation integrity:** All eight load-bearing primary citations checked (El-Chami 2021 BJD; Rauhala 2015 Tox in Vitro; Im 2016 Mol Med Rep; Nicander 2003 ×2; Hua 2014 Exp Eye Res; corneal anti-MMP work) are **real and say what the dossier claims**. One **bibliographic error was corrected**: Rauhala 2015 is *Toxicology in Vitro* **30(1 Pt B):462–475**, not "29(8):1948–1960" (DOI/PMID were already correct). No fabricated studies found. Several sources flagged "[partially unverified]" in §12 (refs 10, 20, 21, 23) are industry/secondary and correctly labeled.

**Funding / size red flags:** No industry-funded efficacy trial is being passed off as independent. The academic papers (El-Chami, Rauhala, Hua, Nicander) are university-led; the supplier material (IFF/GENENCARE, Dermatology Review) is **explicitly tagged as vendor/secondary** in the dossier — good practice. The main weaknesses are **small n** (El-Chami: 8 donors; Nicander: 21 subjects) and **wrong model/route** (mouse-oral, corneal-in-vitro), all of which the dossier already discloses.

**Main caveats (what could mislead a reader):**
1. The strong mechanistic narrative (TJ proteins, osmoprotection) can read as if betaine *clinically* repairs the barrier in skin. It does not — no in-vivo human topical outcome exists, and the mechanism presupposes transporter uptake unproven at cosmetic doses.
2. The "20–30% hydration / filaggrin" numbers are marketing, not science. Treat as unusable for substantiation.
3. The anti-aging/wrinkle evidence is oral-mouse only.

**Claims to soften / keep out of copy:** "repairs the barrier," "strengthens tight junctions," "boosts filaggrin/NMF," "improves hydration by 20–30%," "anti-aging / reduces wrinkles." (The dossier's §8/§10 already restrict these — verification concurs.)

**Net verdict:** The dossier is **honest and well-calibrated** — it pre-empts most of the overreach itself, grades its own tiers fairly, and labels vendor figures as unverified. It is **not hype**. The only substantive correction was the Rauhala citation. Headline "supporting, soothing/hydration workhorse — not an anti-aging hero" framing is **defensible and correctly graded C overall (B for soothing)**.
