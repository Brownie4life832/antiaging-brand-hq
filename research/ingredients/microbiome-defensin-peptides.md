# Ingredient: Microbiome / Defensin-inducing Peptides (Acetyl Dipeptide-3 Aminohexanoate "Bodyfensine"; Acetyl Heptapeptide-4 "Fensebiome")

**Slug:** microbiome-defensin-peptides · **Class:** Peptides
**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ⬜ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ☑ rejected (as a hero) / ⬜ in a product

> **One-line honest take:** Two small **synthetic signal peptides** from Lipotec/Lubrizol that are marketed as "defensin/microbiome" actives because they nudge keratinocytes to make a bit *more* of the skin's own antimicrobial peptides (β-defensins) and shift the skin microbiome toward "balance." The biology they piggy-back on (β-defensins, microbiome, barrier) is real. The peptides' *own* evidence is **thin, almost entirely supplier-generated in-vitro / small in-vivo work, never independently replicated, and the figures are modest** (e.g., hBD-2 +26%, hBD-3 +10% in cell culture). They are **barrier-and-comfort / microbiome-balance supporting actives, not anti-aging actives** — there is essentially no human wrinkle/firmness/collagen data for either. **Crucially, these are NOT defensins** (those are 3.5–5 kDa proteins — see [`defensins.md`](defensins.md)); they are ~400 Da and ~960 Da peptides that *induce* defensins. Do not conflate the two.

---

## 1. Identity

This dossier covers **two distinct, separately branded peptides** that share a "boost the skin's own defenses / microbiome" positioning. Both are owned by **Lipotec S.A.U.** (Barcelona; founded 1987; acquired by **Lubrizol** — a Berkshire Hathaway company — in 2012) [1][2].

### A. Acetyl Dipeptide-3 Aminohexanoate — "Bodyfensine®"
- **INCI:** Acetyl Dipeptide-3 Aminohexanoate.
- **Sequence:** Ac-Arg-Ala-Ahx-OH (acetylated; Arg–Ala plus a 6-aminohexanoic-acid / Ahx residue) [3]. Despite the "dipeptide" name it behaves as a short tri-residue construct.
- **Molecular weight:** ~**400 Da** (formula ~C17H32N6O5) [10] — *below* the ~500 Da skin-penetration ceiling (notable; see §5).
- **CAS:** 1265905-30-3 [10].
- **Launched/positioned:** ~2009 (Lipotec) as a "reinforcement of skin's natural defense" [4]; featured again 2013 [5].
- **Marketed role:** "skin defense peptide" — *body/face/oral-care*; anti-acne, barrier/defense, microbiome-balance.

### B. Acetyl Heptapeptide-4 — "Fensebiome™"
- **INCI:** Acetyl Heptapeptide-4.
- **Sequence:** Ac-Glu-Glu-Met-Gln-Arg-Arg-Ala (7 residues, acetylated) [6].
- **Molecular weight:** ~**961 Da** (formula ~C37H64N14O14S) [10] — above the 500 Da rule but modest for a peptide (cf. Argireline ~889 Da, Matrixyl ~802 Da).
- **CAS:** 1459206-66-6 [10]. (Note: this CAS is distinct from a *different* "Acetyl Heptapeptide-4 / SNAP-7" anti-redness peptide sometimes listed under CAS 1253115-74-0 — verify which Acetyl Heptapeptide-4 a supplier means.)
- **Marketed role:** "double barrier function reinforcement" + **skin-microbiome balance/diversity** for *urban/pollution-stressed* and *vulnerable/sensitive* skin; more recently extended to *scalp* microbiota [9].

**Commercial form (both):** sold as ~1% aqueous "peptide solutions" (Bodyfensine peptide solution; Fensebiome peptide solution — typically Water + the peptide + caprylyl glycol) [6][7]. Recommended in-product use level commonly cited around the supplier's standard peptide-solution dosing; Fensebiome's published in-vivo work used the **1% peptide solution** [7].

**Naming-confusion flags (important):**
- These are **"defensin-mimetic / defensin-inducing" cosmetic peptides, NOT defensins.** True α/β-defensins (e.g., the DefenAge α-defensin-5 / β-defensin-3 system) are large 3.5–5 kDa innate-immunity proteins — covered in [`defensins.md`](defensins.md). Conflating them is the single most common error with this slug.
- At least one independent ingredient profile mislabels Acetyl Dipeptide-3 Aminohexanoate as "Acetyl Tetrapeptide-3" [11] — that is a **different ingredient** (a hair/peptide active). Treat such cross-references with caution.

## 2. Class & mechanism

**Class:** Synthetic **signal peptides** (host-defense-peptide *inducers* / microbiome-modulating peptides). Not antimicrobials themselves, and not defensins themselves.

The marketing mechanism rests on three layers of real biology, with the peptides acting at the top of the chain:

1. **β-defensin induction (Bodyfensine's core claim).** Human β-defensins (hBD-2, hBD-3) are cationic antimicrobial peptides made by keratinocytes; hBD-3 is broad-spectrum (incl. *S. aureus*, multi-resistant strains) and hBD-2 hits Gram-negatives/Candida — both are **inducible** by microbial contact and cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β) and are part of innate skin immunity [12][13 (Harder 2001)]. β-defensins also have non-antimicrobial roles: keratinocyte migration/proliferation and **tight-junction/barrier support** [14]. Bodyfensine is claimed to *upregulate the genes* for hBD-2 and hBD-3 in keratinocytes, so the skin makes more of its own defenses rather than the product killing bacteria directly [3][4][5].
2. **Microbiome balancing (Fensebiome's core claim).** Rather than a biocide, Fensebiome is positioned as **prebiotic-like**: tilt the surface ecology toward commensals and higher diversity, discourage pathogen colonization (*S. aureus*), and thereby reduce sensitivity/discomfort in "urban-stressed" skin [6][7][8].
3. **Barrier reinforcement ("double barrier").** Fensebiome's pitch is a *physical* barrier (corneocyte cohesion, lower TEWL) **plus** a *microbial/immune* barrier (microbiome + defenses) — hence "double barrier" [6][7]. The barrier biology of host-defense peptides is supported in independent in-vitro work: hBD-2 helps protect/repair the keratinocyte barrier and supports tight-junction integrity [15 (Nat Sci Rep 2023, abstract level)][14].

**The honest mechanistic gap:** Steps in layers 1–3 are legitimate skin biology *for the endogenous defensins/microbiome*. What is weakly established is that **these specific peptides, at cosmetic use levels, drive a biologically meaningful change in living human skin** — the supplier data are small in magnitude (a +26%/+10% gene bump in culture) and the human effects are barrier/comfort surrogates, not demonstrations of durable microbiome remodeling or anti-aging.

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

| Hallmark | Targeted? | Honesty note |
|---|---|---|
| **Skin microbiome / dysbiosis** | **Primary claim** | The on-label angle. Supplier metagenomics shows a *short-term* diversity/balance shift (§4). But "the microbiome declines with age" is an **oversimplification**: with age, facial **C. acnes drops while overall alpha-diversity tends to *rise*** and Corynebacterium/Staphylococcus increase [16][17]. "Restoring diversity" is not unambiguously a younger-skin direction — handle the longevity framing carefully. |
| **Barrier decline / hydration / TEWL** | **Primary claim (Fensebiome)** | Best-supported endpoint: Fensebiome ↓TEWL ~27.8% and ↓corneocyte sloughing 18.6% in supplier in-vivo tests [7]; Bodyfensine claims ↑moisturization ~38% [10-derived/trade]. Real but small, supplier-run, short. |
| **Inflammaging / reactivity ("sensitive skin")** | Claimed | Plausible via β-defensin/microbiome calming; rests on the AD combination-product data (mostly the *other* active — §4) and supplier comfort scores. Indirect. |
| **Antimicrobial resilience / "skin immunity"** | Claimed (mechanistic) | Defensins are genuinely innate immunity [12][13]; the peptides nudge them. This is the most credible *mechanistic* story, but it's an immune/defense story, not an anti-aging one. |
| Collagen / ECM loss, dermal thickness | **Not a target** | No collagen/elastin/wrinkle data for either peptide. Do not imply firming/anti-wrinkle. |
| Oxidative stress, glycation/AGEs, cellular senescence, mitochondrial decline | **Not a target** | No relevant data. Do not claim. |
| Photoaging, pigmentation/tone | **Not a target** | No relevant data. |

**Net:** these are **barrier + microbiome + skin-defense / comfort** actives. Their honest place in a "skin-longevity" story is "keep the barrier and microbial ecosystem resilient," not "reverse aging."

## 4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (core of this dossier)

> **Bottom line up front:** Almost everything is **supplier (Lipotec/Lubrizol) in-vitro plus small supplier in-vivo** work, with **no independent topical RCT isolating either peptide**, and the only peer-reviewed *human* data attach to **Fensebiome inside a combination product** (where a different active does most of the work). Magnitudes are modest. None of it is anti-aging efficacy data.

### Tier 1 — In-vitro (the bulk of the evidence)

**Bodyfensine (Acetyl Dipeptide-3 Aminohexanoate):**
- **Keratinocyte gene-expression assay (Lipotec).** Topically/keratinocyte-applied peptide **increased expression of hBD-2 by 26% and hBD-3 by 10%** [3][5]. This is the headline number across every secondary source — and it is a **single supplier in-vitro figure**, modest in size, on gene/peptide expression, not a demonstrated change in skin infection or aging. Reported in trade press (Davì/Cebrián et al., Lipotec, *Happi* 2013) [5]; not a peer-reviewed independent study.

**Fensebiome (Acetyl Heptapeptide-4):**
- **In-vitro immune/defense + bacterial-interaction assays (Lipotec).** Supplier work reports the peptide "boosts the skin's own defense system" and improves dermal cell cohesion/barrier integrity, with imaging of keratinocyte interactions with *S. epidermidis* (commensal) vs *S. aureus* (pathogen) [6][7]. Specific defensin/tight-junction numbers (hBD-2, claudin/occludin) are referenced in supplier literature but **were not retrievable at the primary numeric level** for this dossier — treat as supplier-claimed, unquantified here. [unverified — exact in-vitro hBD-2/tight-junction figures]

**Foundational (independent) biology these peptides borrow from:**
- **Harder et al. 2001, *J Biol Chem*** [13] — isolation/characterization of hBD-3 as an inducible, broad-spectrum keratinocyte antimicrobial peptide. *Real, independent — but about endogenous hBD-3, not the cosmetic peptide.*
- **hBD-2 barrier mechanism work** (e.g., *Sci Rep* 2023) [15] and β-defensin keratinocyte-migration/barrier reviews [14] — support that *more* β-defensin can be barrier-protective. *Independent, but again about the endogenous defensins.*

**Tier-1 verdict:** The *enabling biology* (defensins, microbiome, barrier) is solid and independent. The *peptides' own* in-vitro evidence is supplier-generated, small in magnitude, and not independently replicated.

### Tier 2 — Animal
- **None located** specific to these peptides. (No bridging animal efficacy data.)

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL
- **None.** Bodyfensine is mentioned for *oral-care* (toothpaste/mouthwash, topical to oral mucosa) [4], but there is **no oral-supplement use** and no ingestion data. No oral-to-topical borrowing applies here.

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters)

**4a. Supplier in-vivo (open, no independent control reported):**
- **Fensebiome — urban-skin metagenomics study (Lipotec).** Urban female volunteers, **twice-daily application to the cubital fossa for 7 days**; metagenomic sequencing showed **increased bacterial diversity and "better balance" of the microbiota** on treated skin [7]. *Very short (7 days), small/undisclosed n, supplier-run, surrogate (sequencing) endpoint, no durability data.*
- **Fensebiome — barrier-protection in-vivo (Lipotec).** Pre-application before a surfactant/irritation challenge gave **TEWL reduction ~27.8%** and a protective effect on surfactant-damaged skin; corneocyte-cohesion testing showed **18.6% fewer sloughed corneocytes** (less scaliness) [7]. *Supplier-run, short, mechanistic surrogates — but these are the most concrete human-skin numbers available for either peptide.*
- **Bodyfensine — body/defense claims (Lipotec).** Trade/supplier sources cite consumer/clinical figures such as **sebum −25%** and **skin moisturization +38%** [10-linked trade summary]; primary study design (n, duration, control) was **not retrievable** for this dossier. [unverified — Bodyfensine in-vivo sebum/moisturization study design]

**4b. Peer-reviewed human (Fensebiome only — and inside a combination product):**
- **Mainzer et al. 2024, *Cosmetics* 11(4):136** [18] — **Post-marketing surveillance (questionnaire) study**, not a controlled trial. ~88 members of the Polish Association for Atopic Diseases with atopic dermatitis used an **emollient-plus cream containing BOTH Ophiopogon japonicus oligofructans AND Acetyl Heptapeptide-4** and answered a questionnaire after **14 days**. Reported subjective improvement/biocompatibility. **Limitations (severe for our purposes):** no control arm, self-report only, 14 days, and — decisively — **it cannot isolate the peptide from the oligofructans or the emollient base.**
- **Mainzer et al. 2019, *Acta Derm Venereol* (DOI 10.2340/00015555-3224)** [19] — the actual *controlled* AD trial (randomized, single-blind, placebo-controlled, multicenter; **n=234**: 90 children + 144 adults; 60 days twice-daily) showing reduced SCORAD (erythema, pruritus, BSA) and fewer relapses. **But this trial tested *Ophiopogon japonicus oligofructans*, NOT Acetyl Heptapeptide-4** — the peptide is not in it [19]. So the strongest "clinical" data in this ingredient's orbit **belong to a different active.**
- Author lists on these AD papers include Lipotec/Lubrizol-affiliated scientists (e.g., Bordes, Closs) — i.e., **supplier-linked**, not independent.

**4c. Scalp extension (Fensebiome):**
- Lubrizol Life Science Beauty has released **scalp-microbiota** testing for Fensebiome (2024-era) [9] — same supplier, same "balance diversity" framing; primary numeric results were **not retrievable** for this dossier and should be treated as supplier marketing until a primary source is in hand. [unverified — scalp study design/results]

**What is "proven for topical human use" vs. borrowed:**
- **Reasonably supported (low tier, supplier-run):** Fensebiome gives a **short-term shift in skin microbiome diversity** and **modest barrier protection (↓TEWL, ↓corneocyte sloughing)** over days. Bodyfensine gives **modest comfort/sebum/moisturization** signals. These are *barrier/microbiome/comfort* outcomes.
- **Borrowed / not proven for these peptides:** all the heavy biology — actual antimicrobial protection in vivo, durable microbiome remodeling, atopic-dermatitis efficacy (that's the *oligofructans*), and anything anti-aging (collagen, wrinkles, firmness, senescence). The defensin/barrier independent literature [13][14][15] is about the **endogenous** peptides, not these cosmetic inducers.

### Independent replication
- **Essentially none.** Every efficacy figure traces to Lipotec/Lubrizol or supplier-linked authors. No independent lab has published a controlled topical trial isolating Acetyl Dipeptide-3 Aminohexanoate or Acetyl Heptapeptide-4. This is the dossier's biggest evidence weakness.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

A genuine *relative* strength versus real defensins, but with a caveat:

- **These peptides are small.** Acetyl Dipeptide-3 Aminohexanoate is **~400 Da (under the ~500 Da rule** [20]); Acetyl Heptapeptide-4 is **~961 Da** (above it, but in the same range as Argireline/Matrixyl, which are formulated for surface/upper-epidermal signaling) [10][20]. They are **~4–13× smaller than true α/β-defensins (3.5–5 kDa)**, so the catastrophic penetration problem of the DefenAge-type proteins (see [`defensins.md`](defensins.md) §5) is **much less severe** here.
- **But the target may be superficial anyway.** Both peptides are *signal* peptides meant to act on **keratinocytes and the skin surface/microbiome** — i.e., the action is largely at the stratum corneum / upper epidermis and the microbial film, where even modest penetration suffices. That's mechanistically convenient.
- **Caveat:** small ≠ proven delivery of *functional* peptide to the right cells at the right dose. Peptides are also subject to skin-surface proteolysis. No published skin-penetration/quantification study for either peptide was located. [unverified — formal penetration data for either peptide]

**Net:** penetration is **plausible and far less of a problem than for true defensins**, but it is asserted from molecular size and mechanism, not demonstrated with permeation data.

## 6. Formulation

- **Use level:** Supplied as ~1% aqueous "peptide solutions"; Fensebiome's in-vivo work used the **1% peptide solution** [7]. In-product actual-peptide concentration is low (sub-percent of active). Follow supplier dosing; expect typical signal-peptide use in the low fractions of a percent of pure peptide.
- **pH / stability:** Short acetylated peptides; keep within a mild cosmetic pH window and avoid extremes/strong oxidizers-reducers. Acetyl Heptapeptide-4 contains a **methionine** (oxidation-prone) — antioxidant/chelator support and packaging that limits oxidation are sensible. Caprylyl glycol in the commercial solutions doubles as a mild preservative/stabilizer [6]. Both should be added in the cool-down/water phase.
- **Pairings (good):** humectants (glycerin, HA), barrier lipids (ceramides), niacinamide, panthenol, beta-glucan, prebiotics/postbiotics — i.e., a **barrier-and-microbiome supporting stack**, which is also their honest job. Compatible with the brand's barrier-repair thesis.
- **Conflicts:** Strong low-pH acids (AHA/BHA) and strong oxidizers in the *same step* are best avoided (peptide/Met stability). No hard incompatibility with retinoids, but sequence to avoid irritation that would defeat a "soothing/defense" positioning.
- **Sensory:** Water-thin solutions, no color/odor issues reported; easy to formulate into serums, lotions, creams, even oral-care (Bodyfensine) [4].

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation/tolerability:** Marketed specifically for **vulnerable/sensitive/reactive** skin; supplier and combination-product data report good tolerability and even reduced discomfort/scaling [6][7][18]. Independent profiles rate Acetyl Dipeptide-3 Aminohexanoate non-comedogenic (0) and "generally considered safe," with the usual caveat of possible idiosyncratic irritation — patch test [11].
- **Sun sensitivity:** None expected; not a photosensitizer.
- **Pregnancy/breastfeeding:** **No specific data** for either peptide; default to "insufficient data — consult a clinician," do not make pregnancy-safe claims [11].
- **Sensitization:** Peptides can in principle sensitize; no signal reported, but long-term data are absent.
- **Microbiome-safety nuance:** Because the pitch is "shift the microbiome," be careful not to over-claim *control* of a living ecosystem; effects shown are short-term and surrogate. No safety concern, but a humility point.
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** **Fully synthetic** peptides — no human/animal-tissue provenance (a clean advantage over secretomes/exosomes/PRP and over biologically sourced defensins). Standard peptide-purity/heavy-metal QC applies.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Status:** Both are **cosmetic ingredients, not drugs**, used as skin-conditioning/soothing actives. Each finished formula still needs its own safety assessment (EU Reg. EC 1223/2009; US cosmetic rules).
- **The cosmetic-vs-drug line is the central risk here.** The natural way to describe these peptides — "boosts the skin's antimicrobial defenses," "protects against infection," "treats acne," "rebalances the microbiome," "for atopic/eczema-prone skin" — drifts straight into **drug/structure-function and disease territory** (acne, atopic dermatitis, infection are drug claims). The AD evidence also belongs to a *medical-device cream* and a *different active*, so importing it is doubly risky.
- **✅ Safer cosmetic language (if used):** "helps support the skin's natural defenses," "supports a balanced-looking skin microbiome," "helps reinforce the moisture barrier," "soothing/comforting for sensitive-feeling skin," "helps reduce the look of rough, flaky skin."
- **⚠️ Risky / drug-like — flag for substantiation/legal:** "boosts defensins by 26%," "antimicrobial/antibacterial," "prevents infection," "treats acne," "treats/relieves eczema/atopic dermatitis," "antiseptic," "anti-aging." The "+26%/+10% defensin" figures are *single supplier in-vitro* numbers and as label claims would need substantiation. (See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).)
- **IP:** Bodyfensine and Fensebiome are **Lipotec/Lubrizol trademarks**; the raw materials are bought from them. There is no patent moat *for us* and no exclusivity — but also no novelty advantage; many brands can and do use these.

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Cost tier:** **Mid.** Branded Lipotec/Lubrizol peptide solutions cost more than commodity peptides (e.g., copper peptide) but far less than exotic regeneratives (exosomes, growth factors, branded senotherapeutics). Sold as a finished ~1% solution, so formulation is turnkey.
- **Supply:** Stable, single-source-branded (Lubrizol/Lipotec) via standard distributors (Knowde, UL Prospector, etc.); generic Acetyl Dipeptide-3 Aminohexanoate / Acetyl Heptapeptide-4 also available from peptide suppliers (verify sequence/CAS and purity — note the Acetyl Heptapeptide-4 CAS ambiguity in §1).
- **Diligence:** confirm CAS/sequence, purity, Met-oxidation control, and that the supplier's efficacy dossier is what's claimed.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

| Marketing claim | Reality |
|---|---|
| "Defensin peptide" / implies it *is* a defensin | **False framing.** These are ~400 Da / ~960 Da synthetic peptides that *induce* the body's defensins; they are not defensins (3.5–5 kDa). Marketing leans on the impressive word "defensin." |
| "Boosts your skin's defenses (β-defensins)" | Real *direction*, but the only number is a **single supplier in-vitro** hBD-2 +26% / hBD-3 +10% — modest, unreplicated, in cell culture, not a demonstrated in-vivo protection benefit. |
| "Balances/restores the skin microbiome" | Supported only by a **7-day, supplier-run** metagenomics shift; no durability, no independent replication, surrogate endpoint. And "restore youthful diversity" is scientifically muddy — diversity often *rises* with age [16][17]. |
| "Clinically proven for sensitive/atopic skin" | The controlled AD trial tested **Ophiopogon oligofructans, not the peptide** [19]; the peptide appears only in an uncontrolled **14-day questionnaire** combination-product study [18]. Disease (eczema) claims are drug territory regardless. |
| "Anti-aging / youthful skin" | **No collagen, wrinkle, firmness, or any anti-aging data** for either peptide. This is borrowed glow from the "microbiome longevity" trend. |
| "Double barrier reinforcement" | The physical-barrier half has the best (still small, supplier) data (↓TEWL ~28%, ↓corneocyte sloughing ~19%); the "immune barrier" half is mechanistic/borrowed. |

What's *genuinely* good: real underlying biology, clean synthetic sourcing, good tolerability, easy formulation, small size (decent penetration odds), and an honest fit as a **barrier/microbiome supporting layer**.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**⚠️/❌ — Skip as a hero; at most a *conditional supporting* "barrier + microbiome" co-ingredient, never an anti-aging claim-carrier.**

**One-line reasoning:** Real but borrowed biology wrapped around **thin, supplier-only, unreplicated, non-anti-aging evidence**, with a claims profile that constantly tempts drug-territory language — a weak fit for a transparency-led longevity brand, except as a quiet supporting barrier/microbiome active.

- **If used at all:** position strictly as **barrier-comfort / microbiome-balance support** (Fensebiome is the better-evidenced of the two for that), pair with the brand's barrier-repair stack (ceramides, niacinamide, glycerin/HA, panthenol, postbiotics), and discipline claims to "supports natural defenses / balanced-looking microbiome / reinforces the barrier" — **never** "antimicrobial," "treats acne/eczema," "boosts defensins X%," or "anti-aging."
- **Longevity-relevance note:** Tangential. A resilient barrier and a balanced microbiome are legitimately part of "skin longevity," so there's a *narrative* hook — but these peptides have **no aging-hallmark efficacy data**, and the "microbiome ages/declines" story they ride on is oversimplified [16][17]. For barrier and microbiome support we already have cleaner, better-evidenced options (barrier lipids/ceramides, niacinamide, postbiotics/ferments — see [`microbiome-postbiotics.md`](microbiome-postbiotics.md), [`ceramides.md`](ceramides.md), [`niacinamide.md`](niacinamide.md)).
- **Do not confuse with** the true-defensin play (DefenAge α/β-defensins, [`defensins.md`](defensins.md)) — different molecules, different (also weak) evidence base, different IP situation.
- **Revisit trigger:** an *independent*, controlled topical trial isolating either peptide and showing a barrier/microbiome (or, implausibly, anti-aging) benefit.

Log in [`../../ideas/not-considering.md`](../../ideas/not-considering.md) (as a hero) with reason: defensin/microbiome *inducer* peptides with thin supplier-only, unreplicated, non-anti-aging evidence and a drug-claim-prone profile; only a conditional barrier/microbiome supporting role, where cheaper better-evidenced actives already cover the job.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Verified:** 2026-06-19. Method: for each headline claim, checked whether it is (a) human + topical or quietly borrowed from in-vitro/other-format/other-active data; (b) supplier-generated vs independent; (c) about these *cosmetic peptides* vs about the *endogenous defensins/microbiome* they invoke.

### Overall evidence grade: **D+ (weak; supplier-only, indirect, non-anti-aging)**

Rationale: The *enabling biology* (β-defensins [12][13][14][15], microbiome-aging [16][17], the 500 Da rule [20]) is real and independently published — but it is **not evidence for these peptides**. The peptides' *own* evidence is a handful of **supplier (Lipotec/Lubrizol) in-vitro and small in-vivo** results (hBD-2 +26% / hBD-3 +10% in culture; Fensebiome 7-day microbiome shift, TEWL −27.8%, corneocyte sloughing −18.6%), plus one **uncontrolled 14-day questionnaire** on a **combination product** [18], while the only properly controlled AD trial in the orbit tested a **different active** (oligofructans) [19]. No independent topical trial isolates either peptide, and **no anti-aging endpoint (collagen/wrinkle/firmness) exists at all.** That is a textbook D-grade for the marketed positioning; it earns the "+" only because the peptides are small (plausible delivery), well-tolerated, cleanly sourced, and the barrier surrogates (TEWL/corneocyte) are at least objective.

### Source-by-source verdict
- **[13] Harder 2001 (hBD-3) — VERIFIED** (authors/journal/year/finding confirmed via PubMed). Endogenous defensin biology, correctly framed as borrowed.
- **[18] Mainzer 2024 *Cosmetics* — VERIFIED real**; confirmed it is a **post-marketing questionnaire** on a **combination** Ophiopogon + Acetyl Heptapeptide-4 cream (~88 AD patients, 14 days) — cannot isolate the peptide. Correctly represented.
- **[19] Mainzer 2019 *Acta Derm Venereol* — VERIFIED real** (randomized, single-blind, placebo-controlled, n=234, 60 days). **Critical confirmation: the active is Ophiopogon japonicus oligofructans, NOT the peptide.** The dossier flags this as the key honesty point.
- **hBD-2 +26% / hBD-3 +10% [3][5] — VERIFIED as the consistently cited figure**, but traced to **Lipotec trade/supplier sources**, not an independent peer-reviewed study; magnitude is modest. Represented as such.
- **Fensebiome in-vivo (TEWL −27.8%, corneocyte −18.6%, 7-day microbiome shift) [7] — VERIFIED as supplier-reported** via UL Prospector feature; n/control not disclosed. Represented as supplier-run, short, surrogate.
- **Molecular weights [10] — VERIFIED** (~400 Da and ~961 Da) across multiple chemical databases; load-bearing for the penetration argument and the "not a defensin" point.

### Main caveats (load-bearing)
1. **Not defensins.** The whole "defensin" frame is borrowed nomenclature; these are small inducer peptides. (§1, §10.)
2. **Supplier-only, unreplicated.** 100% of efficacy data is Lipotec/Lubrizol or supplier-linked. No independent isolating trial. (§4.)
3. **The best "clinical" data isn't theirs.** The controlled AD trial tested oligofructans, not the peptide [19]; the peptide's only peer-reviewed human appearance is an uncontrolled combination-product questionnaire [18].
4. **No anti-aging data.** Zero collagen/wrinkle/firmness/senescence evidence — this is a barrier/microbiome/comfort active mis-shelved next to anti-aging peptides.
5. **Microbiome-aging story is oversimplified.** Diversity often *rises* with age while C. acnes falls [16][17]; "restore youthful diversity" is not cleanly supported.
6. **Claims land in drug territory** (antimicrobial, acne, eczema, infection) — must be disciplined hard.

### Bottom line
The dossier's verdict (**skip as a hero; conditional barrier/microbiome supporting role at most; never an anti-aging or drug-style claim-carrier**) is well-supported. No fabricated citations; items that could not be verified at the primary level (Bodyfensine in-vivo design; Fensebiome scalp numbers; exact in-vitro tight-junction figures) are marked **[unverified]**. Grade **D+** = real biology, real (clean, small) ingredients, but evidence that is supplier-only, indirect, non-anti-aging, and not isolating.
