# Ingredient: Guggulsterone (Commiphora mukul) (INCI: see §1 — no INCI for the *isolate*; "Commiphora Mukul Resin Extract" is the cosmetic INCI for the source extract)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ⬜ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ☑ rejected as a hero / ☑ conditional (supporting, as a *standardized resin extract*, brightening/anti-inflammatory angle only) / ⬜ in a product
**Class (as filed):** NAD+ / sirtuin / AMPK / cellular-energy actives — **see honesty flag below.**

> **One-line honest summary:** Guggulsterone is a plant steroid (a "phytosteroid," 4,17(20)-pregnadiene-3,16-dione) from the Ayurvedic gum resin *guggul* (Commiphora mukul / C. wightii). It is a genuinely interesting, well-studied bioactive — but its real, evidenced mechanism is **NOT** the NAD+/sirtuin/AMPK "cellular-energy" axis it's been filed under. Its hard pharmacology is **farnesoid-X-receptor (FXR) antagonism, NF-κB inhibition, STAT3 inhibition, androgen-receptor antagonism, and cAMP-pathway suppression of melanogenesis** — an anti-inflammatory / anti-androgen / tyrosinase-down profile, not a longevity-energy-sensor profile. For skin specifically the evidence is **in-vitro fibroblast, melanocyte and keratinocyte work plus one in-vivo mouse psoriasis model (dosing route to skin unconfirmed)**; the only *human* data are **ORAL** (a small positive acne trial; a rigorous *negative* cholesterol trial that also produced a hypersensitivity rash). **Human topical clinical evidence: zero.** It is also anti-proliferative / pro-apoptotic (its biggest research literature is oncology), and its botanical source is **IUCN Critically Endangered and now CITES-listed** — a serious ESG problem. **Verdict: skip as a hero; at most a *conditional supporting* tone/anti-inflammatory note using a sustainably-sourced standardized resin extract, with honest "supports the appearance of" language.** Do not file or market it as a "NAD+/sirtuin/AMPK longevity active" — that would be unsupported.

---

## ⚠️ Class-label honesty flag (read first)

This ingredient was filed under **"NAD+ / sirtuin / AMPK / cellular-energy actives."** After a thorough literature search, that classification is **not supported** for guggulsterone:

- I found **no credible primary literature** establishing guggulsterone as a direct **NAD+ booster, sirtuin (SIRT1) activator, or AMPK activator**. (Guggulsterone *does* induce "beiging"/thermogenic gene programs in 3T3-L1 adipocytes [16], and adipocyte beiging is downstream-adjacent to PGC-1α/AMPK biology — but that is an indirect, adipose-tissue, in-vitro link, not evidence that guggulsterone is an AMPK/sirtuin active.)
- Guggulsterone's **actual, repeatedly-demonstrated molecular targets** are: **FXR (antagonist)** [4][6], **NF-κB / IκB kinase (inhibitor)** [5][6][13], **STAT3 (inhibitor, via SHP-1 induction)** [4], **androgen receptor (antagonist)** [12], **cAMP/CREB→MITF melanogenesis (suppressor)** [9], and Wnt/β-catenin, Akt, JNK and cell-cycle proteins in cancer models [11][12].

The mechanistically-honest class for this molecule is **"anti-inflammatory / nuclear-receptor-modulating phytosteroid (FXR antagonist) with anti-androgen and tyrosinase-suppressing activity."** It belongs alongside botanical anti-inflammatories and brightening/anti-androgen actives — **not** alongside true NAD/sirtuin/AMPK longevity actives (acetyl-L-carnitine, AICAR, Ca-AKG, NAD precursors). The rest of this dossier evaluates it on its *real* biology and flags the mismatch wherever the filed class would tempt an over-claim.

## 1. Identity

- **Common names:** Guggulsterone; guggulsterones (the E and Z mixture); "guggul ketosteroids." The source resin: **guggul / gugul / guggulu / Indian bdellium**. The processed lipid fraction used clinically: **gugulipid / guggulipid**.
- **Botanical source:** Gum-oleo-resin of **_Commiphora mukul_ (Hook. ex Stocks) Engl.**, the accepted synonym of which is **_Commiphora wightii_ (Arn.) Bhandari** (family Burseraceae) — a small thorny desert tree/shrub of India, Pakistan and Oman. (Same genus as myrrh, *C. myrrha*.) Note the species-name confusion across the literature: *mukul* and *wightii* are the same plant. [3][17]
- **Chemical identity:** **Pregna-4,17(20)-diene-3,16-dione** — a C₂₁ keto-steroid (phytosteroid). Two geometric isomers about the C17–C20 exocyclic double bond:
  - **Z-guggulsterone** (cis) — CAS **39025-23-5** — generally considered the more bioactive isomer.
  - **E-guggulsterone** (trans) — CAS **39025-24-6**.
  - **E/Z mixture** — CAS **95975-55-6**.
- **Molecular formula / weight:** **C₂₁H₂₈O₂, MW ≈ 312.45 g/mol.** Rigid steroidal core (0 rotatable bonds), TPSA ≈ 34 Å², lipophilic; satisfies Lipinski/Veber drug-likeness. [3][7]
- **Forms you would actually buy:**
  - **Pure guggulsterone (Z, E, or E/Z)** — a *research/pharma biochemical* (Selleck, Sigma, Santa Cruz, MedChemExpress, etc.); **no cosmetic INCI as an isolate**. [8][18]
  - **Gugulipid / guggul extract** — ethyl-acetate/standardized lipid fraction, typically standardized to a stated **% guggulsterones** (often labeled ~2.5–10%); the form used in oral supplements and the oral clinical trials. [1][2]
  - **Commiphora Mukul Resin Extract** — the **recognized cosmetic INCI** for the source resin extract (functions declared as *skin conditioning / tonic*); this, not the isolate, is the realistic cosmetic raw material. [10][14]
- **INCI status (decisive nuance):** There is **no established cosmetic INCI for "guggulsterone" as a purified isolate.** The cosmetic-legal route is **"Commiphora Mukul Resin Extract"** (and related Commiphora INCIs), standardized to guggulsterone content. So a brand cannot honestly put "Guggulsterone" on an INCI list — it would appear as the resin extract. [10][14]

## 2. Class & mechanism (how it actually works)

Guggulsterone is a **multi-target phytosteroid**. The mechanisms with the best evidentiary support, ranked by how skin-relevant they are:

1. **NF-κB / IκB-kinase inhibition → anti-inflammatory (the most skin-relevant mechanism).** Guggulsterone blocks IKK activation and IκBα phosphorylation/degradation, suppressing NF-κB nuclear translocation and the downstream pro-inflammatory transcriptome (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, iNOS/COX-2). Demonstrated in macrophages, IBD models, and keratinocytes. [5][6][13] In skin terms this is an **inflammaging / irritation-damping** lever.
2. **STAT3 inhibition (via induction of the phosphatase SHP-1).** The Z-isomer suppresses constitutive and IL-6-induced STAT3 — Ahn et al. (Aggarwal lab), *Cancer Res* 2008. [4] STAT3 is a driver of keratinocyte proliferation/inflammation (hence the psoriasis result, §4).
3. **FXR (farnesoid X receptor) antagonism — its "signature" pharmacology.** Guggulsterone is a canonical FXR antagonist; this underlies its (disputed) lipid-lowering rationale. FXR has limited direct skin-aging relevance, so this is the mechanism most likely to be *mis-borrowed* into skin claims. [4][6]
4. **cAMP-pathway suppression of melanogenesis (the most direct "skin-tone" mechanism).** Guggulsterone downregulates **MITF, tyrosinase, TRP-1 and TRP-2** and suppresses α-MSH- and forskolin-induced melanogenesis in melanocytes — i.e. it acts on the **cAMP→CREB→MITF** axis at the transcriptional level. [9]
5. **Androgen-receptor antagonism + anti-androgen/anti-sebum activity.** Guggulsterone lowers AR protein and promoter activity (shown in prostate-cancer cells) and the resin has historic **anti-acne** use plausibly tied to anti-androgen / anti-inflammatory / sebum effects. [12][1]
6. **MMP-1 / elastase inhibition + collagen-I support (the "anti-wrinkle" in-vitro story).** A guggul-resin/triheptanoin preparation increased type-I collagen synthesis, inhibited MMP-1 dose-dependently, and inhibited elastase >50% in dermal fibroblasts. [1] (This is a *resin-preparation* result, not pure guggulsterone.)
7. **Pro-apoptotic / anti-proliferative / cell-cycle effects (the dominant research literature — and a safety caveat).** Across many tumor lines guggulsterone induces apoptosis (↓ Bcl-2, survivin, cFLIP, IAPs; ↑ caspases, JNK, ROS), arrests the cell cycle (↓ cyclin D1, c-Myc) and inhibits Akt and Wnt/β-catenin. [4][11][12] It also *suppresses* keratinocyte proliferation (psoriasis model, §4). This anti-proliferative bias is the opposite of what you want a renewal-oriented leave-on doing to healthy basal keratinocytes/fibroblasts at high dose — see §7.
8. **Antioxidant / Nrf2:** general "antioxidant" activity is attributed to guggul, but I found **no strong guggulsterone-specific Nrf2/HO-1 mechanistic paper**; treat antioxidant claims as weak/indirect. [7]

**Skin-relevant translation (honest):** The *coherent* skin story for guggulsterone is **anti-inflammatory (NF-κB/STAT3) + tone-evening (MITF/tyrosinase down) + anti-androgen/anti-acne**, with an *in-vitro-only* anti-wrinkle (MMP-1/elastase/collagen) signal from the resin preparation. The **NAD/sirtuin/AMPK "cellular energy/longevity"** framing it was filed under is **not its mechanism** and should not be claimed.

## 4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

> **The single most important honesty point:** Guggulsterone has a *large* primary literature — but it is overwhelmingly **oncology, metabolic/lipid, and inflammatory-bowel-disease** work, done **in vitro or via oral/systemic dosing**. The **skin** evidence is a handful of **in-vitro** papers (fibroblast, melanocyte, keratinocyte) plus **one topical/in-vivo mouse psoriasis model**. The only **human** evidence is **ORAL** — a small positive *acne* trial and a rigorous *negative* cholesterol trial. **Human topical skin evidence = zero (no RCT, no lower-tier clinical, no published cosmetic finished-product trial, no human-skin penetration study).** A "topical guggulsterone anti-aging" claim today is built on borrowed mechanism + in-vitro skin-cell data.

### Tier 1 — In vitro (cell models) — *where the skin-relevant data actually live*

- **Ramachandran C., Quirin K-W., Resek A., Melnick S.J. (2012), *Int J Cosmet Sci* 34(2):155–160 (DOI 10.1111/j.1468-2494.2011.00695.x; PMID 22084831).** A proprietary **Commiphora mukul gum-resin + triheptanoin preparation (GU-TC7), standardized to 2% guggulsterones**, on **CCL-110 human skin fibroblasts at 20–100 µg/mL**: mild proliferative effect, **increased type-I collagen synthesis**, **dose-dependent MMP-1 inhibition**, **>50% elastase inhibition** (vs none for CoQ10 comparator). *Limitations: in-vitro single cell line; tests a **resin+lipid blend**, not pure guggulsterone, so the active(s) responsible are unclear; abstract states no formal limitations; no in-vivo/human arm.* [1] — **the most-cited "guggul is anti-wrinkle in skin cells" datapoint.**
- **Koo J-H., Rhee K-S., Koh H-W., Jang H-Y., Park B-H., Park J-W. (2012), *Int J Mol Med* 30(4):974–978 (DOI 10.3892/ijmm.2012.1057; PMID 22797252).** Guggulsterone on **B16 murine melanoma cells**, **1, 5, 10, 25 µM**, with IBMX stimulation: melanin fell to **~104.5%, 54.1%, 39.4%, 33.8%** of control across the dose range (i.e. up to ~66% reduction at 25 µM), with parallel **tyrosinase activity** decline and **↓ tyrosinase, MITF, TRP-1, TRP-2 mRNA**; it suppressed **α-MSH- and forskolin-induced** melanogenesis (cAMP pathway); **no cytotoxicity up to 25 µM**. *Limitations: murine melanoma line (not human melanocytes); authors explicitly note no in-vivo work; no human skin-tone outcome.* [9] — **the most direct "skin tone" finding.**
- **Anti-inflammatory in macrophages — Kim J-M. et al. (2016), *Drug Des Devel Ther* 10:1219–1226 (PMID/Dovepress DDDT).** Guggulsterone dose-dependently suppressed LPS-induced **IL-1β, TNF-α, iNOS** in RAW264.7 macrophages via **↓ IκB phosphorylation / NF-κB**; non-toxic at tested doses; mechanism confirmed with NF-κB inhibitor BAY 11-7082. *Limitations: macrophage line, not skin cells; supports the inflammaging mechanism, not a skin outcome.* [13]
- **STAT3 / SHP-1 — Ahn K.S., Sethi G., Sung B., Goel A., Ralhan R., Aggarwal B.B. (2008), *Cancer Res* 68(11):4406–4415 (DOI 10.1158/0008-5472.CAN-07-6696).** Z-guggulsterone inhibits constitutive and IL-6-induced STAT3 via induction of the tyrosine phosphatase **SHP-1**, sensitizing tumor cells to apoptosis. *Limitations: cancer cells; relevant as mechanism (STAT3 is keratinocyte-relevant) and as a **safety caveat** (pro-apoptotic).* [4]
- **Apoptosis / anti-proliferation across tumor lines (the dominant literature) — reviewed in Shishodia S. & Aggarwal B.B. and later reviews [11][12].** ↓ Bcl-2/survivin/cFLIP/IAPs/cyclin-D1/c-Myc; ↑ caspases/JNK/ROS; ↓ AR, Akt, Wnt/β-catenin. *Relevance: establishes guggulsterone as a **cytostatic/pro-apoptotic** agent — a genuine safety consideration for a leave-on (§7).* [4][11][12]
- **Adipocyte "beiging" — Nsuntar/related (2019), *Int J Mol Sci* / PMC6473927.** Guggulsterone activated thermogenic/beiging gene programs in 3T3-L1 adipocytes (direct) and via RAW264.7 macrophages (indirect). *Relevance: the only "energy-metabolism-adjacent" datapoint — and it is adipose, in-vitro, and NOT a sirtuin/AMPK skin proof.* [16]

### Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo) — *one genuinely skin-relevant model; rest systemic*

- **Psoriasis (in-vivo skin disease model) — (2025), *Biochem Pharmacol* (article S0006-2952(25)00007-3).** "Guggulsterone ameliorates psoriasis by inhibiting keratinocyte proliferation and inflammation through induction of miR-17 directly targeting JAK1 and STAT3." In an **imiquimod-induced psoriasis mouse model**, guggulsterone **reduced psoriasiform lesions**; in **human keratinocytes** it suppressed **proliferation, migration, and pro-inflammatory cytokines/chemokines/antimicrobial peptides**, via **miR-17-5p ↑ → JAK1/STAT3 ↓**. *Limitations: a **disease (psoriasis)** model, not a cosmetic/aging model; the headline effect is **anti-proliferative** on keratinocytes (therapeutic for psoriasis, but a caution for healthy renewing skin); mouse + human-cell, no human topical trial.* **NOTE (verification): in this class of imiquimod study the imiquimod is applied topically but the test compound is typically dosed systemically (oral gavage or i.p.); I could not confirm from accessible text that guggulsterone here was applied *topically* to the skin. Do not assume topical delivery — treat as an in-vivo skin-disease result of uncertain route.** [15] — **the closest thing to an in-vivo skin result for guggulsterone, and it is an anti-inflammatory/anti-proliferative disease effect, not a cosmetic-aging or confirmed-topical one.**
- **Systemic anti-inflammatory / IBD / metabolic / neuro models.** Guggulsterone is protective in colitis, neuroinflammation (APP/PS1 mice, TLR4), and metabolic models — all **oral/systemic**, non-skin. [5][6] *Relevance: corroborates the NF-κB anti-inflammatory mechanism in vivo; not skin.*

### Tier 3 — Human (ORAL/systemic) — *the only human data, and they are mixed-to-negative*

- **Acne (the one positive, directly-relevant human trial) — Thappa D.M., Dogra J. (1994), *J Dermatol* 21(10):729–731 (DOI 10.1111/j.1346-8138.1994.tb03277.x).** **Nodulocystic acne, n=20**, randomized to **oral gugulipid (≈25 mg guggulsterone) vs tetracycline 500 mg, both BID × 3 months.** Inflammatory-lesion reduction **68% (gugulipid) vs 65.2% (tetracycline)** — difference not significant; **fewer relapses on gugulipid (2 vs 4)**; notably, **patients with oily skin responded better to gugulipid.** *Limitations: tiny (n=20), single-center, old, **oral not topical**, no placebo arm, nodulocystic (severe) acne — not a cosmetic/aging endpoint. Still, it is the **best human evidence the ingredient has**, and it is for **oral** use.* [2]
- **Cholesterol (the rigorous NEGATIVE trial — and a safety signal) — Szapary P.O. et al. (2003), *JAMA* 290(6):765–772 (DOI 10.1001/jama.290.6.765; PMID 12915429).** RCT, **n=103**, standard- and high-dose standardized gugulipid vs placebo, 8 weeks. **No LDL benefit — LDL actually rose +4% (standard) / +5% (high) vs placebo.** Importantly, **6 participants on gugulipid developed a hypersensitivity rash** (0 on placebo), resolving on discontinuation. *Relevance: (a) deflates the "guggul superfood" health-halo; (b) is the **clearest human safety signal** — an oral-route hypersensitivity/allergenicity flag relevant to topical sensitization risk (§7).* [3]
- **Reviews of guggul human use (MSKCC, Drugs.com) [17][19].** Catalogue oral use for hyperlipidemia/arthritis/acne/weight with weak-to-negative efficacy and documented adverse effects (GI upset, headache, **rash/hypersensitivity**, thyroid and drug-interaction concerns). All **oral**.

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters for a skincare brand)

- **None identified.** After multiple targeted searches I found **no peer-reviewed human topical/clinical skin study of guggulsterone or guggul resin extract** — no RCT, no split-face, no lower-tier clinical, no published finished-product efficacy trial, and **no human-skin penetration/PK study.** Commercial cosmetic use exists (Commiphora Mukul Resin Extract is an INCI used in anti-wrinkle/anti-acne formulas, and there are patents [10][14][20]), but **patents and ingredient listings are not clinical evidence.**
- **Implication:** Any topical guggulsterone anti-aging claim is **borrowed** from (a) oral acne/anti-inflammatory data, (b) in-vitro fibroblast/melanocyte/keratinocyte work, and (c) one mouse psoriasis model. There is **no human topical efficacy or safety dataset** to stand on.

**Bottom line by tier:** Mechanistically rich (NF-κB/STAT3/MITF/AR) with two solid in-vitro skin-cell findings (collagen/MMP-1/elastase from a resin blend; melanin/tyrosinase down) and one topical mouse psoriasis result. **Best human evidence = a small *oral* acne RCT (positive) and a rigorous *oral* cholesterol RCT (negative, + hypersensitivity rash).** **Human topical skin evidence = zero.** Dominant overall biology = **anti-inflammatory + anti-proliferative/pro-apoptotic.**

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **Favorable on paper:** Guggulsterone is a **small (MW 312), rigid, lipophilic steroid** that satisfies Lipinski/Veber drug-likeness — physicochemically the *kind* of molecule that *can* partition into the stratum corneum. [7]
- **But proven absorption is poor and route-dependent:** As a phytosterol-class compound, oral absorption is intrinsically low (the broader phytosterol class is **<5% absorbed**); even the favorable rat oral figure (~43% absolute bioavailability, t½ ~10 h) reflects an optimized oral context, and guggulsterone shows **~50 µM aqueous solubility ceiling and high plasma-protein binding**. [7] None of this is skin-penetration data.
- **No human (or ex-vivo human skin) penetration/PK data found** quantifying delivery of guggulsterone to viable epidermis/dermis from a topical vehicle. Skin delivery is therefore **plausible but unproven**.
- **Isomer instability is a delivery wrinkle (literally):** E-guggulsterone **isomerizes to Z** under **heat, light, and acid** (e.g. ~50:50 E:Z after 12 h light exposure; up to 55% Z at 140 °C) [21]. A finished product's E/Z ratio — and thus its presumed bioactivity (Z is usually the more active isomer) — can drift with processing and shelf life unless controlled.
- **Practical read:** A *standardized resin extract* in a well-designed lipophilic vehicle could deliver *some* guggulsterone to skin, but **no human data confirm meaningful penetration or an active skin concentration**, and isomer drift complicates standardization.

## 6. Formulation

> Provided for the realistic scenario (a **standardized Commiphora Mukul Resin Extract**, not the pure isolate). The verdict is *skip-as-hero / conditional-supporting* (§11).

- **Effective concentration:** **No established human topical efficacy concentration.** In-vitro skin-cell effects sit at **1–25 µM guggulsterone** (melanocytes) [9] and **20–100 µg/mL of a 2%-guggulsterone resin blend** (fibroblasts) [1]. Commercial cosmetic use of Commiphora Mukul Resin Extract is typically at **low single-digit percentages of the extract**, delivering sub-percent guggulsterone — no dose-response is clinically validated.
- **pH:** A neutral steroid with no ionizable groups in the relevant range; broad pH tolerance. **Avoid strongly acidic systems**, which (with heat/light) accelerate E→Z isomerization and could shift the actives profile. [21]
- **Stability:** **Light- and heat-sensitive for isomer ratio** (§5) [21]; lipophilic/oxidizable steroid in a resin matrix → formulate with **antioxidants, opaque/airless packaging, and controlled processing temperature**; the resin itself is sticky/aromatic and can pose **color and odor** challenges.
- **Solubility / phase:** Lipophilic — belongs in the **oil phase / emulsion**, not a watery serum; the native resin is gummy and needs a proper solubilizer/extract format.
- **Pairings (coherent, on its *real* mechanism):**
  - **Tone/brightening stack:** with **niacinamide, vitamin-C derivatives, tranexamic acid, licorice/azelaic** — complements its MITF/tyrosinase-down action. [9]
  - **Calm/anti-inflammatory stack:** with **centella, allantoin, bakuchiol, panthenol** — complements NF-κB/STAT3 inhibition. [13][15]
  - **Anti-acne/oily-skin angle:** with **niacinamide, zinc, salicylic acid** — aligns with the anti-androgen/oily-responder signal. [2][12]
  - **Avoid over-stacking with strong proliferation-suppressors** given guggulsterone's own anti-proliferative bias (§7).
- **Sensory:** Resin extracts carry a **warm, balsamic/woody odor and amber color** — a fragrance/aesthetics consideration; can muddy "clean/clear serum" positioning.

## 7. Safety

- **Anti-proliferative / pro-apoptotic bias (the headline mechanistic caution).** Guggulsterone's largest body of work is **inducing apoptosis and arresting the cell cycle** in many cell types [4][11][12], and it **suppresses keratinocyte proliferation** (psoriasis model) [15]. That is *therapeutic* for hyperproliferative disease but is **not obviously desirable** for a leave-on aimed at healthy skin renewal at high local doses. In-vitro skin work showed **no cytotoxicity at the tested low doses** (≤25 µM melanocytes [9]; 20–100 µg/mL resin blend [1]) — so the risk is dose-dependent, but it argues for **conservative concentrations**.
- **Hypersensitivity / sensitization signal (the clearest human safety data).** In the rigorous oral cholesterol RCT, **6/~50 gugulipid users developed a hypersensitivity rash** (vs 0 placebo), resolving on stopping [3]. Reviews likewise list **skin rash/hypersensitivity** among guggul's adverse effects [17][19]. This is an **allergenicity flag** directly relevant to topical use — **HRIPT/patch-testing of any finished product is essential**, and it is a real sensitization risk for a leave-on.
- **Irritation / photo-safety (topical):** **No human topical patch-test/HRIPT/phototoxicity data found** for guggulsterone or the resin extract. Unknown — must be generated.
- **Pregnancy / hormonal:** Guggulsterone is a **steroid with androgen-receptor antagonist and bile-acid/thyroid-axis activity** [12][17]; oral guggul is generally **advised against in pregnancy** and can affect thyroid function and drug metabolism (CYP/Pgp; documented interactions e.g. with propranolol, diltiazem) [17][19]. Even though topical systemic exposure is low, the **endocrine-active, hormone-receptor-modulating** nature argues for caution and pregnancy-avoidance labeling absent topical safety data.
- **Sourcing-linked adulteration risk:** Because the source plant is scarce (§9), **guggul resin is frequently adulterated**; quality/identity control (HPLC standardization, heavy-metal and adulterant testing) is essential for safety and label honesty.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (cosmetic vs drug — per CLAUDE.md)

- **Cosmetic legality of the source:** **Commiphora Mukul Resin Extract** is an **established cosmetic INCI** with declared functions *skin conditioning / tonic* (CosIng/COSMILE) [10][14] — so a *standardized resin extract* is a legitimate cosmetic raw material in the EU/US. The **purified isolate "guggulsterone" has no cosmetic INCI** and would have to be declared as the resin extract.
- **Where claims cross into drug territory (high risk for this ingredient):**
  - **"Treats acne"** — the strongest evidence (Thappa 1994) is for **ORAL gugulipid treating nodulocystic acne**; "treats acne" is a **drug claim** and is *not* supported for topical use. Even "for acne-prone skin / helps reduce the look of blemishes" should be used cautiously and only as cosmetic appearance language. [2]
  - **"Anti-inflammatory," "reduces inflammation"** — **drug-like**; in cosmetics use *"helps soothe / visibly calms the look of redness."* [13][15]
  - **"Brightens / fades dark spots"** — keep to **cosmetic appearance**: *"helps even the look of skin tone,"* *"visibly brightens."* The melanin data are **murine in-vitro only** [9] — do not imply a clinical depigmenting effect.
  - **"Boosts collagen," "reduces wrinkles"** — **not supported** (in-vitro resin-blend fibroblast data only [1]); avoid. At most *"supports the look of firmness"* with substantiation caveats.
  - **"NAD+/sirtuin/AMPK / cellular energy / longevity"** — **do NOT claim.** Unsupported for this molecule (§ honesty flag). This would be the most damaging over-claim given the brand's evidence-first positioning.
- **Per CLAUDE.md claims discipline:** Draft only with ✅ cosmetic verbs ("supports," "helps improve the appearance of," "visibly," "soothes the look of"); flag any efficacy claim for substantiation/legal. Avoid all ⚠️ drug verbs ("treats," "cures," "anti-inflammatory," "boosts collagen").

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Critically Endangered source — a serious ESG/brand-risk issue.** *Commiphora wightii / mukul* is assessed **IUCN Critically Endangered (2015)** after an estimated **>80% population decline over ~three generations** from unsustainable resin tapping, slow growth, poor germination and habitat loss; it has now received **CITES protection** (international trade controls / sustainability-assessment requirements). [17][22] For a science-and-ethics-forward brand, sourcing wild guggul is **reputationally hazardous** and increasingly trade-restricted.
- **Mitigation if pursued:** insist on **cultivated, non-destructively-tapped, CITES-compliant, traceable** supply (cultivation programs exist, e.g. in Kachchh, Gujarat) [22]; or use **synthetic guggulsterone** for any isolate work (regioselective syntheses of E-guggulsterone are published [21]) — though synthetic isolate lacks a cosmetic INCI and raises the "is it still cosmetic?" question.
- **Cost tier:** The **resin extract** is a **low-to-moderate-cost** Ayurvedic botanical; **pure guggulsterone (Z/E)** is a **research-grade reagent** (expensive per gram) and not a practical bulk cosmetic input. The real cost/risk driver is **sustainable, adulterant-free, standardized** sourcing — not headline price.
- **Adulteration:** Scarcity drives frequent adulteration of guggul resin; demand HPLC standardization to % guggulsterones plus identity and contaminant testing.

## 10. Hype vs evidence

- **The health-halo (oral) is largely deflated.** Guggul's most famous claim — cholesterol lowering — **failed a rigorous Western RCT** (LDL *rose*) [3]. Cancer claims are **preclinical**. So the "ancient Ayurvedic super-resin" framing is not supported by modern controlled human data.
- **The skin story is real but thin and mis-shelved.** There *are* legitimate, on-mechanism in-vitro skin signals (tyrosinase/MITF down; MMP-1/elastase down; NF-κB/STAT3 down) and one topical mouse psoriasis result — but **no human topical evidence**, and the strongest human datum is an **oral** acne trial.
- **The biggest hype risk is the filed class.** Marketing this as a **"NAD+/sirtuin/AMPK longevity / cellular-energy"** active would be **inventing a mechanism it doesn't have** — exactly the kind of over-claim this brand's positioning is built to avoid.
- **Honest framing:** "A traditional Ayurvedic resin whose isolated steroid (guggulsterone) shows, *in lab studies*, anti-inflammatory and tone-evening activity, and which has a small *oral* acne trial behind it — promising but not proven for topical anti-aging, and ethically constrained by its endangered source."

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**⚠️ SKIP as a hero / longevity active — at most CONDITIONAL SUPPORTING (tone-evening + soothing) using a sustainably-sourced, standardized Commiphora Mukul Resin Extract, with strictly cosmetic claims.**

Reasoning:
1. **Misclassified.** It is **not** a NAD+/sirtuin/AMPK "cellular-energy" active; using it to populate that pillar would be unsupported and off-thesis (§ honesty flag).
2. **No human topical evidence.** Zero topical RCTs or even lower-tier clinical/penetration data; the human data are **oral** (mixed-to-negative outside acne).
3. **Safety frictions.** A documented **hypersensitivity-rash** signal (oral) [3], an **anti-proliferative/pro-apoptotic** mechanistic bias [4][11][12][15], and **endocrine/hormone-receptor** activity [12][17] — all argue against a leave-on hero role without bespoke topical safety work.
4. **Serious sourcing/ESG problem.** A **CITES-listed, IUCN-Critically-Endangered** botanical is a poor fit for a brand that wants to lead on credibility and ethics, unless cultivated/traceable supply is locked down [22].
5. **Where it *could* earn a minor slot:** as a **supporting botanical** in a **brightening or calming** product, framed honestly ("supports a more even-looking tone / helps visibly soothe"), at conservative concentration, with HRIPT and sustainable sourcing — competing against better-evidenced, less-fraught actives (niacinamide, azelaic, tranexamic acid, licorice, centella, bakuchiol), which likely **win on evidence and ethics** anyway.

**Longevity note:** Guggulsterone's *true* longevity-adjacent biology is **inflammaging suppression** (NF-κB/STAT3) — a real geroprotective axis — plus tone-evening. That is a legitimate, modest story. But it is **not** a NAD/sirtuin/AMPK energy-metabolism story, and the brand should resist the temptation (created by the filing class) to tell it as one. If the brand wants a credible "cellular-energy/longevity" hero, look to better-evidenced, cosmetic-legal, ethically-clean actives instead; keep guggulsterone, if at all, as a clearly-scoped supporting anti-inflammatory/brightening botanical.

Log under **conditional / mostly not-considering** in [`../../ideas/`](../../ideas/), with the class-mislabel and sourcing reasons recorded.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Verified:** 2026-06-19. Independent re-check of the headline claims by re-reading the dossier and confirming the load-bearing citations against PubMed/journal/JAMA sources.

### Overall grade: **D** (for the *filed* use-case: topical anti-aging)
There is **zero human topical evidence** of any tier for guggulsterone or guggul resin extract — no RCT, no split-face, no lower-tier clinical, no human-skin penetration/PK study. Every "skin" claim is **borrowed** from oral human acne data, in-vitro skin-cell work, or an in-vivo mouse *disease* model. By the rubric (A = strong human-topical RCT … D = unproven topically/hype), topical anti-aging guggulsterone is a **D**. The dossier already states this plainly; the grade simply formalizes it.
- *For the narrower realistic role* it actually recommends (a supporting brightening/soothing botanical on **mechanism + in-vitro** grounds): still **C at best** — indirect, in-vitro, animal-only, never demonstrated topically in humans.

1. **No human topical data — full stop.** The single biggest fact; the dossier states it repeatedly and correctly.
2. **The best human evidence is ORAL** (acne, n=20; cholesterol, n=103) and is mixed-to-negative outside acne.
3. **The strongest "skin" datapoints are in-vitro and partly not even the pure molecule** (the anti-wrinkle signal [1] is a resin+lipid blend).

5. **CITES-listed / IUCN Critically Endangered source** = real ESG blocker; well-captured.
6. **Class mislabel** ("NAD/sirtuin/AMPK") is the highest over-claim risk and is correctly neutralized up front.
