# Ingredient: Mycosporine-like Amino Acids (MAAs) (INCI: see §1 — no single MAA INCI; sold as algae extracts)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product
**Class:** Sunscreen / photoprotection filters & adjuncts (natural UV-absorbing antioxidant; "biological UV filter")

> **One-line honest summary:** MAAs are nature's UV-absorbing molecules — algae's own sunscreen — and the *photophysics is genuinely excellent*: they soak up UVA/UVB and dump the energy as heat without making free radicals, and they're photostable. But for **human topical skin**, the evidence is thin and mostly **in-vitro / cell-culture**, with only two small, mostly-uncontrolled human studies (both on the commercial liposome product Helioguard 365). They are **not approved UV filters** in the EU or US, so they legally **cannot** be sold or claimed as "sunscreen." Best read: a credible **antioxidant / SPF-boosting / "blue-light & UVA-defense" supporting active with a beautiful marine-longevity story — not a hero sunscreen, and not a proof-backed anti-wrinkle hero.**

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common name:** Mycosporine-like amino acids (MAAs). A **family of 30–70+ related molecules**, not one compound. ("MYCAS" database lists ~74.) [7]
- **The skincare-relevant members** (each is its own molecule with its own λmax):
  - **Shinorine** — CAS 70579-25-8; λmax ~333–334 nm; MW ~332; ε ~44,700 M⁻¹cm⁻¹ [1][7]
  - **Porphyra-334** — CAS 70579-26-9; λmax 334 nm; MW ~346; ε ~42,300 M⁻¹cm⁻¹ [1][7]
  - **Palythine** — λmax 320 nm; MW ~244; ε ~36,200 [1]
  - **Mycosporine-glycine** — λmax 310 nm; MW ~243; ε ~28,800 (the most antioxidant-active member) [1]
  - **Asterina-330** (330 nm), **Palythinol** (332 nm), **Palythene/Usujirene** (357–360 nm — the most red-shifted, deepest-UVA), **Mycosporine-2-glycine** (anti-glycation star), **Mycosporine-serinol**. [1][7]
- **INCI / labeling reality (important):** There is **no single "MAA" INCI**. MAAs reach cosmetics as **standardized algae/cyanobacteria extracts**, and the label shows the extract, not "shinorine." The two market leaders:
  - **Helioguard 365** (Mibelle Biochemistry) — porphyra-334 + shinorine from red alga *Porphyra umbilicalis*, **liposome-encapsulated**; INCI typically *Lecithin (and) Porphyra Umbilicalis Extract (and) Water (and) ...*. [datasheet, 6]
  - **Helionori** (Gelyma) — palythine + porphyra-334 + shinorine from *Porphyra umbilicalis*; INCI an aqueous *Porphyra Umbilicalis Extract* system. [9]
  - Purified single MAAs (shinorine, porphyra-334) are sold as **research/reference chemicals** (e.g., Biosynth CAS 70579-26-9), not yet as cosmetic-grade actives at scale. [reg search]
- **Chemistry:** Small (<400 Da), colorless, **highly water-soluble**, zwitterionic. Two core scaffolds: an **aminocyclohexenone** ring (mono-substituted; e.g., mycosporine-glycine) or an **aminocyclohexenimine** ring (bi-substituted; e.g., shinorine, porphyra-334), with amino-acid/amino-alcohol substituents that tune the absorption wavelength. [7]
- **Source organisms:** cyanobacteria (*Nostoc*, *Synechocystis*, *Scytonema*), red algae (*Porphyra/Pyropia umbilicalis*, *P. yezoensis*), green algae, dinoflagellates, lichens (*Lichina pygmaea*, *Collema cristatum*), corals, and marine animals that bioaccumulate them. **Not made by higher plants or vertebrates** — humans cannot synthesize them. [3][7]

## 2. Class & mechanism

MAAs are best understood as **biological UV filters + water-soluble antioxidants** — a dual-function "photoprotection adjunct."

1. **UV absorption with heat dissipation (the headline mechanism).** MAAs have very high molar extinction coefficients (ε ≈ 12,400–58,800 M⁻¹cm⁻¹) across ~310–362 nm — i.e., strong **UVA** and partial **UVB** coverage; they are described as "the strongest UVA-absorbing compounds in nature." Critically, after absorbing a photon they return to ground state via **ultrafast non-radiative internal conversion** (through an S1/S0 conical intersection), releasing the energy as **heat** rather than fluorescence or photochemistry. Whittock et al. (2020) measured >75% of excited molecules relaxing within ~1.8 ns and only ~2–6.5% photodegradation after 2 h solar-simulated exposure — i.e., they are **photostable and do not generate reactive photoproducts.** This is the cleanest, most rigorous part of the whole MAA story. [10][1]
2. **Antioxidant / ROS scavenging.** Several MAAs (notably **mycosporine-glycine**) scavenge ROS, quench singlet oxygen, and inhibit lipid peroxidation. Mycosporine-glycine showed ~**8× the antioxidant activity of ascorbic acid at pH 8.5** (de la Coba). Note: the headline antioxidant member is **mycosporine-glycine**; **porphyra-334 and shinorine are weak in the *DPPH* assay specifically** [5], but the picture is **assay-dependent** — in cell models porphyra-334 does dose-dependently cut intracellular UVA-induced ROS [4], and some reports describe shinorine/porphyra-334 as effective ROS scavengers and even Keap1–Nrf2 antagonists [1]. Honest read: their *primary* value is UV-screening; their direct radical-scavenging is real but secondary and method-sensitive — do not flatly claim them as "strong antioxidants" or flatly dismiss them. [1][11][5][4]
3. **Anti-inflammatory.** In keratinocytes/macrophages, MAAs suppress UV-induced **COX-2** (→ PGE2), **iNOS**, and **NF-κB** signaling. [5][1]
4. **ECM-protective (indirect anti-aging).** By blocking the UV insult upstream (and via direct collagenase/elastase inhibition), MAAs limit UV-driven **MMP** induction and preserve procollagen/elastin in cell models. [4][5][1]
5. **Anti-glycation.** Mycosporine-2-glycine inhibits formation of advanced glycation end-products (AGEs), with reported IC50s ~75–90 µM, comparable to rutin. [11]
6. **DNA protection.** By screening UV before it reaches DNA, MAAs reduce CPD/thymine-dimer formation (shown in microbial/yeast models; inferred for skin). [1]

**Honest mechanistic translation:** #1 is rock-solid *physics* and the real reason to care about MAAs. #2–#6 are real *in cells/biochemical assays* but are largely **downstream of, or dependent on, the UV-screening function** and have **not been demonstrated for a topical cosmetic dose in living human skin**.

## 4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

> **The single most important honesty point:** MAAs have **excellent benchtop photophysics** and a **deep in-vitro/cell-culture file**, but the **human topical** evidence is **two small, largely uncontrolled studies on one liposomal product (Helioguard 365)** — no rigorous, independent, placebo-controlled RCT on a defined MAA dose exists. And critically, despite "natural sunscreen" marketing, MAAs are **not approved UV filters** anywhere in the West, so the strongest application (sun protection) is the one you legally cannot sell.

### Tier 1 — In vitro (cell & biochemical models) — the bulk of the evidence

- **Photophysics / mechanism — Whittock et al., 2020, *Frontiers in Chemistry*.** Transient-absorption spectroscopy of a mycosporine motif: >75% non-radiative ground-state recovery within ~1.8 ns; only 2.0% (ethanol) / 6.5% (DMSO) photodegradation after 2 h solar simulation. *Proves heat-dissipation + photostability — at the molecular level, not in skin.* [10]
- **Porphyra-334 vs UVA photoaging — Ryu et al., 2014, *Int. J. Molecular Medicine* 34:796–803.** Human dermal fibroblasts (CCD-986sk), 10 J/cm² UVA, P-334 10–40 µM: dose-dependent ↓ROS; **40 µM ↓MMP-1 mRNA by 56.2%**; procollagen rose from 217 → ~254–272 ng/mL; **elastase activity ↓82.5%.** *In vitro; single cell line; culture doses.* [4]
- **Anti-inflammation / anti-aging genes — Suh et al., 2014, *Marine Drugs* 12:5174.** HaCaT keratinocytes + MAAs from *Chlamydomonas hedleyi* (0.03–0.3 mM). Mycosporine-glycine 0.3 mM ↓UV-induced **COX-2** to control; shinorine effective only at 0.03 mM; **porphyra-334 had no COX-2 effect**; all three restored UV-suppressed **elastin & PCOLCE**; only mycosporine-glycine had real antioxidant capacity (~15% of total scavenging). *In vitro; single line.* [5]
- **Collagenase / elastase inhibition — Hartmann et al. (in Punchakara 2023 review).** Shinorine, porphyra-334, palythine inhibit *Clostridium* collagenase, IC50 104.0 / 105.9 / 158.9 µM (competitive, by docking). *Biochemical assay, not skin collagenase per se.* [11]
- **Anti-glycation — Tarasuntisuk et al., 2018, *Lett. Appl. Microbiol.*** Mycosporine-2-glycine inhibits AGE formation (lysozyme model) more potently than a shinorine/porphyra-334 mix; IC50 ~75–90 µM range, ≈ rutin. *In vitro biochemistry.* [11]
- **Wound healing (cell migration) — Punchakara 2023 review (citing Choi et al.).** Shinorine/porphyra-334 (0.05 mg/mL), mycosporine-glycine (0.1 mg/mL) promoted HaCaT proliferation/migration via FAK/JNK/ERK. *In vitro scratch assay.* [11]
- **Antioxidant / lipid-peroxidation — de la Coba et al., 2009, *J. Appl. Phycology* 21:161.** MAAs from red macroalgae/lichen reduce lipid peroxidation in vitro; mycosporine-glycine ~8× ascorbic acid at pH 8.5. *Aqueous-phase chemistry; not skin.* [11]
- **Commercial-actives, cell level — Helioguard 365 / Helionori.** Helioguard 365 (porphyra-334 + shinorine in liposomes) at 0.25–2% raised UVA-irradiated HaCaT viability to ~97.8% and cut DNA damage dose-dependently; Helionori 2% raised viability up to +57% (keratinocytes) / +135% (fibroblasts) after UVA. *In vitro; vendor-aligned data.* [1][9]

### Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

- **Mouse photoaging — Ying et al., 2019, *Marine Drugs* (P. yezoensis MAAs).** ICR mice, **topical** MAAs 120 µL/day at 5/10/20 mg/mL, chronic UVA 20.81 + UVB 0.47 J/cm² over ~1 month. Highest dose: ↑SOD +37.9%, ↑GSH-Px +34.5%, ↑CAT +22.2%, ↓MDA −57.1%; ↓NF-κB −20.9%, ↓IL-6 −40.9%, ↓IL-1β −28.3%; thinner stratum corneum, better-organized collagen. *Mouse, not human; the best in-vivo topical-skin dataset that exists, and authors explicitly say human trials are still needed.* [8]
- **Mouse ECM protection — Rui et al. (in reviews).** Topical porphyra-334 + shinorine on ICR mice ↓MMP-1, MMP-3, TNF-α; protected collagen/elastin/hydroxyproline. *Mouse.* [1]
- **Dietary nori / HPV-transgenic mice — Marine-derived studies.** Oral *P. umbilicalis* reduced DNA damage and dysplastic skin lesions. *Oral, mouse, disease model — not topical, not human.* [search]

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL

- **Essentially none specific to MAAs as an isolated oral active.** A few *dietary nori/Porphyra* studies exist but conflate MAAs with the whole seaweed and other nutrients. **Do not import any oral data into topical claims.**

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters — and it is very thin)

- **Helioguard 365, in-vivo human arm (Schmid et al., as reported in reviews / Mibelle data).** Two small studies:
  1. **n=10**, **2% Helioguard 365** *boosted SPF of a sunscreen from 7.2 → 8.2* (a modest SPF-boost claim) and ↓lipid peroxidation ~35% after single application.
  2. **n=20 women**, **5% Helioguard 365** twice daily ×4 weeks: skin **firmness +~10–12%**, **smoothness improved**, **wrinkle depth −~20%.**
  *Critical caveats: small, **no/weak placebo control reported**, **vendor-originated**, single product (a liposomal extract — benefit may owe to liposomes/lecithin/extract, not MAAs per se), short duration, women-only. This is the entire human topical file. It does **not** constitute independent RCT-grade proof.* [6][1]
- **Helionori, human-level data:** the published support is **in-vitro fibroblast/keratinocyte** UVA-protection; no rigorous human RCT located. [9]
- **No independent, placebo-controlled human RCT on a defined MAA concentration** was found in the literature search. [all searches]

**Bottom line by tier:** Proven → MAAs absorb UVA/UVB and dissipate it as heat without free radicals, and are photostable (physics, Tier 1) [10]. Strong in-vitro → ↓ROS, ↓MMP-1, ↓COX-2, anti-glycation, collagen protection [4][5][11]. One decent **mouse** topical photoaging study [8]. Human topical → **two small, largely uncontrolled, vendor-aligned studies on one liposomal product** [6]. **No standalone-sunscreen approval, no independent human RCT.**

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **The core delivery problem:** MAAs are **small but highly hydrophilic/zwitterionic**, so they don't readily cross the lipophilic stratum corneum on their own. This is *acceptable* if you only want them sitting on/near the surface as a UV-absorbing film — but **limits** their ability to act as an intracellular antioxidant/anti-aging agent in viable epidermis/dermis.
- **Why the market leader is liposomal:** **Helioguard 365 encapsulates MAAs in liposomes specifically to improve skin uptake** — i.e., the industry already concedes that free MAA penetration is poor and engineered delivery is needed. Other delivery work (chitosan–MAA conjugates, ZnO–shinorine conjugates, nanoemulsions) exists in research. [6][1]
- **No quantitative human penetration data** (how much reaches viable epidermis/dermis, intracellular concentrations) was located. Treat penetration as *engineered-but-unquantified*.
- **Practical read:** For a **surface UV-screen / antioxidant film + SPF boost**, penetration isn't required and MAAs are well-suited. For **"delivers antioxidants/collagen support deep in the dermis," penetration is unproven** and you'd lean on a liposomal/encapsulated grade.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:** No validated human efficacy dose for raw MAAs. Practically:
  - Commercial extracts used at **~1–5%** of finished formula (Helioguard 365 human studies used 2% and 5%; in-vitro actives 0.25–2%). Actual MAA content within these extracts is **fractional** (often ~0.1% MAAs in the liposome dispersion). [6]
  - Patents span a wide **0.001–15%** of formulations. [12]
  - **Do not imply a validated dose-response** — there isn't one in humans.
- **pH:** MAAs are **highly stable at room temperature across pH ~4.5–8.5** — i.e., compatible with most cosmetic pH windows (acids, niacinamide, etc.). Instability appears only at **high pH + high heat** (e.g., pH 10.5 at 50 °C; degradation at 85 °C). [1]
- **Stability:** Excellent thermal stability (stable to ~60 °C; some sources to higher), good photostability (the whole point), and Helioguard 365 is rated stable 3 months at 4–37 °C. Watch: mycosporine-glutamine-type members can photolyze in the presence of flavins + air (formulation-specific; the cosmetic imine MAAs are robust). [1][10]
- **Pairings (conceptual):**
  - **Approved UV filters** — the *honest* use: an SPF/UVA **booster** alongside real filters (it raised SPF 7.2→8.2 in the human study). [6]
  - **Niacinamide, ceramides** — barrier/longevity story; pH-compatible.
  - **Vitamin C / E** — antioxidant network; MAAs cover UVA wavelengths C/E don't filter.
  - **"Blue light / digital aging" actives** — MAAs absorb into near-visible/long-UVA; a credible "environmental defense" pairing (mechanistically defensible for UVA; blue-light claims are softer).
- **Conflicts:** Few. Avoid strongly alkaline + hot processing. Water-soluble, so they live in the aqueous phase — easy to formulate into serums, essences, lotions.
- **Sensory:** Colorless, water-soluble, odor-neutral at use levels (Helionori is an aqueous extract; Helioguard is a liposome dispersion) — sensory-friendly. Algae extracts can carry faint marine notes; standardized actives are largely neutral.

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation/sensitization:** No specific human irritation or sensitization signal at cosmetic levels; MAAs are colorless, water-soluble, naturally present in foods (nori). Human studies (n=10, n=20) reported no adverse events. Algal-extract studies showed **no cytotoxicity in human cell lines** (some murine-cell cytotoxicity tied to immunomodulation — not a human-skin flag). Still: **no published HRIPT/patch-test data on a finished MAA product** was found — generate our own. [1][6]
- **Sun safety (a real claims trap, not a toxicity issue):** MAAs are *not* approved sunscreens. The danger is **consumer over-reliance** — implying a MAA serum "protects from the sun" could lead users to skip real SPF. Copy must never position MAAs as a sunscreen substitute.
- **Pregnancy/breastfeeding:** No specific topical pregnancy data; MAAs are dietary (seaweed) and topically benign in principle. Default to conservative "consult your doctor" copy; no known mechanism of concern.
- **Sourcing/allergen:** Marine/algae source → relevant for **seaweed/iodine-aware** and vegan-positioning customers (algae-derived = plausibly vegan if no animal co-processing). Wild-harvest variability and heavy-metal/iodine contamination are real QC concerns for algae extracts — require COA (MAA content + heavy metals). Fermentation-derived MAAs (E. coli / yeast / *Yarrowia*, see §9) sidestep wild-harvest and contamination issues. [§9]

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **THE central regulatory fact:** Shinorine, porphyra-334, and other MAAs are **NOT on the EU approved UV-filter list (Regulation (EC) 1223/2009, Annex VI)** and are **NOT FDA-approved sunscreen actives** (not in the US OTC sunscreen monograph). Japan/some Asian markets allow limited cosmetic use. **Therefore, in the EU/US you may sell them as cosmetic ingredients (antioxidant / skin-conditioning / extract) but you may NOT label or market a product as a "sunscreen," state an SPF, or claim "UV protection / protects against sun" — that would make it an unapproved drug/sunscreen.** [reg searches, 1]
- **Cosmetic ingredient status:** Used legally as **algae extracts** (e.g., *Porphyra Umbilicalis Extract*) with antioxidant/skin-conditioning functions; this is how Helioguard/Helionori are sold.
- **The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame):** "Sunscreen," "SPF," "blocks/filters UV," "prevents sunburn," "prevents skin cancer," "repairs photodamage," "boosts collagen by X%" = **drug/sunscreen territory**. Importing the *photoprotection* literature into product claims is the trap.
- ✅ **Safer cosmetic language:** "inspired by the UV-defense molecules algae make to survive intense sun," "antioxidant support against environmental and UVA-range stress," "helps defend the look of skin against environmental aging," "supports skin's resilience," "a marine-derived antioxidant." (Even "environmental/UVA defense" should be hedged and never imply it replaces SPF.)
- ⚠️ **Risky / substantiation-or-legal-review:** "natural sunscreen," "SPF from algae," "protects you from the sun," "blocks UV rays," "prevents sun damage/photoaging," "reduces wrinkles by 20%" (the n=20 vendor figure is not adequate substantiation), "boosts collagen."
- **Substantiation reality:** To claim *anything* performance-related on a finished formula we'd want **our own human testing**; for any sun/SPF wording we'd need it formulated/registered as a sunscreen with **approved filters** (MAAs could ride along as a booster but cannot carry the claim). See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Forms/suppliers:**
  - **Standardized algae extracts** — Helioguard 365 (Mibelle), Helionori (Gelyma); both from *Porphyra umbilicalis*. These are the realistic cosmetic-grade routes today.
  - **Purified single MAAs** (shinorine, porphyra-334) — currently **research/reference-chemical** grade (e.g., Biosynth), expensive, not yet scaled for cosmetics.
  - **Fermentation/biotech** — heterologous production in *E. coli*, *S. cerevisiae*, and *Yarrowia lipolytica* is advancing (and is the likely future of cost-effective, sustainable, contaminant-free MAAs). Several groups/patents (e.g., WO2019094447). [§search]
- **Cost tier:** **High** today. Wild-harvest extraction has **low yield and purity** (the de la Coba authors note making a high-SPF sunscreen with P-334 as sole filter "is not easy" due to ~40–50% purification yields), which keeps purified MAAs pricey; standardized extracts are mid-to-premium. Fermentation should lower cost over time but isn't mainstream yet. [2][9]
- **Supply/standardization risk:** Algae extracts vary by species, season, and harvest; **MAA content must be quantified on COA**. Sustainability + heavy-metal/iodine QC matter. Fermentation-derived grades reduce these risks and strengthen a "clean biotech" story.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

- **"Natural / algae sunscreen."** The most common marketing line and the most legally dangerous. MAAs **absorb UV beautifully in a cuvette**, but they are **not approved UV filters** in the EU/US and **cannot be sold or claimed as sunscreen**. Marketing them as sun protection risks both regulatory action and **real consumer harm** (skipping actual SPF).
- **The cell-culture-to-skin leap.** Most "MAAs reduce MMP-1 / boost collagen / scavenge ROS" claims are **in-vitro at culture concentrations**, often on a single cell line — not demonstrated for a topical dose in living human skin.
- **"Clinically proven to reduce wrinkles 20%."** That number is from a **small (n=20), vendor-aligned, weakly-controlled** Helioguard 365 study — and the active was a **liposomal extract**, so credit can't be cleanly assigned to MAAs. Not RCT-grade.
- **One molecule ≠ all MAAs.** Antioxidant power lives mostly in **mycosporine-glycine**; the famous **porphyra-334/shinorine** are weak *in the DPPH assay* and their real strength is UV-screening (their direct antioxidancy is real but assay-dependent — see §2). Marketing often blurs 30+ different molecules into one super-ingredient.
- **Penetration glossed over.** "Delivers deep antioxidant defense" ignores that free MAAs penetrate poorly — which is exactly why the leading product is **liposomal**.
- **Blue-light claims.** MAAs trail into long-UVA / near-visible, so a *UVA* defense story is defensible; sweeping **"blue light protection"** claims outrun the data.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ **CONDITIONAL → SUPPORTING.** A scientifically elegant, story-rich **antioxidant / environmental-defense supporting active** — *not* a hero, *not* a sunscreen, and not (yet) a proof-backed anti-wrinkle claim ingredient.

**One-line:** MAAs are "the sunscreen algae invented" — gorgeous narrative, genuinely excellent UV photophysics, solid in-vitro/animal anti-photoaging data — but human topical proof is two small vendor studies, they're **not legal as sunscreen filters**, free-MAA penetration is poor (hence liposomal grades), and they're costly. Use as a **science-forward marine antioxidant adjunct** with strictly honest, **never-a-sunscreen** copy.

**When MAAs make sense for us:**
- As an **antioxidant / UVA-range "environmental defense"** layer in a **day serum/essence or after-sun**, paired with (not instead of) real SPF and a vitamin C/E network.
- As a **narrative differentiator** for a marine/longevity-leaning brand identity (algae surviving brutal UV → "skin resilience"). Note: brand story is OPEN per CLAUDE.md — this is an *option*, not an assumed direction.
- Realistically via a **standardized, ideally liposomal or fermentation-derived** grade (Helioguard 365-type) with a COA, not raw purified MAAs.

**When to skip:**
- If the role we need is **actual sun protection** — use approved UV filters; MAAs can't carry that claim or that job.
- If budget is tight and the ingredient must *earn* hero billing on human proof — the human RCT evidence isn't there.

**Longevity-relevance note:** UV is the **#1 evidence-based driver of skin aging**, so an ingredient class whose entire identity is *photoprotection + antioxidant defense* is thematically perfect for a skin-**longevity** brand. The catch is purely translational/regulatory: the proven part (UV physics) is the part we legally can't claim, and the part we could claim (topical anti-aging in humans) isn't proven yet. MAAs are an exceptional **education/differentiation** ingredient and a credible **supporting antioxidant** — and a strong candidate for **our own finished-formula human testing** if we want to make real (non-sunscreen) defense/resilience claims.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Verified:** 2026-06-19 (independent re-check of the load-bearing citations against primary/PMC sources).

### Overall grade: **C (with one A-grade island)**

The MAA evidence base is **bimodal**: the *photophysics* (UV-absorb-and-dump-as-heat, photostability) is **A-grade benchtop science**, but it is **not human, not topical-on-skin, and — critically — the part you legally cannot claim** (MAAs are not approved UV filters). Everything that *would* be a sellable cosmetic claim (topical anti-aging, antioxidant defense, wrinkle reduction in living human skin) rests on **in-vitro cell work + one mouse study + two small, vendor-aligned, weakly-controlled human studies on a single liposomal product**. There is **no independent placebo-controlled human topical RCT on a defined MAA dose.** Net: **C — weak/indirect for human-topical anti-aging claims**, despite genuinely excellent mechanism. This dossier was already unusually honest about this; verification confirms its self-assessment rather than overturning it.
