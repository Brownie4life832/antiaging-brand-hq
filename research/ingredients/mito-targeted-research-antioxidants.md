# Ingredient: Mito-targeted research antioxidants — MitoTEMPO, MitoVitE, SkQR1 (INCI: none harmonised for cosmetic use; research-grade reagents)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ⬜ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ✅ rejected / ⬜ in a product
**Class:** Autophagy & mitochondrial-targeted actives (mitochondria-targeted antioxidants; TPP⁺-conjugated and Skulachev-ion families) · **Slug:** `mito-targeted-research-antioxidants`

> **TL;DR for a busy reader.** This dossier covers the *research-reagent tier* of mitochondria-targeted antioxidants — the molecules you find in PubMed and a Sigma-Aldrich catalogue, not on a serum INCI list: **MitoTEMPO / MitoTEMPOL** (a TEMPO/TEMPOL nitroxide — an SOD-mimetic — bolted to triphenylphosphonium, TPP⁺), **MitoVitE** (the vitamin-E/Trolox antioxidant head bolted to TPP⁺; the *original* targeted antioxidant, Smith & Murphy 1999), and **SkQR1** (a plastoquinone–rhodamine-19 "Skulachev ion," the fluorescent cousin of SkQ1). They share one elegant idea: attach an antioxidant to a lipophilic cation so the mitochondrial membrane potential sucks it into the matrix at **100–1000×** the cytosolic concentration (Nernst: ~10× per 61.5 mV), delivering it exactly where age-related ROS is generated. The biology is real and the foundational chemistry is excellent. **But as *skincare actives* these are essentially non-starters today.** The evidence ladder for each is: strong in-vitro → solid animal (almost all **systemic — IP/IV/oral/drinking-water — injection or feeding, never a cream**) → **no human oral skin data** → **zero human topical-skin RCTs.** The only human-skin-*cell* work in the whole class used **MitoQ** (a different, commercialised cousin), not these three. Worse, every one of these is **biphasic**: an antioxidant at low (sub-µM) dose and a documented **pro-oxidant** that depolarises mitochondria and fragments mtDNA at higher dose (e.g. MitoTEMPOL/MitoQ at ~2 µM in Pokrzywinski 2016). They have **no cosmetic INCI, no skin-penetration data, no cosmetic-grade supply chain, and trade names that collide with approved/abandoned drugs** (MitoQ skincare exists commercially; SkQ1/Visomitin is a drug). For an evidence-led brand these are **fascinating mechanism, useless as named hero actives, and a claims/supply minefield.** SKIP — track as a science watch-item. See Verdict (§11). Sister dossiers: [`skq1-visomitin.md`](skq1-visomitin.md), [`coenzyme-q10.md`](coenzyme-q10.md), [`idebenone.md`](idebenone.md).

## 1. Identity

These are **three distinct molecules grouped by design philosophy** (mitochondrial targeting via a lipophilic cation), not a single ingredient. None is a standard cosmetic raw material.

### 1a. MitoTEMPO / MitoTEMPOL
- **Common names:** MitoTEMPO; MitoTEMPOL (the two are closely related and often conflated — see note). "Mito-TEMPO."
- **Chemistry:** A **piperidine nitroxide** antioxidant (TEMPO / TEMPOL = 4-hydroxy-2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidine-1-oxyl) conjugated via a linker to a **triphenylphosphonium (TPP⁺)** lipophilic cation. MitoTEMPO is typically supplied as the chloride/mesylate salt. [1][2]
  - *Nitroxide vs hydroxylamine:* inside mitochondria the nitroxide (MitoTEMPOL) is rapidly **reduced by ubiquinol to its hydroxylamine (MitoTEMPOL-H)**, and Trnka et al. (2008) showed the **hydroxylamine is the more effective lipid-peroxidation chain-breaker** — i.e. the active species in cells is partly the reduced form. [2]
- **CAS:** commonly cited as 1334850-99-5 (MitoTEMPO). Research-reagent grade (Sigma-Aldrich/MedChemExpress/Cayman).
- **INCI:** **none.** Not a registered cosmetic ingredient.

### 1b. MitoVitE (mitochondria-targeted vitamin E)
- **Common names:** MitoVitE; MitoVit-E; "Mito Vitamin E"; chemically **TPPB**.
- **Chemistry:** the **chromanol antioxidant head of vitamin E** (the Trolox / α-tocopherol moiety) linked by a short alkyl chain to **TPP⁺**: 2-[2-(triphenylphosphonio)ethyl]-3,4-dihydro-2,5,7,8-tetramethyl-2H-1-benzopyran-6-ol bromide. [3]
- **Historical importance:** this is the **first mitochondria-targeted antioxidant ever made** — Smith, Porteous, Coulter & Murphy, *Eur J Biochem* 1999. It is the proof-of-concept that begat the entire field (MitoQ, MitoTEMPO, SkQ, etc.). [3]
- **CAS / INCI:** research reagent; **no cosmetic INCI.**

### 1c. SkQR1
- **Common names:** SkQR1 ("Skulachev ion, Quinone, Rhodamine"); the rhodamine analogue of SkQ1.
- **Chemistry:** a **plastoquinone** antioxidant head joined by a **decyl (C10) linker** to a **rhodamine-19** cation (instead of SkQ1's TPP⁺). The rhodamine is **fluorescent**, so SkQR1 doubles as a *visualisation tool* for uptake — one of its main lab uses. [4][5]
- **Developer:** the Skulachev group (Moscow State University) / Mitotech — same stable as SkQ1/Visomitin. Patented. [4]
- **CAS / INCI:** research reagent; **no cosmetic INCI.**

**Honest framing:** the brief groups these as "research antioxidants" — that label is exactly right. **MitoTEMPO, MitoVitE and SkQR1 are pharmacology *tools*, not cosmetic ingredients.** They have no cosmetic identity, and the closest thing to "topical relevance" in the family belongs to MitoQ and SkQ1, which have their own dossiers.

## 2. Class & mechanism

The unifying mechanism is **two-part by design** (Murphy & Smith, *Annu Rev Pharmacol Toxicol* 2007 — the canonical review): [7]

1. **Targeting (the lipophilic cation: TPP⁺ or rhodamine-19).** Lipophilic cations cross phospholipid bilayers without a transporter and are driven down the electrical gradient into the highly negative mitochondrial matrix. By the **Nernst equation, every 61.5 mV of membrane potential gives a ~10-fold concentration step**, so across a typical inner-membrane potential the antioxidant accumulates **100–1000× higher inside mitochondria than in the cytosol** — concentrating the active exactly where the bulk of age-related ROS is produced (the electron-transport chain). [3][7]
2. **The antioxidant head does the chemistry:**
   - **MitoTEMPO/-L** — the nitroxide acts as a **superoxide dismutase (SOD) mimetic**, catalytically dismutating superoxide (O₂•⁻ → H₂O₂/O₂), then the H₂O₂ is cleared by catalase/glutathione peroxidase. It also **chain-breaks lipid peroxidation** (mainly as its hydroxylamine) and can blunt Fenton chemistry. Reaction with superoxide is **catalytic** (regenerated, not consumed). [1][2]
   - **MitoVitE** — the vitamin-E chromanol is a classic **chain-breaking lipid antioxidant**, but delivered ~80× more concentrated to the membrane than native vitamin E, so it protects mitochondrial membranes "far more effectively than vitamin E itself." [3]
   - **SkQR1** — like SkQ1, the **plastoquinol** form chain-breaks lipid peroxidation and protects **cardiolipin** (the signature inner-membrane lipid) from peroxidation; "rechargeable" by the respiratory chain. [4][5]
3. **Downstream signalling.** By lowering mitochondrial ROS (mtROS), these compounds dampen ROS-driven programmes implicated in aging: NF-κB inflammation, the mitochondrial-ROS→senescence loop, apoptosis, and (in some models) they modulate **mitophagy / mitochondrial biogenesis** (PGC-1α/NRF1/TFAM) — the "autophagy & mitochondrial" rationale for the class. [9][12]

- ⚠️ **The pro-oxidant flip side is intrinsic, not incidental.** Every redox-active mito-targeted antioxidant is **biphasic**: antioxidant at low concentration, **pro-oxidant at high concentration**, *and* the very accumulation that makes them potent also drives **membrane depolarisation** when over-loaded. Pokrzywinski et al. (2016) tested three TPP⁺ mito-antioxidants at **~2 µM** in cancer cells (MDA-MB-231 breast, H23 lung): MitoQ and the mito-vitamin-E analogue **mitochromanol-acetate (MitoCA) raised superoxide ~2.2–4.8-fold and caused rapid (<1 h) mitochondrial depolarisation**, with **decreased mtDNA integrity**; **MitoTEMPOL ("MitoT") was the mildest** — no early superoxide rise in breast cells and only a modest, later rise in lung cells. So the pro-oxidant/depolarising ceiling is real for the class at low-µM doses, though MitoTEMPOL itself showed the weakest pro-oxidant signal of the three. This parallels the biphasic ceiling flagged for SkQ1 (antioxidant ~25 nM, pro-oxidant ~800 nM). **Concentration is a safety variable, not just an efficacy dial.** [10][14]

## 4. Evidence review by tier — the core of this dossier

> Read this as a ladder. For these three reagents the rungs are: **(1) strong, foundational in-vitro chemistry → (2) substantial animal data, almost entirely delivered SYSTEMICALLY (injection/feeding), in non-skin organs → (3) NO human oral skin data → (4) ZERO human topical-skin RCTs.** The only human-skin-*cell* evidence in the entire mito-targeted class used **MitoQ** (a commercialised cousin), not MitoTEMPO/MitoVitE/SkQR1. Be explicit: almost everything attractive here is **borrowed from a different molecule (MitoQ), a different route (systemic), or a different tissue (heart/kidney/brain).**

### Tier 1 — In-vitro / biochemical (genuinely strong; this is where the class lives)
- **Smith, Porteous, Coulter & Murphy (1999). "Selective targeting of an antioxidant to mitochondria."** *Eur J Biochem* 263(3):709–716. **The founding paper.** MitoVitE (TPPB) accumulated several-hundred-fold in isolated mitochondria driven by membrane potential, reaching **~80× endogenous vitamin-E levels** and protecting mitochondria from oxidative damage "far more effectively than vitamin E itself." Isolated mitochondria / cells — proof of concept, not skin. [3]
- **Trnka, Blaikie, Smith & Murphy (2008). "Antioxidant properties of MitoTEMPOL and its hydroxylamine."** *Free Radic Res* 43(1):4–12. Characterised MitoTEMPOL as a **catalytic SOD-mimetic** (regenerated over a 30-min reaction; superoxide reactivity comparable to untargeted TEMPOL), rapidly reduced in cells to **MitoTEMPOL-H**, which is the **superior lipid-peroxidation inhibitor.** Biochemical/in-vitro. [2]
- **Murphy & Smith (2007). "Targeting antioxidants to mitochondria by conjugation to lipophilic cations."** *Annu Rev Pharmacol Toxicol* 47:629–656. The canonical mechanism review (Nernst targeting, 100–1000× accumulation; MitoQ/MitoVitE/MitoPBN/MitoPeroxidase family). [7]
- **Plotnikov et al. (2010). "New-generation Skulachev ions exhibiting nephroprotective and neuroprotective properties."** *Biochem (Mosc)* 75(2):145–150. SkQR1 chemistry (plastoquinone–decyl–rhodamine-19) and antioxidant characterisation; uptake driven by membrane potential. [4]
- **Fernandes et al. (2022). "Targeting Hydroxybenzoic Acids to Mitochondria as a Strategy to Delay Skin Ageing: An In Vitro Approach."** *Molecules* 27(19):6183. **The single most skin-relevant primary paper in this whole space.** Tested four TPP⁺-conjugated hydroxybenzoic-acid reagents (MitoBEN MB1–MB4) **with MitoQ and SkQ1 as comparators**, in **primary normal human dermal fibroblasts (NHDF)** and a **3D EpidermFT reconstructed-skin model.** Endpoints: senescence/ECM transcripts (MMP1/3/9, TIMP1, COL1A1, COL3A1, elastin), mitochondrial biogenesis (TFAM, NRF1, PPARGC1A), H₂O₂-protection, inflammation (TNF, IL-6, IL-8, HIF1A). Best reagent (MB2, pyrogallol, C6 spacer) reduced senescence markers (notably MMP3) and outperformed MitoQ/SkQ1 on a safety index. ⚠️ **Crucially: in-vitro only, transcript-level, no protein validation, no animal/human, and the protagonists are novel MitoBEN reagents — not MitoTEMPO/MitoVitE/SkQR1.** It validates the *strategy* in skin cells, not these specific molecules. [9]
- **Oyewole & Birch-Machin (2014). "Comparing the effects of mitochondrial targeted and localized antioxidants with cellular antioxidants in human skin cells exposed to UVA and hydrogen peroxide."** *FASEB J* 28(1):485–494 (Newcastle). **MitoQ** (mito-targeted) vs **tiron** (mito-localised) vs cellular antioxidants in **human dermal fibroblasts/keratinocytes** under **UVA + H₂O₂**; mito-targeted antioxidants better protected mtDNA/mitochondrial function. The closest "human skin cell" UVA-protection data in the class — **but it is MitoQ, in vitro, not these three.** [11]
- **Comparative MitoTEMPO vs SkQ1 (2024).** *Free Radic Biol Med* (S0891-5849(24)00609-9): head-to-head efficiency of MitoTEMPO and SkQ1 under oxidative stress, reinforcing the **biphasic/dose-dependent** behaviour and that effectiveness is concentration-window-bound. [14]

### Tier 2 — Animal / ex-vivo (substantial — but note the route and the organ: NOT skin, NOT topical)
- **MitoVitE — Mao et al. (2010). "Effect of a mitochondria-targeted vitamin E derivative on mitochondrial alteration and systemic oxidative stress in mice."** *Br J Nutr.* Reduced systemic oxidative stress; **oral/dietary**, not skin. [13]
- **MitoVitE — hepatic / metabolic.** "A mitochondria-targeted vitamin E derivative decreases hepatic oxidative stress and inhibits fat deposition in mice" (*J Nutr*). Lower liver lipid and adiposity. **Systemic feeding, liver/fat — not skin.** [3-related]
- **MitoVitE — neuro (mixed/negative & a toxicity signal).** In a neonatal-rat hypoxic-ischaemic striatum model, infused MitoVitE was **not neuroprotective and was neurotoxic at higher doses** — an important reminder the class can harm. (*Exp Neurol* 2006.) [3-related]
- **MitoVitE — chemo-neuropathy.** "MitoVitE limits paclitaxel-induced oxidative stress and mechanical hypersensitivity in a rat pain model" (*Br J Anaesth* 2017) — efficacy comparable to duloxetine. **Systemic, not skin.** [3-related]
- **MitoTEMPO — broad organ-protection corpus (all systemic).** Hundreds of papers: cardiac dysfunction/hypertension (Dikalova et al., *Circ Res* 2010, the canonical MitoTEMPO in-vivo paper), sepsis, diabetic complications, ischaemia-reperfusion, lung/kidney injury — typically **IP injection, mg/kg.** None topical, none skin-aging endpoints. [1]
- **MitoTEMPO — senescence (cells/animals, not skin).** "MitoTEMPO alleviates senescence of bone-marrow mesenchymal stem cells in ovariectomized rats" (*J Cell Physiol* 2024): reversed premature senescence by restoring lysosomal function and suppressing the mitochondrial unfolded-protein response. **Bone-marrow stem cells / osteoporosis model — not skin, not topical.** [12]
- **SkQR1 — nephro/neuroprotection (systemic injection).** Plotnikov et al. (2010, 2012): **single intraperitoneal injection** of SkQR1 reduced infarct size in focal brain ischaemia, protected kidney in ischaemia-reperfusion, myoglobinuria and pyelonephritis, and showed a kidney→brain "remote preconditioning" cross-talk (↑urinary erythropoietin). **All IP-injected, kidney/brain — no skin, no topical.** [4][5]

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL / SYSTEMIC
- **None for skin, for any of these three.** MitoVitE, MitoTEMPO and SkQR1 have **no human trials reporting skin endpoints** (oral or otherwise). (For context, the *cousin* MitoQ has two human Phase II systemic trials — Parkinson's and hepatitis C — neither with skin outcomes; and a small oral-MitoQ vascular-aging study. None of that transfers to these three or to topical skin.) [6][8]

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the format we actually care about)
- **MitoTEMPO: zero.** No peer-reviewed human topical-skin study of any kind.
- **MitoVitE: zero.** No peer-reviewed human topical-skin study.
- **SkQR1: zero.** No peer-reviewed human topical-skin study.
- **For the class overall:** the only marketed topical is **MitoQ skincare** (MitoQ Ltd, NZ), whose anti-aging skin claims rest on **in-vitro/mechanistic data + manufacturer assertion**, not published human topical-skin RCTs; and **SkQ1/MitoVitan** (Mitotech), also without published skin RCTs (see [`skq1-visomitin.md`]). **None of the three molecules in *this* dossier has any human topical-skin efficacy evidence whatsoever.** [6][8]

**Bottom line by tier:** foundational, high-quality in-vitro chemistry (Tier 1) → a large but **systemic, non-skin** animal corpus (Tier 2) → **nothing human-oral for skin** (Tier 3) → **a complete vacuum at the human-topical-skin rung** (Tier 4). The most skin-relevant data point (Fernandes 2022) validates the *targeting strategy* in skin cells using *different* reagents and only at the transcript level.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **Targeting works *inside* the cell; getting *across the stratum corneum* is a separate, unstudied problem.** The 100–1000× mitochondrial accumulation operates *once the molecule is inside a viable cell*. Whether MitoTEMPO/MitoVitE/SkQR1 cross intact human stratum corneum from a topical vehicle, and reach viable epidermal/dermal cells **within the antioxidant (not pro-oxidant) window**, is **not established in any published study.** No quantitative human skin-permeation data exist for any of the three.
- **They are charged (cationic), amphiphilic molecules.** A permanent positive charge generally **hampers passive percutaneous absorption**; this is the same penetration blind spot flagged for SkQ1.
- **SkQR1's rhodamine is a visible/fluorescent dye** — even if it penetrated, colour/staining and photochemistry would be formulation problems.
- **Implication:** for skin we have **neither a penetration dataset nor an efficacy dataset** — a double blind spot for an evidence-led brand.

## 6. Formulation

- **Active concentration:** governed by the **biphasic window**, which is **sub-µM** (efficacy in cells typically nM–low-µM; pro-oxidant/depolarising effects emerge by ~2 µM in the Pokrzywinski data). These are **trace-dose** molecules, the opposite of a 0.5–2% niacinamide-type active. **More is not better and can flip pro-oxidant.** [10][14]
- **pH / stability:** redox-active (nitroxide ↔ hydroxylamine for MitoTEMPO; quinone redox for SkQR1; chromanol oxidation for MitoVitE). All are **oxidation- and light-sensitive** and would need airless, opaque packaging and antioxidant systems — the same handling as CoQ10/idebenone, but with **no published cosmetic stability data.**
- **Pairings/conflicts:** conceptually complement aqueous antioxidants (vitamin C derivatives) and barrier lipids; **no published compatibility data.** Avoid stacking redox load toward the pro-oxidant ceiling. SkQR1's dye character conflicts with clean/colourless aesthetics.
- **Sensory / colour:** SkQR1 is **coloured (rhodamine)**; MitoTEMPO/MitoVitE are research powders with no cosmetic sensory track record.
- **Reality check:** these are **sold as mg-scale research reagents in DMSO/solvent**, not as cosmetic-grade raw materials with COAs, IFRA/allergen documentation, or scale supply. Formulating with them would be off-label improvisation.

## 7. Safety

- **The defining concern is the intrinsic pro-oxidant/depolarising ceiling.** Pokrzywinski et al. (2016) — at ~2 µM in human cancer lines, **MitoQ and the mito-vitamin-E analogue (mitochromanol-acetate) raised superoxide ~2.2–4.8×, depolarised mitochondria (<1 h) and decreased mtDNA integrity**; **MitoTEMPOL was the mildest of the three** (no early superoxide rise in breast cells, modest later rise in lung). The biphasic ceiling is a class property; MitoTEMPOL's own pro-oxidant signal in this study was the weakest. MitoQ separately causes **mitochondrial swelling/depolarisation in kidney tissue** at higher doses. MitoVitE was **neurotoxic at higher doses** in a neonatal-rat model. So all three can do **net oxidative harm** if over-concentrated or if penetration is uncontrolled — and we lack the skin dose-response to know where the line is. [10][15]
- **No dermal irritation/sensitisation datasets** exist for any of the three. No HRIPT, no patch-test data, no cumulative-irritation data.
- **Sun sensitivity:** none are sunscreens; SkQR1's rhodamine raises a **phototoxicity** question (fluorophores can be photoreactive) that is unstudied for skin.
- **Pregnancy/breastfeeding:** no cosmetic safety data; default caution.
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** fully synthetic; no animal/wildcraft tension. The constraints are **IP, regulatory identity, and supply**, not ethics (see §9).

## 8. Regulatory & claims

- **These are not approved cosmetic ingredients.** Using a **research-grade reagent** with **no INCI** in a cosmetic is a regulatory non-starter in most markets (EU/UK CPNP and US cosmetic-ingredient expectations both assume an identifiable, safety-assessed ingredient). This alone is close to disqualifying.
- **Drug-heritage claims trap (acute here).** The family overlaps with **drugs**: SkQ1 is an *approved* eye-drop (RU) whose US Phase 3 **failed**, and MitoQ is in human drug trials. Borrowing "clinically proven / mitochondrial medicine" language for a cream built on these reagents would imply drug-level substantiation we don't have — squarely the structure/function overreach CLAUDE.md warns against. [6][8]
- ✅ **Defensible cosmetic language (if a compliant version ever existed):** "antioxidant," "helps defend the look of skin against environmental/oxidative stress," "supports the look of skin." Mechanism education must stay **appearance-based**.
- ⚠️ **Risky / would need substantiation or legal review (and we lack the data):** "targets mitochondrial aging," "repairs/recharges your mitochondria," "anti-wrinkle," "boosts cellular energy," "reverses cellular senescence," "1000× more powerful than CoQ10" (that figure refers to *targeting/accumulation in vitro*, not clinical skin efficacy), any longevity-outcome implication.
- See [`../CLAUDE.md`](../../CLAUDE.md) guardrails and [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Research-reagent supply, not cosmetic supply.** MitoTEMPO and MitoVitE are sold in mg quantities by life-science suppliers (Sigma-Aldrich/Merck, Cayman, MedChemExpress) for lab use; **SkQR1 is IP-locked to Mitotech/Skulachev** like SkQ1. **None ships at cosmetic grade with a clean INCI, IFRA/allergen file, and scale COA.**
- **Practical consequences:** no second source, no regulatory identity, no cosmetic stability/penetration data, and (for SkQR1) single-company IP dependence. All are serious supply-chain and formulation-control risks for a brand whose thesis is "buy commodity bases, invest in the brand."
- **Cost:** *material* cost per finished unit would be low (trace dosing), but **regulatory, stability, and (for SkQR1) licensing overhead is high**, and you'd be marketing on an asset you neither control nor can legally call a cosmetic ingredient.

|---|---|

## 11. Verdict for the brand

❌ **SKIP (as hero, supporting, or named active) — science watch-item only.**
**One line:** These are research-grade pharmacology tools with beautiful targeting chemistry, a large but **systemic, non-skin** animal record, a documented **pro-oxidant/depolarising ceiling**, **no cosmetic INCI, no skin-penetration data, and zero human topical-skin evidence** — multiple independent dealbreakers for an evidence-led, claims-disciplined premium brand.

- **Why not even "conditional":** unlike a thin-but-usable commodity (e.g. idebenone), each of these fails on **regulatory identity** (no INCI / research-reagent status), **substantiation** (no human topical-skin data), and **control** (SkQR1 is IP-locked; all lack cosmetic supply/stability/penetration files). Any *one* of those would warrant caution; together they are disqualifying for product use now.
- **Longevity relevance (genuine — hence "watch"):** mechanistically this is a near-perfect fit for the **mitochondrial-decline / oxidative-stress / cellular-senescence / inflammaging** hallmarks and for a "skin-longevity" worldview. The thing to track is the **strategy**, validated in human skin cells by **Fernandes 2022 (MitoBENs/MitoQ/SkQ1 in NHDF + 3D skin)** [9] and **Oyewole & Birch-Machin 2014 (MitoQ in human skin cells under UVA)** [11]. If a **cosmetic-grade, INCI-bearing, penetration-characterised mito-targeted antioxidant with a real human topical RCT** ever appears, revisit immediately.
- **Better-evidenced neighbours for the same story:** for a mitochondrial/antioxidant longevity narrative with more (still imperfect) human topical support, prefer **CoQ10 / idebenone** ([`coenzyme-q10.md`](coenzyme-q10.md), [`idebenone.md`](idebenone.md)), **ergothioneine** ([`ergothioneine.md`](ergothioneine.md)), or **vitamin C/E + ferulic** ([`vitamin-c.md`](vitamin-c.md), [`vitamin-e-tocopherols.md`](vitamin-e-tocopherols.md), [`ferulic-acid.md`](ferulic-acid.md)). ## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Verified:** 2026-06-19 (initial). **Independently re-verified:** 2026-06-19 — Smith 1999 (Eur J Biochem 263:709–716; TPPB/MitoVitE, several-hundred-fold matrix accumulation), Pokrzywinski 2016 (PLoS One e0168283; FDA authors; MDA-MB-231 + H23), Fernandes 2022 (Molecules 27:6183; MitoBEN MB1–4 in NHDF), and Plotnikov 2010 (Biochem Mosc 75:145; SkQR1 = plastoquinone–decyl–rhodamine, single IP injection, brain/kidney) were each re-fetched against PubMed/PMC/journal pages and confirmed real and faithfully represented. The re-check surfaced **one overstatement** (the Pokrzywinski superoxide fold-figure was over-attributed to MitoTEMPOL), now corrected in §2/§7/§10 and noted below. Method: re-checked headline efficacy/mechanism claims against the cited primary literature via web search/fetch, asking for each molecule: is the evidence *human and topical-to-skin*; are the studies real and faithfully represented; what route/tissue; and is there a pro-oxidant ceiling?

### Overall evidence grade (for TOPICAL SKIN use): **D / D-** — essentially unproven on human skin; hype-adjacent if marketed as a skin active.

Grade rationale: the top rung (human topical-**skin** RCT) is **empty for all three** molecules. Unlike SkQ1 (which at least has real, if failed, human *eye-drop* trials) or MitoQ (human *systemic* trials + a marketed cream), MitoTEMPO/MitoVitE/SkQR1 have **no human clinical data of any kind for skin** and remain **research reagents without a cosmetic INCI.** The strongest skin-relevant evidence (Fernandes 2022) uses *different* reagents (MitoBENs) and is in-vitro/transcript-level. By the grading scale (A = strong human-topical RCT; B = moderate human-topical; C = weak/indirect; D = essentially unproven topically), these grade a clear **D** (arguably D- given the added regulatory-identity and pro-oxidant problems). The biology is legitimate; the gap is the missing human-skin format **and** the missing cosmetic-ingredient status.

### Citation accuracy: claims check out as written.
- **MitoVitE = first mito-targeted antioxidant (Smith 1999, Eur J Biochem 263:709–716; ~80× endogenous vitamin E; TPPB).** Confirmed and faithfully represented. [3]
- **MitoTEMPOL chemistry (Trnka 2008): SOD-mimetic, catalytic, reduced to hydroxylamine which is the better lipid-peroxidation inhibitor.** Confirmed. [2]
- **100–1000× accumulation via Nernst (~10×/61.5 mV) — Murphy & Smith 2007.** Confirmed; correctly flagged as a *delivery/in-vitro* metric, not a clinical claim. [7]
- **SkQR1 = plastoquinone–decyl–rhodamine-19; nephro/neuroprotection by single IP injection (Plotnikov 2010/2012).** Confirmed; route (IP) and tissue (kidney/brain) faithful — **no skin/topical.** [4][5]
- **Fernandes 2022 (Molecules 27:6183):** real; MitoBEN reagents + MitoQ/SkQ1 comparators in NHDF + 3D skin; transcript-level; **in-vitro only**; protagonists are *not* MitoTEMPO/MitoVitE/SkQR1. The dossier's hedging is correct and important. [9]
- **Pro-oxidant ceiling (Pokrzywinski 2016, PLoS One, FDA authors; MDA-MB-231 + H23 cancer lines):** Confirmed — the single most important safety fact — but with a **per-molecule correction** applied to the dossier. The ~2.2–4.8× superoxide rise and rapid (<1 h) depolarisation at ~2 µM were driven by **MitoQ and mitochromanol-acetate (the mito-vitamin-E analogue, "MitoCA")**; **MitoTEMPOL ("MitoT") was the mildest** (no early superoxide increase in breast cells, only a modest later rise in lung). The earlier dossier text grouped all three under the 2.2–4.8× figure, which slightly overstated MitoTEMPOL's pro-oxidant signal; §2, §7, §10 have been edited to attribute the strongest effects to MitoQ/MitoCA while keeping the biphasic ceiling as a class property. [10]

### Main caveats (what an adversarial reader should hold onto)
1. **Tissue & route mismatch is total.** Zero human topical-skin efficacy data and zero human topical-skin **penetration** data for all three. The animal record is systemic (IP/oral) and non-skin (heart/kidney/brain/liver). This is decisive.
2. **The skin-cell evidence belongs to the *strategy*, not these molecules.** Fernandes 2022 and Oyewole/Birch-Machin 2014 validate mito-targeting in skin cells using **MitoBENs/MitoQ**, in vitro — not MitoTEMPO/MitoVitE/SkQR1, never in people.
3. **Biphasic pro-oxidant ceiling is intrinsic.** Pro-oxidant/depolarising by ~2 µM; MitoVitE neurotoxic at higher doses; MitoQ depolarises kidney mitochondria at high dose. Concentration is a safety variable and the skin dose-response is unknown.
4. **No cosmetic INCI / research-reagent status** (plus SkQR1 IP lock-in) compounds the evidence problem with a regulatory-identity and supply problem — independently disqualifying for product use.
5. **Single-developer concentration of the science.** A large share of the foundational work (MitoVitE/MitoTEMPO/MitoQ) is the **Murphy–Smith** group; SkQR1/SkQ is the **Skulachev/Mitotech** group. Independent skin-specific replication is essentially absent.

### Claims that were softened / should stay soft
- **"1000× more powerful"** — kept tagged as an in-vitro *accumulation/delivery* figure, not clinical skin efficacy.
- **"Reverses senescence in skin," "targets mitochondrial aging," "anti-wrinkle," "clinically proven," "boosts cellular energy"** — all correctly flagged as mechanistic extrapolation / wrong-molecule / wrong-route / wrong-tissue in §8 and §10. No further softening needed.
- **Watch-item rule:** if these are ever revisited, do **not** cite the systemic animal data, the MitoQ/MitoBEN skin-cell data, or the SkQ1 eye-drop trials as support for a *skin* claim about MitoTEMPO/MitoVitE/SkQR1 — they are different molecules, routes, or tissues.

**Net:** the dossier is accurate, well-sourced, and self-skeptical; the **SKIP** verdict is sound. The biology is genuinely exciting and on-trend for skin-longevity, but as *named cosmetic actives* these three are unusable today on three independent grounds — **no human topical-skin evidence, no cosmetic INCI/penetration data, and an intrinsic pro-oxidant ceiling**. Grade **D for topical-skin evidence** stands.
