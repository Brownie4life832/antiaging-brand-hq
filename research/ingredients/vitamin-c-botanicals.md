# Ingredient: Vitamin-C Botanicals — Kakadu Plum, Amla, Yuzu, Tomato/Lycopene

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product
**Slug:** vitamin-c-botanicals · **Class:** Botanicals & plant bioactives

> **Reading note.** This brief covers four very different "vitamin-C botanicals" that the market groups together under a single halo — *natural, plant-sourced vitamin C*. They are **not interchangeable**, and the phrase is doing a lot of marketing work that the chemistry does not support. Three (Kakadu plum, Amla, Yuzu) are **fruit/peel extracts whose ascorbic acid is real but unstable, low-dose, and rarely the active that's actually doing the work**; their better-evidenced actives are *polyphenols* (ellagic/gallic acid, hesperidin), not vitamin C. The fourth (Tomato/Lycopene) **contains essentially no vitamin C of note** — its hero molecule is the carotenoid lycopene, and its strongest human evidence is **oral, not topical**. This file keeps a hard line between (a) what the *whole-fruit nutrition* literature shows, (b) what *cell/animal* work shows, and (c) what is actually proven for *human topical cosmetic use* — which, across all four, is thin.

---

## 0. The honest punchline (read this first)

- **"Botanical vitamin C" is mostly a story, not a delivery system.** Topical ascorbic acid only works when it is at **≥8–10% and pH <3.5** (Pinnell 2001 [12]; Correia 2023 [13]). Botanical fruit extracts deliver ascorbic acid at a *tiny fraction* of that, often partly oxidized to inactive dehydroascorbic acid before it even reaches the bottle. So the real-world contribution of the "vitamin C" in these extracts to a finished formula is **negligible as a vitamin-C actor**.
- **The actives that *do* have data are the polyphenols, not the vitamin C.** Kakadu plum's standout is **ellagic acid** (~23–37 mg/g dry weight) and a Nrf2-induction story; Amla's are **gallic/ellagic acid + emblicanins**; Yuzu's are **hesperidin and limonoids (nomilin)**. These have antioxidant, anti-MMP, and tyrosinase-inhibitory data — almost all **in-vitro or animal**, not human topical RCT.
- **Tomato/lycopene is the outlier in both directions.** It is the *best human evidence* of the four (multiple oral RCTs showing reduced UV erythema, less UV-induced MMP-1, preserved procollagen — Rizwan 2011 [14]; Grether-Beck 2017 [15]; Stahl 2001 [16]) — but that evidence is **dietary/oral**, and there is **no comparable topical lycopene RCT** for visible anti-aging. Lycopene topically is also a deep-red, oxidation-prone, staining pigment that is hard to formulate.
- **There are real non-efficacy reasons to care about these:** Kakadu plum carries genuine **ethical-sourcing / Indigenous benefit-sharing** weight (it is an Aboriginal Australian traditional food with a documented biopiracy history) and contains **oxalic acid** (an oral concern, not topical); Yuzu, as a citrus, raises **phototoxicity (furocoumarin)** questions if peel oil is used.
- **Net for the brand:** Treat these as **supporting "antioxidant botanical" / story-and-sensory** ingredients, **never as a substitute for a real, stabilized vitamin C** if a vitamin-C claim is the goal. Their honest sell is *polyphenol antioxidant support* + a credible natural-origin / heritage narrative — not "high-potency vitamin C."

Always read the *full* INCI.

## 2. Class & mechanism

**Class:** Botanicals & plant bioactives (antioxidant polyphenol/carotenoid extracts), marketed under the "natural vitamin C" umbrella.

**The proposed mechanisms (and which molecule actually drives each):**

1. **Direct free-radical scavenging (antioxidant).** Ascorbic acid is a classic aqueous-phase antioxidant and regenerates vitamin E; but in these extracts the *dominant* antioxidant capacity tracks the **polyphenols** (ellagic/gallic acid) and, for tomato, the **carotenoids** (lycopene is one of the most efficient singlet-oxygen quenchers known). Kakadu plum's antioxidant capacity is extreme on lab assays (FRAP ~1,714 µmol Fe²⁺/g DW; DPPH ~1,311 µmol Trolox/g DW — Bobasa 2024 [1]).
2. **Nrf2 / ARE pathway induction (indirect antioxidant).** The 2025 scoping review (Ram et al. 2025 [2]) frames Kakadu plum's photoprotective potential largely through **phytochemicals acting as Nrf2 inducers**, upregulating endogenous antioxidant defenses (HO-1, etc.) — an *indirect*, more durable antioxidant mechanism than direct scavenging. This is a polyphenol story, not a vitamin-C story.
3. **Collagen support (pro-procollagen / anti-MMP).** Ascorbic acid is a required cofactor for prolyl/lysyl hydroxylase in collagen synthesis (well established for vitamin C generally). Amla *extract* (not isolated AA) raised procollagen and TIMP-1 and lowered MMP-1 in human fibroblasts (Fujii 2008 [4]); tomato/lycopene reduced UV-induced MMP-1 and preserved procollagen I **in human skin in vivo via oral dosing** (Rizwan 2011 [14]).
4. **Anti-inflammatory / photoprotective.** Amla suppressed COX-2, NF-κB, AP-1 and PGE₂ in UVB-irradiated keratinocytes (Kunchana 2021 [3]); lycopene reduces UV erythema in humans (oral) (Stahl 2001 [16]; Grether-Beck 2017 [15]).
5. **Pigment / tone (tyrosinase inhibition).** Ascorbic acid interferes with melanogenesis; additionally Yuzu's **nomilin** is a tyrosinase inhibitor (Kim 2022 [9]) and Yuzu callus extract out-performed arbutin on melanin reduction in cells (Adhikari 2017 [11]); Amla inhibits tyrosinase and MMP-2/9 (Wang 2021 [5]).

**Mechanistic honesty:** Across all four, the *cosmetically demonstrated* effects are largely attributable to **polyphenols/carotenoids**, with ascorbic acid as a minor (and unstable) contributor. The "vitamin C" framing is a consumer shorthand, not a faithful mechanism.

## 4. Evidence review by tier

> Quality ladder: in-vitro → animal → human ORAL → human TOPICAL (RCT > controlled non-RCT > open/uncontrolled). The honest takeaway up front: **almost all of the skin-specific evidence for Kakadu plum, Amla, and Yuzu is in-vitro or animal.** The only robust *human* evidence in this whole group is for **oral tomato/lycopene** — a format the brand would not be selling. Human **topical RCT** evidence for any of these four, specifically, is **very limited to absent.**

### 4A. In-vitro

- **Kakadu plum composition/antioxidant (Bobasa et al. 2024 [1]).** WA wild-harvested fruit: ascorbic acid 25.2–131.5 mg/g DW; free ellagic acid ~23.2 mg/g DW; total ellagic acid 34–36.6 mg/g DW; total phenolics ~140 mg GAE/g DW; FRAP ~1,714 µmol Fe²⁺/g DW; DPPH ~1,311 µmol Trolox/g DW. *Limitation:* compositional/assay study, no skin model.
- **Amla → fibroblasts (Fujii et al. 2008 [4], J Ethnopharmacol 119:53–57).** Amla extract increased fibroblast proliferation and **procollagen** production concentration/time-dependently; **MMP-1 decreased**, **TIMP-1 increased**. *Limitation:* cell culture; extract (not isolated AA); no human skin.
- **Amla → UVB keratinocytes (Kunchana et al. 2021 [3]).** HaCaT cells, *P. emblica* 10–1000 µg/mL, UVB 40 mJ/cm². 50 µg/mL raised viability 75.9%→93.8% and lowered PGE₂ (1423→615 pg/mL); at 100 µg/mL apoptosis fell 20.4%→7.7% and PGE₂ fell to ~356 pg/mL; reduced ROS; suppressed COX-2, NF-κB, AP-1; ↑catalase. *Limitation:* in-vitro only; authors say in-vivo validation needed.
- **Amla → UVB photoaging fibroblasts (Adil et al. 2010 [6], J Ethnopharmacol).** *E. officinalis* fruit protected human fibroblasts against UVB photoaging (antioxidant, anti-MMP). *Limitation:* in-vitro.
- **Yuzu antioxidant chemistry (Yoo et al. 2004 [8]).** Antioxidant capacity tracks vitamin C across maturation/cultivars (peel + flesh), with flavonoids (hesperidin) contributing. *Limitation:* analytical, not skin.
- **Yuzu seed nomilin (Kim et al. 2022 [9]).** Nomilin showed antioxidant activity and downregulated melanogenesis in B16F10 melanoma cells via PKA/CREB. *Limitation:* mouse melanoma cell line, isolated compound.
- **Yuzu *callus* extract (Adhikari et al. 2017 [11]).** Tissue-culture callus (p-hydroxycinnamoylmalic acid; 24.5 mg GAE/g): fibroblast proliferation +154%, procollagen I C-peptide +176%, scratch closure +161% (all at 500 µg/mL); melanin reduction 1.85× vs arbutin; nanoliposome ↑skin flux 17.7×. *Critical limitation:* this is a **callus (lab-grown cell mass), NOT yuzu fruit/peel** — do not transfer these numbers to a "yuzu extract" on a label. In-vitro only.

### 4B. Animal

- **Kakadu plum collagen preservation (mouse).** Secondary sources cite a 2021 mouse study suggesting *T. ferdinandiana* helps preserve skin collagen [unverified — primary citation not confirmed; treat as indicative, not proven].
- **Amla wound healing (rat) (Sumitra et al. 2009 [10], "Emblica officinalis exerts wound healing action via collagen and ERK1/2").** Topical emblica accelerated wound contraction/closure with collagen upregulation and ERK1/2 activation in rats. *Limitation:* wound model in rodents, not human cosmetic aging.
- **Kakadu plum photoprotection mechanisms** synthesized in the scoping review (Ram et al. 2025 [2]) draw on indirect/animal/cell Nrf2 data; the review itself concludes the *direct* skin evidence base is limited and calls for human studies.

### 4C. Human — ORAL (strongest tier here, but wrong format for a topical brand)

- **Tomato paste RCT (Rizwan et al. 2011 [14], Br J Dermatol 164:154–162).** RCT, **n=20** healthy women; 55 g tomato paste (**16 mg lycopene**) in olive oil vs olive oil alone, **12 weeks**. Buttock-skin biopsies (UVR-exposed vs not, pre/post): tomato paste **reduced UVR-induced MMP-1**, **increased procollagen I deposition**, and **reduced the UVR-induced mtDNA 3895-bp deletion**. *Limitations:* small n, women only, oral, surrogate/biopsy endpoints (not patient-visible wrinkle outcomes), short.
- **Lycopene/lutein crossover (Grether-Beck et al. 2017 [15], Br J Dermatol 176:1231).** Double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover; **n≈65 enrolled / 59 completed**; tomato nutrient complex 10 mg/day lycopene (and lutein 10 mg/day) over two 12-week phases. TNC **completely inhibited UVA1- and UVA/B-induced upregulation of HO-1, ICAM-1, MMP-1 mRNA**. *Limitations:* oral; gene-expression endpoints (not MED/clinical); ~30/arm; carry-over noted for lutein.
- **Tomato extract & UV erythema (Stahl et al. 2001 [16] and Aust et al. 2005 [17]).** Oral tomato products raised serum lycopene/phytofluene/phytoene and **reduced UV-induced erythema**; greater protection with phytofluene/phytoene-containing tomato extract than synthetic lycopene alone. *Limitations:* small, oral, erythema (acute) endpoint.
- **Systematic review of oral photoprotection (Natarelli et al. 2025 [18]).** Confirms a consistent but modest oral-carotenoid signal for reduced UV sensitivity; emphasizes heterogeneity and that effects are adjunct, not sunscreen-replacing.
- **No comparable oral RCTs of skin-aging endpoints** exist specifically for Kakadu plum, Amla, or Yuzu fruit at the same quality.

### 4D. Human — TOPICAL (the format that matters — and the weakest tier)

- **Topical vitamin C generally (the borrowed evidence).** Topical *ascorbic acid* has a genuine human topical evidence base for photoaging/melasma — but only when properly dosed: **systematic review (Correia & Magina 2023 [13], J Cosmet Dermatol)** found RCT-level support for topical vitamin C in photoaging and melasma; **Pinnell 2001 [12]** established that AA needs **pH <3.5** and up to ~**20%** for meaningful percutaneous absorption (tissue saturates after ~3 daily applications; tissue half-life ~4 days). **This evidence is for stabilized/high-dose ascorbic acid, NOT for low-dose botanical fruit extracts**, and cannot honestly be claimed on their behalf.
- **Amla — topical human (the closest thing to direct evidence).** The best-documented topical human trial is a **randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled study, n=20, 84 days**, of a topical gel showing improved skin lightening, elasticity, hydration, and wrinkle appearance — but it tested an *amla **branch** extract* standardized to sinapic/ferulic acids, **not the fruit** (Chaikul et al., the trial reported within the Wang/ref-[5] line of work [5]; academic authors, Mae Fah Luang University, no declared competing interests). A separate small study (n≈39, Asian females 30–65) used a *blend* containing *P. emblica*. *Limitations:* small; the cleanest RCT is on **branch, not the vitamin-C-associated fruit**, so its result does not transfer cleanly to "amla (fruit) vitamin C"; the blend study can't isolate amla's contribution. **This is the single best topical human signal in the group — and it is for a different plant part than the one the marketing halo implies.**
- **Kakadu plum — topical human:** No published human topical RCT for visible anti-aging located. The 2025 scoping review (Ram et al. 2025 [2]) explicitly synthesizes *indirect* evidence and calls for direct human studies — i.e., it confirms the gap.
- **Yuzu — topical human:** No published human topical anti-aging RCT located for yuzu fruit/peel extract. The strongest yuzu data (Adhikari 2017 [11]) is in-vitro callus.
- **Tomato/Lycopene — topical human:** No robust topical RCT for visible anti-aging located; the human evidence is oral.

**Bottom line by format:** Proven for **human topical** use, *specifically as these botanicals*: essentially nothing at RCT strength (Amla being the lone, weak exception). Proven for **human oral** use: tomato/lycopene (UV resilience, biopsy-level photoaging markers). Everything else is **in-vitro/animal** or **borrowed** from properly formulated synthetic vitamin C.

## 5. Topical bioavailability

- **Ascorbic acid is the problem child.** L-AA is hydrophilic, charged near skin pH, and oxidizes readily. Penetration requires **pH <3.5** and high concentration (Pinnell 2001 [12]). Botanical extracts (a) contribute AA at far below effective levels and (b) often arrive **partly pre-oxidized to dehydroascorbic acid** — Kakadu plum showed dehydroascorbic acid rising to 9–14% of total vitamin C over 4 months frozen storage (Williams et al. 2016 [19]). So the "vitamin C" in a botanical extract is **not a reliable topical vitamin-C delivery vehicle**.
- **The polyphenols penetrate/act differently.** Ellagic/gallic acid and flavonoids (hesperidin) are more about *surface/upper-epidermis antioxidant and Nrf2 signaling* than deep dermal collagen induction; their skin pharmacokinetics in finished cosmetics are poorly quantified.
- **Lycopene** is lipophilic and substantive but **deeply colored and oxidation-prone**; topical delivery and stability are genuine formulation hurdles, and its proven mechanism (systemic carotenoid accumulation in skin) is an **oral** one — topical application does not replicate dietary skin-loading.
- **Practical implication:** If the brand wants a defensible *vitamin C* function, it must come from a **stabilized synthetic AA or derivative** (e.g., L-AA at proper pH, or SAP/MAP/ethyl ascorbic acid/THDA), with the botanical present for **polyphenol antioxidant support + story**, labeled honestly.

## 6. Formulation

- **Concentration:** Botanical fruit extracts typically used at **0.1–5%** (often <1%) — chosen for antioxidant/marketing, not to hit a vitamin-C dose. Amla topical studies used formulations around **0.1% extract** [5]. Lycopene as a tint/antioxidant is used at low ppm levels.
- **pH:** If any genuine AA function is intended, **<3.5** (irritating; conflicts with a gentle barrier-repair positioning). Most botanical extracts are added to near-neutral systems where their AA is inactive — fine if you're honest about it being a polyphenol play.
- **Stability:** The Achilles' heel. AA-containing extracts brown/oxidize; expect color drift. Mitigation: **airless/opaque packaging, low O₂ fill, chelators, co-antioxidants (vitamin E, ferulic), anhydrous or low-water systems, encapsulation/liposomes**. Lycopene needs light/O₂ protection and will impart red/orange color and staining risk.
- **Pairings:** Vitamin E + ferulic acid (classic AA-stabilizing/synergistic trio, evidence is for AA systems); niacinamide (well-tolerated, complementary tone/barrier); the botanicals pair conceptually with **a real stabilized vitamin C** if the brand wants both story and substance. Avoid combining acidic AA with retinoids/high-pH actives in the same step.
- **Sensory:** Fruit extracts can carry color/odor; Kakadu/Amla powders are tannic and can be astringent/brown. Yuzu offers a pleasant **citrus scent narrative** (but watch phototoxicity if peel oil, see §7). Lycopene's red color is a sensory liability or a "natural tint" depending on framing.

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation/sensitization:** Fruit-extract forms are generally well tolerated at cosmetic levels. *True* low-pH AA systems can sting/irritate and disrupt the epidermis (Pinnell 2001 [12]) — a tension with a barrier-repair brand. CIR has reviewed citrus-derived ingredients as safe as used with qualification on phototoxic constituents (Burnett et al. 2019 [20]).
- **Sun/phototoxicity:** **Yuzu is a citrus** — *expressed peel oil* can contain phototoxic **furocoumarins (bergapten/5-MOP)**. Yuzu expressed oil bergapten is low (~0.0001%) vs bergamot (~0.2%) (Burnett et al. 2019 [20]; Tisserand Institute [21]), but **furocoumarin-free / non-peel-oil** forms should be specified to avoid phototoxicity risk. Water/glycol *fruit* extracts are not the same risk as expressed peel oil. None of these replace SPF; antioxidants are adjunctive to sunscreen.
- **Pregnancy:** No specific topical red flags at cosmetic levels for these botanicals; standard "consult a clinician" caution. (Oral high-dose supplements are out of scope.)
- **Oxalic acid (Kakadu plum) — an *oral* concern, flagged for honesty:** Kakadu plum can be high in **oxalic acid**; high-oxalate intake is linked to impaired calcium absorption and kidney-stone risk, and oxalate is the metabolic end-product of ascorbic acid (Williams et al. 2016 [19]). **This is a dietary/ingestion issue, not a topical-cosmetic one**, but it matters for any "superfood/ingestible" line extension and for narrative integrity.
- **Sourcing/ethics (material risk):** Kakadu plum is an **Aboriginal Australian traditional food** with a documented **biopiracy controversy** (a 2007 cosmetic patent on Kakadu plum extract raised Indigenous IP concerns), wild-harvest supply variability, and fire-driven wild-population decline (SBS [22]; The Conversation [23]; Brady et al. 2019 [24]). Sourcing must address **benefit-sharing / Nagoya-style provenance** — see §9.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md)

- **Cosmetic vs drug line.** Antioxidant/"helps defend against environmental stress," "visibly brightens," "supports radiance," "helps improve the appearance of" are defensible **cosmetic** claims. Crossing into "boosts collagen," "repairs photoaging," "prevents wrinkles," or any structure/function language = **drug-adjacent**; flag for substantiation/legal (CLAUDE.md guardrail).
- **The big honesty trap: the "vitamin C" claim.** Calling a product a "vitamin C serum" because it contains Kakadu plum/Amla while the AA is sub-therapeutic and unstable is **substantiation-weak and arguably misleading**. If "vitamin C" appears on front-of-pack, the formula should contain a **genuinely functional, stabilized vitamin C**, with the botanical as a named co-star — not as the sole justification for the claim.
- **Borrowed-evidence trap.** Do **not** cite topical-AA RCTs (Pinnell/Correia) or oral-lycopene RCTs (Rizwan/Grether-Beck) as if they prove *this botanical extract, applied topically* works. They prove a different molecule and/or a different route. Keep claims tied to the *form and route actually sold*.
- **Geographic note:** "Kakadu plum" sourcing/labeling intersects with Australian Indigenous-IP and provenance-authentication efforts (Ram/QAAFI provenance work [25]); ensure supplier documentation supports any "ethically/wild-harvested" claim.

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Kakadu plum:** Wild-harvested in N. Australia (NT harvest ~Mar–May; WA ~Jan–Feb), hand-picked and frozen to preserve vitamin C; >100 Indigenous harvesters/season; farmgate value rising fast (~A$0.2M in 2012 → ~A$1.6M in 2019 → projected ~A$3.5M by 2025) (SBS [22]; Brady 2019 [24]). **Cost tier: high**, supply variable/seasonal, premium "native botanical" positioning. **Must** vet for Indigenous benefit-sharing and provenance authentication.
- **Amla:** Widely cultivated (India); **low-cost, abundant**, many standardized extracts (e.g., tannin-standardized "Emblica"). Easiest of the four to source reliably.
- **Yuzu:** Cultivated (Japan, Korea, China); moderate cost; fruit/peel/seed and callus forms available — **specify form** (and furocoumarin status if peel oil).
- **Tomato/Lycopene:** Lycopene is a commodity (food/supplement supply chains); **low–moderate cost**; cosmetic-grade stabilized/encapsulated lycopene costs more. Plentiful supply.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**Verdict: SUPPORTING / CONDITIONAL — not a hero, and never a stand-alone "vitamin C" claim.**

- **Don't** build a "vitamin C serum" around these and let the botanical carry the vitamin-C claim — the chemistry (low, unstable AA; pH needs that fight a gentle barrier brand) and the evidence (no topical RCTs) won't support it honestly. That's a claims-discipline and credibility risk for a science-led brand.
- **Do** consider them as **supporting antioxidant + narrative** ingredients, where their honest sell is *polyphenol antioxidant support* and *natural-origin / heritage story*, paired with a **real stabilized vitamin C** (or another proven active) that does the substantiable work.
- **Best single pick if forced to choose one:** **Amla** — it has the only (still weak) human topical signal *(and note that signal is for an amla **branch** extract, not the fruit — see §4D)* plus the cleanest in-vitro collagen/anti-MMP/anti-inflammatory data, is cheap and reliably sourced, and avoids citrus phototoxicity. **Kakadu plum** is the strongest *story/marketing* asset (premium, native, "highest vitamin C") but carries the heaviest **ethical-sourcing obligations** and the same evidence gap. **Tomato/lycopene** is genuinely interesting but its proof is **oral** — relevant only if the brand ever does an ingestible/"beauty-from-within" line, where it would be a credible hero. **Yuzu** is mostly sensory/story (scent, brightening narrative) with a phototoxicity caveat.
- **Longevity note:** The longevity-relevant mechanism here is **Nrf2-mediated endogenous antioxidant induction and UV/oxidative-stress mitigation (inflammaging, photoaging, MMP-driven ECM loss)** — and the cleanest human demonstration of *that systemic resilience* is the **oral lycopene** data. For *topical* skin-longevity, these botanicals are plausible adjuncts to the real pillars (SPF, retinoids, niacinamide, barrier lipids, a stabilized vitamin C), not drivers. Position as "antioxidant defense / environmental resilience," substantiate modestly, and keep the vitamin-C claim attached to a molecule that earns it.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

*Independent re-check performed 2026-06-19. Each key citation was located in PubMed/PMC or the publisher and read against the claim it supports. This dossier is unusually rigorous and self-aware — it already enforces the human/topical vs in-vitro/oral/borrowed distinction throughout, flags its own weak/industry-linked sources, and does not let any botanical carry a "vitamin C" claim. Verification confirmed the citations are real and say what is claimed, with three minor overstatements now corrected.*

**Overall grade: C** (weak/indirect *for the botanicals as topical actives*). This grade is for the four botanicals applied topically as cosmetic actives, which is what the brand would sell. It is the honest composite, and the dossier's own §11 verdict ("SUPPORTING / CONDITIONAL — never a stand-alone vitamin-C claim") already lands here. Sub-grades by claim line:

- **Topical anti-aging efficacy of Kakadu plum, Amla (fruit), Yuzu — Grade D.** No human topical RCT exists for any of them as sold. Evidence is in-vitro (Fujii 2008 [4], Kunchana 2021 [3], Bobasa 2024 [1]) or animal (Sumitra 2009 [10]). The dossier states this plainly.
- **Topical anti-aging of Amla *branch* extract — Grade C (verified real RCT, wrong plant part).** Ref [5]'s n=20 / 84-day double-blind placebo-controlled trial is genuine and from academic authors (Mae Fah Luang University, no declared competing interests — *not* primarily industry-linked as the original note implied). But it tests an amla **branch** extract standardized to sinapic/ferulic acids, **not the fruit** consumers associate with "amla vitamin C." Corrected in §4D, §10, §11.
- **Oral tomato/lycopene UV resilience — Grade B (strong, but oral, not topical).** Rizwan 2011 [14] (n=20 RCT), Grether-Beck 2017 [15] (DBPC crossover), Stahl 2001 [16] (n=9+10) all verified and accurately summarized. This is real, good human evidence — for a route the brand isn't selling. Borrowed for topical = Grade D.
- **"Vitamin C" delivery from botanical fruit extracts — Grade D (effectively disproven topically).** Pinnell 2001 [12] (pH <3.5, up to 20%, derivatives don't raise skin AA) and Williams 2016 [19] (dehydroascorbic acid 9–14% over 4 mo frozen storage; oxalic acid an oral concern) both verified exactly. They actively undercut the "botanical vitamin C" story, which the dossier correctly weaponizes against the marketing claim.

**Citations checked and confirmed accurate:** Bobasa 2024 [1] (Kakadu composition/FRAP/DPPH — exact match to Karajarri averages); Kunchana 2021 [3] (numbers match, with PGE₂/apoptosis attribution corrected — see below); Fujii 2008 [4] (procollagen↑, MMP-1↓, TIMP-1↑, no MMP-2 effect); Wang/Chaikul [5] (real academic RCT, branch extract); Adhikari 2017 [11] (callus extract, +154% / 1.76× / 161% / 1.85× / 17.67× all match; correctly flagged as callus-not-fruit); Pinnell 2001 [12]; Correia & Magina 2023 [13] (7 RCTs, 139 volunteers, topical AA effective — borrowed evidence, correctly labeled); Rizwan 2011 [14]; Grether-Beck 2017 [15]; Stahl 2001 [16]; Williams 2016 [19].

**Main caveats (read before writing any copy):**
1. **The entire human evidence base is oral (tomato/lycopene) or borrowed (synthetic topical AA).** Zero human topical RCTs exist for these four botanicals as the fruit/peel actives a label would feature.
2. **The one real topical RCT (Amla [5]) is on the *branch*, not the fruit** — do not present it as proof that "amla" (the vitamin-C gooseberry) reduces wrinkles topically.
3. **Lycopene's proof is dietary skin-loading**, which topical application does not replicate; "lycopene protects skin" is an *ingestion* claim.
4. **Botanical "vitamin C" is sub-therapeutic and partly pre-oxidized** before it reaches the bottle (Williams 2016 [19]); a front-of-pack "vitamin C" claim needs a stabilized AA/derivative doing the work.
5. **Industry/abstract-level sources remain in the mix** ([5] blend study, [18], vendor figures) — efficacy numbers from these are not independently confirmed at full-text method level.
