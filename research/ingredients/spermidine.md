# Ingredient: Spermidine (INCI: Spermidine; sometimes "Spermidine HCl" or via wheat-germ extract)

**Researched:** 2026-06-18 · **Status:** ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product

## What it is
Spermidine is a naturally occurring polyamine and one of the most prominent molecules in the longevity field because it is a well-documented **inducer of autophagy** (the cell's "self-cleaning / recycling" process) and a geroprotector that extends lifespan in yeast, worms, and flies. In skincare it is sold on the "autophagy = cellular renewal" narrative. Dietary sources include wheat germ, soy, and aged cheese.

## What it does (claimed → evidenced)
| Benefit | Evidence strength | Source(s) |
|---|---|---|
| Induces autophagy & extends lifespan (model organisms) | strong — but in yeast/worms/flies & cell models, not human skin | Nature Aging review [3]; Eisenberg/PMC [4] |
| Prolongs hair-follicle anagen phase (human) | moderate — but **oral** supplementation, RCT; hair, not skin | Dermatol Pract Concept RCT [2]; Clausius Press RCT [1] |
| Supports epidermal renewal / "skin autophagy" topically | weak / largely undocumented in humans | trade summaries [5] |
| Visible anti-aging benefits from a topical serum | anecdotal / marketing | [5] |

## How it fits Kintsugi
Conceptually a *perfect* fit for the brand: autophagy is the literal cellular version of "repair / renew / mend," and spermidine is a marquee longevity molecule with real (non-skin) science. The honesty problem is large: the strong evidence is for **oral** supplementation and **non-skin** outcomes (lifespan in animals; hair follicles in humans). Human **topical skin** efficacy is essentially undocumented. It's a fantastic *storytelling/education* asset but a weak *efficacy* claim today — useful as a supporting "longevity science" ingredient, not a proof-backed hero.

## Formulation notes
Small, water-soluble molecule (better penetration prospects than peptides/proteins). Often introduced via wheat-germ extract (a natural source) or as purified spermidine. Stability and effective topical concentration for a skin benefit are not well established in public literature — formulate conservatively and don't overstate dose-response. Pairs naturally with other "cellular renewal" actives (niacinamide/NAD story, resveratrol).

## Concerns & cautions
- Polyamines are involved in cell proliferation; at cosmetic topical levels no specific safety alarm is documented, but the high-dose proliferation biology argues for sensible use levels and not overclaiming.
- Wheat-germ-derived sources: possible relevance for wheat/gluten-sensitive *contact* concerns (low, but disclose source).
- No specific photosensitivity flag; pregnancy data for topical use is lacking — conservative copy advised.

## Claims caution
- ✅ Safe cosmetic language: "inspired by autophagy / cellular-renewal science," "supports the look of fresh, renewed skin," "a longevity-science-inspired active."
- ⚠️ Risky / drug-like: "activates autophagy in your skin," "extends skin cell lifespan," "anti-aging at the cellular level," "detoxifies cells." Autophagy induction in human skin from a topical is not demonstrated; keep it as mechanism/education, not efficacy. Importing oral/animal longevity data into topical claims is the trap. See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).

## Cost / sourcing
Mid cost tier; available as purified spermidine and via wheat-germ extracts. Supply is reasonable (rides the oral-supplement longevity boom). Standardization of "spermidine content" matters when using plant extracts.

## Verdict for Kintsugi
⚠️ — **Supporting "longevity narrative" active; not an evidence-backed hero (yet).** Outstanding fit for the brand story and the autophagy/renewal metaphor, but topical human skin evidence is thin to absent. Use as a science-forward supporting ingredient with strictly honest, mechanism-framed copy — its value is differentiation and narrative, not proven topical efficacy.
