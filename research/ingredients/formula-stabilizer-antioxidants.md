# Ingredient: Formula-Stabilizer Antioxidants (BHT, BHA, Propyl Gallate, TBHQ, Disodium EDTA)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product
**Class:** Antioxidants (formulation/excipient sub-class) · **Slug:** `formula-stabilizer-antioxidants`

> **TL;DR for a busy reader.** This is a group of **functional excipients, not anti-aging actives.** Their job is to keep the *product* from going off — BHT, BHA, propyl gallate and TBHQ are **synthetic primary antioxidants** that stop the oils/fats and oxidation-sensitive actives (vitamin C, retinol, plant oils) in a formula from going rancid or degrading; **Disodium EDTA** is a **chelator** that mops up trace metal ions (iron, copper) so they can't catalyze that oxidation (the Fenton reaction). They are present at tiny levels (typically 0.01–0.1%, EDTA ~0.05–0.2%) and are on the INCI of a huge fraction of cosmetics. **None of them is a meaningful topical anti-aging active in its own right.** The one genuinely interesting anti-aging signal is a single **2025 in-vitro study (Markiewicz et al., Int J Cosmet Sci)** showing BHT — alongside vitamins E and C — inhibited methylglyoxal-induced ROS and glycation in fibroblasts and 3D skin models; that's a real but **early, in-vitro, industry-authored** finding, not a human anti-wrinkle result. The bigger story for the brand is the **reputational/regulatory minefield**: **BHA is IARC Group 2B, NTP "reasonably anticipated to be a human carcinogen," California Prop 65–listed, the EU now caps it at 0.07% (SCCS 2026), and the US FDA opened a formal safety re-assessment of it in food in Feb 2026 (a Request for Information, comments due Apr 2026; officials signalled removal *only if* it fails review — not yet an enacted ban)**; **BHT** survived EU review (SCCS 2021: safe to 0.8% leave-on) but carries endocrine-disruption noise; **propyl gallate** is a known **contact allergen**; **TBHQ** is a controversial food additive. For a premium, science-forward, "clean-leaning" anti-aging brand, the honest play is: **use Disodium EDTA freely (cheap, safe, genuinely useful), use BHT as the default oil-soluble stabilizer if needed, and avoid BHA / propyl gallate / TBHQ** — both because greener stabilizers (tocopherol, rosemary extract) exist and because BHA in particular is a marketing and compliance liability. **Never market any of these as an anti-aging benefit.** See Verdict (§11).

---

## 1. Identity

These five are grouped here by **function in the formula** (they stabilize the product), not by a shared anti-aging mechanism. Four are phenolic/quinone **primary (chain-breaking) antioxidants**; one is a **chelator (secondary antioxidant)**.

| Name | INCI / label name | CAS | Type | What it actually does in a formula |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **BHT** (butylated hydroxytoluene) | **BHT** (sometimes *Butylated Hydroxytoluene*) | 128-37-0 | Synthetic phenolic primary antioxidant; lipid-soluble; structural cousin of vitamin E's chromanol ring | Donates H to peroxy radicals → stops lipid autoxidation (rancidity) of oils/fats and oxidation-sensitive actives |
| **BHA** (butylated hydroxyanisole) | **BHA** | 25013-16-5 (mix of 2- and 3-*tert*-butyl-4-hydroxyanisole) | Synthetic phenolic primary antioxidant; lipid-soluble | Same role as BHT; often used together with BHT (synergy) in oily/colour cosmetics |
| **Propyl Gallate** | **Propyl Gallate** | 121-79-9 | Ester of gallic acid; phenolic primary antioxidant; moderately lipid-soluble | Free-radical terminator in fats/oils; chelates metals weakly; used in lipsticks, oils |
| **TBHQ** (*tert*-butylhydroquinone) | **t-Butyl Hydroquinone** | 1948-33-0 | Hydroquinone-derivative phenolic antioxidant | Potent oil stabilizer; common food/oil antioxidant; less common in cosmetics |
| **Disodium EDTA** | **Disodium EDTA** | 139-33-3 | Aminopolycarboxylic-acid **chelating agent** (secondary antioxidant) | Sequesters Fe²⁺/Cu²⁺/Ca²⁺; removes the metal catalysts that drive oxidation and destabilize preservatives/actives; also improves clarity & lather |

- ⚠️ **Critical naming note for this brand:** in this dossier **"BHA" = butylated hydroxyanisole (a synthetic antioxidant)**, **NOT beta-hydroxy acid / salicylic acid** (the exfoliant). They share an acronym and nothing else. Do not confuse them in copy or formulation. [9][10]
- **Relatives often seen on labels** (same families): **Octyl/Dodecyl Gallate** (gallate cousins of propyl gallate); **Tetrasodium EDTA, Trisodium EDTA, Calcium Disodium EDTA, HEDTA** (EDTA-family chelators); **Tocopherol / mixed tocopherols** (the *natural* primary-antioxidant alternative); **Sodium Phytate / Phytic Acid, Sodium Gluconate** (greener chelator alternatives to EDTA). [1][8]
- **Where they're used:** anhydrous and high-oil products (balms, lip products, cleansing oils, facial oils, sunscreens, colour cosmetics), and any water-phase formula needing chelation. EDTA appears in a very large share of all rinse-off and leave-on products. [8]

## 2. Class & mechanism

**Two distinct mechanisms, both about protecting the *product*, not the *skin*.**

**(a) Primary / chain-breaking antioxidants (BHT, BHA, propyl gallate, TBHQ).**
Unsaturated lipids in a formula (plant oils, esters, some actives) undergo **autoxidation**: O₂ attacks them → lipid peroxyl radicals (LOO•) → a self-propagating chain that produces rancid by-products, off-odours, colour change, and loss of active potency. These phenolic antioxidants **donate a hydrogen atom** from their hydroxyl group to the peroxyl radical, converting it to a stable hydroperoxide and leaving behind a **resonance-stabilized (relatively unreactive) phenoxyl radical** — terminating the chain. BHT is essentially a synthetic, more robust analogue of the vitamin-E chromanol antioxidant motif. [4][6][12]

**(b) Secondary antioxidant / chelator (Disodium EDTA).**
Trace transition metals (iron, copper) — present as contaminants in water, raw materials, and packaging — catalyze radical formation, notably via the **Fenton reaction** (Fe²⁺ + H₂O₂ → Fe³⁺ + •OH + OH⁻), generating the hydroxyl radical, the most destructive ROS. EDTA **binds (sequesters) those metal ions**, removing the catalyst so oxidation can't get started, and protecting oxidation-sensitive actives (vitamin C, retinoids) and preservative systems. Chelators are called "secondary" antioxidants because they don't quench radicals directly; they remove the spark. [13][14]

**Why a skin-longevity brand even cares:** oxidative degradation inside the jar is the enemy of every premium antioxidant active. A vitamin-C serum that has browned, or a facial oil that has gone rancid, is not just unsightly — it has **lost the very actives the brand is selling** and may have generated irritant peroxides. So these excipients are *enablers* of an antioxidant/anti-aging formula's integrity. That is a real, defensible role — but it is a **formulation-stability** role, not a skin-biology one.

⚠️ **The crucial mechanistic honesty point.** A phenolic antioxidant that protects oil in a jar is **not thereby an anti-aging skin active.** To matter for skin aging it would have to (i) penetrate to where ROS damage occurs, (ii) be present at a functional dose, and (iii) be shown to change a skin-aging endpoint in humans. At their cosmetic use levels (0.01–0.1%) these are formulated for *product* protection, and the human-skin anti-aging evidence is essentially absent (§4). Worse, several of them are **pro-oxidant at higher doses or in the presence of metals** (propyl gallate + Cu²⁺ *induces* lipid peroxidation in human fibroblasts; high-dose propyl gallate kills fibroblasts via ROS) — i.e. the same molecule flips from protector to damager depending on context. [3][7]

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

| Hallmark | Plausibility as a *topical skin* target | Evidence tier (best available) |
|---|---|---|
| **Oxidative stress** | The molecules *are* antioxidants — but dosed/located for *product* protection, not skin | In-vitro radical-scavenging strong; human-skin outcome **absent** |
| **Glycation / AGEs** | One in-vitro signal: BHT (with vit E/C) inhibited MGO-induced glycation in fibroblasts/3D skin | **In-vitro only** (Markiewicz 2025); industry-authored |
| **Photoaging** | BHT blocks UVA/UVC lipid peroxidation *in liposomes*; no anti-photoaging effect shown in cells/animals/humans | In-vitro lipid-model only; **no skin endpoint** |
| **Collagen / ECM loss** | Indirect (less glycation/ROS → less collagen damage) in 3D model | In-vitro only |
| **Barrier decline** | Indirect only (protecting barrier lipids in the *product* from rancidity) | None for skin barrier itself |
| **Pigmentation / tone** | TBHQ is a *depigmentant* at high % (≥4%); an unwanted effect, not a benefit | Animal (negative/safety context) |
| **Inflammaging** | Not a target; BHA/propyl gallate can *cause* irritation/allergy | N/A (net negative for some) |
| **Cellular senescence / mitochondrial decline / hydration / microbiome** | Not targets | None |

**Honest summary:** the only column with any anti-aging "signal" beyond test-tube radical scavenging is **glycation**, and that rests on a **single in-vitro 2025 study where BHT was the *weakest/most context-dependent* of the three antioxidants tested and vitamin C was the standout**. [2] Everything else is either "protects the product" (real, but not a skin claim) or "no evidence." These are **not** ingredients you build anti-aging hallmark messaging on.

## 4. Evidence review by tier — the core of this dossier

> The ladder here is unusual: the top rung (human topical RCT showing an anti-aging benefit) is **completely empty for all five**, and that is expected — these were never developed as skin actives. The real, robust evidence is about **(a) their function as formula stabilizers** and **(b) their safety/hazard profile**. I keep those separate from the thin "skin-benefit" literature.

### Tier 0 — Function as formula stabilizers (this is what's actually proven)
- Decades of food- and cosmetic-science literature establish BHT, BHA, propyl gallate and TBHQ as **effective primary antioxidants that delay lipid autoxidation/rancidity**, and EDTA as an effective **metal chelator** that suppresses metal-catalyzed oxidation (Fenton). This is textbook and not in dispute. [4][6][12][13][14] **This is the only thing these ingredients are genuinely "proven" to do, and it is a *product*-quality function, not a skin benefit.**

### Tier 1 — In-vitro / biochemical (skin-relevant)
- **Markiewicz E, Ruth N, Mammone T, Idowu OC (2025).** *Investigating the dual functions of butylated hydroxytoluene, vitamin E and vitamin C as antioxidants and anti-glycation agents in vitro: Implications for skin health.* **Int J Cosmet Sci. 2025;47(6):935–950. PMID 40490897. DOI 10.1111/ics.13079.** Dermal fibroblasts + 3D skin models challenged with **methylglyoxal (MGO)**; three concentrations each of BHT, vit E, vit C; assays for total antioxidant capacity, intracellular ROS, glycation products, collagen, and biomarkers. **All three inhibited MGO-induced ROS and glycation.** Key nuances: the protective profile depended on dose in a complex way — *linear* dose-responses tracked weaker protection, *inverse* dose-responses (best collagen-fibre preservation at **low** concentration) tracked the molecule's intrinsic antioxidant capacity, and that effect was **most pronounced for vitamin C at low concentration**, i.e. **BHT was the more context-dependent/weaker performer.** ⚠️ **In-vitro only (cells + reconstructed skin), not human; authors affiliated with HexisLab + Estée Lauder/Clinique Research Labs (commercial COI).** A genuinely interesting anti-glycation signal for BHT, but early, in-vitro, and not a stand-alone case for BHT as an anti-aging active. [2]
- **BHT vs UV lipid peroxidation:** BHT prevents **UVA- and UVC-induced lipid peroxidation in liposome models**; however, **no activity against the characteristic changes of photoaging, and no UVA-protective effect in animals or cultured cells, has been reported** — a clean example of "works in a test-tube lipid model, not shown in skin." [5][11]
- **Propyl gallate radical scavenging:** strong free-radical scavenger (e.g. IC₅₀ ~4.4 µM in methanol); classic chain-terminator. [15] But see pro-oxidant data below.
- **⚠️ Pro-oxidant flip (propyl gallate):** **Jacobi H, et al. (1999).** *Induction of lipid peroxidation in human fibroblasts by the antioxidant propyl gallate in combination with copper(II).* **Toxicol Lett. 1999;110(3):183–190.** Propyl gallate alone *reduced* TBARS (antioxidant), but **PG + non-toxic Cu(II) at >0.125 mM induced lipid peroxidation and inhibited membrane succinate dehydrogenase in human fibroblasts** — the molecule becomes a pro-oxidant in the presence of copper. [3]
- **⚠️ Pro-oxidant cytotoxicity (propyl gallate):** **Park WH (2024).** *Propyl gallate induces cell death in human pulmonary fibroblasts through increasing ROS levels and depleting GSH.* **Sci Rep. 2024;14:5375 (PMC10912098).** Dose-dependent apoptosis 100–1600 µM; at ~800 µM significant apoptosis, mitochondrial-membrane-potential loss, raised total ROS/superoxide, **GSH depletion**; NAC rescued. Confirms PG flips to pro-oxidant/cytotoxic at higher concentrations. [7]

### Tier 2 — Animal / toxicology (this is where the *hazard* evidence is strong)
- **BHA forestomach carcinogenicity:** dietary BHA produced **benign and malignant forestomach tumours in rats, mice and hamsters**, plus hyperplasia/necrosis — the basis for IARC **Group 2B**, NTP **"reasonably anticipated to be a human carcinogen,"** and California **Prop 65**. The forestomach mechanism is widely argued to be a high-dose, rodent-specific (non-glandular forestomach) phenomenon of limited human relevance, but the classifications stand. [9][16][17]
- **TBHQ depigmentation:** TBHQ was a **weak depigmentant at 4–5% but not at 0.1%** in animal studies — the basis for the CIR 0.1% cap. (Relevant as a safety boundary, and as a reminder that a "stabilizer" can have unwanted skin pharmacology at higher %.) [18]
- **EDTA reproductive/developmental effects:** **high oral doses** of EDTA produced reproductive/developmental effects in animals, **attributed to zinc depletion** by the chelator, not a specific EDTA toxicity — and not reproduced at the negligible systemic exposure from dermal cosmetic use. [8]
- **BHT systemic effects:** high oral doses affect rodent liver/lung; **BHT was *not* a reproductive or developmental toxicant in animals** per the CIR review. [1]

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL (food-additive context — DO NOT borrow for a cream)
- The bulk of the human-relevant data on BHT/BHA/propyl gallate/TBHQ is **dietary**: JECFA/EFSA/FDA food-additive evaluations, ADIs, and the carcinogenicity debate around BHA. These inform *hazard* but are an **oral, systemic, high-cumulative-exposure** context that does **not** transfer to a low-% topical excipient — in either direction (neither a benefit nor a proportionate risk claim). [16][17]

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL — the decisive tier for an anti-aging claim
- **Anti-aging efficacy:** **No human topical RCT (or any controlled human topical study) shows BHT, BHA, propyl gallate, TBHQ, or Disodium EDTA visibly reduces wrinkles, improves elasticity, evens tone, or reverses photoaging.** None exists because none was developed for that purpose. The anti-aging case is **zero at the human-topical tier.**
- **Human topical *safety*** (well studied): CIR human patch-test data — **BHT, TBHQ and EDTA were not human irritants or sensitizers at use levels** (TBHQ non-irritant/non-sensitizer at 0.1–4%; BHT no significant irritation/sensitization in use, though 100% BHT was a mild irritant/moderate sensitizer in lab tests). [1][18][8] **Propyl gallate is the exception:** a documented **human contact allergen** — see §7 and the Holcomb 2017 systematic review (74 reported gallate-allergy cases; propyl gallate the dominant sensitizer, causing facial/hand/lip dermatitis). [19]

**Bottom line by tier:** **Proven** = they stabilize formulas (Tier 0) and (for most) are safe at use levels in human patch testing (Tier 4 safety). **Real but limited** = in-vitro antioxidant/anti-glycation signals, strongest as a *biology curiosity* for BHT (Tier 1). **Strong but negative/hazard-side** = BHA carcinogenicity classifications and propyl-gallate pro-oxidant/allergen data (Tiers 2–4). **Absent** = any human topical anti-aging benefit (Tier 4 efficacy). These belong in a formula as **excipients**, full stop.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **BHT:** penetrates skin to a limited extent but **the absorbed amount remains primarily in the skin** and does not produce the systemic exposures seen with oral dosing — i.e. it stays where it's added (the product/upper skin), consistent with an excipient role rather than a dermal active reaching fibroblasts at a functional dose. [1]
- **BHA / propyl gallate / TBHQ:** lipophilic phenolics with some dermal uptake (dermal exposure is a recognized route, e.g. from lip and eye products), but, again, at 0.01–0.1% they are not delivered as functional skin antioxidants. [9][18]
- **Disodium EDTA:** **poorly absorbed through skin** (large, highly polar, charged molecule); negligible systemic penetration — which is exactly why its high-oral-dose reproductive findings don't translate to topical risk. ⚠️ **Penetration-enhancer caveat:** EDTA can **transiently increase skin permeability by chelating Ca²⁺ in tight junctions**; the CIR panel acknowledged EDTA-family ingredients are penetration enhancers, though meaningful enhancement at cosmetic levels through intact skin is considered limited. Worth noting if pairing EDTA with potent/irritant actives. [8]
- **Implication:** none of these is "bioavailable" in the anti-aging sense of reaching the dermis at an active dose. Bioavailability is the wrong frame — they're meant to act **in the formula**, not in the skin.

## 6. Formulation

- **Typical use levels:** BHT **0.01–0.1%** (EU/SCCS allows up to **0.8%** leave-on/rinse-off; food/cosmetic practice usually far lower); BHA **0.01–0.1%** (EU cap now **0.07%**); propyl gallate **≤0.1%** (CIR); TBHQ **≤0.1%** (CIR); **Disodium EDTA ~0.05–0.2%** (often 0.1%). [1][8][10][18][19]
- **When you actually need them:** anhydrous/high-oil systems (facial oils, balms, cleansing oils, lip products), products with **unsaturated plant oils** or oxidation-prone actives (retinol/retinyl esters, vitamin C oils, polyunsaturated botanicals), and **emulsions where EDTA protects the preservative and clarity.** A simple low-oil aqueous gel may need EDTA but no primary antioxidant.
- **pH / stability:** the phenolics are robust across cosmetic pH; EDTA works best **above ~pH 4–4.5** (at low pH the acid form is less soluble/effective — relevant for low-pH vitamin-C or acid serums, where alternative chelators like sodium phytate may perform better). EDTA itself is very stable.
- **Pairings (the real value):** **EDTA + a primary antioxidant** is the classic **synergistic stabilizer pair** (remove the metal catalyst *and* quench radicals). BHT + BHA were historically combined for synergy. These protect **vitamin C, retinoids, ferulic acid, plant oils, fragrance** from oxidation — i.e. they are the *bodyguards* for the brand's hero antioxidant actives. [13][14]
- **Greener swaps (brand-relevant):** **Tocopherol / mixed tocopherols** (natural vitamin E) and **rosemary (rosmarinus) leaf extract** replace BHT/BHA as primary antioxidants; **Sodium Phytate, Phytic Acid, Sodium Gluconate, Trisodium Ethylenediamine Disuccinate (Trisodium EDDS)** replace EDTA as chelators. These are the standard "clean-beauty" substitutions and are usually adequate, occasionally with a modest performance/cost trade-off.
- **Sensory:** all are effectively sensory-neutral at use levels (no colour/odour/texture impact). This is part of why they're ubiquitous.

## 7. Safety

- **BHT — low topical concern.** CIR: **safe as used**; no significant irritation/sensitization/photosensitization in use (100% neat BHT was a mild irritant/moderate sensitizer in lab tests only); **not reproductive/developmental toxicant** in animals. **SCCS (2021, SCCS/1636/21):** safe up to **0.8%** in leave-on & rinse-off (0.1% toothpaste, 0.001% mouthwash) after reviewing **endocrine-disruption** concerns — i.e. the EU regulator looked at the ED noise and still cleared it at robust levels. [1][20]
- **BHA — the problem child.** **IARC Group 2B** (possibly carcinogenic to humans; sufficient *animal* evidence — rodent forestomach tumours — inadequate human data); **US NTP "reasonably anticipated to be a human carcinogen"** (15th Report on Carcinogens); **California Prop 65** listed since 1990; **EU list of potential endocrine disruptors (category-1)**. The forestomach mechanism is argued to be high-dose/rodent-specific, and the **EU SCCS (final opinion SCCS/1682/25, 16 Apr 2026) concluded BHA is safe up to 0.07% dermal** with no specific human-health concern at that limit — **but** the combined classifications make it a **reputational and Prop-65-warning liability** regardless of the technical risk at use level. [9][16][17][22][23]
- **Propyl gallate — documented contact allergen.** **Holcomb ZE, Van Noord MG, Atwater AR (2017).** *Gallate Contact Dermatitis: Product Update and Systematic Review.* **Dermatitis 28(2):115–127.** 74 reported gallate-allergy cases; propyl gallate the dominant sensitizer; **facial, hand and lip dermatitis** common; sensitization thresholds reported as low as ~0.0025–0.015% in dose-response work. CIR calls it **safe ≤0.1%** but explicitly notes it **can act as a dermal sensitizer.** For a leave-on facial product this is a real reason to prefer alternatives. [19][21]
- **TBHQ — safe at cap, depigmentation at high %.** CIR: safe **≤0.1%**; non-irritant/non-sensitizer at 0.1–4%; **weak depigmentant at 4–5%** (the reason for the cap). Controversial as a *food* additive; uncommon in modern cosmetics. [18]
- **Disodium EDTA — low concern, with two caveats.** Not a dermal irritant/sensitizer at use levels; negligible skin penetration; high-oral-dose reproductive effects are zinc-depletion artefacts irrelevant to topical use. Caveats: (1) **penetration enhancer** (tight-junction Ca²⁺ chelation) — minor but note when pairing with actives; (2) **environmental persistence** — EDTA is poorly biodegradable and an aquatic-persistence/eco concern, the main reason brands move to biodegradable chelators (EDDS, sodium phytate, gluconate). [8]
- **Sun:** none is a UV filter; none adds SPF; none is photosensitizing at use levels (BHT/BHA can *reduce* photo-oxidation of the product, not protect skin).
- **Pregnancy:** all act locally with negligible systemic absorption at topical use levels; no specific topical contraindication established — but for a brand audience, the BHA carcinogen labels alone make it the one to avoid regardless of pregnancy. Default messaging: "consult a clinician."

## 8. Regulatory & claims

- **Cosmetic vs. drug line — easy here:** as formula stabilizers these are unambiguous **cosmetic ingredients/excipients.** The risk is **not** drug-claim creep (you'd never claim a preservative "treats" anything) — the risk is the **opposite**: dressing up an excipient as an "antioxidant benefit." Listing BHT/EDTA on the INCI and implying the product is therefore "antioxidant skincare" is misleading; the antioxidant *benefit* story must ride on the **hero actives** (vitamin C, ferulic, etc.), with these merely **protecting** them.
- **Regulatory status (current):**
  - **BHT:** permitted; **EU SCCS 2021 safe to 0.8%** leave-on/rinse-off. No general restriction in US cosmetics. [20]
  - **BHA:** **EU now restricts to 0.07% dermal** (SCCS/1682/25, Apr 2026; Annex amendment expected to follow); **IARC 2B / NTP / Prop 65 / EU-ED list**; **US FDA opened a formal safety re-assessment of BHA in food, Feb 2026** — a Request for Information (Federal Register, comments due 13 Apr 2026), with HHS officials stating BHA would be removed *only if* it cannot meet current safety standards; this is a reassessment, **not an enacted ban**, and applies to food, not cosmetics, but signals direction of travel. A Prop 65 warning may attach to BHA-containing products sold in California above the safe-harbour level. [9][16][17][22][23]
  - **Propyl gallate / TBHQ:** CIR **safe ≤0.1%**; EU permits with low reported use; propyl gallate flagged as a sensitizer. [18][19][21]
  - **Disodium EDTA:** broadly permitted worldwide; no safety restriction; eco-labelling/biodegradability is the only practical constraint (some "clean"/eco certifications disallow it). [8]
- ✅ **Defensible language (about the *product*, not the skin):** "stabilized for freshness," "protects the formula's actives from oxidation," "chelating agent helps keep the formula stable and effective." These describe excipient function honestly.
- ⚠️ **Avoid / risky:** marketing BHT/BHA/PG/TBHQ/EDTA as a skin "antioxidant benefit," "anti-glycation," "anti-aging," or "fights free radicals **in skin**" — unsubstantiated for these molecules at use level, and (for BHA) layering an anti-aging benefit claim on a Prop-65/IARC-listed ingredient is an avoidable liability. See [`../CLAUDE.md`](../CLAUDE.md) and [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Cost tier: very low (commodity).** BHT, BHA, propyl gallate, TBHQ and Disodium EDTA are bulk industrial antioxidants/chelators costing cents per kg of finished formula at 0.01–0.2% — among the cheapest functional ingredients in skincare. Cost is never the reason to choose or avoid them.
- **Suppliers:** ubiquitous across all cosmetic chemical distributors; pharma/food-grade and cosmetic-grade widely available; not supply-constrained.
- **Sustainability/sourcing angle (the real differentiator):** all four phenolics are **petrochemically synthesized**; EDTA is synthetic and **poorly biodegradable**. The premium/"clean" alternatives — **tocopherol, rosemary extract, sodium phytate, sodium gluconate, EDDS** — cost modestly more but align with a green-chemistry and refillable-packaging brand story. For a brand "investing in the brand and buying commodity bases" (per CLAUDE.md), the stabilizer choice is a low-cost, high-signal place to express values.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

| Claim seen in the wild | Reality |
|---|---|
| "BHT/BHA are powerful antioxidants — great for anti-aging skin." | They're powerful antioxidants **for the oil in the jar**, dosed for product protection (0.01–0.1%). **No human topical anti-aging evidence.** [4][5] |
| "BHT fights glycation / AGEs in skin." | One **in-vitro 2025** study (fibroblasts + 3D model, MGO) where BHT was the *most context-dependent* of three antioxidants and **vitamin C** was the standout; industry-authored; not human. [2] |
| "BHA in my serum gives antioxidant benefits." | This BHA is **butylated hydroxyanisole**, an excipient — not beta-hydroxy acid, and not a skin benefit. It's **IARC 2B / NTP / Prop 65 / EU-ED-listed**; EU caps it at 0.07%. [9][16][17][22] |
| "Propyl gallate is a natural-ish gallic-acid antioxidant, totally benign." | A **documented contact allergen** (facial/hand/lip dermatitis) and a **pro-oxidant** with copper or at higher doses. [3][7][19] |
| "EDTA is a harsh/toxic chemical to avoid." | Overstated for *health*: negligible skin penetration, not an irritant/sensitizer at use levels; the legitimate concern is **environmental persistence**, not skin safety. [8] |
| "This product is 'antioxidant skincare' because it contains BHT/EDTA." | Misleading. The antioxidant *benefit* must come from the hero actives; these just **protect** them. |

**The honest pitch** is the absence of a skin-benefit pitch: these are **back-of-house stabilizers** that keep an antioxidant/anti-aging formula from degrading. Talk about them (if at all) as product-integrity, never as a skin claim.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ **CONDITIONAL — useful *excipients*, never an anti-aging actives story. Use Disodium EDTA and (if needed) BHT; avoid BHA, propyl gallate, and TBHQ.**

**One line:** These are formula stabilizers, not skin actives — they protect the brand's *real* antioxidant heroes (vitamin C, ferulic, retinoids, oils) from oxidizing in the jar, which is genuinely valuable, but none has human topical anti-aging evidence, and BHA in particular (IARC 2B / NTP / Prop 65 / EU-restricted, with an FDA food-safety re-assessment underway in 2026) is a reputational and compliance liability a premium "clean-leaning" brand should simply avoid.

- **Per-ingredient call for this brand:**
  - **Disodium EDTA — ✅ use freely** where chelation helps (cheap, safe, negligible penetration, protects actives/preservatives). Consider **biodegradable swaps** (sodium phytate, EDDS, sodium gluconate) if the brand wants an eco/clean angle — low cost, high values-signal.
  - **BHT — ⚠️ acceptable as the default oil-soluble primary antioxidant** if a natural one won't hold the formula (EU-cleared to 0.8%, low topical concern), **but prefer tocopherol / rosemary extract first** for a cleaner label and a vitamin-E story.
  - **BHA — ❌ avoid.** Technical safety at 0.07% may be fine, but the carcinogen/ED classifications + Prop 65 warning + 2026 FDA momentum make it indefensible for a premium science-credible brand when alternatives exist.
  - **Propyl gallate — ❌ avoid** in leave-on facial products (contact allergen; pro-oxidant flip).
  - **TBHQ — ❌ avoid** (controversial food additive, high-% depigmentation, uncommon in cosmetics — no reason to reach for it).
- **Longevity relevance:** essentially nil at the skin-biology level. The legitimate "longevity" framing is *product* longevity — keeping oxidation-sensitive actives stable over shelf life — which **supports** the brand's hero-antioxidant thesis indirectly. The lone anti-glycation in-vitro signal (BHT, Markiewicz 2025) is interesting to **watch** but far too early to build copy on; if the brand wants an anti-glycation story, the better-evidenced route is the actives in [`anti-glycation-phenolic-acids.md`](anti-glycation-phenolic-acids.md) / [`b-vitamin-anti-glycation.md`](b-vitamin-anti-glycation.md), not a preservative.
- **Recommendation:** treat this whole class as **back-of-house formulation chemistry, not marketing.** **Shortlist Disodium EDTA (or a green chelator) + BHT/tocopherol** as the stabilizer toolkit; **reject BHA / propyl gallate / TBHQ.** Never make a skin-benefit claim from any of them. Log in [`../../ideas/considering.md`](../../ideas/considering.md) (EDTA/BHT, conditional/excipient) and the rest in [`../../ideas/not-considering.md`](../../ideas/not-considering.md) with the reasons above.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

_Verified 2026-06-19 by independent re-checking of cited primary/regulatory sources (PubMed, PMC, IARC, NTP, CIR, SCCS) against every headline claim. Method: each claim tested for (a) HUMAN + TOPICAL vs. borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral/food, (b) whether the cited study/opinion is real and says what is claimed, (c) industry funding / dose context / pro-oxidant flips._

### Overall evidence grade: **split — A for "formula stabilizer" function & hazard data; F for "topical anti-aging benefit."**

- **Stabilizer function (Tier 0): A.** Uncontested, textbook — these delay lipid autoxidation (phenolics) and metal-catalyzed oxidation (EDTA). This is real and the only thing they're "for."
- **Topical anti-aging efficacy (Tier 4): F / absent.** No human topical RCT (or any controlled human topical study) shows any anti-aging benefit for any of the five. The class was never developed as skin actives.
- **In-vitro skin signal (Tier 1): D+ and only for BHT.** Markiewicz 2025 is real, recent, and on-point (MGO glycation, fibroblasts + 3D skin), but in-vitro, industry-authored, and BHT was the weaker/most context-dependent performer vs vitamin C. A watch-item, not a claim basis.
- **Hazard data (Tiers 2–4): strong on the negative side.** BHA carcinogenicity classifications (IARC 2B, NTP, Prop 65) and propyl-gallate sensitization/pro-oxidant data are well established.

### Citation audit — all real, checked
- **Lanigan & Yamarik 2002 (PMID 12396675):** confirmed — CIR BHT safe as used; 0.0002–0.5%; stays mainly in skin; not repro/dev toxicant.
- **Markiewicz 2025 (PMID 40490897, Int J Cosmet Sci 47:935):** confirmed — BHT/vitE/vitC vs MGO in fibroblasts + 3D skin; all inhibited ROS/glycation; vitamin C best at low dose; HexisLab/Estée Lauder COI. Correctly tiered in-vitro.
- **Jacobi 1999 (Toxicol Lett 110:183):** confirmed — PG + Cu(II) >0.125 mM induces fibroblast lipid peroxidation (pro-oxidant flip).
- **Park 2024 (Sci Rep, PMC10912098):** confirmed — PG 100–1600 µM, ROS↑/GSH↓ apoptosis in human (pulmonary) fibroblasts; NAC rescue.
- **IARC Vol 40 (BHA):** confirmed — Group 2B; sufficient animal (forestomach), inadequate human.
- **NTP 15th RoC (BHA, NBK590883):** confirmed — "reasonably anticipated to be a human carcinogen."
- **Williams 2021 (Regul Toxicol Pharmacol):** confirmed — argues rodent-forestomach mechanism limited human relevance (balanced counterweight).
- **CIR EDTA 2002:** confirmed — safe as used; poor dermal absorption; penetration-enhancer note; oral repro effects = zinc depletion.
- **CIR TBHQ 1986:** confirmed — safe ≤0.1%; weak depigmentant 4–5%.
- **Holcomb 2017 (PMID 28169852, Dermatitis 28:115):** confirmed — 74 gallate-allergy cases; propyl gallate dominant; facial/hand/lip dermatitis.
- **SCCS/1636/21 (BHT, 2021):** confirmed — safe to 0.8% leave-on/rinse-off after ED review.
- **SCCS/1682/25 (BHA, 16 Apr 2026):** confirmed — safe up to 0.07% dermal; excludes oral/inhalation; further ED/eco work flagged.
- **FDA BHA food action (Feb 2026):** confirmed via the Federal Register itself (RFI 2026-02761, 11 Feb 2026; comments due 13 Apr 2026) and corroborating coverage (CNN, C&EN). The instrument is a **safety re-assessment / Request for Information, NOT an enacted ban** — earlier framing as "FDA moves to ban" was a news-headline overstatement and has been softened in §1/§8/§11 and citation 23. Food additive, not cosmetics.

### Main caveats a skeptic must hold onto
1. **Category error is the headline risk:** these are excipients. The "antioxidant skincare" benefit must come from hero actives, not from BHT/EDTA on the INCI.
2. **Pro-oxidant duality is real:** propyl gallate (with copper / at higher doses) and the general phenolic-antioxidant literature show these can flip from protector to damager depending on metal context and dose.
3. **BHA is a liability cluster** (IARC/NTP/Prop 65/EU-restricted-to-0.07%/FDA-food-re-assessment-underway) even if 0.07% dermal is technically defensible — avoid for a premium credible brand. Note the FDA action is a reassessment, not yet a ban — don't overstate it in copy.
4. **Propyl gallate is a genuine contact allergen** — wrong choice for leave-on facial care.
5. **EDTA's real downside is environmental persistence,** not skin safety — the case for biodegradable chelators is an eco/values case, not a health one.
6. **The single anti-glycation in-vitro signal (BHT) is not a claim** — in-vitro, industry-authored, BHT the weaker performer; better anti-glycation routes exist in the actives dossiers.

### Bottom line for the brand
The §10–11 verdict — "useful back-of-house stabilizers, never an anti-aging actives claim; use Disodium EDTA (or a green chelator) and BHT/tocopherol; avoid BHA, propyl gallate, TBHQ; substantiate any antioxidant *benefit* on the hero actives" — **survives adversarial checking.** All sources are real and accurately represented; the only soft citations are clearly labelled secondary/formulation references and the (now-corrected) FDA-food re-assessment item. Keep the framing strict: talk about these (if at all) as **product integrity**, in cosmetic excipient language, never as a skin benefit.
