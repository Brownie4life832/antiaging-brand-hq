# Ingredient: Eye-contour peptides — Eyeseryl & Eyeliss complexes (INCI: Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5; Dipeptide-2; Palmitoyl Tetrapeptide-7; Hesperidin Methyl Chalcone)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering (leaning ⚠️ conditional / supporting) · **Class:** Peptides (mostly **enzyme-inhibitor** + **signal/matrikine** + a flavonoid co-active; marketed as "eye-contour / anti-puffiness / anti-dark-circle" peptides)

> **Scope note.** This is a *class* dossier for the family of synthetic peptides sold for the under-eye area, organized around the two flagship branded complexes:
> - **Eyeseryl®** (Lipotec/Lubrizol) = **Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5** alone — an anti-edema / anti-glycation peptide.
> - **Eyeliss™** (Sederma/Croda) = a three-part complex: **Dipeptide-2** (Valyl-Tryptophan) + **Palmitoyl Tetrapeptide-7** (Pal-GQPR) + **Hesperidin Methyl Chalcone** (HMC, a flavonoid, not a peptide).
>
> These are frequently lumped together in "eye peptide" serums (often *also* stacked with Argireline/Acetyl Hexapeptide-8 and Matrixyl 3000). The honest headline up front: **the evidence base is overwhelmingly manufacturer-generated, small, open-label, and confounded by multi-ingredient formulas.** There is essentially **no independent, peptide-isolated, placebo-controlled RCT** for any single component. Treat all the dramatic percentages as supplier marketing until proven otherwise. See [`argireline-neuropeptides.md`](argireline-neuropeptides.md) and [`peptides.md`](peptides.md) for the broader peptide context.

Plain language: lab-made short amino-acid chains (plus a citrus flavonoid) sold to "de-puff" eye bags, fade dark circles, and firm crepey under-eye skin. They are **cosmetics applied to the surface** — not injectables, not drugs. The peptides here are tiny: Dipeptide-2 is just two amino acids; Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5 and Pal-GQPR are four.

**Key derivatives / adjacent actives often co-formulated:** Acetyl Hexapeptide-8 (Argireline), Palmitoyl Tripeptide-1 (the other half of Matrixyl 3000), Acetyl Tetrapeptide-2, Chrysin and N-Hydroxysuccinimide (the "Haloxyl" complex, a separate Sederma dark-circle blend targeting heme breakdown), and caffeine. Eye serums rarely contain just one of these.

## 2. Class & mechanism

Unlike Argireline (a neurotransmitter-inhibiting peptide) these are mostly **enzyme-inhibitor** and **signal/matrikine** peptides, plus a vascular flavonoid. Each component has a *different* proposed mechanism aimed at a *different* cause of the "tired eye" look:

**Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5 (Eyeseryl) — anti-edema + anti-glycation.**
- **Reduces vascular permeability.** In-vitro endothelial assays reported ~50% reduction in vascular permeability at 1 mg/mL, the idea being to stop plasma fluid leaking into the loose periorbital interstitium (the water component of "eye bags").
- **ACE (angiotensin-converting enzyme) inhibition.** Reported to inhibit ACE-1 at ~100 µg/mL; ACE inhibition lowers angiotensin II → less local fluid retention/pressure. (This is a *local cosmetic* claim — it is not, and must not be sold as, a systemic blood-pressure or drug effect.)
- **Anti-glycation.** Histidine-rich; claimed to interfere with advanced glycation end-product (AGE) formation on dermal proteins, protecting collagen/elastin from glycation-induced stiffening (relevant to crepey under-eye texture). Mechanistically plausible (histidine is a known carbonyl scavenger) but demonstrated chiefly in vitro.

**Dipeptide-2 (Val-Trp) — lymphatic drainage via ACE inhibition.** Val-Trp is a well-known dietary ACE-inhibitory dipeptide (it appears in the food-peptide literature). In Eyeliss it is positioned to **improve lymphatic micro-circulation and fluid drainage** from the under-eye area, again targeting the *fluid/puffiness* component.

**Palmitoyl Tetrapeptide-7 (Pal-GQPR) — anti-inflammatory matrikine.** The GQPR sequence derives from an immunoglobulin (IgG) fragment. It **down-regulates IL-6** (interleukin-6, an inflammatory cytokine, including UV-induced IL-6) in keratinocytes/fibroblasts. Less chronic low-grade inflammation → theoretically slower ECM/collagen breakdown ("inflammaging" angle) and firmer, less irritated skin. The palmitoyl (C16 fatty-acid) tail is a **lipidation** strategy to improve lipophilicity and stratum-corneum penetration. It is the same peptide used in **Matrixyl 3000** for general wrinkles.

**Hesperidin Methyl Chalcone (HMC) — capillary stabilizer / venotonic.** A citrus flavonoid used pharmaceutically as a vascular-wall reinforcer; reduces capillary permeability/fragility (manufacturer ex-vivo: ~25% reduction). The rationale: leaky/fragile capillaries contribute to both puffiness (fluid) and the bluish "vascular" type of dark circle.

**The unifying story (and its honesty problem):** Eye bags and dark circles are *multifactorial* — fluid (lymph/vascular), pigment (melanin), vascular show-through (hemoglobin/heme), fat herniation, tear-trough/bony shadowing, and skin laxity. These actives only plausibly touch the **fluid and vascular** levers (and, for Pal-GQPR, mild inflammation/firmness). They **cannot** address fat herniation, bony/structural shadowing, or deep dermal melanin — the components that drive the most stubborn cases. Independent dermatology reviews are explicit that periorbital concerns are "less responsive to standard [topical] therapies due to multifactorial etiology," with fillers/fat grafting and lasers — not creams — rated most effective for the structural/pigment causes.

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

| Hallmark | Relevance to this class | Evidence quality for *topical human* use |
|---|---|---|
| **Hydration / water-loss & local fluid (edema)** | Primary headline target — "de-puffing" via reduced vascular permeability + lymphatic drainage | Weak–moderate; positive but manufacturer/open-label data |
| **Inflammaging** | Pal-GQPR ↓ IL-6 (incl. UV-induced) | In-vitro/mechanistic; not isolated in human topical trials |
| **Glycation / AGEs** | Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5 anti-glycation (histidine) | In-vitro mainly; not demonstrated in periorbital skin clinically |
| **Collagen / ECM loss** | Indirect: less inflammation + less glycation → claimed firmer, more elastic under-eye skin | Weak; firmness/elasticity numbers are manufacturer-reported |
| **Pigmentation / tone (dark circles)** | Only the *vascular* sub-type plausibly (capillary stabilization, less heme leakage); does **not** target melanin | Weak; dark-circle improvement claims are largely subjective/manufacturer |
| **Barrier decline** | Minor (HMC claimed to support barrier); not a core action | Weak |
| Photoaging (static wrinkles) | Minor/indirect via IL-6 and firmness; this is an *eye-area* play, not a photoaging hero | Weak |
| Oxidative stress | Minor (HMC antioxidant; histidine) | In-vitro |
| Cellular senescence, mitochondrial decline, microbiome | **Not targeted** | None |

Bottom line: this class is a **narrow, cosmetic "appearance of tired eyes" toolkit** (puffiness, crepiness, vascular dark circles). It engages the *fluid*, *inflammaging*, and *glycation* hallmarks only superficially and does **not** touch the deeper longevity hallmarks (senescence, mitochondria, robust dermal collagen synthesis) that anchor the brand's skin-longevity / barrier-repair thesis.

## 4. Evidence review by tier  *(the core)*

> **Honest headline:** The published, peer-reviewed evidence for these specific peptides, *as isolated topical actives*, is **thin to absent**. The widely-quoted percentages (29.7% puffiness reduction, 70%/95% responders, 30% elasticity, etc.) trace to **manufacturer (Lipotec/Sederma) in-house studies and supplier brochures**, not independent journals. The only solid peer-reviewed human data come from **multi-ingredient eye serums/creams** where these peptides are stacked with Argireline, Matrixyl 3000, ferments, etc. — so the peptide's *individual* contribution **cannot be isolated**. There is **no independent placebo-controlled RCT of Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5, Dipeptide-2, or Palmitoyl Tetrapeptide-7 alone** that I could verify.

### Tier 1 — In vitro / mechanistic
- **Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5:** ~50% reduction of **vascular permeability** at 1 mg/mL (endothelial assay); **ACE-1 inhibition** at ~100 µg/mL; **anti-glycation** activity (histidine-mediated AGE interference). *Source: manufacturer (Lipotec/Lubrizol) technical data and ingredient databases; mechanistically plausible but originator-generated.* [unverified — primary peer-reviewed paper not located; figures appear only in supplier literature and secondary ingredient encyclopedias]
- **Dipeptide-2 (Val-Trp):** ACE-inhibitory activity is well established in the **food/nutraceutical peptide literature** (Val-Trp is a classic ACE-inhibitory dipeptide). That biochemistry is real; its *translation to lymphatic drainage in topical periorbital skin* is the unproven leap.
- **Palmitoyl Tetrapeptide-7:** down-regulation of **IL-6** in keratinocytes/fibroblasts (incl. UV-stimulated) — Sederma/secondary-literature data; consistent with the matrikine concept and reported across peptide reviews. [partly unverified — exact primary citation is Sederma in-house]
- **Hesperidin Methyl Chalcone:** ex-vivo ~25% reduction in capillary permeability (manufacturer); flavonoid antioxidant/anti-inflammatory activity is broadly documented in the flavonoid literature.

*Limitation:* in-vitro plausibility ≠ clinical proof, and several of the headline numbers cannot be traced to a peer-reviewed primary source.

### Tier 2 — Animal
- **None located** specific to these peptides for periorbital/edema endpoints. (Pal-GQPR/IL-6 work is cell-based, not animal.) Marketing does not lean on animal data here.

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL
- **Not applicable as a cosmetic use case.** (Side note: Val-Trp and related ACE-inhibitory peptides *are* studied as oral antihypertensive food peptides — a completely different format/use; **none of that transfers to topical eye-cream efficacy** and must never be cited as if it did.)

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL  *(the only tier that matters for our product)*

**4a. Manufacturer / supplier studies — the source of every famous percentage (low rigor)**
- **Eyeseryl (Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5), Lipotec in-vivo study:** ~20 female volunteers (18–65), a cream with **10% Eyeseryl solution = ~0.01% actual peptide**, twice daily. Reported: **70% of volunteers improved by day 15, 95% by day 60**; **up to ~29.7% reduction in under-eye bag volume at 28 days**; **~30% increase in periorbital skin elasticity at 30 days**. *Limitations:* tiny n, **open-label / no published placebo arm**, manufacturer-run, not in a peer-reviewed journal; outcomes part-subjective. The "0.01% pure peptide" point matters — the *active* dose is extraordinarily low.
- **Eyeliss (Dipeptide-2 + Pal-GQPR + HMC), Sederma in-vivo study:** ~20 panelists, **3% Eyeliss**, ~56 days. Reported: **~65% improved at 28 days, ~70% at 56 days**; self-assessment **~62% smoother eye contour, ~52% reduced bags**. *Limitations:* small, manufacturer-run, sample sizes/controls/statistics not fully disclosed; can't separate the three components.
- Recommended use levels (supplier): **Eyeliss up to 3%**; Eyeseryl typically a few % of solution (→ ~0.01% peptide).

**4b. Peer-reviewed human data — but only on MULTI-ingredient formulas (cannot isolate the peptide)**
- **Li F, et al. 2023.** *J Cosmet Dermatol.* 22(12):3236–3245. DOI: 10.1111/jocd.15849 (PMID **37335808**). **32 Asian women (20–45)**, a commercial multi-peptide eye serum used AM/PM × **28 days**, measured with PRIMOS/objective imaging. **The serum contained 10% Argireline + 4% Matrixyl 3000 (Palmitoyl Tetrapeptide-7 + Palmitoyl Tripeptide-1) + 2% Eyeliss (Dipeptide-2 + Pal-GQPR + HMC).** Results: number of crow's-feet wrinkles ↓ ~**31.8% (D14)** and ~**33.3% (D28)**; significant ↓ in wrinkle number/depth/volume by D28; hydration, elasticity, firmness rose. No adverse events. *Limitations:* **no placebo/vehicle control**, single-arm, short, and — critically — **the formula bundles Argireline + Matrixyl 3000 + Eyeliss together, so it cannot attribute any effect to Dipeptide-2/Pal-GQPR/Eyeliss specifically.** It demonstrates a *finished multi-peptide serum* works on crow's-feet, not that *these eye-contour peptides* do.
- **Yang/Y. et al. 2024.** *Skin Res Technol.* 30(6):e13790. DOI: 10.1111/srt.13790 (PMID **38932444**). Multi-component anti-aging eye **cream** (yeast/rice ferment filtrate, N-acetylneuraminic acid, **Palmitoyl Tripeptide-1 + Palmitoyl Tetrapeptide-7**), 12 weeks. Reported subject-assessed **~73% ↓ puffiness, ~93% ↓ dark circles**, plus measured hydration +28%, elasticity +19%, "collagen" +55%. *Limitations:* again **multi-ingredient, not peptide-isolated**, dramatic subject-reported figures, manufacturer-flavored; the ferment filtrate and sialic acid could drive much of the effect. Does not isolate Pal-GQPR.
- Numerous other "eye serum/cream" trials (e.g., the multi-peptide and ferment formulas, TriHex-peptide eye treatments) show improvements but **all share the same fatal confound**: they test finished blends, not the individual eye-contour peptides.

**4c. The independent counterweight (be honest)**
- **Systematic reviews of periorbital concerns** consistently rate **topical** treatments as *modest/low-evidence* for dark circles and bags, and rate **structural interventions (fillers, fat grafting, lasers)** as most effective for the volume/pigment causes. E.g., the periorbital-hyperpigmentation systematic review (Sarkar/colleagues; PubMed **32740208**, *Dermatol Surg* 2020, 39 studies) and a 2025 systematic review/meta-analysis of dark-circle treatments (Pour Mohammad et al., *Dermatol Ther* 2025, DOI 10.1155/dth/9155535) both conclude POH is multifactorial and **poorly responsive to topicals alone**, with the strongest data for procedures, not peptides. Peptides are not singled out as evidence-backed standouts.
- General peptide reviews (e.g., Schagen 2017, *Cosmetics* 4(2):16; Ferreira et al. and the "Synthetic peptides for sensitive skin" review, *Pharmaceuticals* 2021, PMC8400021) note that for many marketed cosmetic peptides — **explicitly including the eye-contour ones** — most efficacy information sits in **patents and supplier brochures, not peer-reviewed journals**, and that independent replication is lacking.

**Tier-4 verdict:** For **topical human use**, benefits are **plausible but unproven at the level of the individual peptide.** Every famous percentage is manufacturer-sourced; every peer-reviewed positive result is from a **multi-ingredient blend** that can't credit these peptides specifically; and independent reviews judge topicals weak for the underlying conditions. Honest grade for the isolated actives: **C− / D+ (plausible, well-tolerated, but not independently substantiated).** A finished, well-formulated eye serum *containing* them can still produce a real, measurable cosmetic improvement (Li 2023) — that's a product-level claim, not an ingredient-level proof.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

The under-eye barrier is *thin*, which helps, but the peptides differ sharply:
- **Dipeptide-2 (~303 Da)** is small enough to have a realistic chance of some epidermal penetration — small peptides cross the stratum corneum more readily than large ones. But it is hydrophilic.
- **Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5 (~492 Da)** — acetylation modestly improves stability/lipophilicity vs. a bare peptide; penetration is plausible but **not well quantified** in independent data.
- **Palmitoyl Tetrapeptide-7 (~695 Da)** uses **palmitoylation** specifically to boost lipophilicity and stratum-corneum transit — the standard industry trick (same as Matrixyl peptides). Still, large lipopeptides face delivery limits.
- **HMC** is a small flavonoid with reasonable skin compatibility (the methyl-chalcone modification improves stability/penetration over raw hesperidin).

**Honest caveat:** there is **no rigorous independent permeation study** quantifying how much of any of these reaches the dermis/periorbital capillaries at use levels — and the *actual* peptide dose is very low (Eyeseryl ≈ 0.01% peptide). The mechanisms require reaching capillaries/lymphatics/fibroblasts; whether enough peptide gets there topically is **assumed, not demonstrated.** As with most cosmetic peptides, penetration is the soft underbelly of the whole story.

## 6. Formulation

| Parameter | Guidance |
|---|---|
| Effective concentration | **Eyeliss: up to ~3%** of the trade complex (supplier rec). **Eyeseryl: ~3–10%** of the solution (→ ~0.003–0.01% actual peptide). Pal-GQPR via Matrixyl 3000 typically 2–4% of the blend. |
| pH | Mildly acidic to neutral (~5.0–6.5) suits peptide stability and skin tolerance; histidine-containing Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5 is pH-sensitive — avoid extremes. |
| Solubility / phase | Dipeptide-2 & Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5 are water-soluble; Pal-GQPR is lipidized (oil-dispersible); HMC needs solubilizing. Eyeliss/Eyeseryl are sold as ready emulsion-compatible solutions. |
| Stability | Reasonable in well-preserved aqueous/emulsion systems; protect from heat and oxidation; standard preservation essential (aqueous = microbial risk; eye-area products demand tight micro control). |
| Pairings (sensible) | Caffeine (vasoconstriction/de-puff), niacinamide (barrier/tone), HA & humectants, Haloxyl-type heme actives for *vascular* dark circles, vitamin K, low-irritation antioxidants, ceramides/barrier lipids for crepey skin. Often co-formulated with Argireline + Matrixyl 3000 (as in Li 2023). |
| Conflicts | Strong low-pH actives (potent AHA/BHA, L-ascorbic acid) in the *same step* can destabilize/charge-disrupt peptides and irritate thin eye skin; high-ionic-strength systems can salt out charged peptides. Keep potent acids/retinoids in a separate routine step. |
| Sensory | Light, water-thin to gel; pleasant in eye serums. Eye-area = fragrance-free, low-irritant, ophthalmologically-tested formats; avoid stinging actives and essential oils near the eye. |

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation / sensitization:** Generally **excellent tolerability** at cosmetic levels; peptides here are low-irritant and the reported studies note no significant adverse events. Eye-area products still warrant ophthalmologist/HRIPT-style testing and fragrance-free formulation because periorbital skin is thin and reactive; rare milia/irritation reported are usually base-related, not peptide-related.
- **Sun sensitivity:** None known; not photosensitizers. (HMC is mildly antioxidant.)
- **Pregnancy / breastfeeding:** No specific human safety studies for these peptides in pregnancy. Systemic absorption is expected to be negligible, so topical use is generally considered low-concern — but with **no pregnancy-specific evidence, make no pregnancy-safety claim**; defer to "consult your doctor."
- **ACE-inhibition caution (framing):** Although Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5 and Dipeptide-2 inhibit ACE in vitro, topical periorbital use is a **local cosmetic** effect; there is no evidence of systemic ACE/blood-pressure effects from a cosmetic, and we must **never imply a drug/cardiovascular action**.
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** Peptides are fully synthetic (solid-phase synthesis) → vegan-compatible, no animal/wild sourcing concerns. HMC is citrus-derived then chemically modified. Footprint is ordinary synthetic-chemistry manufacturing.

## 8. Regulatory & claims  *(per CLAUDE.md guardrails)*

- **Status:** Cosmetic ingredients in **EU** (Reg. (EC) 1223/2009) and **US** (FDA cosmetic unless claims cross into "drug"). "Cosmeceutical" has **no legal standing**.
- **The drug line.** Anti-edema/"reduces fluid," "ACE inhibitor," "improves lymphatic drainage," "reduces vascular permeability," and especially **dark-circle/eye-bag "treatment"** language can drift toward **structure/function (drug) claims** and toward **unsubstantiated efficacy** (since we lack independent proof). Keep everything **appearance-based**.
- ❌ Do NOT say: "treats/eliminates eye bags," "ACE inhibitor / reduces vascular permeability" (mechanism-of-the-body, drug-flavored), "drains lymph," "removes dark circles," "boosts collagen by 55%," "reduces puffiness by 29.7% / 73%" (manufacturer figures we can't substantiate), "Botox/filler alternative."
- ✅ Defensible cosmetic language: "helps **visibly reduce the look of** puffiness," "helps the eye area look **less tired / more refreshed**," "**smooths the appearance of** crepiness and fine lines," "helps **brighten the look of** the under-eye area," "**supports** firmer-looking eye-area skin." Appearance-only, no body-mechanism claims, no efficacy percentages unless we run and substantiate our own study on **our** finished formula.
- **Dark-circle claims are the riskiest** — because the condition is multifactorial and topicals are weak for it, any "fades dark circles" promise is both substantiation-risky and likely to disappoint vascular/pigment/structural cases. If used, keep to "**helps the under-eye area look brighter/more even**" and consider before/after photography on our own panel.
- Any mechanism word ("ACE," "lymphatic," "vascular," "edema") or any percentage → **flag for legal/substantiation** before use.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

| Marketing claim | Reality |
|---|---|
| "Clinically proven to reduce puffiness 29.7% / 73%" | Numbers are **manufacturer/open-label** (Eyeseryl Lipotec; multi-ingredient cream) — small n, no published placebo arm; not independently replicated. |
| "Eyeliss erases eye bags" | Sederma in-house data on ~20 panelists; can't isolate the three components; structural/fat-driven bags are **not** addressable topically. |
| "Fades dark circles" | Dark circles are **multifactorial** (pigment, vascular, structural shadow); these actives only plausibly touch the *vascular/fluid* sub-type; systematic reviews rate **topicals weak** and procedures (fillers/lasers) strongest. |
| "ACE inhibitor / boosts lymphatic drainage" | In-vitro/mechanistic and (for Val-Trp) borrowed from **oral food-peptide** science; not demonstrated for topical periorbital skin, and drug-flavored language to avoid. |
| "Boosts collagen / +55% collagen" | From a **multi-ingredient** eye cream (ferment + sialic acid + peptides), not these peptides isolated; subject/measure-flavored. |
| "Penetrates to the capillaries/lymphatics" | No rigorous independent permeation proof; actual peptide dose is very low (~0.01% for Eyeseryl). |
| Peer-reviewed RCTs exist | The peer-reviewed studies (Li 2023; Yang 2024) test **finished multi-peptide blends**, not the individual eye peptides — product-level evidence, not ingredient-level. |

This is a **moderately over-claimed** category: the mechanisms are reasonable and tolerability is good, but the gap between "clinically proven 70–95% improvement" marketing and the *independent, isolated-ingredient* evidence is large. The dark-circle claims are the most overreaching relative to what topicals can actually do.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**⚠️ CONDITIONAL — at most a SUPPORTING "eye-area appearance" set, never a hero, and only if we choose to play in eye products at all.**

One-line reasoning: The mechanisms (anti-edema, capillary stabilization, anti-inflammatory matrikine) are plausible and the peptides are well-tolerated, but **there is no independent, isolated-ingredient proof** — every strong number is manufacturer-generated or from a confounded multi-peptide blend — and the underlying concerns (bags, dark circles) are multifactorial and weakly responsive to topicals, so these earn a quiet **supporting** role with appearance-only claims, not hero billing or any percentage/mechanism promise.

**Longevity-relevance note:** Low. This class targets the *cosmetic look of tired eyes* (fluid, crepiness, vascular tone) and only superficially touches inflammaging and glycation. It does **not** engage the durable skin-longevity hallmarks (senescence, mitochondrial decline, robust dermal collagen synthesis, barrier-lipid repair) that anchor the brand's barrier-repair / skin-longevity thesis. If we ever build a dedicated eye product, a sensible stack would be **barrier lipids + niacinamide + caffeine + one of these peptide complexes (Eyeliss most defensible) + good photoprotection habits**, marketed on "looks more rested," not "treats eye bags/dark circles." Spend the hero narrative and budget on better-evidenced actives (retinoids, niacinamide, ceramides, vitamin C, NAD/longevity actives); keep eye-contour peptides as an optional supporting note.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

*Verified 2026-06-19 against ingredient databases (INCIDecoder, ci.guide, ChemicalBook), PeptideJournal, the peer-reviewed multi-peptide eye trials (Li 2023 PMID 37335808; Yang 2024 PMID 38932444), peptide reviews (Schagen 2017; Pharmaceuticals 2021 PMC8400021), and dermatology systematic reviews on periorbital hyperpigmentation (PMID 32740208; Dermatol Ther 2025).*

### Overall evidence grade: **C− / D+ for the isolated peptides (plausible, well-tolerated, not independently substantiated); ~C for a finished multi-peptide eye serum (single-arm peer-reviewed signal)**

For the **only tier that matters (human, topical, objective endpoint)**, no study isolates Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5, Dipeptide-2, or Palmitoyl Tetrapeptide-7 against placebo. The strongest peer-reviewed human data (Li 2023) is a **single-arm** test of a serum that **bundles Argireline + Matrixyl 3000 + Eyeliss**, so it proves a *product*, not these ingredients. The famous percentages are manufacturer/open-label. Independent dermatology reviews rate topicals weak for the target conditions. Hence a low grade for the ingredients themselves, edging up only because tolerability is excellent and the mechanisms are biochemically reasonable.

### What checks out
- **Chemistry verified:** Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5 = Ac-βAla-His-Ser-His, CAS 820959-17-9; Dipeptide-2 = Val-Trp, CAS 24587-37-9, MW ~303; Palmitoyl Tetrapeptide-7 = Pal-GQPR, CAS 221227-05-0, MW ~695; HMC CAS 24292-52-2. Cross-checked across ChemicalBook and multiple databases.
- **Complex compositions verified:** Eyeliss = HMC + Dipeptide-2 + Pal-GQPR (Sederma); Eyeseryl = Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5 (Lipotec/Lubrizol). Multiple concordant sources.
- **Li 2023 is real** (PMID 37335808; DOI 10.1111/jocd.15849) and its formula composition (10% Argireline / 4% Matrixyl 3000 / 2% Eyeliss) is as described — confirming the multi-ingredient confound.
- **Yang 2024 is real** (PMID 38932444; Skin Res Technol; DOI 10.1111/srt.13790).
- **Mechanism-borrowing critique is correct:** Val-Trp's ACE-inhibition pedigree is from oral food-peptide science; vascular-permeability/ACE/IL-6 numbers for the cosmetic actives are in-vitro/manufacturer.

### Errors / over-certainty to avoid (built into the body)
1. **Do not present any percentage as independently proven.** All headline numbers are manufacturer/open-label or from confounded blends — flagged throughout and in §10/§12.
2. **Do not credit the peptides for the peer-reviewed results.** Li 2023 and Yang 2024 test blends; the peptide-specific contribution is unknowable from them.
3. **Carbonic anhydrase mechanism (sometimes cited for "eye" peptides) was NOT confirmed** for Acetyl Tetrapeptide-5 — the supported mechanisms are ACE inhibition, vascular-permeability reduction, and anti-glycation. The dossier deliberately omits carbonic-anhydrase as a mechanism.
4. **In-vitro numbers (50% vascular permeability @1 mg/mL; ACE-1 @100 µg/mL; ~25% HMC capillary permeability) are supplier-sourced** and marked [unverified] where a peer-reviewed primary could not be located.
5. **Dark-circle efficacy is the most over-claimed** — corrected against systematic-review evidence that topicals are weak and the condition multifactorial.

### Main caveats to carry forward
- **No isolated-ingredient RCT exists** — this is the central honesty point; never imply otherwise.
- **The category is multifactorial and topical-resistant** (bags/dark circles) — manage expectations and claims accordingly.
- **Actual active doses are tiny** (Eyeseryl ≈ 0.01% peptide), and penetration to the relevant targets is assumed, not proven.
- For the brand, this is a **supporting/optional** eye-area set with **appearance-only** claims — not a hero, not a longevity lever.
