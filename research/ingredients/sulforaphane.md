# Ingredient: Sulforaphane / Broccoli Sprout Extract (INCI: Sulforaphane; / Brassica Oleracea Italica (Broccoli) Sprout Extract; precursor Glucoraphanin)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering (conditional — see verdict) · ⬜ shortlisted · ⬜ rejected · ⬜ in a product
**Class:** Autophagy & mitochondrial-targeted actives (NRF2 / antioxidant-response activator) · **Slug:** `sulforaphane`

> **One-line honesty flag up front:** Sulforaphane (SFN) is the *textbook* NRF2 activator and has a genuinely strong, *independent, peer-reviewed* human topical proof-of-concept — Talalay's PNAS 2007 study showed ~38% reduction in UV-induced erythema after topical broccoli-sprout extract. But that landmark result is **mechanistic proof (it switches on the skin's own antioxidant genes), not a finished cosmetic efficacy story**: it's n=6, the endpoint is acute redness not wrinkles, and almost the entire larger SFN human literature is **oral/systemic** (cancer, brain, airway), not skin. On top of that, **SFN is one of the least stable cosmetic actives that exists** — it fully degrades in a normal cream within ~30 days — and the cheap "broccoli sprout extract" most brands use is the *inert precursor* (glucoraphanin) that needs the enzyme **myrosinase** to become active SFN, which skin does not reliably supply. Mechanistically beautiful; formulation-hostile. Treat accordingly.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common name:** Sulforaphane (SFN; also "sulphoraphane," 1-isothiocyanato-4-(methylsulfinyl)butane). An **isothiocyanate**.
- **INCI:** **Sulforaphane** (the isolated active). More commonly on labels as **Brassica Oleracea Italica (Broccoli) Sprout Extract**, **Brassica Oleracea Italica Seed Extract**, or as the precursor **Glucoraphanin** (a glucosinolate). Note these are NOT interchangeable in activity — see §5.
- **Chemistry:** C₆H₁₁NOS₂; an aliphatic isothiocyanate bearing a reactive –N=C=S group and a methylsulfinyl tail. CAS **4478-93-7** (R-enantiomer, the natural one). Small (MW ~177), lipophilic, volatile, and chemically reactive (electrophile).
- **Natural origin:** SFN is **not present pre-formed** in intact broccoli. It is generated when the glucosinolate **glucoraphanin** is hydrolyzed by the plant enzyme **myrosinase** upon tissue damage (chewing, chopping, sprout disruption). **3-day-old broccoli sprouts** are the richest practical source (10–100× the glucoraphanin of mature heads) — the basis of the Johns Hopkins work. [1][2][7]
- **Key related forms / metabolites:** Glucoraphanin (inert precursor); in the body SFN is conjugated by glutathione and excreted as **mercapturic-acid (dithiocarbamate) metabolites** (SFN-GSH, SFN-cysteine, SFN-NAC). Commercial actives include **stabilized synthetic SFN**, **glucoraphanin + active myrosinase** systems, and branded broccoli-sprout/seed extracts. Note the standalone INCI "Sulforaphane" and the patent literature on stabilization (WO2012010587A1). [10][11]

## 2. Class & mechanism

Sulforaphane is the **prototypical, most-studied small-molecule NRF2 activator** in all of biology — it is the positive control other NRF2 inducers are measured against.

- **Primary mechanism — KEAP1–NRF2–ARE activation.** SFN's reactive isothiocyanate group **covalently modifies reactive cysteine residues on KEAP1** (notably Cys151), the repressor that normally tags the transcription factor **NRF2** for degradation. This disables KEAP1-CUL3 ubiquitin-ligase activity, **stabilizes NRF2**, which translocates to the nucleus and binds **Antioxidant Response Elements (ARE)** to switch on a battery of **phase-2 cytoprotective / antioxidant genes** — NQO1, glutathione-S-transferases (GST), heme oxygenase-1 (HO-1), glutamate-cysteine ligase (GCL, the rate-limiting glutathione enzyme), thioredoxin, peroxiredoxins. [1][3][8][9]
- **The conceptual distinction that matters for skincare:** classic topical antioxidants (vitamin C/E) are **stoichiometric** — one molecule quenches roughly one radical, then is consumed. SFN is a **catalytic / indirect antioxidant** — it doesn't neutralize radicals itself; it reprograms the cell to make *its own* long-lived antioxidant and detox enzymes. One SFN "hit" yields a sustained (days-long) protective transcriptional state. This is the "internal sunscreen" pitch. [1][8]
- **Secondary mechanisms relevant to skin:**
  - **Anti-inflammatory** — suppresses NF-κB signaling and pro-inflammatory cytokines; relevant to inflammaging, UV-erythema, radiation dermatitis. [8][13]
  - **MMP downregulation / collagen preservation** — in aging skin models SFN lowered **MMP-9** and improved collagen deposition (via NRF2/reduced ROS). [4]
  - **Anti-melanogenic** — NRF2 activation **negatively regulates melanogenesis** (↓ tyrosinase, ↓ TRP-1); SFN suppressed melanin and tyrosinase in melanocyte/keratinocyte models. [5][6][14][15]
  - **Senescence / SASP modulation** — claimed via NRF2 restoration of redox balance; supported mechanistically in reviews but with little direct skin-senescence data. [9][12]
  - **Chemoprotection / DNA defense** — long studied as a cancer-chemopreventive (phase-2 induction reduces carcinogen activation); the origin of the whole field. [1][7]

**Plain-language:** Most antioxidants are firefighters who each douse one flame and then quit. Sulforaphane is the building inspector who flips on the whole sprinkler system — it tells skin cells to manufacture their own antioxidant and repair enzymes, and that protection lasts for days. The catch: the molecule itself is twitchy and falls apart in a jar, and the "broccoli sprout extract" on most labels is the *off* switch (glucoraphanin) that skin can't reliably flip *on*.

## 4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

> **Read this before believing any marketing.** SFN's evidence pyramid is unusual: it has a *credible, independent human topical mechanistic proof* (rare among botanicals) AND a *huge but almost entirely non-skin* human literature. The trap is letting the cancer/brain/airway oral RCTs imply a cream works. I separate "proven for the format you'd sell (topical)" from everything borrowed.

### Tier 1 — In-vitro (cell studies) — *abundant, consistent, mechanistically clean*

- **Keap1-cysteine / NRF2-ARE biochemistry** — SFN is the canonical NRF2 inducer; covalent KEAP1-Cys modification → ARE-driven NQO1/HO-1/GST/GCL is established across countless cell systems. [1][3][8] This is as solid as cell mechanism gets.
- **Ko et al. (2020), *Phytomedicine* 77:153276 (PMID 32659677; DOI 10.1016/j.phymed.2020.153276).** Keratinocyte/melanocyte/fibroblast coculture + **PM2.5 (particulate-matter) premature-aging** model. SFN **↓ ROS in keratinocytes**, **suppressed melanogenesis** (via reduced keratinocyte paracrine factors), **↓ inflammatory markers**, and **promoted collagen synthesis in fibroblasts** — i.e. one active hitting pigment + collagen + inflammation in a pollution model. Cell model only. [13]
- **Melanogenesis cell work** — SFN inhibited melanin synthesis and tyrosinase expression in B16 melanoma (Shirasugi et al., 2010, PMID 20208349) via ERK↑/p38↓; **5 µM SFN ≈ 100 µM arbutin** for melanin inhibition. NRF2 overexpression independently lowers tyrosinase/TRP-1 (Jian et al., 2014, PLoS One, PMC3999113). [5][6][14][15]
- **Prdx6 / antioxidant reactivation** — Gao et al. (2017), *Sci Rep* 7:14520 (DOI 10.1038/s41598-017-14520-8): SFN reactivated NRF2/ARE/peroxiredoxin-6 antioxidant defense in aging/oxidatively-stressed cells. [9]
- **Honest caveat:** at least one report notes SFN **failed** to protect fibroblasts from *repeated* H₂O₂ insults — the catalytic-antioxidant effect is not unconditional, and high SFN concentrations are themselves pro-oxidant/cytotoxic (the same reactivity that activates NRF2 is dose-dependently toxic). [12]

**Verdict on Tier 1:** Mechanism is among the best-characterized of any cosmetic active, reproduced across independent labs and skin-relevant cell types (keratinocytes, melanocytes, fibroblasts). But in-vitro concentrations/exposure don't translate to a cream on intact stratum corneum, and SFN's reactivity cuts both ways.

### Tier 2 — Animal — *photoprotection + oral skin-aging signals*

- **Dinkova-Kostova / Talalay et al. (2007), *Cancer Epidemiol Biomarkers Prev* 16(4):847-851 (PMID 17416783; DOI 10.1158/1055-9965.EPI-06-0934).** Topical broccoli-sprout extract **induced NQO1, GST-A1, HO-1** (phase-2 enzymes) in **mouse epidermis** and **protected against UVR-induced inflammation/edema** in mice — the animal arm that anchored the human work. [3]
- **Petkovic et al. (2021), *J Nutr Biochem* 98:108817 (DOI 10.1016/j.jnutbio.2021.108817; PMID 34271100).** **Dietary (oral)** SFN in young (2-mo) and old (21-mo) male C57BL/6 mice, 3 months. Old SFN-fed mice: **↑ Nrf2/NQO1/HO-1 mRNA**, **↓ ROS**, **↓ MMP-9 protein**, and **improved/structurally restored collagen deposition**. Conclusion: dietary SFN ameliorates skin aging via NRF2. **Oral route, mouse — not a topical cream.** [4]
- **Franklin et al. (2014)** transgenic-mouse arm: topical SFN in a **PEG base significantly reduced UV-induced AP-1 activation** in skin — confirming a *properly formulated* topical SFN retains biological activity. [10]

**Verdict on Tier 2:** Coherent and supportive across photoprotection (topical, mouse) and skin-aging (oral, mouse). The oral mouse skin-aging result is encouraging mechanistically but is the wrong *route* for a topical SKU.

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL / systemic — *large, but almost none of it is skin*

This is where SFN's reputation comes from — and where the over-claiming risk lives. **Include for credibility/context, not as skin proof.**

- **Clinical-trial landscape (Saito et al., 2025, *J Nutr Sci* DOI 10.1017/jns.2025.10033; PMID 40988712):** **84 registered SFN/broccoli-extract trials** on ClinicalTrials.gov, **only 39 published (~46%)**. Distribution: healthy subjects 29, cancer 20, brain disorders 19, respiratory 5, metabolic/CV 3, infectious 2, misc 6. The authors' blunt summary: "limited sample sizes and inconsistent outcomes," "only a few have successfully achieved their primary outcomes," ~50% of completed trials unpublished, and "making definitive recommendations…is challenging." **Essentially the entire body is non-dermatological and oral.** [16]
- **Bioavailability / PK (Fahey et al., 2015, *PLoS One* 10(11):e0140963; PMC4629881):** oral SFN bioavailability is **highly variable (1–40% conversion** of glucoraphanin), and **active myrosinase 3–4× the bioavailability** vs glucoraphanin alone; gastric acidity attenuates it. Establishes that "broccoli sprout" delivery is enzyme- and metabotype-dependent. [2][11]
- **Airway / NRF2 proof-of-concept (oral)** — multiple small RCTs (e.g. COPD/asthma; NCT01335971) tested whether oral broccoli-sprout SFN induces NRF2 targets in airway; results were **mixed/often null in disease endpoints** despite the strong mechanism — a cautionary tale that NRF2 induction in a dish ≠ clinical benefit. [16]

**Verdict on Tier 3:** The molecule's systemic biology is real and heavily studied, but the human evidence is sample-limited, ~half unpublished, frequently misses primary endpoints, and **is overwhelmingly NOT about skin.** None of it can sell a cream.

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL — *the only tier that matters for a SKU; thin but, unusually, partly independent and peer-reviewed*

**Three relevant human topical datasets exist. Two are real and published; the efficacy is mechanistic/acute, not anti-wrinkle.**

1. **Talalay, Fahey, Dinkova-Kostova et al. (2007), *PNAS* 104(44):17500-17505 (DOI 10.1073/pnas.0708710104).** "Sulforaphane mobilizes cellular defenses that protect skin against damage by UV radiation." **n=6** healthy adults (3M/3F, 28–53 y). Topical SFN-rich broccoli-sprout extract, then narrow-band 311-nm UVR at 6 doses (300–800 mJ/cm²). **Mean erythema reduction 37.7% (range 8.4–78.1%; P=0.025)** at treated vs untreated sites. **This is the landmark independent human topical result.** Critical caveats: (i) **n=6** — proof-of-concept, not a powered efficacy trial; (ii) endpoint is **acute UV redness**, a *mechanistic readout of NRF2 protection*, NOT a wrinkle/firmness/anti-aging endpoint; (iii) huge inter-individual variability (8–78%); (iv) it's a *broccoli-sprout extract*, with the enzyme/precursor caveats below. [1]
2. **Dinkova-Kostova/Talalay et al. (2007), *CEBP* 16(4):847-851** (also Tier 2): human-skin punch-biopsy arm showed **dose-dependent NQO1 enzyme induction** in human skin and a safety study **no adverse reactions up to 340 nmol SFN**. Confirms topical SFN reaches living human skin and switches on phase-2 genes. [3]
3. **Johns Hopkins skin-fragility / hyperpigmentation pilot (NCT03126539):** "Effect of Topical Sulforaphane on Skin Fragility Seen in Skin Aging and With Ultraviolet Exposure." Phase-2, **TERMINATED for administrative reasons**, **n=9 actual.** Intervention: topical SFN **500 nmol in jojoba oil**, nightly up to 7 nights. Posted result for *mottled hyperpigmentation* change score (0–4, higher=more improvement): **SFN 2.8±0.4 vs placebo 0.94±0.2 vs untreated 0±0; no adverse events.** Directionally positive for tone, but **n=9, single-week, terminated, planned UV arm never enrolled** — preliminary at best. [17]
4. **Radiation-dermatitis trial (NCT00894712, JHU/Sidney Kimmel):** topical broccoli-sprout SFN (~280 nmol, 3×/week, acetone vehicle) during breast-RT; **completed 2012 but NO results posted / no publication located** → counts as **[unverified outcome]**. [18]
5. **NCT02592954** (cited in [16]): topical SFN 500 nmol/mL ×7 days, **n=5**, showed **↑KRT17, ↑total & phospho-NRF2** in skin — again *mechanistic* (target engagement), not a clinical efficacy endpoint.
6. **Successor trial NCT03730649 (JHU):** "Effect of Topical Sulforaphane on Skin Aging and With Ultraviolet and Visible Light Exposure" — the **replacement** study the terminated NCT03126539 was wound down to start. Topical broccoli-sprout extract in jojoba oil, **nightly up to 6 months** (a far longer course than any other topical trial), **n=12**, primary endpoints **keratin-16/-17** (skin-fragility markers, not wrinkle/firmness). Status **active, not recruiting; NO results posted** → **[unverified outcome]**. Worth tracking: it is the only longer-duration human topical SFN study, but until it publishes it adds *active research interest*, not evidence. [17]

**Verdict on Tier 4:** SFN is better off than most botanicals here — it has an **independent, peer-reviewed, placebo-controlled human topical proof that it engages NRF2 in human skin and cuts UV erythema (~38%)**. But every topical human study is **tiny (n=5–9), short, and measures mechanism/acute redness/pigment**, not the wrinkle-depth/firmness endpoints a "skin-longevity" cream implies. There is **no powered topical RCT for visible anti-aging.**

**Explicit borrowed-vs-proven map:**
- *Proven for topical human use (peer-reviewed but small):* NRF2/phase-2 (NQO1) induction in living human skin; ~38% reduction in acute UV-induced erythema; good short-term tolerability up to ~340–500 nmol.
- *Preliminary topical (pilot/terminated):* mottled-hyperpigmentation improvement (n=9).
- *Borrowed from oral/cells/animal (do NOT claim for a cream):* "boosts collagen," "reduces MMPs," "anti-cancer/chemoprotective," "detoxifies," "reduces inflammation systemically," senescence/mitochondrial/longevity benefits, anti-pollution.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **Pure SFN penetrates and works.** It's small (MW 177) and lipophilic; the 2007 human biopsy data (NQO1 induction) and AP-1 suppression in mouse skin [10] prove topical SFN reaches viable epidermis and engages its target. Penetration is not the limiting problem for the *active molecule*.
- **The precursor problem is the limiting problem.** The cheap, "natural" ingredient most brands buy is **broccoli-sprout/seed extract or glucoraphanin** — the **inert precursor**. Converting it to active SFN requires the enzyme **myrosinase**. Plant myrosinase is heat/processing-labile, and **human skin does not reliably supply myrosinase activity** (unlike the gut, where microbial myrosinase helps). A 2023 dermo-cosmetic NRF2 review (Frantz et al., *BioFactors*, DOI 10.1002/biof.1907) explicitly flags this: topical glucoraphanin/broccoli extracts "[require] activation by a specific enzyme, which might not be present on skin." So an extract can be label-impressive yet biologically near-inert. [19]
- **Implication:** to be efficacious topically you need **(a) pre-formed/stabilized SFN**, or **(b) glucoraphanin co-formulated with active myrosinase** that survives formulation and acts on/in skin. Both are non-trivial. A plain "broccoli sprout extract" claim with no myrosinase/SFN-content disclosure is a red flag.
- **No independent Franz-cell permeation profile** with delivered-dose quantification to dermal fibroblasts was located → permeation depth is **[partly unverified]**; biopsy gene-induction is the best available evidence of skin delivery.

## 6. Formulation

> **This is sulforaphane's Achilles heel.** It may be the most formulation-hostile longevity active in this whole research set.

- **Effective concentration / dose:** Human topical work used **doses, not %** — ~**340–500 nmol per application** (≈ 60–90 µg SFN), broccoli-sprout extract standardized to SFN. There is **no consensus cosmetic %**; report SFN content, not extract weight.
- **Stability (the dealbreaker):** Franklin et al. (2014), *Drug Dev Ind Pharm* 40(4):494-502 (PMID 23611476): SFN follows **first-order degradation**, is **extremely temperature-sensitive (~3.1× faster per +10 °C at pH 4)**, undergoes **base-catalyzed degradation**, and **completely degraded within 30 days in a conventional pharmaceutical cream.** Stability improved markedly in **non-aqueous bases (PEG ointment, oleaginous base)**, and the PEG formulation retained biological activity (↓AP-1 in vivo). Thermal-degradation chemistry confirmed separately (Jin et al., *J Agric Food Chem* 1999, DOI 10.1021/jf990082e). [10][20]
- **pH:** prefers **acidic** conditions; **degrades faster as pH rises (base-catalyzed)**. Avoid alkaline/high-water emulsions. [10][20]
- **Practical formulation strategy:** anhydrous or low-water systems, **cool storage, opaque/airless, minimal exposure to heat during manufacture**, antioxidants/chelators, possibly encapsulation. Or formulate the **glucoraphanin + stabilized myrosinase** "activate-on-application" approach. Expect a **short shelf life** vs typical cosmetics regardless — a real commercial liability.
- **Pairings (rational):**
  - **Sunscreen (SPF)** — strongly synergistic and on-mechanism (SFN is an *internal* antioxidant amplifier, not a UV filter; it is NOT a substitute for SPF). [1]
  - **Niacinamide** — barrier/tone, well-tolerated, no conflict.
  - **Ceramides / barrier lipids** — fit the brand's repair thesis; anhydrous-friendly.
  - **Vitamin E / squalane** — antioxidant + lipophilic carrier; complements an oleaginous SFN base.
- **Conflicts / cautions:** **high-pH products, hot processing, and water-heavy emulsions** are the enemies; low-pH exfoliant acids could co-stress skin (sequence separately). [10][20]
- **Sensory:** isothiocyanates are **pungent/sulfurous (the "broccoli/cabbage" smell)**; SFN and its degradants are volatile and malodorous — a real masking/fragrance challenge in finished product.

## 7. Safety

- **Topical tolerability (human):** the 2007 safety study reported **no adverse reactions up to 340 nmol** topical SFN; the 2007 efficacy study and the n=9 pilot reported **no adverse events**; the n=5 mechanism study was tolerated. Short-term topical use at studied doses appears well tolerated. [1][3][17]
- **Reactivity caveat:** SFN is a reactive electrophile (the property that activates NRF2). **High concentrations are cytotoxic/pro-oxidant in vitro**, and there is theoretical sensitization potential for a reactive isothiocyanate — so **dose/concentration discipline and patch/RIPT testing are essential**; "more is not better." [12]
- **Sun sensitivity:** No evidence SFN is photosensitizing; mechanistically it is **photo-protective** (reduces UV erythema). Still **not a sunscreen** and must be paired with SPF. [1]
- **Pregnancy/lactation:** Broccoli/SFN is a food component (oral broccoli-sprout beverages used in pregnancy-adjacent trials), but **no specific topical-in-pregnancy safety data** exist → default to cautious cosmetic language / "consult your doctor." **[unverified for topical]**
- **Oral safety (context):** generally well tolerated in trials at ~100–150 µmol/day; mild GI effects reported. Not directly transferable to topical. [16]
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** broccoli-sprout/seed feedstock is renewable and food-grade; synthetic/stabilized SFN avoids agricultural variability. Main quality issue is **standardization** (SFN content and myrosinase status vary wildly between suppliers/lots).

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Cosmetic vs drug line:** Sulforaphane / broccoli-sprout extract / glucoraphanin in a topical product are **cosmetic** ingredients. But SFN's strongest data are **chemoprevention/anti-cancer and disease (airway/brain)** — invoking any of that ("protects against skin cancer," "chemoprotective," "repairs DNA," "detoxifies," "treats radiation dermatitis," "boosts collagen") would push hard into **drug** territory (US FDA) and demand substantiation/legal review. The cancer-prevention origin of the science is a **claims minefield** — keep it out of consumer copy entirely.
- **Substantiation reality:** the one robust topical human result is **acute UV-erythema reduction (n=6)** — that supports a *defensible* "helps defend skin against the visible effects of UV-induced oxidative stress" *appearance/antioxidant* angle (with SPF context), **not** "anti-wrinkle," "firms," or "boosts collagen," which rest only on cells/oral-mouse data.
- **Safer cosmetic phrasing (✅):** "broccoli-sprout-derived sulforaphane, the prototypical activator of skin's own NRF2 antioxidant defenses," "helps support the skin's natural antioxidant response," "helps defend against the visible effects of environmental/UV-induced oxidative stress" (with SPF), "helps visibly improve uneven tone" (only if you run/own pigment data).
- **Avoid / flag for legal (⚠️):** "chemoprotective / anti-cancer," "detoxifies," "repairs DNA," "boosts collagen by X%," "reverses aging," "treats radiation dermatitis," any anti-pollution/"detox" hard claim, any import of the oral/cancer literature onto a cosmetic label.
- See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md) claim cheat-sheet.

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Forms to buy:** (a) **stabilized/pure synthetic SFN** (specialty/research suppliers; higher cost, defined potency); (b) **glucoraphanin + active myrosinase** systems or branded broccoli-sprout/seed extracts (more "natural"-marketable, but potency/activation must be verified); (c) plain broccoli-sprout/seed extract (cheap, often **biologically inert without myrosinase** — buyer beware).
- **Cost tier:** **Moderate** as a raw material (broccoli sprouts are cheap; standardized/stabilized SFN actives cost more) — **but total cost of ownership is HIGH** once you price in stabilization, anhydrous formulation, cold-chain/short-shelf-life, odor masking, and the QC needed to guarantee actual SFN content. The hidden formulation cost, not the powder price, is the real expense.
- **Strategic note:** "Broccoli sprout / sulforaphane" has a **recognizable, science-credible, food-derived story** (Johns Hopkins/Talalay halo) and is **under-used in prestige skincare** relative to its name recognition — a genuine differentiation opportunity *if* you solve stability and use a real (SFN-yielding) form.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

- **Hype:** "internal sunscreen," "broccoli that erases wrinkles," "detoxifies skin," "anti-cancer skincare," "the most powerful antioxidant." Much of this **conflates the (real) cancer-chemoprevention and oral NRF2 science with a cream**, and ignores that most products use the **inert precursor**.
- **Reality check #1:** the celebrated human topical result is **n=6 and measures acute UV redness**, not visible aging. [1]
- **Reality check #2:** the broad human literature is **84 trials / ~39 published, mostly oral, sample-limited, frequently missing primary endpoints** — and almost none dermatological. [16]
- **Reality check #3:** **stability** — SFN fully degrades in a normal cream in ~30 days; many marketed "sulforaphane" products may contain little active SFN by purchase. [10]
- **Reality check #4:** the **myrosinase gap** — broccoli-sprout extract/glucoraphanin on a label is often biologically near-inert on skin. [19]
- **Reality check #5:** SFN's reactivity is **dose-dependently toxic/pro-oxidant**; it's not a "more = better" hero. [12]
- **What's genuinely strong:** the **mechanism** (best-characterized NRF2 activator), an **independent peer-reviewed human topical target-engagement + erythema proof** (rare for a botanical), and **multi-hallmark cell coverage** (oxidative stress, pigment, collagen, inflammation).

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**⚠️ CONDITIONAL — a credible "science-story" supporting active, NOT a stability-friendly hero (yet).**

One line: *Sulforaphane is the textbook NRF2 activator with a rare independent human-topical proof that it switches on skin's own antioxidant defenses and cuts UV redness — a beautiful, differentiated longevity narrative — but it is one of the most formulation-hostile actives available, most commercial "broccoli sprout" forms are biologically inert without myrosinase, and there is no powered topical anti-wrinkle RCT, so it can't carry hard efficacy claims like retinol/niacinamide.*

- **Best role:** a **supporting "antioxidant-defense / NRF2" active** in a *day/protective* product paired with SPF, niacinamide, and barrier lipids — giving the brand a credible, food-derived, mechanism-led story (the "catalytic vs. stoichiometric antioxidant" angle is genuinely compelling and on-thesis for skin longevity).
- **Promote toward hero only if** (a) you use a **proven SFN-yielding form** (stabilized SFN or glucoraphanin + active myrosinase), (b) you **solve stability** (anhydrous base, cold-chain, validated active-content QC at shelf-life end), and (c) ideally you **commission your own controlled topical study** on an appearance endpoint. Keep all claims in the antioxidant/UV-defense appearance lane.
- **Skip if** the brand can't tolerate short shelf life / anhydrous textures / odor masking, can't QC actual SFN content, or wants to use cheap broccoli-sprout extract as a "claim ingredient" (that would be evidence-dishonest and off-brand).
- **Longevity relevance:** **High in concept** — NRF2/phase-2 induction is a core, well-credentialed mechanism against *oxidative stress, photoaging, and inflammaging* (three real hallmarks), and the catalytic-antioxidant framing is exactly the kind of mechanism-forward, science-backed story this brand wants. The discipline required is on **formulation honesty and claims**, not on whether the biology is real.

Log under [`../../ideas/considering.md`](../../ideas/considering.md) as a conditional/supporting active pending a stable, SFN-yielding formulation and (ideally) own topical appearance data.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

_Verification pass: 2026-06-19 (independently re-verified 2026-06-19 against primary sources). Method: each headline study in §4 was checked for (a) existence, (b) whether the cited result matches the source, (c) HUMAN+TOPICAL vs borrowed (in-vitro/animal/oral), and (d) size/independence/funding. Re-confirmed live against PNAS (abstract), PubMed (Franklin 2014, Petkovic 2021), ClinicalTrials.gov API v2 (NCT03126539, NCT00894712, NCT03730649), and J Nutr Sci/PMC (Saito 2025). PNAS full text and Wiley/BioFactors full text are paywalled (403); their numbers were confirmed via abstract + independent secondary reporting._

### Overall evidence grade for TOPICAL skin efficacy: **C+ (weak-to-moderate; strong mechanism, thin clinical)**

Rationale: Higher than a pure-hype "C/D" because the tier that matters (human topical) includes a **genuinely independent, peer-reviewed, placebo-/site-controlled PNAS study ([1])** plus a **human-biopsy NQO1-induction confirmation ([3])** — i.e. target engagement in living human skin is proven, not hand-waved. But it stays below B because every human topical dataset is **tiny (n=5–9), short-term, and measures mechanism/erythema/pigment rather than visible aging**, the pigmentation pilot was **terminated**, the radiation-dermatitis trial has **no posted results**, and the large human literature is **oral and non-dermatological**. For *oral/systemic* SFN the grade would still only be **B-/C+** (84 trials but ~half unpublished, frequent primary-endpoint misses).

### What verified out cleanly

- **PNAS 2007 [1]** — REAL. n=6, narrow-band 311-nm UVR, **37.7% mean erythema reduction (8.4–78.1%, P=0.025)** confirmed. Correctly tagged as small mechanistic proof, not anti-wrinkle.
- **CEBP 2007 [3]** — REAL. Human-skin phase-2 (NQO1) induction + safety to **340 nmol** confirmed; PMID 17416783; DOI correct.
- **Franklin 2014 [10]** — REAL. **Full degradation in cream within 30 days**, ~3.1×/10 °C temperature sensitivity, PEG/oleaginous stabilization, retained ↓AP-1 in vivo — all confirmed (PMID 23611476).
- **Petkovic 2021 [4]** — REAL. J Nutr Biochem 98:108817; **oral/dietary, mouse**; ↓MMP-9, improved collagen, ↑Nrf2/NQO1/HO-1. Correctly tiered as animal-oral, NOT topical.
- **Ko 2020 [13]** — REAL. Phytomedicine 77:153276 (PMID 32659677); PM2.5 coculture; pigment + collagen + ROS effects. In-vitro only.
- **Fahey 2015 [2]** — REAL. 1–40% conversion variability; myrosinase 3–4× bioavailability. Establishes precursor/enzyme dependence.
- **Saito 2025 [16]** — REAL. 84 trials / 39 published; honest "definitive recommendations challenging" framing. Confirms literature is mostly oral/non-skin.
- **ClinicalTrials.gov NCT03126539 [17]** — REAL via API. **Terminated** (reason stated: "administrative reasons, we started a new study"), **n=9**, SFN 500 nmol/jojoba, **results ARE posted**: mottled-pigment 2.8±0.4 vs 0.94±0.2 vs 0, no AEs, UV arm not enrolled. Direction (higher=more improvement) confirmed from outcome description.
- **NCT03730649 [17] (NEW — was missing from prior pass)** — REAL via API. The **successor** to NCT03126539. Topical broccoli-sprout extract/jojoba, nightly **up to 6 months** (longest topical course on record), **n=12**, primary endpoints **keratin-16/-17** (skin-fragility markers). **ACTIVE_NOT_RECRUITING, hasResults=false.** Adds active research interest, not evidence; reinforces that **no powered topical anti-aging RCT has yet published.**
- **NCT00894712 [18]** — REAL via API. Completed 2012, n=12; **hasResults=false** → outcome correctly flagged unverified.

### Caveats / soft spots flagged

1. **Source [12]** (SFN failing to protect fibroblasts from repeated H₂O₂ / dose-dependent toxicity) is asserted from secondary mentions; I could not pin it to one specific primary paper → left **[unverified]** but retained as a legitimate, literature-supported caution about SFN's reactivity.
2. **Sources [14][15]** are mechanism-corroboration (pigment/Nrf2) rather than load-bearing skin-efficacy citations; the load-bearing pigment data are [5][6][13][17].
3. **Frantz 2023 [19]** myrosinase-gap point was captured from the review's indexed summary (Wiley full text blocked); the specific claim (topical glucoraphanin needs myrosinase that skin may lack) is consistent with the broader literature and the precursor biochemistry, but the exact in-text wording is **[partly unverified]**.
4. **Scoping review [21]** and reviews [22][23] confirm the *direction and breadth* of the photoprotection/NRF2 story but are review-level, not new primary data; they are used for landscape, not as efficacy proof.

### Claims that should be SOFTENED or kept boxed

- ❌ "Internal sunscreen" / "protects against skin cancer" / "chemoprotective" / "detoxifies" → these import the **cancer/oral** literature onto a cosmetic and are drug-like. Keep out of consumer copy.
- ❌ "Boosts collagen," "reduces wrinkles" → rest only on **mouse-oral + cells**; not shown for a human topical appearance endpoint.
- ✅ Defensible: "helps activate skin's own NRF2 antioxidant defenses," "helps defend against the visible effects of UV-induced oxidative stress" (with SPF), "helps support an even-looking tone" (only with own/strong pigment data).
- ⚠️ Never let a "broccoli sprout extract" label imply efficacy without **disclosed active SFN content / myrosinase activation** — that's the central honesty trap for this ingredient.

### Bottom line

The dossier's argument is sound and unusually favorable-but-disciplined: SFN has **better human-topical provenance than most botanicals** (independent, peer-reviewed target-engagement + erythema proof) wrapped around a **best-in-class mechanism**, but is hamstrung by **chemical instability** and the **myrosinase/precursor gap**, with **no powered topical anti-aging RCT**. Topical-efficacy grade: **C+**. Verdict (conditional/supporting) is appropriate; the brand's risk is formulation honesty and claims, not whether the biology is real.

**Independent re-verification (2026-06-19) confirms the grade holds.** Every load-bearing citation checked out against its primary source with no overstatement found: PNAS 2007 (n=6, ~37% / 8–78% erythema reduction, topical human), Franklin 2014 (full cream degradation in 30 days, ~3.1×/10 °C, PEG/oleaginous stabilize), Petkovic 2021 (oral/dietary mouse — correctly tiered as borrowed), Saito 2025 (84 trials / 39 published, overwhelmingly oral/non-skin), NCT03126539 (terminated, n=9, results posted), NCT00894712 (completed, no results). The dossier already separates proven-topical from borrowed honestly and does not import the oral/cancer literature into claims. **Only correction made:** added the previously-missing successor trial **NCT03730649** (active, n=12, up to 6 months topical, keratin endpoints, unpublished) — it strengthens "active research interest" while *reinforcing* that no powered topical anti-aging result has published. No claims required softening; the dossier was already defensible. Grade: **C+ (topical efficacy)** — strong mechanism, thin-but-real human-topical proof of target engagement, no visible-aging RCT.
