# Ingredient: Polyglutamic Acid (INCI: Polyglutamic Acid; salt form: Sodium Polyglutamate)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ shortlisted (supporting role) · **Class:** Humectants & hydration actives
**Slug:** `polyglutamic-acid`

> **One-line verdict:** A genuinely high-capacity humectant with a strong *mechanistic* and *in-vitro* case but a thin *independent human-topical* evidence base — best used as a **supporting hydration/film-forming layer alongside hyaluronic acid**, not as a stand-alone anti-aging hero.

---

## 1. Identity

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Common name | Polyglutamic acid (PGA); poly-γ-glutamic acid (γ-PGA); poly-gamma-glutamate |
| INCI | **Polyglutamic Acid**; salt: **Sodium Polyglutamate** |
| CAS | 25513-46-6 (acid); 28829-38-1 (sodium polyglutamate) commonly cited |
| Chemical class | Anionic polyamino-acid biopolymer (a peptide of repeating glutamic-acid units) |
| Key forms / derivatives | **γ-PGA** (gamma; amide bond between α-amino and γ-carboxyl groups — the cosmetic-relevant, fermentation-derived form); **α-PGA** (alpha; the peptide-bond form, more often pharma/drug-delivery); **sodium polyglutamate** (neutralized salt used in most leave-on cosmetics) |
| Molecular weight | Highly variable by grade — from ~10 kDa to >1,000 kDa, with research strains reaching ~6,975 kDa (Ko et al. 2025 [4]). MW drives film-forming vs. penetration behavior. |
| Source / origin | Microbial **fermentation**, classically by *Bacillus subtilis* (incl. *B. subtilis natto*) and *B. licheniformis* on glutamate-rich substrates (historically fermented soybean / nattō). Plant-free and animal-free; vegan-compatible. |

**Plain-language:** PGA is a large, water-loving biopolymer made of chained glutamic-acid (a common amino acid) units, produced by fermentation. It behaves like a "smart sponge / breathable film" on the skin surface, holding water and slowing its evaporation.

---

## 2. Class & mechanism

PGA is a **humectant and surface film-former**, not an exfoliant despite the "acid" in the name (it is not an AHA/BHA and does not lower skin pH or exfoliate). Proposed mechanisms, in rough order of evidential support:

1. **Hygroscopic water binding (well-supported, in-vitro).** The dense carboxyl groups bind large amounts of water. Measured hygroscopicity ~86.8% (72 h, 90% RH) and water-holding ~56.9% (9 days) for γ-PGA from *B. subtilis* D7 (Lee et al. 2014 [3]).
2. **Occlusive/film "microgel" effect (plausible, mechanism-level — not measured on human skin).** High-MW γ-PGA forms a thin, breathable surface film that swells with water and is *expected* to **reduce transepidermal water loss (TEWL)** — i.e., to seal in moisture rather than penetrate. Note: **no cited study actually measured TEWL on human skin for PGA**; this is inferred from the film-forming/humectant mechanism, not demonstrated. This is the basis of the "PGA caps HA" pairing logic.
3. **Stimulation of natural moisturizing factor (NMF).** PGA is reported to raise stratum-corneum levels of NMF components — pyrrolidone carboxylic acid (PCA), lactic acid, urocanic acid (origin: Ben-Zur & Goldman trade article [2]; **not independently confirmed in a peer-reviewed RCT** — treat as mechanism/claim, not proof).
4. **Hyaluronidase inhibition → endogenous HA preservation.** γ-PGA inhibits hyaluronidase in vitro across multiple MWs (patent / in-vitro data [6]), and upregulates HA-synthase genes (HAS-1/2/3) and HA production in keratinocytes (Ko et al. 2025 [4]) — a mechanism for "supporting the skin's own hyaluronic acid."
5. **Barrier-gene and aquaporin upregulation (in-vitro / reconstructed skin).** γ-PGA dose-dependently increased filaggrin, involucrin, loricrin, lipid-synthesis enzymes (SPT, FAS, HMG-CoA reductase), **aquaporin-3 (AQP3)** and CD44, and increased stratum-corneum thickness in a 3D skin model (Ko et al. 2025 [4]).

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

| Hallmark | PGA relevance | Strength (for **topical human** use) |
|---|---|---|
| **Hydration / water loss (TEWL)** | Primary mechanism — humectant + film; TEWL reduction inferred from mechanism, not measured on human skin in cited sources | Weak–moderate (mechanistic + low-tier human sensory/supplier only) |
| **Barrier decline** | Upregulates filaggrin/involucrin/loricrin & barrier lipids; raises SC thickness (in-vitro/3D) | Weak–moderate (no human RCT) |
| **Collagen/ECM loss** | Indirect only — via hyaluronidase inhibition / HA preservation, not collagen synthesis | Weak (in-vitro/mechanistic) |
| **Photoaging (fine lines, roughness)** | Surface smoothing / plumping; one transgenic-cucumber human test showed wrinkle/roughness reduction (confounded — see §4) | Weak |
| **Pigmentation / tone** | Some whitening / melanin-reduction signals in non-isolated systems | Very weak (confounded) |
| **Oxidative stress, glycation/AGEs, senescence, mitochondrial decline, inflammaging, microbiome** | No credible direct evidence as a topical for these | None / not demonstrated |

**Bottom line on hallmarks:** PGA is fundamentally a **hydration/barrier-support** active. Claims tying it to collagen, senescence, or "longevity" pathways are not supported by topical human data.

## 4. Evidence review by tier (core of the dossier)

> **Honesty note:** PGA's *moisturizing biophysics* are well demonstrated in vitro. What is **largely missing is independent, peer-reviewed, placebo-controlled human topical RCT data on isolated PGA at cosmetic use levels.** Much of the consumer-facing "evidence" (5,000× water; 13.5% vs 7.5% glycerol; "4× more hydrating than HA") traces to supplier marketing or a single trade-journal article, not controlled clinical trials.

### Tier 1 — In vitro / biochemical (strongest body of evidence)

- **Ko, H.J., Park, S., Shin, E., et al. (2025).** *Poly-γ-Glutamic Acid from a Novel Bacillus subtilis Strain: Strengthening the Skin Barrier and Improving Moisture Retention in Keratinocytes and a Reconstructed Skin Model.* **Int. J. Mol. Sci.** 26(3):983. DOI: 10.3390/ijms26030983; PMID: 39940752. **[4]**
  - Models: human keratinocyte line + full-thickness reconstructed skin tissue (RST). Ultra-high-MW γ-PGA (~6,975 kDa).
  - Results: dose-dependent ↑ filaggrin, involucrin, loricrin; ↑ barrier-lipid enzymes; ↑ HAS-1/2/3 and HA production; ↑ AQP3 (mRNA + protein) and CD44; ↑ SC thickness at 1%. No cytotoxicity ≤500 µg/mL.
  - **Limitation (authors' own):** *"a significant limitation of this study is the absence of in vivo data."* No human trial. Single ultra-high-MW strain — not generalizable to all commercial grades.
  - **Conflict-of-interest flag (verified 2026-06-19):** Several authors are employed by cosmetic/biotech companies with a commercial interest in γ-PGA (**CoSeedBioPharm Corp.** and **ItsHanbul Corp.**, alongside Jeju National University); funded via a Korean NRF regional-innovation grant. The dossier's single strongest cited study is therefore **industry-affiliated** — its directional findings are plausible but should be read with that bias in mind, not as independent confirmation.

- **Lee, N-R., Go, T-H., Lee, S-M., et al. (2014).** *In vitro evaluation of new functional properties of poly-γ-glutamic acid produced by Bacillus subtilis D7.* **Saudi J. Biol. Sci.** 21(2):153–158. DOI: 10.1016/j.sjbs.2013.09.004; PMID: 24600308. **[3]**
  - Hygroscopicity 86.8% (72 h/90% RH); water-holding 56.9% (9 d); ACE inhibition; antimicrobial activity.
  - **Limitation:** Pure benchtop physicochemistry; no skin model, no human use; cosmetic-relevant endpoints (wrinkles, elasticity) not tested.

- **Hyaluronidase-inhibition data (patent + in-vitro).** γ-PGA at 50/2000/4000 kDa shows high hyaluronidase inhibition; some reports favor lower MW (~100 kDa). Source: US20080247986A1 / related filings **[6]**. **Limitation:** patent-stage in-vitro enzyme assays — not human efficacy.

- **Parati, M., Philip, C., Allinson, S.L., et al. (2024).** *Brown Algae as a Valuable Substrate for the Cost-Effective Production of Poly-γ-Glutamic Acid for Applications in Cream Formulations.* **Polymers (Basel)** 16(14):2091. DOI: 10.3390/polym16142091; PMID: 39065408. **[5]**
  - Seaweed-fed *B. subtilis natto* γ-PGA (62–250 kDa); hygroscopicity comparable/superior to HA & glycerol at 4 h; non-toxic to HaCaT ≤2%; stable, shear-thinning in cream (0.2–2% w/v).
  - **Limitation:** production + benchtop study; no human efficacy testing.

### Tier 2 — Animal / non-human in-vivo

- **Wu et al. (2024).** *Cucumber (Cucumis sativus L.) with heterologous poly-γ-glutamic acid has skin moisturizing, whitening and anti-wrinkle effects.* **Int. J. Biol. Macromol.** (ScienceDirect S0141813024008298). **[7]**
  - Used **zebrafish** (whitening) and a **human facial test**; transgenic-cucumber extract outperformed control, plain γ-PGA, and HA on moisturization; reported wrinkle −19.58%, roughness −24.97%, melanin −5.27%, elasticity (R2 +7.67%, Q1 +5.64%).
  - **Major limitation:** the test article is a **transgenic-cucumber whole extract**, *not isolated PGA* — results are confounded by the plant matrix and **cannot be attributed to PGA alone**. Single study, undisclosed/limited n, likely industry-linked. Treat anti-wrinkle/whitening numbers as **not transferable to a PGA monograph**.

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL

- **No relevant data.** PGA appears in oral/food contexts (e.g., calcium-absorption, gut/constipation studies), but **no human oral data supports skin-aging benefits**, and oral findings do not transfer to topical skincare. Do not borrow.

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL

**RCT (placebo-controlled, isolated PGA):** **None located that meet a rigorous bar.** This is the key honest gap.

**Lower-tier human-topical / trade & supplier data:**
- **Ben-Zur, N. & Goldman, D.M. (2007).** *γ-Poly glutamic acid: a novel peptide for skin care.* **Cosmetics & Toiletries** (trade journal) **[2]**. Reported 0.2% γ-PGA water-retention ≈ 5% glycerol; claimed elasticity/moisturization above HA and collagen; NMF stimulation; consumer-sensory panel (~50 women 30–50; majority reported improvement).
  - **Limitation:** trade publication, **not peer-reviewed**; sensory/self-report endpoints; author affiliations consistent with ingredient promotion. This is the **origin of most repeated marketing claims**.
- Supplier/brand figures — "13.5% hydration increase vs 7.5% for glycerol," "45% water retention to 120 min vs HA 36%," "holds 5,000× its weight in water," "4× more hydrating than HA" — circulate widely (Typology, supplier decks) but **[unverified]** against any independent peer-reviewed clinical source. Treat as **marketing**, not evidence.

**Net of Tier 4:** For *humans, topical, isolated PGA*, the defensible claim is **short-term surface hydration and a smoother/dewier feel** (consistent with humectant + film mechanism and benign safety) — **not** wrinkle reduction, firming, or barrier "repair" in any clinical-trial sense.

---

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- PGA is a **large polymer**; high-MW grades **do not meaningfully penetrate** past the stratum corneum — they act **on the surface** (humectant film, TEWL reduction). This is a feature for film/occlusion, a limit for any "deep" or signaling claim.
- Lower-MW grades may have somewhat more interaction with the upper SC but still are not "delivered" to the dermis. Effects on barrier genes/AQP3 in §4 are **cell-culture/3D-model** observations, not proof of in-skin penetration in humans.
- Practical implication: PGA's value is **at the surface, paired with a penetrating humectant (low-MW HA, glycerol)** that draws water in while PGA holds it.

## 6. Formulation

| Parameter | Guidance |
|---|---|
| **Typical use level** | ~0.1–2% (sodium polyglutamate / γ-PGA). Literature creams 0.2–2% w/v [5]; many marketed serums use ~0.1–1%. Film-forming and tacky above ~1–2%. |
| **pH** | Stable and effective across a broad cosmetic range (~roughly 4–8). As the salt (sodium polyglutamate) it is well-behaved; not pH-dependent for activity like AHAs. |
| **Solubility / phase** | Water-soluble; add to water phase. Powder hydrates into a viscous solution; can add slip/viscosity. |
| **Stability** | Good in aqueous and emulsion systems; shear-thinning, stable in cream matrices [5]. Standard preservation required (it's a fermentation biopolymer). Avoid strongly cationic systems (anionic polymer can complex with cationics). |
| **Pairings (synergistic)** | **Hyaluronic acid** (PGA seals/"caps" HA — the flagship pairing); glycerol & other humectants; niacinamide; ceramides/occlusives; panthenol. Plays the "lock-in" layer over water-drawing actives. |
| **Conflicts / cautions** | Strong cationic surfactants/polymers (charge complexation). No specific incompatibility with retinoids/vitamin C/acids, but at high levels its tacky film can affect layering feel. |
| **Sensory** | Lightweight, "dewy/bouncy" skin feel; can be tacky at higher % — balance with emollients. Adds a fresh, hydrated finish; common in "glass skin" positioning. |

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation/sensitization:** Considered low-irritation, low-sensitization; non-cytotoxic in keratinocyte assays ≤500 µg/mL [4] and ≤2% in HaCaT [5]. Generally well tolerated; rare mild redness/itch possible in sensitive skin. Standard patch-test advice applies.
- **Photosensitivity:** **None expected** — PGA is not a photosensitizer and (unlike AHAs) does not increase sun sensitivity. Daily SPF still recommended as baseline anti-aging.
- **Pregnancy/lactation:** No known concern at topical cosmetic levels; surface-acting, minimal/no systemic absorption. **No teratogenicity or pregnancy-risk signal at cosmetic concentrations** reported. (Standard caveat: not a substitute for individual medical advice; we do not give medical advice.)
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** Fermentation-derived, **vegan/animal-free**; can use waste/algae substrates [5], an ESG-positive story vs. some HA extraction routes. Allergen profile low; not a known common allergen.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Status:** Cosmetic ingredient (skin-conditioning/humectant). Not a drug active. No US OTC monograph; no specific CIR final report located for "Polyglutamic Acid" as of this writing — **[unverified]** (no CIR assessment confirmed). EU: used as a cosmetic skin-conditioning agent.
- **The cosmetic/drug line:** Hydration and "appearance" claims are cosmetic and safe. Anything implying it **treats**, **repairs the barrier** (structurally), **boosts collagen**, or **reverses wrinkles** edges into drug/structure-function territory and would need substantiation/legal review.

**✅ Defensible cosmetic copy:** "intensely hydrates," "helps lock in moisture," "supports a smoother, plumper, dewier appearance," "helps reduce moisture loss," "lightweight hydration that helps hold water at the surface," "pairs with hyaluronic acid to help seal in hydration."

**⚠️ Flag for substantiation / avoid:** "boosts collagen," "repairs the skin barrier," "erases/reduces wrinkles by X%," "holds 5,000× its weight in water" (supplier figure, **[unverified]** for a finished claim), "4× more hydrating than hyaluronic acid," "stimulates your skin's natural moisturizing factor" (mechanistic, from trade source — not RCT-substantiated). Any % efficacy figure must trace to our own testing or a citable peer-reviewed source.

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Cost tier:** Mid-to-premium. Cosmetic-grade powder commonly quoted ~**$150–$250/kg** wholesale (≈$180/kg typical) [8]. Comparable to or slightly above skincare-grade HA (~$120–$250/kg), well below high-grade HA.
- **Use economics:** Active at low % (0.1–2%), so cost-in-formula is modest. A "premium hydration" narrative is supportable without large COGS impact.
- **Supply:** Multiple (largely China-based) fermentation suppliers; growing capacity. Confirm MW grade, γ vs. salt form, purity, and endotoxin/heavy-metal specs. Sustainable substrate routes (algae/waste) emerging [5] for an ESG sourcing story.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

| Marketing claim | Reality |
|---|---|
| "Holds up to 5,000× its weight in water" | Supplier/marketing figure; **[unverified]** in independent literature. PGA is strongly hygroscopic (measured ~57–87% water metrics in vitro [3]), but the 5,000× number is not a clinical or independently validated claim. |
| "4× / 10× more hydrating than hyaluronic acid" | No independent head-to-head clinical proof. Some in-vitro hygroscopicity edges exist [3][5]; "N× better than HA" is marketing. |
| "Anti-aging / reduces wrinkles" | Only signal is a **confounded transgenic-cucumber** study [7], not isolated PGA in a clean human RCT. Not substantiated. |
| "Repairs the skin barrier / boosts collagen" | Barrier-gene and HA-synthase upregulation is **cell-culture/3D-model** [4], not human-proven; collagen claim unsupported. Drug-adjacent language — avoid. |
| "Stimulates NMF (PCA, urocanic acid)" | Trace to a **trade-journal** source [2]; mechanistically plausible, not RCT-confirmed. |
| "Caps/seals hyaluronic acid for longer hydration" | The most defensible mechanistic claim — consistent with film-forming + TEWL reduction; still phrase as "helps seal in," not a quantified promise. |

**Net:** PGA is **over-claimed in the market** (especially the HA-killer / anti-aging framing) but **under-studied in clean human topical RCTs**. The honest sweet spot is "premium surface hydration that works well with HA."

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**⚠️ SUPPORTING (conditional on positioning) — not a hero.**

One-line: *PGA is a credible, well-tolerated premium humectant whose real strength is sealing in hydration alongside hyaluronic acid — adopt it as a supporting hydration/film layer, never as the brand's anti-aging centerpiece.*

- **Fits the barrier-repair thesis** as a *moisture-retention support* (TEWL reduction, "seals in" HA), complementing — not replacing — ceramides/HA. It is a clean, on-trend "glass skin / lock-in hydration" story with a vegan/fermentation sourcing angle.
- **Does NOT** carry the brand's anti-aging or "skin-longevity" load: no human-topical evidence for collagen, senescence, photoaging reversal, or barrier *repair* in a clinical sense.
- **Conditions:** Use 0.1–2%, pair with HA + occlusives; hold strict claims discipline (hydration/appearance only); source a defined MW grade with clean specs; do not parrot supplier "5,000×/Nx-HA" figures in copy.

**Longevity-relevance note:** Low. PGA addresses the *hydration/water-loss* hallmark (and possibly barrier support, mechanistically) but does not engage the deeper longevity hallmarks (senescence, glycation/AGEs, mitochondrial, inflammaging). Treat it as a *quality-of-skin-now* ingredient, not a longevity actor. Log under supporting hydration actives in [`../../ideas/considering.md`](../../ideas/considering.md).

*Conducted 2026-06-19. Method: re-read every headline claim, then independently verified each cited study against its source-of-record (PubMed/PMC, journal pages, supplier/retailer copy) for (a) human-vs-topical-vs-borrowed evidence, (b) whether the study is real and says what is claimed, (c) industry funding / sample size.*

### Overall evidence grade: **C** (weak / indirect for human-topical efficacy on isolated PGA)

Graded against the human-topical bar (A = strong human-topical RCT; B = moderate human-topical; C = weak/indirect; D = essentially unproven topically / hype). The dossier earns a solid **C rather than D** because (i) the *moisturizing biophysics* are genuinely well demonstrated in vitro, (ii) low-tier human signals (trade sensory panel, the human arm of the cucumber test) point the same direction as the mechanism, and (iii) safety/tolerability is well supported. It does **not** reach **B**, because there is **no independent, placebo-controlled human-topical RCT on isolated PGA** — every human-topical data point is either a non-peer-reviewed trade/sensory source or a confounded whole-plant extract. (If grading only the *in-vitro/mechanistic* body of evidence in isolation, that tier would be a B; but a skincare dossier must be graded on human-topical efficacy, which is C.)

### Main caveats

1. **Zero independent human-topical RCT on isolated PGA.** Every efficacy-grade claim is either in-vitro/3D (Ko, Lee, Parati, patent), oral/food (not transferable), a confounded plant extract (Wu), or non-peer-reviewed trade/sensory/supplier copy (Ben-Zur & Goldman; supplier decks). This is the load-bearing caveat.
2. **The single strongest study is industry-affiliated** (Ko 2025 — CoSeedBioPharm / ItsHanbul). Directionally plausible, not independent confirmation.
3. **"Reduces TEWL" was stated mechanistically as if shown.** No cited source measured TEWL on human skin for PGA; it is inferred from the film/humectant mechanism. (Softened in §2 and §3.)
4. **Borrowed-evidence risk is handled correctly elsewhere.** Oral data (Tier 3) is explicitly walled off; in-vitro barrier-gene/AQP3 results are explicitly labelled cell-culture/3D, not in-skin. Good discipline — keep it.
5. **Small / undisclosed n.** The only human numbers (Ben-Zur sensory panel ~50; Wu human facial arm) have small or undisclosed sample sizes and are not placebo-controlled.
