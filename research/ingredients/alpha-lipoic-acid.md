# Ingredient: Alpha-Lipoic Acid (+ DHLA) (INCI: Thioctic Acid)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ⬜ considering / ☑️ shortlisted (conditional) / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product

> **One-line take:** A genuinely interesting "universal" amphipathic antioxidant with one decent positive split-face RCT and a pile of mechanistic promise — but the human topical evidence is thin and *contradicted*, the molecule is notoriously unstable/photolabile, it can sting and is a documented contact sensitizer. **Conditional supporting actor, not a hero.** Most of its famous evidence is from *oral* use for diabetic neuropathy and does not transfer to a face cream.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common names:** Alpha-lipoic acid (ALA), α-lipoic acid, lipoic acid (LA), thioctic acid.
- **INCI / cosmetic name:** **Thioctic Acid** (EU CosIng ref. 60299). CAS 1077-28-7. [12]
- **Chemistry:** A small (MW ~206 Da) sulfur-containing fatty-acid-derived compound with a dithiolane (5-membered, two-sulfur) ring on an octanoic-acid backbone. Naturally synthesized in mitochondria; an essential cofactor for mitochondrial enzyme complexes (pyruvate dehydrogenase, etc.). [2][8]
- **Key forms / derivatives:**
  - **DHLA (dihydrolipoic acid)** — the *reduced* (dithiol) form. This is the more potent antioxidant; ALA is converted to DHLA intracellularly. The ALA⇌DHLA redox couple is the whole point of the ingredient. [2][3]
  - **R-(+)-lipoic acid** — the natural, biologically active enantiomer; "ALA" sold for skincare is usually the cheaper racemic (R/S) mix. R-ALA is more bioavailable but costlier and even less stable.
  - **Sodium R-lipoate / lipoate salts** — more water-soluble, sometimes used to improve handling.
  - **Encapsulated / nano forms** — cubosomes, micelles, liposomes, silica or metal-salt-coated nanocapsules developed specifically to fix ALA's instability and penetration (see §5–6). [9][10]
- **Solubility:** Amphipathic — soluble in both lipid and aqueous phases. This dual solubility is its headline marketing claim ("the universal antioxidant," the "missing link between vitamin C and vitamin E"). [2][13]

---

## 2. Class & mechanism

**Class:** Antioxidant (amphipathic / "universal"); also chelator and anti-inflammatory.

How it works at the skin/cell level (mechanisms are well-characterized *biochemically*; their relevance to topical skin benefit is the open question):

1. **Direct ROS scavenging (both forms).** ALA and especially DHLA quench a broad range of reactive oxygen species — superoxide, hydroxyl radicals, hypochlorous acid, peroxyl radicals, singlet oxygen. Unusually, *both* the oxidized (ALA) and reduced (DHLA) forms are active, though DHLA is stronger. [2][3]
2. **Antioxidant network regeneration.** DHLA can regenerate other endogenous antioxidants — vitamin C, vitamin E (via the vitamin C / CoQ pathway), and glutathione (GSH) — and raises intracellular GSH levels in vitro and in vivo. This "recycling" role is the basis for pairing claims with vitamins C/E. [2]
3. **Metal chelation.** Both ALA and DHLA chelate redox-active transition metals (iron, copper) via their sulfur/carboxyl groups, reducing metal-catalyzed (Fenton) radical generation. [2]
4. **Anti-inflammatory signaling.** ALA inhibits NF-κB activation, dampening downstream pro-inflammatory and matrix-metalloproteinase (MMP) cascades implicated in collagen breakdown and "inflammaging." [2]
5. **Metabolic / mitochondrial.** As a native mitochondrial cofactor it supports energy metabolism; orally it activates AMPK and improves insulin sensitivity (relevant to glycation, see §3). [2][8]
6. **Anti-glycation.** Can scavenge reactive carbonyl intermediates of glycation, slowing advanced glycation end-product (AGE) formation and collagen cross-linking — shown in animal/diabetic models (mostly oral). [6][7][11]

**Important caveat on mechanism:** A low redox potential predicts ALA *should* be a great topical antioxidant and regenerate vitamins C/E after UV. In practice, the conversion of topical ALA → DHLA in skin appears **inefficient**, which is the leading explanation for why it underperforms expectations as a topical photoprotectant (see §4 and §5). Mechanistic elegance ≠ delivered benefit here. [4][5]

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

| Hallmark | Plausibility | Where the evidence sits |
|---|---|---|
| **Oxidative stress** | High (mechanistic) | Strong in-vitro; weak/contested in topical human skin [3][4] |
| **Photoaging (wrinkles/roughness)** | Moderate | One positive split-face RCT (5%) [1]; uncontrolled reports [16]; **contradicted** for photoprotection [4][5] |
| **Inflammaging** | Moderate (mechanistic) | NF-κB inhibition in vitro [2]; not demonstrated in human skin |
| **Glycation / AGEs** | Moderate | Animal + oral-human data [6][7][11]; **no topical human anti-AGE data** |
| **Pigmentation / tone** | Low–moderate | Mouse nanocapsule model only [9]; "improves skin color" in uncontrolled human reports [16] |
| **Mitochondrial decline** | Moderate (mechanistic) | Native cofactor; strong cell biology, no topical human endpoint |
| **Collagen / ECM loss** | Low–moderate | Indirect (anti-MMP, anti-glycation); roughness proxy in RCT [1] |
| **Barrier / hydration / TEWL** | Low | Not a barrier ingredient; no meaningful TEWL data |
| **Cellular senescence** | Speculative | No direct senolytic/senomorphic skin data |
| **Microbiome** | Not relevant | No data |

Net: its *strongest theoretical* hooks are oxidative stress, inflammaging, and glycation — but the *only* hallmark with a controlled human topical readout is photoaging (roughness/fine lines), and even that is one study against a notable negative.

## 4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

> **Read this first:** ALA's reputation is built largely on **oral** evidence (diabetic neuropathy) and **in-vitro** antioxidant data. The **human topical** evidence base is small — essentially *one* well-designed RCT, a couple of uncontrolled reports, and at least one credible study saying topical ALA does **not** work as a photoprotectant. Be honest about this gap in copy.

### Tier 1 — In-vitro (cell/biochemical)

- **Packer, Witt & Tritschler (1995), *Free Radic Biol Med* 19(2):227-250** — foundational review establishing ALA/DHLA as a redox-recycling "universal antioxidant," scavenging multiple ROS and regenerating vitamins C/E and GSH. Defines the mechanism; not skin-specific outcomes. [3]
- **Superti & Russo (2024), *Antioxidants* 13(10):1228** — recent comprehensive mechanistic review: direct ROS quenching, metal chelation, antioxidant regeneration, NF-κB inhibition. Confirms the biochemistry. [2]
- **Cytotoxicity/antioxidant characterization of lipid-nanoparticle ALA** (Ruktanonchai et al., 2009, *AAPS PharmSciTech*, PMC2663683) — formulation/in-vitro work; ALA antioxidant activity depends heavily on carrier. [13]
- *Limitation of the whole tier:* in-vitro antioxidant potency routinely fails to predict topical clinical benefit (delivery, stability, conversion intervene).

### Tier 2 — Animal / ex-vivo skin

- **Podda, Rallis, Traber, Packer & Maibach (1996), *Biochem Pharmacol* 52(4):627-633** — radiolabeled 5% ALA in propylene glycol on hairless mice; ALA penetrates skin readily and is reduced to DHLA in vivo; max skin levels by ~2 h. The penetration foundation. [14] (Note: vehicle was propylene glycol, a strong penetration enhancer — real cosmetic vehicles differ.)
- **Kubota et al. (2019), *Exp Dermatol* 28(Suppl 1):55-63** — novel metal-salt-coated ALA nanocapsule (nLA, 8–15 nm) on **murine** skin: improved UV-induced **pigmentation** and epidermal thickening, stimulated keratinocyte proliferation/differentiation. Animal model + engineered carrier; not human. [9]
- **Thirunavukkarasu et al. (2005), *Cell Biochem Funct*** — lipoic acid prevented collagen abnormalities/cross-linking in tail tendon of high-fructose-fed rats (anti-glycation, **oral/systemic**). [6]
- **Grdović et al. (2021), *Oxid Med Cell Longev* 2021:6669352** — ALA increased collagen synthesis/deposition in diabetic & nondiabetic **rat kidney** (systemic, not skin). [11]

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL (strong evidence base — but NOT topical)

This is where ALA's real clinical pedigree lives, and it is largely **irrelevant to a face cream** except as background credibility.

- **Hsieh et al. (2023), *Nutrients* 15(16):3634** — meta-analysis, **10 RCTs, n=1,242**. Oral ALA significantly improved Total Symptom Score in diabetic polyneuropathy (MD ≈ −1.69) with a dose-dependent trend (600–1,800 mg/day); helped sensory symptoms but **not** nerve conduction/muscle power. [17]
- **Ziegler et al. (2004), *Diabetic Med* 21(2):114-121** — meta-analysis of ALADIN I/III, SYDNEY, NATHAN II (n=1,258): IV ALA improved neuropathic symptoms. [18]
- **NATHAN 1 (Ziegler et al., 2011), *Diabetes Care* 34(9):2054-2060** — 4-year oral 600 mg/day; well-tolerated, modest neuropathy impairment benefit. [19]
- **Diabetic AGE studies** — oral ALA reduced circulating AGEs in T2D neuropathy patients (e.g., PMC11852413). [7]
- *Take-away:* robust *systemic* antioxidant/metabolic effects at high oral doses. None of this validates topical roughness/wrinkle claims.

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL

**4a. RCT (the single best topical study)**
- **Beitner (2003), *Br J Dermatol* 149(4):841-849** — **randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, split-face**; n=33 women, mean age 54.4; **5% ALA cream** twice daily for **12 weeks**. All four measures (self-eval, clinical, photographic, laser profilometry) favored the ALA side; profilometry roughness fell **50.8% (ALA) vs 40.7% (placebo), P<0.001**. **Burning/warmth was a common side effect, especially in the first 4 weeks.** [1]
  - *Limitations:* single study; modest n; ~10-percentage-point delta over an *active-improving* placebo (vehicle also reduced roughness a lot); industry context; not independently replicated at this quality.

**4b. RCT, engineered vehicle**
- **Sherif, Bendas & Badawy (2014), *Eur J Pharm Biopharm* 86(2):251-259** — double-blind, placebo-controlled; **5% ALA** in a **liquid-crystalline cubosome/poloxamer** gel designed to stabilize/deliver ALA. Reported reduced fine lines (periorbital, upper lip) and improved texture, **no irritation/peeling**. Encapsulation appears to cut the stinging seen with plain 5%. Sample size/duration under-reported in abstract. [10]

**4c. Lower tier (uncontrolled / preliminary)**
- **Perricone (2000), *Aesthetic Surg J* 20(3):218-222** — **uncontrolled** preliminary report; 5% ALA in lecithin cream + DMAE penetration enhancer, 12 wk; claimed fine-line resolution, smaller pores, better tone, "no irritation." No control group; the DMAE confounds attribution. Treat as marketing-adjacent. [16]

**4d. Negative / contradicting topical evidence (do NOT bury this)**
- **Lin, Lin, Burch, Selim, Monteiro-Riviere, Grichnik & Pinnell (2004), *J Invest Dermatol* 123(5):996-998** — "**Alpha-lipoic acid is ineffective as a topical antioxidant for photoprotection of skin.**" In a pig-skin model, topical ALA did **not** deliver meaningful UV photoprotection; the proposed reason is **inefficient conversion of topical ALA → DHLA**, so it fails to regenerate/spare vitamins C and E in skin. Directly undercuts the "antioxidant shield" pitch. [4][5]

**Topical bottom line:** Best-supported, but on a single study (one n=33 RCT, ~10-point delta over an already-improving vehicle, not independently replicated) → *visible reduction in facial roughness/fine lines at 5% over 12 weeks*. This is **suggestive, not "proven."** **Not proven topically** → photoprotection (actively contradicted), anti-glycation in human skin, pigmentation in humans, collagen synthesis in human skin, any antioxidant "shield" claim. Everything beyond the roughness/fine-line readout is **borrowed** from in-vitro, animal, or *oral* data.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **Penetrates, then converts poorly.** ALA does cross the stratum corneum and reach epidermis/dermis (Podda 1996, hairless mouse, propylene-glycol vehicle) and is partly reduced to DHLA in vivo. [14] **But** the conversion to active DHLA in human/pig skin appears **inefficient**, which Pinnell's group cites to explain its failure as a topical photoprotectant. [4][5] Penetration ≠ activation.
- **Stability is the core problem.** ALA is **photolabile and thermolabile** — it degrades under light and heat, losing its 333 nm absorption (i.e., losing activity). This complicates manufacture and shelf life and likely degrades real-world products before use. [5][15] Much of the formulation literature (cubosomes, micelles, ionic-liquid w/o emulsions, silica/metal-salt nanocapsules) exists *specifically* to stabilize and deliver it. [9][10]
- **Vehicle dependence is large.** The good RCT used penetration enhancers/specialized vehicles; nanostructured carriers improve both penetration and tolerability. A plain ALA cream is unlikely to match the studied formulations.

---

## 6. Formulation notes

- **Effective concentration:** Human topical data cluster at **5%** (Beitner RCT; Sherif cubosome; Perricone). Many cosmetics use **0.5–5%**; lower-end concentrations have **no efficacy evidence**. [1][10][12]
- **pH:** ALA is a weak acid (pKa ~4.7); needs a low-ish/buffered system to stay protonated and soluble. Lipoate salts improve aqueous handling.
- **Stability:** The hard part. Protect from **light and heat**; use opaque/airless packaging, antioxidant co-system, and ideally encapsulation (cubosome/liposome/nanocapsule). Expect odor and potential discoloration as it degrades (sulfur chemistry). [9][10][15]
- **Pairings:** Conceptually synergistic with **vitamin C and vitamin E** (network recycling) — but Pinnell's data suggest that synergy may not materialize topically due to poor DHLA conversion. [3][4] Encapsulation/penetration enhancers (DMAE, specialized lipids) raise delivery (and, for plain ALA, irritation).
- **Conflicts:** Avoid stacking with multiple other irritant actives (retinoids, strong AHAs) given ALA's own sting/sensitization risk. Sulfur chemistry can introduce odor.
- **Sensory:** Plain 5% ALA can **sting/burn/warm** the skin (notable in Beitner, first 4 weeks). [1] Potential sulfurous odor and yellow color. Encapsulated forms report better tolerability/no peeling. [10][16]

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation / sensory:** Stinging, burning, warmth common at 5% plain, especially early. [1] Encapsulated vehicles reduce this. [10] Cases of rash/blistering and facial irritation from 5% ALA creams are reported.
- **Contact allergy / sensitization (real, documented):** ALA/thioctic acid is described as a **strong cosmetic sensitizer**. Allergic contact dermatitis case reports include Corazza et al. (2023, *Contact Dermatitis*) — ACD from ALA in an OTC topical; Leysen et al. (2016, *Contact Dermatitis* 74:182) — "further evidence of thioctic acid being a strong cosmetic sensitizer"; plus reports from anti-wrinkle creams and thioctic-acid-containing eye drops. Patch-test relevance is established. [20][21]
- **Sun sensitivity:** Not classically photosensitizing for the user, but ALA is **photo-unstable** (degrades in light) — a stability concern, not a phototoxicity-to-skin concern. Does not replace SPF; SPF remains the #1 evidence-based anti-aging step.
- **Pregnancy / lactation:** Topical antioxidant exposure is low-risk in principle, but **safety data in pregnancy/lactation are lacking**; conservative guidance is to avoid routine use. Defer to clinician. [23]
- **Systemic note (context, oral):** EFSA flagged a possible association between oral ALA intake and **insulin autoimmune syndrome (IAS)** / hypoglycemia, especially with HLA-DR4 polymorphisms. [22] This is an **oral-supplement** concern and not expected from cosmetic topical use, but it shapes the molecule's overall risk narrative and supplement-stacking caution.
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** Manufactured synthetically (not a natural-extract supply chain); see §9.

## 8. Regulatory & claims

- **Status:** Listed as **Thioctic Acid** in EU CosIng (ref. 60299); permitted as a cosmetic ingredient with **no specific Annex II/III prohibition or restriction identified** as of this research. Confirm current CosIng/Annex status before launch. [12] In the US it is a permitted cosmetic ingredient (cosmetic, not an OTC drug monograph ingredient).
- **Cosmetic vs. drug line (per CLAUDE.md guardrails):**
  - ✅ **Defensible cosmetic language (tie to the one RCT, and only if our own formula/use-test supports it):** "helps **visibly** smooth the look of fine lines and improve skin texture," "antioxidant," "helps defend against the look of environmental stress," "supports a more even-looking tone." Keep to *appearance* and *visible* framing. Note even these rest on a single unreplicated study, so a confirmatory in-house use test is advisable before headlining them.
  - ⚠️ **Risky / needs substantiation or legal review:** "boosts collagen," "regenerates vitamins C & E in your skin," "antioxidant shield / neutralizes free radicals in skin," "anti-glycation," "repairs," "reverses photoaging." The free-radical/photoprotection mechanism claims are **specifically contradicted** by Pinnell 2004 for topical use [4] — avoid them. Anti-glycation and collagen claims rest on animal/oral data only.
  - ❌ **Drug-territory / off-limits:** any treat/cure/prevent disease claim; neuropathy/metabolic benefits (those are the *oral* drug-adjacent evidence and must never appear on a cosmetic).
- **Substantiation reality:** We could only substantiate "visible smoothing/texture at ~5%" by either citing Beitner-style data or running our own use test. Most other ALA marketing claims would not survive a strict claims review.

---

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Form:** Synthetic; racemic ALA is a commodity, widely available (e.g., Sabinsa and others list cosmetic-grade ALA). R-(+)-ALA and stabilized/encapsulated grades cost more. [12]
- **Cost tier:** **Low–moderate** as raw racemic powder; **moderate–high** once you pay for stabilization (encapsulation, airless/opaque packaging, antioxidant co-systems) — which you effectively *must* to get a product that matches the studied formulations and doesn't degrade on shelf.
- **Supply considerations:** Stable supply; the real cost is formulation/packaging engineering, plus QC for degradation (color/odor) over shelf life.

---

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

- **"Universal antioxidant / missing link between vitamins C & E"** — true *biochemically* (Packer) [3]; but the topical photoprotection that this story implies was **specifically tested and failed** (Pinnell 2004) because topical ALA→DHLA conversion is inefficient. The headline mechanism does not reliably translate to a cream. [4][5]
- **"Clinically proven to reduce wrinkles"** — overstates *one* split-face RCT (n=33) with a ~10-point delta over an already-improving vehicle. [1] Real, but singular and modest.
- **"Anti-glycation / fights sugar-aging"** — borrowed from **animal and oral-diabetic** data; **no human topical anti-AGE evidence**. [6][7][11]
- **"Brightens / fades pigmentation"** — human evidence is uncontrolled; controlled pigmentation data are **mouse-only** (nanocapsule). [9][16]
- **The neuropathy halo** — ALA's most cited science (ALADIN/SYDNEY/NATHAN, meta-analyses) is **oral, high-dose, for nerve pain** — impressive but irrelevant to skincare and must never be implied on a face product. [17][18][19]
- **Unspoken liabilities the hype omits:** it stings, it's a documented contact sensitizer, and it's one of the *least stable* common antioxidants. [1][20][21][15]

---

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**⚠️ Conditional — supporting actor at best, behind better-evidenced antioxidants.**

**One-line reasoning:** ALA offers a credible amphipathic-antioxidant story and one decent 5% wrinkle/roughness RCT, but it's outclassed on *human topical* evidence and tolerability by vitamin C derivatives and niacinamide, it's hard to keep stable, it stings, and it's a known sensitizer — so it only earns a slot as a deliberately stabilized, well-tolerated supporting ingredient, never as the barrier-repair cream's hero.

- **If used:** insist on **~5%**, an **encapsulated/stabilized** delivery system (cubosome/liposome/nanocapsule), **opaque airless packaging**, patch-test guidance, and conservative *appearance/texture* claims only.
- **If skipped:** entirely defensible — niacinamide, vitamin C, and the ceramide/barrier story carry the thesis with stronger, cleaner evidence and fewer liabilities. Better fit for a future "texture/antioxidant serum" line extension than for the core barrier-repair cream.

**Longevity-relevance note:** ALA is one of the more "longevity-coded" actives — a native mitochondrial cofactor touching oxidative stress, glycation/AGEs, and inflammaging, with real *systemic* (oral) geroprotective interest. That biology is a strong **narrative** asset for a skin-longevity brand. But narrative ≠ delivered topical benefit: in skin, only the visible-smoothing readout is demonstrated. Use the longevity science as honest background, not as a topical claim.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

_Verified 2026-06-19 against primary sources. Method: each headline efficacy claim was checked for (a) whether the evidence is HUMAN + TOPICAL vs. borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral data, (b) whether the cited studies are real and say what is claimed, (c) study size and funding/independence._

### Overall evidence grade: **C** (weak / largely indirect for topical use)

**Why C and not B:** The entire human-topical efficacy case rests on **one** positive RCT (Beitner 2003, n=33), which is real and accurately summarized but is small, unreplicated at that quality, shows only a ~10-percentage-point roughness delta over a vehicle that itself improved roughness 40.7%, and carries probable (unverified) commercial context. A single small unreplicated split-face study is not "moderate human-topical" evidence (B); it is a positive signal sitting one tier above unproven. **Why C and not D:** it is not hype — there *is* a real, well-designed positive human-topical RCT plus a supportive engineered-vehicle RCT, so it clears the "essentially unproven topically" bar. Grade rises to B only if the roughness/fine-line benefit is independently replicated in humans (or confirmed by our own controlled use test).

### Citation integrity check (all load-bearing citations verified real and fairly represented)
- **[1] Beitner 2003, *Br J Dermatol* 149(4):841-849** — REAL; numbers confirmed against the journal abstract: n=33 women, mean age 54.4, split-face, 5% ALA, 12 wk, four assessment methods, laser-profilometry roughness **−50.8% (ALA) vs −40.7% (placebo), P<0.001**. Dossier characterization is accurate. *Caveat:* I could **not** independently confirm a sponsor / conflict-of-interest disclosure (2003 paper predates routine COI statements). The dossier's "industry context" is therefore an **inference, not a verified fact** — it is hedged appropriately and should stay hedged.
- **[4] Lin/Pinnell 2004, *J Invest Dermatol* 123(5):996-998** — REAL; title and conclusion confirmed. Authors found **no appreciable photoprotective effect** of topical ALA alone or added to vitamins C+E. The dossier does NOT bury this contradicting study — it foregrounds it. Good.
- **[17] Hsieh 2023, *Nutrients* 15(16):3634** — REAL; confirmed ORAL, diabetic polyneuropathy, TSS primary outcome, dose subgroups (600/1200/1800 mg/day). Correctly fenced off as oral and "irrelevant to a face cream."
- **[22] EFSA 2021, 19(6):6577** — REAL; confirmed the Panel concluded oral ALA "is likely to increase the risk of developing insulin autoimmune syndrome." Correctly framed as an oral-only concern.
- **[9] Kubota 2019** (pigmentation) and **[10] Sherif 2014** (cubosome RCT) — both real and correctly tiered (Kubota = murine only; Sherif = small/under-reported engineered-vehicle RCT). The dossier already flags Sherif's sample-size/duration as under-reported.
- Spot-checks found **no fabricated citations** and **no quiet substitution** of animal/oral data for human-topical claims. The Tier 1–4 structure honestly segregates evidence by species/route, which is exactly the right defense against the borrowing problem.

### Main caveats
1. **One-study problem.** Topical efficacy = a single small unreplicated RCT. Treat its result as a *signal*, not a settled fact.
2. **Mechanism ≠ delivered benefit.** The "universal antioxidant / regenerates C & E" story is in-vitro/biochemical and was **specifically tested and failed topically** (Pinnell 2004) due to inefficient skin ALA→DHLA conversion. Do not let mechanistic elegance leak into copy.
3. **The credibility halo is oral.** ALA's impressive clinical pedigree (neuropathy meta-analyses) is oral/high-dose and must never be implied on a cosmetic.
4. **Liabilities are real and well-cited:** stinging at 5% plain, documented contact sensitizer, and notably unstable (photo/thermolabile). These are genuine, not hedging.
5. **Funding of the key RCT is unverified** — assume possible commercial interest but state it as unconfirmed.

### Claims that should be softened (and were)
- ~~"Proven (one good RCT) → visible reduction in facial roughness/fine lines"~~ → softened to **"best-supported, but suggestive not proven"** (§4d). A single n=33 unreplicated study does not support the word "proven."
- The "defensible cosmetic language" list (§8) now carries an explicit rider that **even these claims rest on one study** and an in-house use test is advisable before headlining them.
- **Already adequately hedged (no change needed):** the "anti-glycation," "brightens/pigmentation," "antioxidant shield/photoprotection," and "boosts collagen / regenerates C & E" claims are already routed to the ⚠️/❌ buckets in §8 and §10 as borrowed or contradicted — these were not overstated.

### Net
A rare example of a dossier that is *already* adversarially honest about its own weak spots. The grade is **C** because of the underlying evidence, not because the write-up overclaims — after the two softenings above, the document is defensible as written. Keep ALA as a deliberately stabilized **supporting** actor only, never a hero, and never substantiate beyond visible-smoothing (and only with our own confirmatory data).
