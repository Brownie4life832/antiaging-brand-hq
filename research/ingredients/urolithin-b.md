# Ingredient: Urolithin B (INCI: Urolithin B — not formally INCI-listed; see §8)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ⬜ considering · ⬜ shortlisted · ☑ rejected for now (skip — see verdict) · ⬜ in a product
**Class:** Autophagy & mitochondrial-targeted actives · **Slug:** `urolithin-b`

> **One-line honesty flag up front:** Urolithin B is the **less-studied, less-potent sibling of Urolithin A.** It has a real, interesting systemic biology (muscle anabolism, anti-inflammation, bone, neuro), but **zero skin-specific studies, zero topical human data, no cosmetic INCI registration, and it is NOT a meaningful mitophagy inducer** — the one mechanism that gives the urolithin class its longevity story belongs almost entirely to Urolithin A. Practically every "anti-aging skin" idea for UroB is **borrowed** from UroA or from non-skin organ data. Treat as speculative for skincare.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common name:** Urolithin B (UroB, Uro-B, UB).
- **INCI:** No confirmed INCI / EU CosIng cosmetic registration was located as of 2026-06. This is a **material difference from Urolithin A**, which *is* INCI-listed and China-registered as a cosmetic raw material. Sold as a research chemical / bulk nutraceutical raw material, not a registered cosmetic active. **[unverified — confirm against live CosIng if a SKU is contemplated]** [2][14]
- **Chemistry:** 3-Hydroxy-6H-dibenzo[b,d]pyran-6-one (3-hydroxybenzo[c]chromen-6-one). **CAS 1139-83-9.** Formula **C₁₃H₈O₃**, MW **212.20 g/mol**. A dibenzopyranone (a "urolithin"). [10][13]
- **Structure note that drives everything below:** UroB has a **single hydroxyl (3-OH)**. Urolithin A has **two (3-OH and 8-OH)**. That missing 8-OH is repeatedly implicated as the reason UroA out-performs UroB on antioxidant capacity, anti-inflammatory potency, and (most importantly) mitophagy. UroB is essentially a "deoxy" UroA. [8][10]
- **Natural origin:** A **postbiotic** — not in food directly. The gut microbiome converts dietary **ellagitannins / ellagic acid** (pomegranate, walnuts, berries) into urolithins. UroB is a *downstream/terminal* metabolite (characteristic of "metabotype B" producers, who also make isourolithin A). Commercial UroB is **synthesized** for purity/dose. [3][8][10]
- **Related forms / metabolites:** Urolithin A (the lead), isourolithin A, urolithin C/D (more hydroxylated, stronger antioxidants). In the body UroB circulates mainly as **UroB-glucuronide** (and sulfate) conjugates — and the glucuronides are largely **inactive** in immune assays. [8][12]

## 2. Class & mechanism

Urolithin B sits in the "mitochondrial / autophagy longevity active" bucket **by family association**, but its own mechanism profile is different from UroA's.

- **NOT a primary mitophagy activator.** This is the single most important mechanistic correction. The urolithin-class mitophagy story (PINK1/Parkin, restored mitochondrial turnover) is **UroA's** signature; in reviews UroB is **explicitly not described as a mitophagy inducer** and is noted to act through different pathways. Do **not** market UroB on "mitophagy." [8][9]
- **Muscle anabolism (its best-characterized, most distinctive mechanism).** UroB activates **mTORC1** (↑ phospho-mTOR, rpS6, 4E-BP1) **independently of AKT**, increases protein synthesis, and **represses the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway** (↓ FoxO, MAFbx/MuRF1). Effect is **androgen-receptor (AR)-dependent**, with proposed AR↔mTORC1 crosstalk via AMPK. [1]
- **Anti-inflammatory.** Inhibits **NF-κB** (↓ IκBα phosphorylation/degradation), suppresses JNK/ERK/Akt phosphorylation, enhances AMPK; lowers NO and pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, IFN-γ), raises IL-10. Shown in activated microglia and other models. [4][8]
- **Antioxidant (modest).** Upregulates **HO-1 via Nrf2/ARE**, lowers ROS and malondialdehyde, raises SOD — but UroB is a **weaker direct antioxidant** than UroA and than the more-hydroxylated urolithins C/D (and weaker than ellagic acid on DPPH). [4][7][8]
- **MMP-9 inhibition (the one mechanistically skin-relevant in-vitro hit).** In a cell-free enzyme study, UroB was actually the **strongest MMP-9 inhibitor of the three** tested (IC₅₀ 13.17 µM vs ellagic acid 17.14 µM vs UroA 33.29 µM) with the **highest binding affinity** (lowest K_D). MMP-9 degrades ECM/collagen, so this is *theoretically* anti-photoaging — but it is purified-enzyme data, no cells, no skin. [6]
- **AhR antagonism.** Like UroA, UroB antagonizes aryl-hydrocarbon-receptor activity (↓ TCDD-induced CYP1A1). AhR drives pollution-/UV-linked skin inflammation and pigmentation, so this is a plausible "anti-pollution" angle — but again non-skin data. [11]
- **Anti-glycation (AGEs).** In an accelerated-aging mouse brain model, oral UroB **downregulated AGEs, carboxymethyl-lysine and RAGE**. AGEs cross-link dermal collagen/elastin, so this is conceptually on-thesis for skin — but it was measured in **brain tissue, oral dosing, not skin.** [5]
- **SERM / endocrine activity.** Urolithins (incl. UroB) are **phytoestrogen-like selective estrogen receptor modulators** — estrogenic without estradiol, antiestrogenic with it — and UroB is an **aromatase inhibitor**. UroB's ER binding is *much weaker* than UroA's (ERα/ERβ IC₅₀ ~20/11 µM for UroB vs ~0.4/0.75 µM for UroA). Relevant as a *safety/positioning* consideration, not a benefit. [15][16]

**Plain-language:** UroB is a gut-derived polyphenol metabolite that, in cells and animals, calms inflammation, builds muscle (via mTOR + the androgen receptor), nudges antioxidant defenses, and can block a collagen-chewing enzyme (MMP-9) in a test tube. What it does **not** do is the headline "clean out your mitochondria" trick that made Urolithin A famous — and **none of it has been tested on skin.**

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

| Hallmark | UroB relevance | Strongest evidence tier (see §4) |
|---|---|---|
| **Collagen / ECM loss** | Potent **MMP-9** inhibitor (enzyme assay) | In-vitro **cell-free enzyme only** |
| **Glycation / AGEs** | ↓ AGEs/CML/RAGE | Animal (brain, oral) — **not skin** |
| **Oxidative stress** | ↓ ROS, ↑ Nrf2/HO-1/SOD (modest; weaker than UroA) | In-vitro / animal (non-skin) |
| **Inflammaging** | ↓ NF-κB, TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β; ↑ IL-10 | In-vitro / animal (non-skin) |
| **Mitochondrial decline** | **Weak — not a mitophagy inducer** (UroA's domain) | None (mechanistically absent for UroB) |
| **Cellular senescence** | No UroB senescence/SASP skin data found | None for UroB |
| **Photoaging** | AhR antagonism + MMP inhibition are *plausible* but untested on skin | None for UroB (UVA/UVB data is UroA's) |
| **Barrier / hydration / TEWL** | No data | None |
| **Pigmentation / tone** | AhR/aromatase are speculative angles only | None |
| **Microbiome (skin)** | UroB is a *gut* postbiotic; no skin-microbiome data | None |

**Honest read of the table:** Every cell with real biology is from a **non-skin** model; every skin-specific cell is empty. The collagen (MMP-9) and glycation lines are the most "marketable," and both are the furthest from a real cream (enzyme-in-a-tube and mouse-brain-after-oral-dosing, respectively).

## 4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

> **The decisive fact:** there is **no published study of Urolithin B on human skin, on skin cells (keratinocytes/fibroblasts), or applied topically in any species.** I searched specifically for UroB + skin/keratinocyte/fibroblast/wound-healing/photoaging/senescence and found **nothing** — the hits are all Urolithin A. So the topical tier (Tier 4), the tier that actually matters for a skincare SKU, is **empty**. Everything below is borrowed.

### Tier 1 — In-vitro (cell-free + non-skin cells) — *real but indirect*

- **Houssein-Zadeh, Sadeghi & Dehghan (2025), *Iran J Pharm Res* 24(1):e148332.** Cell-free recombinant **MMP-9** inhibition (protease assay + surface plasmon resonance + docking). **UroB IC₅₀ = 13.17 µM (strongest of the three), K_D = 4.3×10⁻⁵ M (highest affinity);** ellagic acid 17.14 µM; UroA 33.29 µM; mixed non-competitive/uncompetitive inhibition. **Limitation:** purified enzyme only — **no cells, no skin, no permeation**; an IC₅₀ in a cuvette says nothing about delivered dermal dose. DOI: 10.5812/ijpr-148332. [6]
- **Lee et al. (2019), *Phytomedicine* 55:50-57.** UroB in **LPS/LTA/poly(I:C)-activated BV2 microglia** (+ LPS-injected mouse brain): ↓ NF-κB (↓ IκBα phospho/degradation), ↓ JNK/ERK/Akt, ↑ AMPK, ↓ NO and pro-inflammatory cytokines, ↑ IL-10, ↑ HO-1 via Nrf2/ARE, ↓ ROS/NADPH-oxidase. **Limitation:** neuro-immune model, not skin; concentrations not skin-validated. PMID 30668443; DOI 10.1016/j.phymed.2018.06.032. [4]
- **Comparative urolithin immune study — Mele/Marín et al. (2020), *Eur J Nutr* (PMC8137622).** Across macrophage/neutrophil assays, **Urolithin A was the most active** anti-inflammatory metabolite (TNF-α↓, IL-10↑ via ERK1/2); **glucuronide conjugates were inactive.** UroB present but **less active than UroA** — i.e. head-to-head, UroB loses. PMID 32960290. [12]
- **Antioxidant comparisons (Djedjibegovic et al. 2020, *Oxid Med Cell Longev* 2020:5194508; D'Amico/in-silico work, PMC10045577; Kallio et al. ORAC):** UroB is a **modest** antioxidant — ORAC ~5.77 Trolox-eq (vs UroA ~6.67), and in-silico physiological-pH scavenging of "all urolithins **except urolithin B**" exceeded Trolox/ellagic acid. So UroB is the **weakest-antioxidant urolithin** by several measures. [7][8]

**Verdict on Tier 1:** UroB has genuine, reproducible cell/enzyme activity (NF-κB↓, Nrf2↑, MMP-9↓). But (a) it's all non-skin, (b) where compared head-to-head, **UroA generally wins**, and (c) in-vitro enzyme/cell data is the weakest predictor of cosmetic performance.

### Tier 2 — Animal — *exists for muscle / heart / bone / brain; none for skin*

- **Rodriguez et al. (2017), *J Cachexia Sarcopenia Muscle* 8(4):583-597.** UroB's flagship paper. C2C12 myotubes (15 µM) + C57BL mice (10 µg/day osmotic pump, 28 days, n=6-10). **Induced muscle hypertrophy** (tibialis, gastrocnemius, soleus, quadriceps) and **reduced denervation atrophy** via **mTORC1 (AKT-independent)**, ubiquitin-proteasome suppression, and **androgen-receptor dependence**. **Limitations:** systemic (pump, not oral, not topical); **male mice only**; no skin endpoints. PMID 28251839; DOI 10.1002/jcsm.12190. [1]
- **Chen et al. (2021), *Front Pharmacol* 12:768097.** D-galactose accelerated-aging mice; **oral UroB 50/100/150 mg/kg/day × 8 wk.** Improved memory; ↓ cytochrome-c-mediated neuronal apoptosis; ↑ PI3K/AKT/ERK survival signaling; **↓ AGEs, CML, RAGE in brain.** **Limitations:** oral, brain endpoints — the AGE finding is *not* skin. PMID 34867396; DOI 10.3389/fphar.2021.768097. [5]
- **Bone — Qu et al. (2022), *J Cell Mol Med* 26(15) (PMC9357644)** and **Lu et al. (2022), PMC9528769.** UroB **suppresses RANKL-induced osteoclastogenesis / bone loss** via ↓ NF-κB/MAPK/Akt, ↓ c-Fos/NFATc1, ↑ Nrf2/ROS-scavenging. Osteoporosis models — not skin. PMIDs 35781786 / 35708050. [17][18]
- **Cardiac (per Chen 2022 review [8]):** post-MI functional improvement, ↓ ventricular arrhythmia, ↓ TMAO-induced cardiomyocyte damage (rodent). Not skin.

**Verdict on Tier 2:** A respectable *systemic* animal record (muscle, brain, bone, heart). **Zero skin/dermal animal models.** Any "proven in animals for skin" claim is **[unverified / false].**

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL — *essentially none for UroB alone*

- There are **no interventional human trials of isolated Urolithin B** (contrast UroA, which has multiple oral RCTs — Andreux 2019, Singh/ATLAS 2022, Denk/MitoImmune 2025). The only human UroB data are **pharmacokinetic/observational** after pomegranate or walnut intake, where UroB appears at **very low plasma levels** (≈0.01 µmol/L vs ≈0.14 µmol/L for UroA after pomegranate juice), mostly as glucuronide. The 2022 review explicitly calls for "future clinical trials." [8][12]

**Verdict on Tier 3:** UroB has **no standalone human efficacy evidence** in any format, let alone skin. Its dietary plasma levels are an order of magnitude below UroA's.

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL — *empty*

- **No topical human (or animal) study of Urolithin B exists.** None. (UroA at least has one sponsor preprint; UroB has nothing.) Any topical UroB benefit is pure extrapolation from §4 Tiers 1-2 and from Urolithin A's data. [search-confirmed absence, 2026-06]

**Explicit borrowed-vs-proven map:**
- *Proven for topical human use:* **nothing.**
- *Proven in non-skin systems (do NOT claim for a cream):* muscle hypertrophy, anti-atrophy (animal/cell), neuroprotection/cognition + brain AGE↓ (animal, oral), anti-osteoclast/bone (animal), cardiac protection (animal), NF-κB/Nrf2 modulation (non-skin cells), MMP-9 enzyme inhibition (cell-free).
- *Borrowed from Urolithin A (a different molecule — never imply UroB shares this proof):* mitophagy/PINK1-Parkin, UVA/UVB fibroblast photoprotection, the topical wrinkle/hydration/erythema preprint, dermal collagen-gene transcriptomics.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- UroB is a **small, lipophilic** monohydroxy dibenzopyranone (MW 212, **more lipophilic than UroA** because it has one fewer OH) — physicochemically *plausible* for stratum-corneum partitioning. [10]
- **No skin-penetration / Franz-cell / permeation study of UroB was located.** Penetration depth and delivered dermal dose are entirely **[unverified]**.
- "Poor solubility limits drug-delivery potential" is flagged even for systemic use [8] — aqueous solubility will be a real formulation hurdle topically (likely needs a solubilizer/oil phase or a delivery system).
- Oral data are **doubly irrelevant** to a cream: UroB's dietary plasma levels are tiny *and* circulate as largely-inactive glucuronides. Topical is the only route that could plausibly deliver active aglycone to skin — and it is unstudied.

## 6. Formulation

> All of this is **inferred** — there is no published cosmetic formulation work on UroB.

- **Effective concentration (topical):** **Unknown — no topical study.** By loose analogy to UroA (trials at 0.5-1%), a 0.1-1% exploratory range *might* be a starting point, but there is **no efficacy anchor** for UroB. **[unverified]**
- **pH:** Phenolic lactone (dibenzopyranone); presumably stable in a mild acidic-to-neutral serum/cream range, but no published optimal-pH window. Confirm with supplier TDS. **[unverified]**
- **Solubility/stability:** **Poorly water-soluble** [8]; as a polyphenol-class lactone it is plausibly light/oxidation-sensitive → opaque/airless packaging, possible need for a solubilizing/encapsulation system. Synthetic high-purity grade reduces batch variability vs extract. Concrete stability data are supplier-held. **[partly unverified]**
- **Pairings (rational, but speculative):**
  - **Niacinamide** — barrier/tone; safe co-formulation.
  - **Antioxidants (vit C/E, ferulic)** — complementary to Nrf2/ROS theme.
  - **Humectants (HA, glycerin, trehalose)** — fit barrier-repair thesis; no expected conflict.
  - **Anti-glycation actives (carnosine)** — narrative pairing if leaning on the AGE angle.
- **Conflicts:** No documented hard incompatibilities; as with any polyphenol, **strong low-pH exfoliants, high-strength retinoids, benzoyl peroxide** could theoretically stress it or compound irritation — sequence/format separately until stability is confirmed. **[unverified]**
- **Sensory:** Off-white/pale powder; at <1% should be sensory-neutral in a well-built emulsion; verify for any faint color at higher loads.

## 7. Safety

- **No dedicated topical or oral human safety study of isolated UroB was located.** Safety is inferred from the urolithin class and animal data.
- **Class/animal tolerability:** Oral UroB was well-tolerated in rodents up to 150 mg/kg/day × 8 wk (no toxicity reported) [5]; muscle/bone studies report no adverse effects [1][17]. (Note: most formal urolithin tox work — e.g. Heilman 2017 GRAS data — is on **Urolithin A**, not B; do not transfer UroA's GRAS status to UroB.) **[UroB GRAS status: unverified]**
- **Irritation/sensitization (topical):** **No data.** Mandatory in-house RIPT/HRIPT + stability-linked irritation testing before any consumer use.
- **Endocrine consideration (the real flag):** UroB is a **phytoestrogen-like SERM and aromatase inhibitor**, and its muscle action is **androgen-receptor-mediated** [1][15][16]. ER binding is weak (µM) and dietary exposure is tiny, so systemic risk from a topical cosmetic dose is *likely low* — **but** for a brand likely marketing to **peri-/post-menopausal women** (a hormone-sensitive audience), "endocrine-active polyphenol" deserves explicit risk assessment and conservative claims. **[needs formal assessment]**
- **Sun sensitivity:** No evidence of photosensitization; mechanistically (Nrf2/AhR) it leans protective, but **untested on skin** and **not a sunscreen.**
- **Pregnancy/lactation:** **No data**; given SERM/aromatase-inhibitor activity, default to caution and "consult your doctor" language. **[unverified]**
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** Synthetic manufacture (not pomegranate extraction) → consistent purity, no agricultural supply volatility. No major sustainability flags identified for the synthetic route.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Cosmetic status is weaker than UroA's.** UroA is INCI-listed / CosIng-present / China-registered as a cosmetic active; **no equivalent cosmetic registration for UroB was found.** Using UroB in a cosmetic would likely require establishing its regulatory acceptability (INCI assignment, safety dossier) in target markets first. **[verify against live CosIng / regulatory counsel before any SKU]** [2][14]
- **Cosmetic vs drug line:** Anything implying UroB **treats/prevents disease or alters skin structure/function** ("boosts collagen," "rebuilds muscle," "reduces inflammation," "anti-glycation repair") risks **drug** classification (US FDA) and would need substantiation/legal review. The substantiation problem is **more acute than for UroA** because UroB has **no skin/topical data at all.**
- **The central trap:** UroB is one Google search away from being conflated with Urolithin A's mitophagy/longevity halo. **Borrowing UroA's "mitophagy / cellular renewal / clinically studied" story for a UroB product would be misleading** — they are different molecules, and UroB is specifically **not** a mitophagy inducer.
- **Safer cosmetic phrasing (✅, if used at all):** "a pomegranate-derived postbiotic studied for cellular health," "antioxidant," "helps support skin against environmental stress" (generic, with care).
- **Avoid / flag for legal (⚠️):** "mitophagy," "boosts collagen," "anti-glycation," "muscle/firming via the same pathway as muscle growth," "clinically proven," any cross-format (muscle/bone/brain) benefit on a skincare label, and any claim that leans on **Urolithin A** evidence.
- See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md) claim cheat-sheet.

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Form to buy:** **Generic synthetic UroB only.** Available as research-grade / bulk raw material (CAS 1139-83-9) from suppliers such as **Cayman Chemical (≥98%)**, **Aladdin Scientific (≥95%)**, **Stanford Advanced Materials**, and bulk Chinese "muscle-health raw material" vendors (≥99%); also retail research quantities on Amazon (e.g. Akersunder 98%). **No branded cosmetic-grade UroB** (no UroB analogue to UroA's Mitopure®) was found. [10][13][14]
- **Cost tier:** **High** (specialty synthesized molecule), though likely **less than branded UroA/Mitopure** because there's no premium brand or clinical-asset markup. Raw-material $/kg not publicly standardized; expect a premium active cost with **no efficacy story to justify it cosmetically.**
- **Strategic note:** With UroB you get **none of UroA's advantages** (no clinical halo, no INCI registration, no "ingredient people have heard of") and a **higher substantiation burden** — while still paying specialty-active prices. If the brand wants the urolithin narrative, **Urolithin A is the rational choice**; UroB offers no compelling reason to substitute.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

- **Hype:** "Urolithin" + "mitochondrial longevity" + "muscle/anti-aging" — riding entirely on Urolithin A's coattails and on UroB's genuine *muscle* paper.
- **Reality check #1 — wrong mechanism for the hype:** UroB is **not a mitophagy inducer**; the longevity/mitochondrial story that sells urolithins is **UroA's.** [8][9]
- **Reality check #2 — no skin data whatsoever:** **Zero** UroB studies on skin cells, skin, or topical use in any species. The collagen angle is a **cell-free MMP-9 assay** [6]; the anti-glycation angle is **mouse brain after oral dosing** [5].
- **Reality check #3 — loses head-to-head:** In direct comparisons (immune cells, antioxidant assays) **UroA out-performs UroB** [7][12]; UroB is the **weakest-antioxidant** common urolithin [8].
- **Reality check #4 — trivial dietary exposure & inactive conjugates:** Plasma UroB after pomegranate is ~0.01 µmol/L, mostly as inactive glucuronide [8][12]; the "from pomegranate" framing oversells a metabolite most people barely produce.
- **Reality check #5 — endocrine, not inert:** UroB is a SERM/aromatase inhibitor with AR-dependent muscle effects [1][15][16] — a *caution*, not a benefit, especially for a hormone-sensitive audience.
- **What's genuinely real:** A solid *systemic* biology (muscle anabolism, NF-κB↓, Nrf2↑, bone, neuro, MMP-9 enzyme inhibition) — just not, in any tested way, **skin** or **topical.**

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**❌ SKIP (for now) — interesting molecule, wrong evidence, weaker sibling.**

One line: *Urolithin B has real systemic biology but **no skin data, no topical data, no cosmetic registration, and is not even a mitophagy inducer** — so it can't credibly carry the longevity story, and Urolithin A does everything UroB might do, with actual skin evidence and an INCI listing, for a comparable premium.*

- **Why skip:** (1) **Empty topical/skin tier** — nothing to substantiate. (2) **Mechanistic mismatch** — UroB ≠ mitophagy, so it doesn't even deliver the class's signature longevity hook. (3) **Dominated by UroA** — wherever they're compared, UroA wins, and UroA already has a (preprint-grade) topical dataset + INCI status. (4) **Regulatory drag** — no confirmed cosmetic registration. (5) **Endocrine flag** for a likely hormone-sensitive audience.
- **Reconsider only if:** (a) independent **skin-cell or topical human** data on UroB emerges, **and** (b) a specific, differentiated angle appears (e.g. a validated topical **MMP-9 / anti-glycation** position UroA doesn't own), **and** (c) cosmetic regulatory status is cleared. Even then, weigh against simply using **Urolithin A** (see [`urolithin-a.md`](urolithin-a.md)).
- **If the brand wants the "urolithin / postbiotic mitochondrial" narrative at all → use Urolithin A, not B.**
- **Longevity relevance:** **Conceptually adjacent but weak in practice.** UroB touches inflammaging, oxidative stress, glycation and (systemically) muscle/bone aging — but it **misses the mitochondrial/mitophagy hallmark** that anchors the urolithin longevity claim, and has no skin translation. As a *skin-longevity* active it is among the least-substantiated options reviewed.

Log under [`../../ideas/not-considering.md`](../../ideas/not-considering.md) with reason: "No skin/topical/INCI evidence; not a mitophagy inducer; dominated by Urolithin A — revisit only on new topical/skin data."

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

_Verification pass: 2026-06-19. Method: each headline study cited in §4 was independently checked for (a) existence, (b) whether the cited result matches the source, (c) whether the evidence is HUMAN + TOPICAL vs. borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral data, and (d) whether it is even about Urolithin B (vs. Urolithin A, which dominates the search results). Sources re-confirmed via journal/PubMed/PMC records._

### Overall evidence grade for TOPICAL skin efficacy: **D (essentially unproven for skin)**

Rationale: The tier that matters for a cream — human topical — is **completely empty for Urolithin B**, and so is the skin-cell tier (no keratinocyte/fibroblast study exists). This is a full grade *below* Urolithin A (graded C, because UroA at least has one topical preprint + skin-fibroblast studies). UroB's real evidence is **systemic** (muscle, brain, bone, heart) and **non-skin in-vitro** (NF-κB, Nrf2, a cell-free MMP-9 assay). None of it has been shown to translate to skin, and UroB **lacks UroA's defining mitophagy mechanism.** For *systemic* UroB the grade would be ~C+ (decent animal/cell biology, no human RCTs); for **topical skin it is D.**

### What verified out cleanly (claims are real and accurately described)

- **Rodriguez 2017 [1]** — REAL. Muscle hypertrophy via AKT-independent mTORC1 + ubiquitin-proteasome suppression + **androgen-receptor dependence**; C2C12 + male C57BL mice; **osmotic-pump (systemic), not oral/topical.** Correctly tagged non-skin.
- **MMP-9 study [6]** — REAL. **UroB IC₅₀ 13.17 µM (strongest of the three), highest binding affinity**; ellagic acid 17.14, UroA 33.29; **cell-free enzyme only, no skin.** Correctly tiered as the weakest kind of "collagen" evidence.
- **Lee 2019 [4]** — REAL. NF-κB↓/Nrf2-HO-1↑/IL-10↑ in **microglia**, not skin. Correctly tagged.
- **Chen 2021 [5]** — REAL. **Oral** UroB in D-gal aging mice; **AGE/CML/RAGE↓ in brain tissue**, not skin. Correctly quarantined from skin claims.
- **Chen 2022 review [8]** — REAL. Confirms the load-bearing negatives: UroB is **not** described as a mitophagy inducer, **no skin/topical applications**, **no standalone human trials**, **weak antioxidant**, **very low plasma levels.**
- **Mitophagy mismatch — verified and, if anything, understated.** Independent check (2026-06): UroA induces mitophagy by **activating AMPK / inhibiting mTOR** (the physiological mitophagy brake) → PINK1/Parkin turnover; UroB, by contrast, is reported to **activate Akt/mTOR/ULK1** in some models, i.e. it pushes the mTOR axis in the **direction opposite** to mitophagy. So "UroB is not a mitophagy inducer" is correct and arguably conservative — its best-characterized mTOR activation is mechanistically *anti*-mitophagy. This makes borrowing UroA's "mitochondrial renewal" story for UroB not just unsupported but directionally wrong.
- **Comparative immune study [12]** and **antioxidant comparisons [7]** — REAL; both show **UroA > UroB**, supporting the "dominated by the sibling" conclusion.
- **SERM/endocrine [15][16]** — REAL; urolithins are phytoestrogen-like SERMs / aromatase inhibitors; UroB's ER affinity is weaker than UroA's. Correctly framed as a *caution*, not a benefit.
- **Bone studies [17][18]** — REAL anti-osteoclast effects; non-skin.

### Absence-of-evidence confirmation (the most important verification here)

- Targeted searches for **Urolithin B + skin / keratinocyte / fibroblast / wound-healing / photoaging / senescence / topical** returned **only Urolithin A** results. The claim "no UroB skin/topical study exists" is an **evidence-of-absence after directed searching**, not an oversight. If this file is ever challenged, the burden is on the challenger to produce a UroB-on-skin primary source.
- **Cosmetic INCI/CosIng registration for UroB** could not be confirmed and appears absent (UroA's is confirmed). Flagged **[unverified]** rather than asserted either way.

### Main caveats (carry into any marketing/claims work)

1. **No skin or topical UroB data at all** — topical efficacy is unsubstantiated; do not claim any skin benefit.
2. **UroB is NOT Urolithin A** — never borrow UroA's mitophagy/longevity/topical-trial story; UroB specifically is **not a mitophagy inducer.**
3. **Strong evidence is the wrong organ** (muscle/brain/bone/heart) **and the wrong route** (systemic/oral). It establishes molecule biology, not cream performance.
4. **UroB is the weaker sibling** wherever compared — for the urolithin narrative, UroA is the rational pick.
5. **Endocrine activity (SERM/aromatase/AR)** is a caution for a hormone-sensitive audience; assess formally before use.
6. **Regulatory status unclear** — no confirmed cosmetic registration; clear with counsel before any SKU.

### Bottom line

The dossier's argument and tiering are sound and appropriately skeptical: it does **not** overclaim, it correctly identifies that the **topical/skin tier is empty**, it does **not** import Urolithin A's evidence, and it correctly flags the mitophagy mismatch and endocrine caution. Topical-efficacy grade: **D.** For a skin-longevity brand, **Urolithin A is strictly the better choice within this class**; Urolithin B should be **skipped** pending new skin/topical data.
