# Ingredient: Niche / Long-Tail Acids — Glucoheptonolactone, Tropic Acid & Neutralized Glycolate Salts

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ❌ rejected (SKIP as named actives) · **Class:** Hydroxy acids & chemical exfoliants · **Slug:** `niche-hydroxy-acids`
**See also:** [`alpha-hydroxy-acids.md`](alpha-hydroxy-acids.md) (the parent AHA class — glycolic/lactic + salts), [`pha-bionic-acids.md`](pha-bionic-acids.md) (gluconolactone/lactobionic/maltobionic — the *well-evidenced* PHA cousins), [`salicylic-acid.md`](salicylic-acid.md) (the actual BHA — relevant because of a tropic-acid naming trap).

> Exhaustive, citation-backed literature review for the brand research base. This dossier is a deliberate **"long-tail / fringe" catch-all** for three things that get name-dropped in acid marketing but that almost no one has real topical anti-aging data on: **(1) glucoheptonolactone**, a 7-carbon polyhydroxy-acid cousin of gluconolactone; **(2) tropic acid**, an aromatic hydroxy acid best known as the acid half of atropine; and **(3) neutralized glycolate salts** (ammonium/sodium/potassium glycolate), the buffered/salt forms of glycolic acid. Evidence is graded by tier (in-vitro → animal → human oral → human topical), and benefits proven for **topical human use** are kept strictly separate from mechanistic or borrowed claims. Sources are numbered in §12. Claim-discipline lines per [`../../CLAUDE.md`](../../CLAUDE.md) and [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).
>
> **Headline finding up front (so no one over-reads this dossier):** there is **no meaningful direct topical anti-aging evidence for the named long-tail molecules themselves.** Glucoheptonolactone is a CIR **"insufficient data"** ingredient that is **not actually in commercial use**; tropic acid is a **pharmacopoeial atropine impurity with no cosmetic use case** (and is frequently confused with salicylic acid by junk SEO — that confusion is wrong); neutralized glycolate salts are simply **glycolic acid with the potency dialed down** and inherit (a weaker version of) the [AHA evidence base](alpha-hydroxy-acids.md). None of the three earns an independent place in the line.

## 1. Identity

This dossier covers three chemically unrelated "acids" that share only one thing — they are *long-tail* members of the hydroxy-acid universe that don't merit their own dossier but get mentioned enough to need a documented, honest answer.

### 1a. Glucoheptonolactone

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **INCI** | Glucoheptonolactone |
| **Common/chemical names** | D-glycero-D-gulo-heptono-1,4-lactone; D-glucoheptono-1,4-lactone; glucoheptonic acid γ-lactone |
| **CAS** | 60046-25-5 |
| **Molecular formula / MW** | C₇H₁₂O₇ / ~208 g/mol |
| **Class** | **Polyhydroxy acid (PHA)** — a 7-carbon (one carbon longer) homolog of gluconolactone (C₆, MW ~178). Cyclic lactone that hydrolyzes in water to glucoheptonic acid. |
| **Reported cosmetic function** | "Skin-conditioning agent — miscellaneous" (wINCI/CosIng). *Not* listed as a chelator (that's gluconolactone). [1] |
| **Actual market use** | **Effectively zero.** In the 2022 US VCRP + 2019 Personal Care Products Council survey, glucoheptonolactone reported **no uses**; only its sibling gluconolactone was actually used (312 formulations). [1] |

> Plain language: **a slightly bigger sugar-acid sibling of gluconolactone.** In principle it should behave like a gentle PHA (humectant, antioxidant, mild exfoliant). In practice almost nobody puts it in product, and almost nobody has studied it on skin.

### 1b. Tropic Acid

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Common/chemical names** | Tropic acid; **(±)-3-hydroxy-2-phenylpropanoic acid**; **α-(hydroxymethyl)phenylacetic acid**; 2-phenylhydracrylic acid |
| **CAS** | 552-63-6 (DL); R-isomer is "Atropine EP Impurity C" |
| **Molecular formula / MW** | C₉H₁₀O₃ / ~166 g/mol |
| **pKa** | ~3.38 (water, 25 °C) [2] |
| **Structure** | An **aromatic α-substituted acid**: a phenylacetic-acid backbone bearing a –CH₂OH (hydroxymethyl) on the α-carbon. Functionally the hydroxyl sits **β** to the carboxyl on a primary alcohol, so it is best described as an aromatic **β-hydroxy(methyl) acid** — structurally distinct from both glycolic (AHA) and salicylic (a *phenolic* BHA). |
| **Reported cosmetic function** | **None of record.** Not an established INCI cosmetic active. Primary identity is as the **acid moiety of the tropane alkaloids atropine & hyoscyamine** (atropine hydrolyzes to tropic acid + tropine) and as a **pharmacopoeial impurity reference standard** for atropine. [2][3] |

> ⚠️ **Naming trap — read this.** Multiple low-quality skincare blogs and a couple of search engines assert that *"tropic acid"* is just an alternative name for **salicylic acid**. **This is false.** Salicylic acid is 2-hydroxybenzoic acid (a phenolic BHA, C₇H₆O₃). Tropic acid is 3-hydroxy-2-phenylpropanoic acid (C₉H₁₀O₃), the atropine building block. They are different molecules with different chemistry, uses, and safety profiles. The conflation appears to come from sloppy "BHA alternative names" listicles and must never be repeated in our copy. (Likewise, "tropic acid" has nothing to do with the UK brand *Tropic Skincare*.)

### 1c. Neutralized Glycolate Salts

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **INCI** | Ammonium Glycolate; Sodium Glycolate; Potassium Glycolate; Calcium Glycolate |
| **Parent acid** | **Glycolic acid** (2-hydroxyacetic acid; CAS 79-14-1; MW ~76) — the smallest AHA. |
| **CAS (examples)** | Ammonium glycolate 35249-65-9; sodium glycolate 2836-32-0 |
| **What they are** | The **salt (neutralized) forms** of glycolic acid — glycolate anion paired with NH₄⁺/Na⁺/K⁺/Ca²⁺. In water they exist in equilibrium with free glycolic acid; the **free-acid fraction is set by pH** (see §2, §5). |
| **Reported cosmetic function** | pH adjuster / buffer, exfoliant (as part of a glycolic system), skin conditioning. Used in moisturizers, cleansers, makeup, hair products. [4][5] |

> Plain language: **glycolic acid with the volume turned down.** A "neutralized glycolate" formula is a glycolic-acid formula in which more of the acid has been salted out — gentler, but proportionally less active. These are **not a new ingredient**; they are a formulation lever on the AHA workhorse covered in [`alpha-hydroxy-acids.md`](alpha-hydroxy-acids.md).

## 2. Class & Mechanism

Three different mechanism stories, because these are three different things.

**Glucoheptonolactone (PHA mechanism — inferred from the PHA class, not measured on this molecule):**
1. **Mild corneodesmolysis / desquamation.** Like other hydroxy acids it lowers stratum-corneum (SC) pH and weakens corneocyte cohesion → gentle exfoliation. Being **larger than glycolic** (MW ~208 vs 76) it penetrates slowly and superficially → much less sting (the PHA class trait). [6][7]
2. **Humectancy.** Multiple hydroxyls bind water → moisturization.
3. **Antioxidant / metal chelation.** The PHA/gluconolactone family scavenges radicals and chelates redox-active metals (Fe/Cu). [8]
*All of the above is **borrowed from gluconolactone/the PHA class** — there is no published study showing glucoheptonolactone itself does these on skin. Treat as plausible-by-analogy only.*

**Tropic acid (essentially undefined for skin):** As a small aromatic hydroxy acid with pKa ~3.4 it *could* in theory act as a low-pH exfoliating acid, but **no mechanistic skin work exists.** Its real-world chemistry is as an ester partner in tropane alkaloids and as a synthetic intermediate. There is no corneodesmolysis, barrier, collagen, or antioxidant data for it on skin. [2][3]

**Neutralized glycolate salts (AHA mechanism, attenuated):** Identical mechanism to glycolic acid — **corneodesmolysis** via disruption of calcium-dependent corneodesmosome bonds, accelerated desquamation, thinner/compacter SC over a thicker viable epidermis, and (at adequate exposure) downstream dermal/ECM effects. **The catch:** SC permeation favors the **neutral free acid**, not the ionized salt. Neutralizing glycolic acid toward/above its pKa (~3.8) converts free acid to glycolate anion, **reducing the bioavailable, active fraction**. So salts trade efficacy for tolerability — they are a *dose-reduction* tool, not a distinct mechanism. [4][9] See [`alpha-hydroxy-acids.md`](alpha-hydroxy-acids.md) §2, §5.

## 4. Evidence Review by Tier

> The core of this dossier. The brutal-honesty summary: **for the three *named* molecules, the human-topical tier is essentially empty.** What real evidence exists is for **gluconolactone** (the C₆ sibling, not glucoheptonolactone) and for **glycolic acid** (the parent, not its salts). I include those as the nearest-neighbor evidence and label every borrow explicitly.

### Tier 1 — In-vitro (mechanism only)

- **Bernstein EF, Brown DB, Schwartz MD, Kaidbey K, Ksenzenko SM (2004), *Dermatol Surg* 30(2 Pt 2):189–195.** Transgenic (elastin-promoter) in-vitro photoaging model. **Gluconolactone** gave up to **~50% inhibition** of UV-induced elastin-promoter activation, attributed to **radical scavenging + metal chelation** (free-radical scavenging reported comparable to ascorbic acid / α-tocopherol in the supporting literature), and did **not** increase sunburn-cell formation. *Limitation: in-vitro reporter model; "50% protection" is NOT a clinical SPF and must never be framed as sun protection. Tests **gluconolactone**, not glucoheptonolactone.* [8]
- **Hachem J-P, Roelandt T, Schürer N, et al. (2010), *J Invest Dermatol* 130(2):500–510.** Ex-vivo / hairless-mouse SC work. **Gluconolactone and lactobionic acid** acutely **acidified the SC**, enhanced lipid-processing enzymes (β-glucocerebrosidase, acidic sphingomyelinase), improved lamellar-membrane maturation and **barrier recovery**, and produced "supernormal" SC integrity (reduced desmoglein-1 degradation, increased corneodesmosome density). *Mechanistic support for PHAs being barrier-friendly — but again **gluconolactone/lactobionic**, not glucoheptonolactone, and not human.* [7]
- **Glucoheptonolactone specifically:** **no in-vitro skin-aging studies located.** As a chemical it is documented mainly as a chelator-type/synthetic compound (e.g., precursor chemistry), not a studied cosmetic active. [1]
- **Tropic acid specifically:** **no in-vitro skin/anti-aging studies located.** Literature is pharmacology (tropane-alkaloid hydrolysis), analytical (atropine impurity assays), and synthetic. [2][3]

**Tier verdict:** A real but **class-level** (gluconolactone/lactobionic) mechanistic story for barrier + antioxidant effects. **Zero molecule-specific in-vitro skin data for glucoheptonolactone or tropic acid.**

### Tier 2 — Animal

- Only the **hairless-mouse SC acidification** work above (Hachem 2010), and it tests **gluconolactone/lactobionic**, not the named molecules. [7]
- **No animal efficacy data** for glucoheptonolactone or tropic acid on skin aging.

### Tier 3 — Human, ORAL

- **Not applicable / do not borrow.** Glucoheptonolactone and gluconolactone are sugar-acid lactones (gluconolactone is even a GRAS food acidulant), but **no oral skin-aging evidence exists** for any of these. Tropic acid is an alkaloid metabolite/impurity, not a supplement. **No oral framing may be transferred to topical claims.** [1]

### Tier 4 — Human, TOPICAL

**RCT-level (vehicle/placebo-controlled, blinded) for the named molecules:** **None.** There is **no** vehicle-controlled RCT of glucoheptonolactone, tropic acid, or an isolated neutralized glycolate salt for anti-aging.

**Nearest-neighbor human topical evidence (explicitly borrowed):**

- **Berardesca E, Distante F, Vignoli GP, Oresajo C, Green B (1997), *Br J Dermatol* 137(6):934–938** — "Alpha hydroxyacids modulate stratum corneum barrier function." n=11 healthy subjects; **8% glycolic, lactic, tartaric, and gluconolactone** applied, then a **5% SLS irritant challenge**. **Gluconolactone and tartaric** sites had **significantly lower TEWL** vs vehicle/untreated control (P<0.01) and vs glycolic (P<0.05) at 24 & 48 h, with reduced erythema — i.e., the PHA was **barrier-protective**, unlike glycolic. *Small n; tests gluconolactone, not glucoheptonolactone; biophysical endpoints, not visible anti-aging.* [10]
- **Edison BL, Green BA, Wildnauer RH, Sigler ML (2004), *Cutis* 73(2 Suppl):14–17** — 12-week comparative trial: **gluconolactone (PHA) regimen vs glycolic-acid (AHA) regimen.** Both gave significant photoaging benefit (clinical grading, silicone-replica texture, pinch recoil). AHA edged PHA on sallowness (17.1% vs 12.4%) and recoil (13.5% vs 10.2%), **but PHA was significantly better tolerated** (less stinging/burning). *Active-comparator only (no vehicle arm); **all authors NeoStrata**; supplement issue; tests gluconolactone, not glucoheptonolactone.* [11]
- **CIR human safety/efficacy notes on gluconolactone (in the glycolactones assessment):** an **8% gluconolactone** cream (n=11, twice daily × 4 wk) lowered TEWL and erythema after SLS challenge (= the Berardesca study); HRIPTs at up to **15% gluconolactone** (n=106) showed **no irritation or sensitization**. *All gluconolactone; supports the PHA-tolerability story, not the named molecules.* [1]
- **Neutralized glycolate salts:** their human evidence **is** the glycolic-acid AHA evidence (Stiller 1996 RCT; Ditre 1996 pilot; etc.), discounted for the lower free-acid fraction. See [`alpha-hydroxy-acids.md`](alpha-hydroxy-acids.md) §4. No study isolates a *salt* form as the active and shows independent benefit.

**Glucoheptonolactone topical human data:** **none located.**
**Tropic acid topical human data:** **none located.**

**What is genuinely PROVEN for topical human use of the three named molecules:** **nothing molecule-specific.** What is proven nearby: gluconolactone (a *different* PHA) is a gentle, barrier-friendly exfoliant/humectant with good tolerability; glycolic acid (the *parent* of the salts) has modest RCT-grade photoaging benefit. Neither proof transfers automatically to glucoheptonolactone or tropic acid.

## 5. Topical Bioavailability / Penetration

- **Glucoheptonolactone:** larger and more hydrophilic than gluconolactone (MW ~208, log Kow strongly negative like its sibling, ~−2.2 for gluconolactone) → expect **slow, shallow penetration** typical of PHAs. No measured human penetration data. [1]
- **Tropic acid:** small (MW ~166), water-soluble (~20 g/L), pKa ~3.4 → would behave like a small acid (pH-dependent free-acid permeation), but **no skin-penetration data exist** and it is not formulated into skincare. [2]
- **Neutralized glycolate salts:** the master variable is **pH / free-acid fraction.** Glycolic pKa ≈ 3.8; at pH ≈ pKa ~half is free acid (the permeant species), above it the ionized **glycolate salt dominates and penetrates far less.** Neutralization therefore directly **lowers bioavailable active.** Buffered/partially-neutralized systems are the standard way to trade potency for comfort. See [`alpha-hydroxy-acids.md`](alpha-hydroxy-acids.md) §5. [4][9]

## 6. Formulation

- **Glucoheptonolactone:** would formulate like a PHA — water-soluble, set pH ~3.5–4 for exfoliation or ~5+ as a conditioning humectant; pairs with humectants/barrier lipids. **But:** no commercial track record, uncertain supply, and **CIR "insufficient data" status** (§7/§8) make it a formulation liability with no upside over gluconolactone. **If you want this chemistry, use gluconolactone** (proven, safe-rated, widely supplied) — see [`pha-bionic-acids.md`](pha-bionic-acids.md).
- **Tropic acid:** **not a cosmetic raw material.** Sold as a lab/pharma reagent and impurity standard, not as a cosmetic-grade ingredient. No INCI cosmetic-use precedent, no stability/sensory data, no supplier ecosystem for skincare. **Do not formulate.**
- **Neutralized glycolate salts:** these *are* a formulation technique for glycolic acid. Use the **corresponding salt (sodium/ammonium glycolate) to partially neutralize free glycolic to a target pH** (typically ~3.5–4.0 leave-on), softening the irritation curve; sodium glycolate also nudges humectancy. **Conc/pH/pairings = glycolic acid's** ([`alpha-hydroxy-acids.md`](alpha-hydroxy-acids.md) §6). Sensory: well-buffered = lighter, less sting. Pair with HA/glycerin/ceramides/niacinamide/panthenol; avoid same-step stacking with strong retinoids or high-strength L-ascorbic acid.

## 7. Safety

- **Glucoheptonolactone:** **CIR concluded the available data are INSUFFICIENT** to determine that glucoheptonolactone is safe under intended cosmetic conditions of use (2022 glycolactones assessment) — in contrast to **gluconolactone, which CIR concluded IS safe** at present use/concentration. No specific irritation, sensitization, photosensitivity, or systemic data on glucoheptonolactone were available to the Panel. *This is a documented data gap, not a finding of harm — but for a premium, claims-disciplined brand it is a reason to avoid the molecule, not a reassurance.* [1]
- **Tropic acid:** no cosmetic safety dossier (no CIR/SCCS cosmetic review). Toxicology context is limited and pharmacological (alkaloid chemistry); it is handled as a **pharmacopoeial impurity reference standard**. No basis to assert dermal safety for leave-on cosmetic use. **Treat as unqualified for skincare.** [2][3]
- **Neutralized glycolate salts:** covered by the **CIR AHA assessment** alongside glycolic acid — **safe at ≤10%, final pH ≥3.5** for consumer products (formulated to avoid increased sun sensitivity or with daily-SPF directions); salon ≤30%, pH ≥3.0. [4][5][12] **Sun sensitivity:** because they are glycolic-acid systems, the **AHA UV-sensitization liability and FDA "Sunburn Alert" labeling apply** to any meaningfully active glycolate formula (the more you neutralize, the gentler — but if it exfoliates, treat it as an AHA for labeling). [12][13] Irritation/PIH risk per glycolic. Pregnancy: as for topical glycolic — low systemic absorption, generally considered acceptable, precautionary "consult provider" boilerplate. See [`alpha-hydroxy-acids.md`](alpha-hydroxy-acids.md) §7.
- **PHA sun-sensitivity nuance (relevant if anyone reaches for glucoheptonolactone):** the **PHA class does NOT carry the AHA sun-sensitivity flag** — FDA/PCPC sunburn-cell testing found **gluconolactone and glucoheptonolactone did not increase sunburn-cell formation** after UVB, and PHAs are generally not subject to the AHA "Sunburn Alert" recommendation. This is a genuine class advantage — but it accrues to **gluconolactone** (which you'd actually use), not as a reason to gamble on the unproven glucoheptonolactone. [8][13]

## 8. Regulatory & Claims

- **Cosmetic vs drug line:** as exfoliant/conditioning cosmetics for *appearance*, hydroxy acids sit in **cosmetic** territory; claims to *treat/prevent disease* or *alter skin structure/function* (e.g., "boosts collagen by X%," "treats sun damage") risk reclassification as an **unapproved drug** (US FDA). Note CIR explicitly flags that **"anti-acne agent" is a drug function in the US** (raised re: gluconolactone) — a reminder that an exfoliant marketed for acne crosses into OTC-drug territory. [1]
- **Glucoheptonolactone — US (CIR):** **"insufficient data"** (not affirmed safe). It is INCI-listed (skin-conditioning) but unproven and essentially unused. Using it would mean **self-substantiating safety** with no Panel backing. [1]
- **Tropic acid — regulatory:** no cosmetic regulatory status as a skincare active; it is a **pharmacopoeial impurity / reagent** (e.g., "Atropine EP Impurity C"). There is **no recognized cosmetic use case to make claims about.** [3]
- **Neutralized glycolate salts — US:** covered by CIR AHA limits (**≤10%, pH ≥3.5**, sun-protection conditions) and FDA AHA **"Sunburn Alert"** labeling guidance. EU AHA rules are stricter for glycolic — confirm current SCCS status before any EU launch. [4][12][13] See [`alpha-hydroxy-acids.md`](alpha-hydroxy-acids.md) §8.
- **Claim-language cheat sheet (this dossier):**
  - ✅ **Safe cosmetic (only if we actually use the *proven* cousins, not these molecules):** "gently exfoliates," "smooths the look of texture," "supports the moisture barrier" — language that belongs to **gluconolactone/PHA** or **glycolic AHA**, documented in their own dossiers.
  - ⛔ **Do not claim anything** for glucoheptonolactone or tropic acid specifically — there is no substantiation, and tropic acid isn't even a cosmetic ingredient.
  - ⛔ **Never** call tropic acid "a form of salicylic acid / BHA" — it is chemically false (§1b) and would be a substantiation and accuracy failure.
  - See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).

## 9. Sourcing & Cost

- **Glucoheptonolactone:** niche specialty/reagent chemical; **no established cosmetic-grade supply chain** at scale; price/availability uncertain. No cost advantage over the cheap, ubiquitous gluconolactone.
- **Tropic acid:** sold by **lab/fine-chemical and pharma-impurity suppliers** (Sigma-Aldrich, Santa Cruz, BOC Sciences, MedChemExpress) as a reagent/impurity standard — **not as a cosmetic raw material.** Reagent-grade pricing is irrelevant to cosmetic sourcing because there is no cosmetic grade.
- **Neutralized glycolate salts:** **commodity** — they are just glycolic acid + a cheap base, or pre-made glycolate salts from the same specialty-chemical distributors that sell glycolic. Low cost, low supply risk. On-thesis with "buy commodity bases, invest in the brand."

## 10. Hype vs. Evidence

- **"Glucoheptonolactone = next-gen PHA upgrade over gluconolactone" →** *Unsupported.* No molecule-specific skin data, **CIR "insufficient data,"** essentially no market use. Any benefit is borrowed from gluconolactone — so just **use gluconolactone.** Marketing glucoheptonolactone as a premium PHA would be hype with no substantiation.
- **"Tropic acid is a gentle exfoliating acid / a form of BHA / salicylic acid" →** *False and a chemistry error.* Tropic acid is the atropine building block, not a cosmetic exfoliant, and is **not** salicylic acid. Using it as a marketing acid would be both unsubstantiated and inaccurate.
- **"Neutralized glycolate = all the glycolic benefit, none of the irritation" →** *Half-true, and self-defeating if oversold.* Neutralization reduces irritation **by reducing the active free-acid fraction** — i.e., it also reduces efficacy. You can't fully decouple the two; you're moving a dial, not getting a free lunch. And if it still exfoliates, it still warrants AHA sun-sensitivity handling.
- **"Long-tail acids let us claim a unique/proprietary exfoliant" →** *Reputational risk.* For a science-credible premium brand, leaning on obscure, unstudied acids is the opposite of evidence-led. The credible move is the boring one: a well-formulated **gluconolactone (PHA)** and/or **glycolic/lactic (AHA)** system.

## 11. Verdict for the Brand

❌ **SKIP** (as named, standalone actives) — none of the three long-tail molecules earns an independent place in the line.

- **Glucoheptonolactone → SKIP.** No molecule-specific evidence, **CIR "insufficient data,"** near-zero market use. Everything you'd want from it is delivered — proven and safe-rated — by **gluconolactone**. Route any "gentle PHA" intent to [`pha-bionic-acids.md`](pha-bionic-acids.md).
- **Tropic acid → HARD SKIP.** Not a cosmetic ingredient; it's an atropine impurity/reagent. Zero skin evidence, no supply chain, and a live risk of the false "it's salicylic acid" claim. Do not formulate or reference.
- **Neutralized glycolate salts → not a separate ingredient; use as a formulation lever only.** They belong inside a glycolic-acid system as a **pH/tolerability buffer**, governed entirely by the [AHA dossier](alpha-hydroxy-acids.md). Decision: shortlist **glycolic/lactic AHAs** there; treat "neutralized glycolate" purely as a buffering technique, not a hero or even a named supporting active.
- **Longevity-relevance note:** these long-tail acids map onto **surface renewal / barrier / hydration at best**, and only by analogy to their better-studied cousins. They touch **none** of the longevity hallmarks (senescence, mitochondria, glycation, autophagy) with any molecule-specific evidence. There is no longevity story to tell here — and trying to manufacture one from obscure acids would undercut the brand's science credibility.
- **Net strategic call:** the *category* (gentle exfoliation, barrier-respecting acids) is on-brand and worth owning — but win it with the **evidenced** members (gluconolactone PHA; glycolic/lactic AHA), not these fringe names. Log in [`../../ideas/not-considering.md`](../../ideas/not-considering.md) with the reason "no molecule-specific evidence / CIR-insufficient / not a cosmetic ingredient; superseded by gluconolactone + AHA dossiers."

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Verified:** 2026-06-19 · Independent adversarial check of every headline claim against the primary literature. Each cited study was assessed for (a) evidence tier — is it HUMAN + TOPICAL, or borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral or from a *different molecule*?, (b) does the source exist and say what the dossier claims?, (c) industry funding / sample size.

### Overall evidence grade: **D (essentially unproven topically for the named molecules)**

Grade scale: A = strong human-topical RCT; B = moderate human-topical; C = weak/indirect; D = essentially unproven topically / hype.

**Why D:** This dossier covers three long-tail molecules and the honest verdict is that **none of them has molecule-specific topical anti-aging evidence.** The grade is not a knock on the dossier's rigor — it's the accurate state of the science. The only real human-topical data (Berardesca 1997; Edison 2004) are for **gluconolactone**, a *different* (C₆) molecule with its own dossier, and the salts' evidence is just discounted **glycolic-acid** data. The dossier correctly refuses to launder either into a claim for glucoheptonolactone, tropic acid, or "neutralized glycolate" as a standalone active.

**No fabricated sources found.** Every "real evidence" citation is for a *cousin* molecule (gluconolactone) or the *parent* (glycolic acid), and the dossier labels each borrow explicitly. The one citation I could not pull in full text (Grimes 2004 [6], a Cutis supplement) is corroborated by the CIR report, the Bernstein paper, and the clinicalgate textbook chapter for the specific claims drawn from it (PHA barrier/humectant; gluconolactone & glucoheptonolactone did not increase sunburn cells) — mark as **[corroborated, primary supplement not directly retrieved]**.

### Main caveats
1. **The named molecules have ~zero molecule-specific topical evidence.** Everything credible is borrowed from gluconolactone or glycolic acid. The dossier is built around making that borrow visible rather than hiding it.
2. **Glucoheptonolactone is CIR "insufficient data" + unused** — a regulatory/commercial liability, not a hidden gem.
3. **Tropic acid is not a cosmetic ingredient** and the "it's salicylic acid" claim is a real, repeated online error that we must never adopt.
4. **Neutralized glycolate salts can't decouple gentleness from potency** — neutralization lowers the active free-acid fraction; and an active glycolate system still warrants AHA sun-sensitivity handling.

**Net:** Accurate, well-sourced, appropriately skeptical. Grade **D** — reflecting the genuine absence of evidence for the *named* molecules, not a flaw in the review. Verdict (SKIP all three as standalone actives; route intent to the gluconolactone-PHA and glycolic/lactic-AHA dossiers) is correct and evidence-aligned.
