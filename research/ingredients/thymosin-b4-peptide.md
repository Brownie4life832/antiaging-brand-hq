# Ingredient: Acetyl sh-Pentapeptide-1 ("Thymosin-β4 fragment peptide" / Acetyl Thymopentin)

**Slug:** thymosin-b4-peptide · **Class:** Peptides
**Researched:** 2026-06-19 (exhaustive build) · **Status:** ⬜ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ☑ flagged for caution / ⬜ in a product

> **How to read this dossier.** This is an evidence-graded literature review, not marketing copy. The core is the **tiered evidence review (§4)**.
>
> **READ THIS FIRST — there is a naming error baked into the brief, and it is load-bearing.** The brief calls this ingredient *"Thymosin-β4 fragment peptide (Acetyl sh-Pentapeptide-1)."* Those two names refer to **two different molecules from two different parent proteins**, and the cosmetic INCI **Acetyl sh-Pentapeptide-1 is the second one, not the first**:
> 1. **Thymosin-β4 (Tβ4)** is a 43-amino-acid actin-sequestering protein. Its famous bioactive fragments are **Ac-SDKP** (Tβ4 1–4) and the actin-binding domain **LKKTETQ** (Tβ4 17–23). This is the "TB-500 / wound-healing peptide" world.
> 2. **Acetyl sh-Pentapeptide-1** has the sequence **Ac-Arg-Lys-Asp-Val-Tyr (Ac-RKDVY)**. That is acetylated **thymopentin (TP5)** — residues **32–36 of THYMOPOIETIN**, a completely different thymic peptide hormone. It is **not a fragment of thymosin-β4 at all.** [1][2][3][4]
>
> So this dossier covers **acetyl-thymopentin** as the actual cosmetic ingredient (§1–§3, §5–§11), and **separately and explicitly** reviews the **thymosin-β4 fragment** evidence the brief's title points at (§4 Tier "Tβ4"), so the brand cannot accidentally borrow Tβ4's wound-healing data to sell a thymopentin product. **The single most important honest finding: the human topical *cosmetic anti-aging* evidence for this INCI is essentially zero, and almost the entire skin-marketing narrative (IL-8/MMP-9/collagen-protection) is repeated from ingredient-vendor copy, not from primary peer-reviewed studies on this molecule.**

---

## 1. Identity

- **INCI name:** **Acetyl sh-Pentapeptide-1** (current ICID name; the "sh-" prefix denotes a synthetic peptide identical in sequence to a fragment of a human/host protein). **Former INCI: Acetyl Pentapeptide-1.** [1][2]
- **Sequence:** **Ac-Arg-Lys-Asp-Val-Tyr** (Ac-RKDVY); i.e. **N-acetyl-thymopentin**. [1][2][4]
- **CAS:** **97530-32-0** (acetyl form). The parent free pentapeptide **thymopentin (TP5)** is **CAS 69558-55-0**. [1][5][6]
- **Also called:** Acetyl Thymopentin; Acetyl Thymopentin Peptide. [2]
- **Parent protein:** **Thymopoietin** (a 49-residue thymic polypeptide). Thymopentin = thymopoietin **residues 32–36**, the minimal sequence that retains thymopoietin's immune activity. [3][5]
- **Origin:** Fully **synthetic** ("sh-" / chemically synthesized); not extracted from animals.
- **Why the acetyl group exists:** native thymopentin is famously unstable — plasma **half-life ≈ 30 seconds** due to rapid proteolysis [5]. N-terminal acetylation is a standard medicinal-chemistry move to slow aminopeptidase cleavage and improve shelf/skin stability [7][8].

**Key forms / "relatives" to keep straight (do not conflate):**
| Name | Sequence / identity | Parent | Relevance here |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Acetyl sh-Pentapeptide-1** | Ac-RKDVY (acetyl-thymopentin) | Thymopoietin (32–36) | **THIS ingredient** |
| Thymopentin (TP5) | RKDVY | Thymopoietin (32–36) | The non-acetylated drug |
| Thymopoietin | 49-aa peptide | — | Parent hormone |
| **Thymosin-β4 (Tβ4)** | 43-aa protein | Tβ4 gene | **Different molecule** (what the brief's *title* names) |
| **Ac-SDKP** | Ac-Ser-Asp-Lys-Pro | **Tβ4 (1–4)** | True Tβ4 fragment; anti-fibrotic [9][10] |
| **LKKTETQ** | Tβ4 17–23 | **Tβ4** | Actin-binding/migration domain [9] |
| TB-500 | (markets itself as Tβ4 / fragment) | Tβ4 | Grey-market injectable [11] |

## 2. Class & Mechanism

Acetyl sh-Pentapeptide-1 is classed as a **signal / immunomodulatory peptide** (INCI listed functions: *immunomodulator, skin-conditioning; vendor copy adds "soothing/firming"*). Its parent, thymopentin, is a genuine **immune-active peptide hormone** with a real (non-cosmetic) pharmacology.

**Mechanisms — split honestly into "real for thymopentin the drug" vs "asserted for the cosmetic":**

*Established for thymopentin (the systemic immunomodulator):*
- **T-cell modulation.** Thymopentin reproduces thymopoietin's induction of **T-cell differentiation/maturation**, acting through cyclic-GMP signaling in lymphocytes and monocytes [3][5]. This is its reason for existence as an immunology drug.
- **Cytokine/immune normalization** in T-cell-driven disease (e.g. atopic dermatitis), demonstrated by **injection** (§4). [12][13][14]

*Asserted for the cosmetic (mechanistic story repeated by ingredient vendors — see the honesty flag):*
- **IL-8 suppression → reduced MMP-9 release → less degradation of collagen IV / basement-membrane and structural proteins**, yielding "calmer, firmer-looking" skin. [1][2][15]
  - **Critical caveat:** This IL-8↓/MMP-9↓ chain is biologically *plausible* (IL-8 and MMP-9 are real players in inflammatory skin remodeling), **but I could not locate a single primary, peer-reviewed study showing acetyl-thymopentin (or thymopentin) does this *in human skin or keratinocytes at cosmetic doses*.** The claim is recycled verbatim across ingredient-database/marketing pages [1][2][15]. The leading peer-reviewed review of **peptides for sensitive skin** (Ferreira et al. 2021) reviews seven sensitive-skin peptides and **does not mention this one at all** [16]. Treat the mechanism as **vendor hypothesis, not evidence.**

## 4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (core of this dossier)

**Reading guide:** Two separate evidence stories run here. **(A)** the actual cosmetic ingredient = acetyl-thymopentin; **(B)** the molecule the brief's title names = thymosin-β4 fragment. They must never be merged.

### (A) ACETYL-THYMOPENTIN / THYMOPENTIN — the actual INCI

#### Tier A1 — In-vitro
- **Thymopentin's in-vitro pharmacology is real but immunological, not dermatological.** It modulates T-cell/monocyte function via cGMP and triggers T-cell differentiation in lymphocyte assays [3][5]. **No primary in-vitro study was located testing acetyl-thymopentin on keratinocytes/fibroblasts for the marketed IL-8↓/MMP-9↓/collagen-protection chain.** The cosmetic mechanism is asserted, not sourced [1][2][15][16].
- **Stability chemistry is documented:** Heavner et al. and DeGraw et al. designed enzymatically **stabilized thymopentin analogs** precisely because native TP5 is degraded in seconds — confirming why an *acetyl* form is used, but these are chemistry papers, not skin-efficacy papers [7][8].

#### Tier A2 — Animal
- **2025 — Thymopentin nanoplatform for diabetic wound healing** (Wang et al., *J Colloid Interface Sci*): a TP5-integrated self-assembling photo-immunotherapy system promoted keratinocyte migration and suppressed pro-inflammatory cytokines in a **diabetic mouse wound** model [17]. Interesting for the *wound* angle, but it is (a) a **delivery-engineered nanoplatform**, not a cosmetic cream, (b) **animal**, and (c) a **wound** model, not cosmetic anti-aging. Do not borrow.

#### Tier A3 — Human ORAL / SYSTEMIC (this is where thymopentin's real human data live — and it's all by injection)
Thymopentin is a registered immunomodulator drug (trade name **Timunox/Timunome**) given **subcutaneously/IV**, not orally (the 30-second plasma half-life makes simple oral/topical delivery futile without engineering) [5]. Human dermatology trials — **all systemic injection, for atopic dermatitis, an immune disease, not cosmetic aging:**
- **Stiller MJ, et al. 1994**, *J Am Acad Dermatol* 30(4):597–602 — **Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled**, multicenter. **n=39** patients (≥2 y) with **severe** atopic dermatitis (≥20% BSA). **Thymopentin 50 mg subcutaneously, 3×/week, 12 weeks.** Result: significantly greater improvement vs placebo; no drug-related adverse events. Conclusion: "may be a safe effective **adjunct**." **Limitations for us: injected, immune-disease population, adjunctive, small, and nothing to do with cosmetic aging or topical delivery** [12].
- **Leung DY, et al. 1990**, *J Allergy Clin Immunol* — thymopentin **reduced clinical severity** of atopic dermatitis; **subcutaneous**, immunologic endpoints [13].
- **Hsieh KH, et al. 1992**, *Arch Dis Child* — thymopentin in **severe childhood AD**, clinical + immunological evaluation; **injected** [14].
- **Early TP-5 report 1983**, *J Am Acad Dermatol* — thymopoietin pentapeptide improved clinical parameters/lymphocyte subpopulations in AD [18].
- **Haneke E.** — review of thymopentin in dermatology (immunodermatology context) [19].

#### Tier A4 — Human **TOPICAL COSMETIC** (the tier that matters for the brand)
- **NONE LOCATED.** I found **no** controlled trial — RCT or otherwise — of **topical acetyl-thymopentin for cosmetic anti-aging, soothing, firmness, redness, or barrier** endpoints. The only "data" are vendor/ingredient-database assertions and consumer product listings (e.g. Numbuzin NAD+ PDRN toner, Nip+Fab eye patches) [1][2][15][20]. **The marketed cosmetic benefits are, for this molecule, at evidence Tier 0 (assertion).**

### (B) THYMOSIN-β4 FRAGMENT (Tβ4 / Ac-SDKP / LKKTETQ) — what the brief's TITLE names (different molecule)

Included so the brand does not import this evidence onto a thymopentin product.

#### Tier B1 — In-vitro / mechanism
- **Goldstein, Hannappel, Kleinman** reviews: Tβ4 is an **actin-sequestering** peptide; its **LKKTETQ** (17–23) domain binds G-actin to drive **cell migration**; the N-terminal **Ac-SDKP** (1–4) carries **anti-inflammatory/anti-fibrotic/pro-angiogenic** activity; Tβ4 upregulates **VEGF**, angiopoietin-1, and blocks **NF-κB** via IκB [9][21][22].

#### Tier B2 — Animal
- Tβ4 accelerates wound healing in **diabetic, aged, and burn** rodent models, and promotes hair growth in mice/rats via **Wnt/β-catenin** signaling [9][21]. **Animal only for hair; do not claim.**

#### Tier B3 — Human ORAL/SYSTEMIC
- "TB-500" (sold as Tβ4) is an **injectable grey-market** peptide with **no FDA approval and no rigorous human anti-aging trials**; WADA-prohibited in sport [11]. Not transferable to cosmetics.

#### Tier B4 — Human **TOPICAL** (Tβ4, real but for wounds/eye, not cosmetic aging)
- **Treadwell T, Kleinman HK, Crockford D, Goldstein AL, et al.** — Phase 2, **topical Tβ4 gel** (0.01/0.03/0.1%) in **n=143** chronic **pressure/venous stasis ulcers**: accelerated healing by ~1 month in those who healed; safe/well tolerated [9][23][24].
- **Guarnera G, et al. 2007**, *Ann N Y Acad Sci* 1112:407–412 — European prospective, randomized, **topical** Tβ4 venous-ulcer study (planned n=72; interim ~21); **safety/tolerability**, healing trend; **incomplete at publication** [25].
- **Sosne G, et al. 2022**, *Int J Mol Sci* 24(1):554 — **Phase III RGN-259 (0.1% Tβ4) ophthalmic** solution for **neurotrophic keratopathy**, randomized/double-masked, **n=18** (10 vs 8): complete healing 60% vs 12.5% at 4 wk (primary p=0.066, narrowly missed; alt-analysis p=0.040); durable post-treatment benefit [26]. **The strongest human Tβ4 RCT — and it's an eye drug, tiny n, not skin cosmetics.**

**Honest bottom line on (B):** Tβ4 has **genuine human topical data — for chronic-wound and corneal repair, by a pharma developer, at drug-grade — but no cosmetic anti-aging RCT.** None of this belongs to acetyl-thymopentin (the cosmetic), and even Tβ4's own cosmetic case is unproven.

## 5. Topical Bioavailability / Penetration

- **Small (pentapeptide, ~660 Da) but charged and hydrophilic** (two basic Arg/Lys residues, an acidic Asp). Charged, water-loving peptides penetrate the stratum corneum poorly by passive diffusion — the same limitation that dogs copper peptides and most signal peptides.
- **Proteolytic fragility is the headline problem.** Even acetylated, a short RKDVY peptide is exposed to skin/serum peptidases; native TP5's **~30 s** plasma half-life is the cautionary baseline [5]. Acetylation helps but does not make it robust [7][8].
- **No published human skin-penetration (Franz-cell) study** for acetyl-thymopentin was located. The wound/eye Tβ4 successes (§4-B) all used **drug-grade formulations on compromised/absent barriers** (open ulcers, de-epithelialized cornea) — i.e. settings where the barrier is *gone*, which says little about intact-skin cosmetic delivery.
- **Implication:** absent encapsulation/penetration enhancement and stabilization, a plain aqueous acetyl-thymopentin serum likely delivers little intact peptide to living cells — and there is no efficacy study to show it matters either way.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:** **Unknown / not established.** No vendor publishes a substantiated cosmetic use level tied to data; cosmetic peptides typically run **ppm–low-% (≈0.0005–0.05% active)**, but for this molecule any number is **dosing-by-analogy, not by evidence.**
- **pH:** Best kept **mildly acidic-to-neutral (~5–6.5)**. Avoid strongly acidic or alkaline systems that accelerate peptide hydrolysis. (No molecule-specific stability dossier located.)
- **Stability:** **The main formulation risk.** Short peptide → susceptible to hydrolysis/oxidation; needs low water activity or anhydrous/encapsulated systems, chelation, and protection from heat/light. Acetylation is a partial mitigation only.
- **Sensory:** Water-phase, no color/odor issues reported; non-comedogenic (rated 0 on consumer databases) [15].
- **Pairings (sensible):** humectants (glycerin, HA, panthenol), **niacinamide**, beta-glucan, centella — i.e. it would slot into a "calming/soothing" stack.
- **Conflicts:** strong low-pH actives (L-ascorbic acid, AHA/BHA) and high-heat processing threaten peptide integrity — layer/sequence separately. No unique chemical incompatibility documented.

## 7. Safety

- **Overall:** No specific safety red flags identified for topical cosmetic use; consumer databases call it **generally safe** and **non-comedogenic (0)** [15][20]. Systemic thymopentin had a **good tolerability record** in AD trials (no drug-related AEs in Stiller 1994) [12].
- **Irritation/sensitization:** Low expected; as with any peptide/active, possible transient redness/itch — **patch test** advised [15]. No formal HRIPT/CIR cosmetic safety assessment for Acetyl sh-Pentapeptide-1 was located (**note this gap**: many newer "sh-" peptides predate a dedicated CIR review).
- **Immunomodulatory note (handle thoughtfully):** the *parent drug* deliberately modulates **T-cell immunity** by injection. Topical cosmetic exposure is vastly lower and there's no evidence of systemic immune effects from a cream — but the brand should **not** market an "immune-boosting" skincare angle (both unproven and claim-risky), and should be conservative for **immunocompromised or autoimmune** users absent data.
- **Sun sensitivity:** none established (still pair any routine with SPF — sun is the #1 driver of photoaging).
- **Pregnancy/nursing:** **Data lacking for topical use** — defer to a clinician; do not make pregnancy-safety claims [15].
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** synthetic peptide; **no animal/biological-provenance baggage** (unlike growth factors/exosomes). Specify supplier purity/heavy-metal/endotoxin testing.

## 8. Regulatory & Claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Regulatory status:** Marketed as a **cosmetic ingredient** under INCI **Acetyl sh-Pentapeptide-1**; appears in finished products [1][20]. **Crucial line:** **thymopentin is also a registered immunomodulator *drug* (Timunox)** [5][12]. Selling its *immune* activity in skincare would invite a **cosmetic-vs-drug** problem (structure/function & "treats disease" territory). Each finished formula still needs its own safety assessment (EU Reg. EC 1223/2009; US FDA cosmetic rules).
- **The cosmetic vs. drug line — this ingredient sits right on it.** Its only *real* human pedigree is **treating atopic dermatitis by injection** — exactly the kind of "treats a skin disease / modulates immunity" story that is **drug** language. Keep all of that out of copy.
- **✅ Safer cosmetic language (if used at all):** "a soothing peptide," "helps support the look of calm, comfortable skin," "skin-conditioning," "for the appearance of a stronger, more resilient-looking complexion."
- **⚠️ Risky / drug-like — needs substantiation or legal review:** "immunomodulating," "boosts immunity," "reduces inflammation," "inhibits MMP-9 / protects collagen," "prevents collagen breakdown," "treats sensitive skin / eczema / redness," any "thymosin-β4 / regenerative / wound-healing" framing (**wrong molecule** + drug claim), and **any percentage efficacy figure** (none exist for this molecule). The IL-8/MMP-9/collagen-IV claims are **unsubstantiated for this peptide** and should not be stated as fact.

## 9. Sourcing & Cost

- **Cost tier:** **Low–mid as a raw material.** Acetyl Pentapeptide-1 (CAS 97530-32-0) is offered by multiple (mostly Chinese) peptide manufacturers; quoted prices span a wide, quality-dependent range (bulk commodity to specialty-grade per-gram) [6][27]. At cosmetic ppm use levels the cost-in-formula is modest.
- **Supply:** Readily available, multi-supplier, synthetic — easy to source. Specify **purity, residual-solvent, heavy-metal, endotoxin** specs and a real CoA; "sh-" peptides from cheap suppliers vary in quality.
- **Provenance:** Fully synthetic; no ethical/biological-sourcing concerns.

## 10. Hype vs. Evidence

1. **"Thymosin-β4 fragment."** **Wrong molecule.** Acetyl sh-Pentapeptide-1 is **acetyl-thymopentin** (thymopoietin 32–36), not a Tβ4 fragment [1][3][4]. Any brand using the "thymosin-β4 / TB-500 / regenerative wound-healing peptide" halo on this INCI is borrowing a *different* peptide's story. **#1 overreach.**
2. **"Inhibits IL-8 and MMP-9 to protect collagen/elastin and collagen IV."** Biologically plausible, but **no primary study on this molecule in skin** was found — it's vendor copy repeated across databases [1][2][15], and the peer-reviewed sensitive-skin-peptide review omits it entirely [16]. Asserted, not evidenced.
3. **"Clinically studied / proven."** Thymopentin **is** clinically studied — **by subcutaneous injection, for atopic dermatitis (a disease), as an adjunct** [12][13][14]. That is **not** evidence for a topical anti-aging cosmetic, and it's drug-claim territory.
4. **"Immune-boosting / strengthens skin's defenses."** Drug-like, unproven topically, and risky.
5. **"Firming / anti-aging peptide."** No human topical anti-aging data of any tier for this molecule.

What's *genuinely* there: a **synthetic, well-tolerated, easy-to-source peptide** whose **parent drug has real (injected) immunopharmacology** and whose **namesake confusion (Tβ4) has real wound/eye human data** — none of which, honestly, supports a cosmetic anti-aging claim for *this* ingredient yet.

## 11. Verdict for the Brand

**⚠️/❌ SKIP as a hero; at most a CONDITIONAL minor "soothing" supporting actor — and only with disciplined, de-hyped claims.**

One-line reasoning: The **human topical cosmetic evidence for Acetyl sh-Pentapeptide-1 is effectively nil**, its marketed mechanism is **unsourced vendor copy**, and its main appeal rests on a **naming confusion with thymosin-β4** plus **injected anti-eczema drug data** — both of which are **off-limits** for honest, claims-compliant skincare marketing.

**Why not outright kill it:** it's synthetic, cheap, easy to source, non-comedogenic, apparently low-risk, and trend-relevant (it's riding the K-beauty "NAD+/PDRN peptide" wave). If the brand wants a **secondary soothing peptide** in a calming product and is willing to (a) **stabilize/encapsulate** it, (b) **never** call it "thymosin-β4," "immunomodulating," or quote IL-8/MMP-9 numbers, and (c) lean only on soft "soothing/skin-conditioning appearance" language — it could be a defensible *minor* ingredient. But there are **better-evidenced soothing/barrier actives** already in the research base (centella, beta-glucan, niacinamide, panthenol, copper peptides) that do this job with real data.

**Longevity-relevance note:** **Low.** The "skin-longevity" story here is **borrowed and unproven** — thymopoietin/thymic-peptide biology is about *systemic immune aging by injection*, not topical skin renewal, and the regenerative/Tβ4 longevity halo belongs to a **different molecule**. Treat this as a **claims-risk landmine** more than a longevity asset. Recommend logging in [`../../ideas/not-considering.md`](../../ideas/not-considering.md) (or considering.md only as a low-priority "soothing supporting peptide, claims-disciplined").

## 13. Evidence verification (adversarial)

*Independent adversarial pass, 2026-06-19. Method: (a) confirm the molecule's true identity vs the brief's title; (b) for each marketed benefit, check whether evidence is human + topical + cosmetic, or borrowed from another molecule/route/indication; (c) confirm cited studies are real and say what is claimed.*

### Overall evidence grade (for TOPICAL COSMETIC anti-aging use of THIS ingredient): **D / "insufficient."**

There is **no human topical cosmetic efficacy study** for Acetyl sh-Pentapeptide-1. Its real human pedigree (thymopentin, atopic dermatitis, **subcutaneous**) is the wrong route, wrong indication, and drug-claim territory. The wound/eye human data belong to a **different molecule** (Tβ4). This is **D**, not C (no direct human-topical-cosmetic signal exists) and not F (the molecule is real, characterized, well-tolerated, and its parent has legitimate pharmacology).

### What held up
- **Identity correction is solid.** Multiple independent sources confirm Ac-RKDVY = acetyl-thymopentin = thymopoietin 32–36, **distinct from thymosin-β4** [1][2][3][4][5].
- **Thymopentin AD RCT (Stiller 1994, n=39) is real and accurately summarized** — and it is **injected**, **adjunctive**, **disease** treatment [12].
- **Tβ4 human topical data are real but off-target** (wounds: Treadwell/Guarnera; eye: Sosne Phase III n=18) [9][23][25][26].
- **The peer-reviewed sensitive-skin peptide review omits this ingredient** — confirming the cosmetic IL-8/MMP-9 story is not in the primary literature [16].

### Main caveats (load-bearing)
1. **Wrong-molecule halo.** The "thymosin-β4 fragment" title is incorrect for this INCI; any efficacy borrowed from Tβ4 is invalid.
2. **Zero topical cosmetic evidence** for the actual ingredient; marketed mechanism is unsourced vendor copy.
3. **Real human data = injected drug for eczema** = drug claims, not cosmetic anti-aging.
4. **[23] not independently fetched** (summarized from [9][21][24]); flagged.

### Net guidance
The §11 verdict — **skip as hero; at most a claims-disciplined minor soothing supporting peptide; better-evidenced alternatives exist** — survives the audit. The brand's biggest risk with this ingredient is **marketing overreach**: calling it "thymosin-β4," "immunomodulating," "anti-inflammatory," or quoting MMP-9/IL-8 effects would be both **unsubstantiated** and **drug-like**. Grade **D** = "real, safe, cheap molecule; cosmetic anti-aging case unproven; high claims-risk."
