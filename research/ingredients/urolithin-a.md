# Ingredient: Urolithin A (INCI: Urolithin A) — trade name **Mitopure®**

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering (conditional — see verdict) · ⬜ shortlisted · ⬜ rejected · ⬜ in a product
**Class:** Autophagy & mitochondrial-targeted actives · **Slug:** `urolithin-a`

> **One-line honesty flag up front:** Urolithin A has *genuinely impressive* human oral evidence for muscle and immune aging, and a *plausible, mechanistically rich* topical story — **but the entire topical-skin human dataset is a single industry (Amazentis/Timeline) preprint that, as of this writing, has not cleared peer review.** Most "anti-aging skin" excitement is borrowed from the oral/systemic literature. Treat accordingly.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common name:** Urolithin A (UA, UroA, Uro-A).
- **INCI:** Urolithin A. Registered as a cosmetic ingredient in the EU (CosIng) and approved as a new cosmetic raw material in China (2023, anti-aging use). [2][12]
- **Trade name / proprietary form:** **Mitopure®** — a synthetically manufactured, high-purity Urolithin A from **Amazentis SA** (Switzerland; consumer brand **Timeline**). Mitopure is patent-protected and is the form behind essentially all the brand-led clinical work. [9][13]
- **Chemistry:** 3,8-dihydroxy-6H-dibenzo[b,d]pyran-6-one. CAS **1143-70-0**. A dibenzopyranone (a "urolithin"). [11]
- **Natural origin:** UA is a **postbiotic** — it is **not** found in food directly. The gut microbiome converts dietary **ellagitannins / ellagic acid** (pomegranate, walnuts, some berries) into urolithins. Commercial UA (incl. Mitopure) is **synthesized**, not extracted, to guarantee purity and dose. [3][8]
- **Key related forms / metabolites:** Urolithin B, isourolithin A (other microbial urolithins). In the body, oral UA circulates mostly as **UA-glucuronide** and **UA-sulfate** conjugates. [1][6] Some cosmetic suppliers sell bioavailability-enhanced delivery systems (e.g. self-emulsifying "ProLithin A"/BioSNEDS) — supplier claims, not independently verified. [12]

---

## 2. Class & mechanism

Urolithin A is the lead "mitophagy activator" in nutraceutical longevity science.

- **Primary mechanism — mitophagy.** UA is a potent inducer of **mitophagy**, the selective autophagic clearance of damaged mitochondria, principally via the **PINK1/Parkin** pathway (it raises phospho-Parkin in human skeletal muscle). Clearing dysfunctional mitochondria and stimulating biogenesis of fresh ones restores cellular energy capacity. [1][7][8]
- **Secondary mechanisms relevant to skin:**
  - **Antioxidant / NRF2 activation** — UA lowers intracellular reactive oxygen species (ROS) and activates the NRF2 antioxidant program in dermal fibroblasts. [4][5]
  - **SIRT3–FOXO3–PINK1–PARKIN axis** — invoked in UVA-photoaging fibroblast work to explain restored mitochondrial function. [5]
  - **Anti-inflammatory** — reduces pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g. CRP, IL-1β, TNF-α, IFN-γ) in oral human trials; relevant to "inflammaging." [7][10]
  - **ECM modulation (cell/biopsy level)** — upregulates type I (and III) collagen organization genes and **downregulates MMPs** (notably MMP-1) in senescent fibroblasts and in skin-biopsy transcriptomics. [4][14]

**Plain-language:** Skin cells (especially fibroblasts) run on mitochondria. With age and UV, mitochondria get damaged, ROS rises, collagen-making slows and collagen-breaking (MMPs) speeds up — a senescence spiral. UA's pitch is "cellular housekeeping": clear the broken mitochondria, calm oxidative stress, and tip the collagen balance back toward build.

## 4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

> **Read this section before believing any marketing.** I have separated what is proven *for the format you would actually sell* (topical) from what is borrowed from oral/systemic use.

### Tier 1 — In-vitro (cell studies) — *consistent and supportive*

- **Liu et al. (2019), *Rejuvenation Research* 22(3):191-200.** Replicative-senescent human skin fibroblasts. UA **increased type I collagen**, **reduced MMP-1**, lowered ROS, and activated NRF2 antioxidant response. Conclusion: "promising antiaging agent." Cell model only; no concentration-to-skin translation. **Caveat the authors themselves flag:** at the higher tested concentration (50 μM) UA altered cell morphology, inhibited proliferation and induced G2/M cell-cycle arrest — i.e. the in-vitro effect is concentration-dependent and not uniformly benign. DOI: 10.1089/rej.2018.2066 [14]
- **Liu et al. (2022), *Journal of Photochemistry and Photobiology B: Biology* 232:112462.** UA protected human dermal fibroblasts from **UVA-induced photoaging** — attenuated senescence (SA-β-gal), ECM breakdown, SASP and cell-cycle arrest; restored mitochondrial function via **SIRT3-FOXO3-PINK1-PARKIN** and NRF2/mitophagy. DOI: 10.1016/j.jphotobiol.2022.112462 (PMID 35567884) [5]
- **Brand/preprint in-vitro work** (D'Amico/Fouassier et al., bundled with the topical trials): acute exposure of cultured human skin cells **downregulated collagen-degradation genes (MMPs)** and **upregulated collagen-organization genes**, with mitophagy/autophagy induction. [4][3]

**Verdict on Tier 1:** Mechanistically coherent and reproducible across independent groups. But in-vitro fibroblast data is the *weakest* tier for predicting real cosmetic performance — concentrations and exposure in a dish do not mirror a cream on intact stratum corneum.

### Tier 2 — Animal — *exists for systemic disease, not skin*

UA has extensive rodent data for muscle, metabolic, and neuro endpoints (the basis of the oral program) and the safety NOAEL studies (§7). I found **no peer-reviewed animal model specifically for topical skin aging** — the skin program jumped from cell culture to human topical. (Treat any "proven in animals for skin" claim as **[unverified]**.)

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL — *the strongest, most credible UA evidence (but it is NOT skin)*

This is where UA earns its longevity reputation. **None of it is topical or primarily a skin endpoint** — include it for credibility/context, not as skin proof.

- **Andreux et al. (2019), *Nature Metabolism* 1:595-603.** First-in-human RCT, healthy sedentary elderly, n=60. Single doses (250-2,000 mg) + 4-week dosing (250/500/1,000 mg). UA **safe and bioavailable** at all doses; Tmax ~6 h; half-life 17-22 h (parent) / 25-58 h (conjugates). Modulated plasma acylcarnitines and skeletal-muscle mitochondrial gene expression — "molecular signature of improved mitochondrial health." DOI: 10.1038/s42255-019-0073-4 [6]
- **Singh et al. (2022), *Cell Reports Medicine* 3(5):100633 (ATLAS trial).** Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled; middle-aged adults; placebo n=29, 500 mg n=29, 1,000 mg n=30; 4 months. **Primary endpoint (peak power output) NOT met.** Secondary: leg muscle strength improved (~+10-12%, e.g. hamstring peak torque +12% at 500 mg, p=0.027; knee-flexion torque +10.6% at 500 mg, p=0.017) while placebo declined; 6-min walk +33 m at 1,000 mg (clinically meaningful but p=0.098 vs placebo); peak VO₂ +10% (p=0.058 vs placebo); CRP reduced; muscle phospho-Parkin and OXPHOS proteins up. Safe/well-tolerated. **Funded by Amazentis; multiple author COIs.** DOI: 10.1016/j.xcrm.2022.100633 [7]
- **Denk et al. (2025), *Nature Aging* (MitoImmune).** RCT, n=50 healthy adults 45-70, 1,000 mg/day vs placebo × 4 weeks. UA expanded naive-like, less-exhausted **CD8+ T cells** (treatment diff 0.50 pp; 95% CI 0.16-0.83; P=0.0437), raised CD8+ fatty-acid-oxidation capacity (P=0.0061), increased NK cells and mitochondrial biogenesis in immune cells; improved TNF secretion and monocyte bacterial uptake. Well-tolerated; no liver/kidney changes. Evidence for UA against **immune aging / inflammaging.** Industry-linked. DOI: 10.1038/s43587-025-00996-x [10]
- **Systematic review — muscle (2025 preprint, medRxiv 2025.07.10.25331277).** Searched all DBs to May 2025; only **3 eligible RCTs, 174 participants**, doses 500-1,000 mg/day. Low risk of bias and good tolerability, **but** muscle-*mass* showed no improvement, and the body of evidence is small and homogeneous (largely the same sponsor's trials). Honest caveat to the "muscle" hype. [15]
- **Scoping review (Lee et al., 2023, PMC10460156):** across age-related disease, only 3 of 15 included studies were RCTs (all sarcopenia); concludes evidence base is "limited by scarcity of human trials." [8]

**Verdict on Tier 3:** For *oral systemic* use, UA is one of the better-substantiated longevity nutraceuticals — repeated independent-format RCTs, real biomarkers, good safety. But (a) primary endpoints have sometimes failed, (b) the literature is heavily Amazentis-funded, and (c) **this tells us little about a cream.**

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL — *the only tier that matters for a skincare SKU, and it is the thinnest*

**Everything published on topical UA in humans is one bundled report:**

- **D'Amico, Fouassier et al. (2023), medRxiv preprint 10.1101/2023.06.16.23291378** — "Topical application of Urolithin A slows intrinsic skin aging and protects from UVB-mediated photodamage." Three trials:
  1. **Aging study 1 (n=48):** post-menopausal women, >Grade 3 wrinkles, split-face/arm design, 0.5% / 1% UA.
  2. **Aging study 2 (n=108):** middle-aged men and women, 1% UA in day cream / night cream / serum vs untreated site, 8 weeks. Reported: visible wrinkle improvement from ~2 weeks; significant reduction in **wrinkle volume and depth** by 8 weeks; hydration improvement; skin-biopsy transcriptomics showed **upregulated collagen-organization/assembly genes**.
  3. **Photodamage trial (n=22):** placebo-controlled UVB-erythema model; 1% UA applied after UVB **significantly reduced erythema** vs untreated control.
  - **Critical caveats:** (i) **Preprint — not peer-reviewed** as of 2026-06; I found no journal publication. (ii) **Amazentis/Timeline-affiliated authors** — the manufacturer ran the only topical trials on its own product. (iii) Designs lean on **untreated-site comparison** rather than full vehicle-controlled blinding in the larger aging study, which weakens causal attribution vs a true placebo cream. (iv) Small photodamage arm (n=22). [1][3][4]
- **Brand-cited tolerability:** a 6-week Human Repeat Insult Patch Test (HRIPT) reportedly showed no irritation/sensitization — sponsor-reported, not independently published. [16]

**Verdict on Tier 4:** Promising *directionally* (the wrinkle, hydration, erythema and biopsy signals all point the same way and align with the cell mechanism), but it is **one sponsor's unrefereed dataset.** Honest grade for *topical human* efficacy: **emerging / preliminary**, not "clinically proven" in the independent-evidence sense.

**Explicit borrowed-vs-proven map:**
- *Proven for topical human use (preprint-grade):* visible wrinkle appearance, hydration, post-UVB erythema reduction, biopsy collagen-gene upregulation.
- *Borrowed from oral/systemic or cells (do NOT claim for a cream):* "boosts muscle/energy," "immune renewal," "mitophagy in your body," systemic anti-inflammatory effects, longevity claims.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- UA is a **small, relatively lipophilic** dibenzopyranone — physicochemically plausible for stratum-corneum penetration, and the biopsy-level gene changes in the topical trial imply it (or its effect) reaches the dermis/epidermis. [3]
- **However, no independent skin-penetration/permeation (e.g. Franz cell) study was located.** Penetration depth, delivered dose to fibroblasts, and the fate of any metabolites in skin are **[unverified]** outside sponsor data.
- Oral UA's poor parent-compound systemic profile (heavy first-pass glucuronidation/sulfation) is **not directly relevant** to topical, but it underscores that "the gut makes it" ≠ "skin gets it from food." Topical delivery is the only reliable route to skin.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration (topical):** trials used **0.5% and 1%**; 1% is the headline. [3][4] Commercial Mitopure skincare pairs UA with **niacinamide and trehalose** "at efficacious levels" (exact UA % undisclosed). [9][17]
- **pH:** Phenolic/lactone (dibenzopyranone) — generally formulated in a mild acidic-to-neutral range typical of serums/creams. No published optimal-pH window located; **confirm stability window with supplier** before locking a base. **[unverified — get supplier TDS]**
- **Stability:** As a polyphenol-class molecule it is **light/oxidation-sensitive** in principle; opaque/airless packaging advisable. Synthetic high-purity grade (vs plant extract) reduces batch variability. Concrete stability data are supplier-held. **[partly unverified]**
- **Pairings (rational, evidence-aligned):**
  - **Niacinamide** — barrier + tone; used in the commercial pairing. [17]
  - **Trehalose** — humectant/cell-protectant; in the commercial pairing. [17]
  - **Ceramides / HA** — fit the brand's barrier-repair thesis; no conflict expected.
  - **Antioxidants (vit C/E)** — mechanistically complementary (NRF2/ROS).
- **Conflicts:** No documented hard incompatibilities, but **strong acids (low-pH exfoliants), high-strength retinoids, and benzoyl peroxide** could theoretically stress a polyphenol or compound irritation — sequence/format separately until stability is confirmed. **[unverified]**
- **Sensory:** UA is a pale/off-white powder; at 0.5-1% it should be sensory-neutral in a well-built emulsion. Possible faint color contribution at higher loads — verify.

## 7. Safety

- **Oral toxicology (Heilman et al., 2017, *Food and Chemical Toxicology* 108:289-297):** 28-day and 90-day rat dietary studies; **not genotoxic** across a standard battery; **NOAEL = highest dose tested (~3,451 mg/kg/day males; 3,826 mg/kg/day females)**; no target-organ toxicity. [18]
- **Regulatory safety status:** Self-affirmed/FDA-acknowledged **GRAS** as a food/supplement ingredient (US); used at up to 1,000 mg/day orally in trials with good tolerability. [3][6][7][10] (Note: GRAS pertains to *ingestion*, not a cosmetic-safety determination.)
- **Topical irritation/sensitization:** Sponsor-reported HRIPT clean (no irritation/sensitization) over 6 weeks; trials reported good tolerability. **Independent topical-safety data are limited** — recommend in-house RIPT/patch and stability-linked irritation testing. [16]
- **Sun sensitivity:** No evidence UA causes photosensitization; mechanistically it is **photo-protective** (post-UVB erythema reduction). Not a sunscreen — does not replace SPF.
- **Pregnancy/lactation:** **No specific topical-safety data in pregnancy.** Default to cautious cosmetic language; advise consult-your-doctor for the oral form. **[unverified]**
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** Synthetic manufacture (not pomegranate extraction) → consistent purity, no agricultural supply-chain volatility. Sustainability is essentially a manufacturing-process question; no major flags identified.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Cosmetic vs drug line:** UA in a topical product is a **cosmetic** ingredient (INCI-listed; CosIng/China-registered). Anything implying it **treats/prevents disease or alters skin structure/function** (e.g. "boosts collagen," "repairs mitochondria," "reverses aging," "induces mitophagy in your skin") risks crossing into **drug** territory (US FDA) and would need substantiation/legal review.
- **Substantiation problem specific to UA:** Because the only topical human data is a **single non-peer-reviewed, sponsor-run preprint**, efficacy claims rest on weaker ground than for, say, retinol or niacinamide. Borrowing *oral* results ("clinically proven to improve strength/immunity") to sell a **cream** would be misleading and a clear claims-discipline violation.
- **Safer cosmetic phrasing (✅):** "helps visibly improve the appearance of fine lines and wrinkles," "supports skin hydration," "helps defend against the visible effects of UV-induced stress" (with SPF context), "a postbiotic active studied for cellular/mitochondrial health."
- **Avoid / flag for legal (⚠️):** "boosts collagen by X%," "activates mitophagy," "repairs / reverses aging," "clinically proven anti-aging," "renews cells," any cross-format ("muscle/immune") benefit on a skincare label.
- See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md) claim cheat-sheet.

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Form to buy:** Either branded **Mitopure®** (Amazentis — premium, story + clinical asset, likely licensing/MOQ terms) or **generic high-purity synthetic UA** from specialty suppliers (e.g. Selleck-grade research material; cosmetic-grade from suppliers like Mitora/Natural Poland's ProLithin). [9][11][12]
- **Cost tier:** **High.** UA is a synthesized specialty molecule; oral supplement pricing is premium (Mitopure ~$2-4/day historically). Branded skincare sits at luxury tier — Timeline serum/cream **~$250-280** (50 ml cream / 30 ml serum). [13] Raw-material $/kg not publicly listed; expect a **premium active cost** that fits a high-end barrier-repair cream but would strain a mass price point.
- **Strategic note:** Using branded **Mitopure** buys a clinical-asset + recognizable-ingredient halo but cedes pricing power and ties the brand to a competitor (Timeline) that already "owns" topical UA. Generic UA is cheaper and unbranded but then *you* must substantiate efficacy with even thinner public data.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

- **Hype:** "Mitochondrial breakthrough," "the new omega-3," "renews cells," "anti-aging proven by clinical trials." Much of this **conflates the strong oral muscle/immune RCTs with the much weaker topical-skin dataset.**
- **Reality check #1:** The flagship muscle RCT (Singh 2022) **missed its primary endpoint**; benefits were secondary-outcome and partly within-group. [7]
- **Reality check #2:** A 2025 systematic review found **only 3 muscle RCTs**, no muscle-mass benefit, and a sponsor-concentrated literature. [15]
- **Reality check #3:** The **topical** evidence is **one unrefereed preprint from the ingredient's owner**, partly using untreated-site (not full vehicle-blinded) comparisons. [1][3]
- **Reality check #4:** The "naturally from pomegranate" framing is misleading — **30-60% of people are non-converters/low-converters** (metabotype-0/low UM-A) who make little UA from food; commercial UA is synthetic, and that variability is a *marketing point* for the supplement, not the cream. [19]
- **What's genuinely strong:** Mechanism is real and well-characterized (mitophagy/NRF2); safety profile is excellent; the *direction* of skin data is consistent across cells → biopsy → visible endpoints.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**⚠️ CONDITIONAL — supporting/"science-story" active, not (yet) a sole hero.**

One line: *Mechanistically gorgeous and on-thesis for skin-longevity, with a clean safety profile and a strong systemic-aging research halo — but topical human proof is one non-peer-reviewed sponsor preprint, so it can't carry hard efficacy claims the way retinol/niacinamide can.*

- **Best role:** a **supporting "longevity-science" active** layered into the barrier-repair cream alongside proven workhorses (ceramides, niacinamide, retinoid, SPF in routine), giving the brand a credible, differentiated, mitochondria/postbiotic narrative.
- **Promote to hero only if** (a) the topical RCTs reach **independent peer review**, or (b) the brand runs/commissions its **own vehicle-controlled topical study**, and (c) claims stay in safe cosmetic language.
- **Skip if** the brand wants a low price point (cost-prohibitive) or refuses to be associated with Timeline's existing topical-UA ownership and won't differentiate.
- **Longevity relevance:** **Very high in concept** — UA targets *mitochondrial decline, senescence, and inflammaging*, three core hallmarks of aging, making it one of the most "longevity-authentic" actives available. That conceptual fit is precisely why claims discipline matters: the story is strong enough to oversell.

Log under [`../../ideas/considering.md`](../../ideas/considering.md) as a conditional/supporting active pending peer-review or own-data.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

_Verification pass: 2026-06-19. Method: each headline study cited in §4 was independently checked for (a) existence, (b) whether the cited result matches the source, (c) whether the evidence is HUMAN + TOPICAL vs. borrowed from in-vitro/animal/oral data, and (d) funding/size/independence. Sources re-confirmed via journal/PubMed/medRxiv records._

### Overall evidence grade for TOPICAL skin efficacy: **C (weak / indirect / emerging)**

Rationale: The single tier that matters for a cream — human topical — rests entirely on **one non-peer-reviewed, manufacturer-run preprint** ([1], D'Amico/Fouassier 2023). That is better than nothing (it is a real, multi-arm human study with biopsy transcriptomics, not pure hand-waving), which keeps it out of D ("essentially unproven / hype"). But it is one sponsor's unrefereed dataset using partly untreated-site (not full vehicle-blinded) comparison, so it cannot support A or B. Everything stronger in the file (Andreux 2019, Singh 2022, Denk 2025) is **oral/systemic**, not skin, and the in-vitro fibroblast work (Liu 2019, Liu 2022) is the weakest tier for predicting cosmetic performance. **For oral/systemic UA the grade would be B (moderate human evidence); for topical skin it is C.**

The good news: the dossier as written is **fundamentally honest**. It already separates "proven for topical" from "borrowed from oral/cells," already flags the sponsor/preprint problem, and already refuses to let oral RCTs sell a cream. The adversarial pass did **not** find oral or in-vitro data being passed off as topical proof. The defects are in citation accuracy and a couple of omitted caveats, not in the core argument.

### What verified out cleanly (claims are real and accurately described)

### Citation errors found and CORRECTED in this file (2026-06-19)

These were genuine bibliographic defects — the *findings* were right but the *coordinates were wrong*, which is exactly the kind of error that erodes a research dossier's credibility when a reader tries to pull the paper:

1. **Liu 2022 [5]** was cited as *J Dermatol Sci 106(2):109-118, DOI 10.1016/j.jdermsci.2022.04.001*. The paper is actually in **J Photochem Photobiol B 2022;232:112462, DOI 10.1016/j.jphotobiol.2022.112462 (PMID 35567884)**. The originally cited journal/volume/DOI do not point to this paper. **Fixed.**
2. **Liu 2019 [14]** was cited as *Rejuvenation Research 22(1):29-36*. Correct is **22(3):191-200** (DOI unchanged and valid). **Fixed.**

### Omitted caveat added (2026-06-19)

- **Liu 2019 concentration-dependent toxicity:** the authors themselves report that at the higher tested dose (50 μM) UA inhibited proliferation and induced **G2/M cell-cycle arrest** with morphology change. The dossier presented only the benign findings. This is now noted in §4 Tier 1. It is a cell-culture artifact (not a topical-use safety signal), but for an "adversarial" record it belongs on the page.

### Main caveats (carry into any marketing/claims work)

1. **Topical efficacy is single-source and conflicted.** One preprint, one sponsor (Amazentis/Timeline), not peer-reviewed as of 2026-06, partial untreated-site comparison. Do not describe topical UA as "clinically proven."
2. **The strong evidence is the wrong format.** The credible, repeated, independent-ish RCTs (strength, immunity, mitochondrial signature) are all **oral**. They establish the *molecule's* biology, not a *cream's* performance. Borrowing them to sell skincare is the central trap.
3. **Sponsor concentration across the whole UA literature.** Even the oral evidence is heavily Amazentis-funded; the 2025 muscle systematic review [15] found only 3 RCTs / 174 participants and no muscle-*mass* benefit. Independence is thin throughout.
4. **No independent skin-penetration data.** Whether and how much UA reaches dermal fibroblasts topically is sponsor-implied (biopsy gene changes), not confirmed by an independent permeation study. The in-vitro mechanism does not prove in-vivo skin delivery.
5. **Brand-only claims have no published support** — pigmentation/dark-spot/dark-circle benefits (§3) are Timeline marketing, not data. Keep flagged as "brand claim only."

### Claims that should be SOFTENED or kept boxed

- ❌ "Clinically proven" / "proven anti-aging" for topical UA → ✅ "studied in early (preprint-stage) human topical trials." (The §8/§10 language already does this; hold the line.)
- ❌ Any import of oral strength/immune/longevity results onto a skincare label → keep strictly in the §4 "borrowed (do NOT claim for a cream)" box.
- ❌ "Boosts/increases collagen," "activates mitophagy in your skin," "reduces wrinkles" (efficacy, not appearance) → ✅ "helps improve the *appearance* of fine lines," "supports skin hydration." (Matches §8 guidance.)
- ❌ "Penetrates to the dermis to repair fibroblasts" → unsupported by independent penetration data; avoid mechanistic-delivery claims.
- ⚠️ In-vitro fibroblast results (collagen↑, MMP↓, senescence↓) must never be stated as consumer outcomes — they are dish data, and at least one (Liu 2019) is concentration-dependent and partly anti-proliferative.

### Bottom line

Its real weaknesses were **two wrong citations and one omitted in-vitro caveat**, all now fixed. Topical-efficacy grade: **C**.
