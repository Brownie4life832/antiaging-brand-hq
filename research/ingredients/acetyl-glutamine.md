# Ingredient: Acetyl Glutamine (INCI: Acetyl Glutamine)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Class:** Anti-glycation & related (assigned) — see §2 for an honest reclassification · **Status:** ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product

> Brutally honest one-liner: acetyl glutamine is a **stable, cheap, very-well-tolerated amino-acid-derivative skin-conditioner** with a *plausible barrier/hydration story* and **one** supportive in-vitro paper showing anti-senescence + differentiation signals — but its real-world evidence is **a single non-human cell-culture study, no human topical trial at all, and a penetration question mark**, and the **"anti-glycation" class label it was filed under is not supported by any mechanism or data I could find**. Worse, the acetylated form was historically used in the literature as a *negative control* precisely because it is a **poor glutamine precursor**. A modest **supporting / texturant-with-a-story** ingredient, not a hero, and not an anti-glycation active.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common name:** Acetyl glutamine (N-acetyl-L-glutamine; Nα-acetyl-L-glutamine; "NAG" in the one skin paper — *not* to be confused with N-Acetyl **Glucosamine**, also abbreviated NAG, a different sugar-amine ingredient).
- **INCI:** **Acetyl Glutamine** (CosIng function listed: *skin conditioning*) [9].
- **Chemistry:** Acetylated derivative of the amino acid **L-glutamine** — the α-amino group is capped with an acetyl group, leaving the side-chain amide intact.
  - **Molecular formula:** C₇H₁₂N₂O₄ · **MW ≈ 188.2 Da** · **CAS 2490-97-3** [10].
  - SMILES `CC(=O)N[C@@H](CCC(=O)N)C(=O)O`; white crystalline powder, water-soluble, mp ~206–208 °C [10].
- **Why acetylated?** Free L-glutamine is **chemically unstable in aqueous cosmetic formulas** — it cyclizes/degrades (to pyroglutamate + ammonia) over time and at heat. Acetylating the amino terminus is a standard trick to make the molecule **shelf-stable** so it survives formulation and storage [3][6]. That is the genuine, honest reason it exists as a cosmetic raw material: **stability, not superior bioactivity.**
- **Key distinctions (read carefully — the names collide):**
  - **L-Glutamine** — the parent amino acid; most abundant amino acid in the body; the form with the *real* skin/fibroblast biology (see §4). Unstable in formula.
  - **N-Acetyl Glucosamine** — an *aminosugar* (INCI: Acetyl Glucosamine), used for pigmentation/HA/hydration; **different molecule, often also abbreviated "NAG."** Do not let its (better) evidence leak onto acetyl glutamine.
  - **N-Decanoyl-L-Glutaminyl-L-Glutamine amide / Glutaminyl-glutamine peptides** — *glutamine-containing peptides* promoted for barrier/anti-senescence; **different actives**, not acetyl glutamine. Marketing sometimes blurs these.
  - **N-Acetyl-Glutamic acid (acetyl glutamic acid)** — the acid analog (acetylated glutamate), covered in the same CIR safety report family (§7); related but distinct.

## 2. Class & mechanism

**Honest reclassification first.** This dossier was commissioned under the class **"Anti-glycation & related."** I found **no mechanistic basis or any study** showing acetyl glutamine has anti-glycation / carbonyl-scavenging / AGE-inhibiting activity. The structural reasoning is straightforward and damning for that label: the **reactive nucleophile that gives carnosine, aminoguanidine, or free amino acids their anti-glycation activity is the free α-amino group — and in acetyl glutamine that group is *capped by the acetyl*.** What remains (a side-chain amide and a carboxyl) is not a meaningful carbonyl trap. So **acetyl glutamine should not be marketed or filed as an anti-glycation active.** Its real lane is **barrier/hydration skin-conditioning + (weak, in-vitro) anti-senescence/differentiation signaling.**

Proposed / plausible mechanisms (note how much is *theoretical* or *inherited from free glutamine*):

1. **Skin-conditioning / humectancy (its actual cosmetic function).** As a small, hydrophilic amino-acid derivative it behaves like an NMF-adjacent humectant/conditioner — it helps the surface "feel" smooth, supple, hydrated [3][9]. This is the function CosIng actually assigns it.
2. **"Stable glutamine reservoir" (the marketing mechanism — weak).** The pitch is that acetyl glutamine delivers glutamine's benefits in stable form, with the acetyl removed in-skin by **acylases (aminoacylase I)** to release free glutamine. **The biochemistry undercuts this:** N-acetyl-L-glutamine is a **comparatively poor acylase substrate** (hydrolyzed at roughly **~20% the rate of N-acetyl-glutamate** in deacetylation work) [11], and in fibroblast culture **acetyl-glutamine released essentially no free glutamine into the medium and had no effect on DNA synthesis** — it was literally used as a **negative control** for exactly this reason [4]. So the "prodrug-of-glutamine" story is **shaky**: the acetyl may not come off efficiently in skin.
3. **Anti-senescence / differentiation signaling (the one supportive paper).** In Jang 2018 [1], acetyl glutamine itself (not via released glutamine) reduced oxidative-stress-induced senescence markers and *increased* keratinocyte differentiation markers — implying some **direct** activity of the intact molecule, independent of being a glutamine prodrug. Intriguing but single-source and in-vitro only (§4).
4. **Glutamine-borrowed biology (inherited, NOT demonstrated for the acetyl form in skin).** Free glutamine is a fuel/precursor for proliferating skin cells, supports collagen synthesis via the **glutamine→glutamate→P5C→proline** route, and is depleted under metabolic stress/wound states [2][5][7]. **This is glutamine's biology, not acetyl glutamine's** — and given point 2, it cannot be assumed to transfer.

Net framing: **a stable cosmetic skin-conditioner with a thin, single-study anti-senescence/barrier signal and an over-sold "glutamine prodrug" narrative that the biochemistry partially contradicts.** Not an anti-glycation agent.

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

| Hallmark | Mechanistic rationale | Strength of *skin* evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Barrier decline** | ↑ keratin 1 + involucrin (differentiation) in keratinocytes; humectant conditioning | **Weakest-"moderate"**: one in-vitro study + mechanism; **no human data** |
| **Hydration / water-loss** | Small hydrophilic NMF-adjacent conditioner | Plausible/mechanistic; **no controlled human TEWL/hydration data for acetyl glutamine specifically** |
| **Cellular senescence** | ↓ SA-β-gal+ cells & p53/p21 under H₂O₂ stress (in vitro) | In-vitro only (single study) [1] |
| **Oxidative stress** | Reduced H₂O₂-induced senescent phenotype (indirect) | In-vitro only, indirect [1] |
| **Collagen / ECM loss** | ↓ MMP1, ↑ COL1A1 mRNA in stressed fibroblasts (in vitro); glutamine→proline route (borrowed) | In-vitro only [1]; the collagen-via-proline data is for **free glutamine**, not the acetyl form [2] |
| **Inflammaging** | Glutamine modulates IL-6/TNFα/CRP **systemically** (oral/IV) | Not skin/topical; borrowed from nutrition data [8] |
| **Glycation / AGEs** | **No mechanism, no data** — amino group is acetyl-blocked | **None** — class label is unsupported (§2) |
| Photoaging | Not studied | None |
| Pigmentation / tone | Not studied (do not borrow from Acetyl *Glucosamine*) | None |
| Mitochondrial decline | Glutamine is anaplerotic systemically; not shown for skin/topical | None (skin) |
| Microbiome | No data | None |

**Acetyl glutamine has no distinctive, evidence-backed lane.** Its best (still weak) story is **barrier + cellular-stress resilience**, all from a single in-vitro paper — not glycation.

## 4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

> **Read this first:** The evidence is shallow in an unusual way. There is **essentially no deep mechanistic pyramid** for *acetyl glutamine* — most of the impressive biology belongs to **free L-glutamine**, and the **acetyl form was historically a negative control**. For acetyl glutamine itself there is **one** supportive in-vitro paper, **zero** animal studies on skin, **zero** human oral studies (oral data is all free/dipeptide glutamine for non-skin endpoints), and **zero** human topical trials. No RCT of any kind exists for this molecule.

### Tier 1 — In-vitro / mechanism

**For acetyl glutamine specifically (one paper):**
- **Jang HH (2018), *Asian Journal of Beauty and Cosmetology* 16(4):579–589, DOI 10.20402/ajbc.2018.0257** [1] — the **single dedicated skin study** on acetyl glutamine. **In-vitro only**, two cell lines: HaCaT keratinocytes and human dermal fibroblasts (HDFs). Acetyl glutamine ("NAG") and glutamine tested at **0.002–200 mM**; **20 mM** identified as the effective concentration; oxidative stress induced with H₂O₂ (100 µM HaCaT / 200 µM HDF). Findings:
  - **No cytotoxicity up to 200 mM** (WST-1).
  - **Senescence:** at 20 mM, acetyl glutamine reduced H₂O₂-induced SA-β-gal-positive (senescent) cells **more than glutamine** (~28% vs ~36% senescent, i.e., acetyl glutamine left fewer senescent cells), with reductions in **p53/p21**.
  - **Barrier/differentiation:** ↑ **keratin 1** and **involucrin** "remarkably more than" glutamine — interpreted as better support for keratinocyte differentiation / barrier formation.
  - **Fibroblast/ECM:** ↓ **MMP1** mRNA (≈4.3-fold → 2.3-fold under stress) and ↑ **COL1A1** mRNA in stressed HDFs.
  - **Limitations (severe):** in-vitro monoculture; H₂O₂-induced (artificial) senescence model; **20 mM is a high culture concentration** with no demonstrated relationship to what a topical product delivers to living skin; mRNA/marker endpoints, not function; **n = 3 replicates**; single author/lab; the paper itself **does not state limitations**; published in a niche cosmetology journal (limited reach/scrutiny). It is genuinely the most relevant evidence — and it is one small bench study.

**For free L-glutamine (mechanistically informative, but NOT the acetyl form):**
- **Karna E, Miltyk W, Wołczyński S, Pałka JA (2001), *Comp Biochem Physiol B* 130(1):23–32, PMID 11470441** [2] — in cultured human skin fibroblasts, **glutamine stimulated collagen biosynthesis ~230%** (glutamate ~400%, P5C ~3-fold), acting through the **glutamate→P5C→proline** pathway (blocked by DHEA inhibiting G6PD). **Relevance caveat:** this is **free glutamine**, not acetyl glutamine — and the literature says the acetyl form does **not** behave like free glutamine in fibroblasts (next bullet).
- **Coudray-Lucas C, Lasnier E, Renaud F, Ziegler F, Settembre P, Cynober LA, Ekindjian OG (1999), *Clinical Nutrition* 18(1):29–33, "Is α-ketoisocaproyl-glutamine a suitable glutamine precursor to sustain fibroblast growth?", PMID 10459079** [4] — measured ³H-thymidine incorporation (DNA synthesis) in human fibroblasts across glutamine sources. **L-alanyl-L-glutamine promoted DNA synthesis; N-acetyl-L-glutamine had NO effect and was used as the *negative control* "since it is known to be a poor glutamine precursor."** Acylated glutamine was "poorly available" and **did not appear as free glutamine in the medium.** This is the single most important caveat in the whole dossier: **in cell culture, acetyl glutamine did not deliver glutamine.**
- **Deacetylation chemistry:** N-acetyl-L-glutamine is a **comparatively poor acylase-I substrate**, hydrolyzed at ~**20%** the rate of N-acetyl-glutamate in deacetylation studies [11] — corroborating why the acetyl may not efficiently release glutamine in tissue.

**Verdict on Tier 1:** One supportive in-vitro paper on acetyl glutamine itself (anti-senescence + differentiation), genuinely interesting but unreplicated and bench-only; the deeper, more famous glutamine biology is for the **free** amino acid and is **partly contradicted for the acetyl form** by negative-control data.

### Tier 2 — Animal

**None found for acetyl glutamine on skin.** Glutamine appears in rodent wound-healing/regeneration work (e.g., hypoxia-driven keratinocyte glutamine metabolism enhancing skin/hair-follicle regeneration) [5], but that is **free glutamine, systemic/metabolic context, not a topical acetyl-glutamine analog.** No transferable claim.

### Tier 3 — Human, ORAL

**No human oral study of acetyl glutamine for skin exists.** What exists is the **systemic glutamine nutrition** literature:
- **Arribas-López E et al. (2021), *Nutrients* 13(8):2498 — systematic review & meta-analysis of amino acids (arginine & glutamine) on wound healing; 39 human studies** [8]. Glutamine (mostly **IV/enteral alanyl-glutamine dipeptide, 0.2–0.57 g/kg/day**) improved nitrogen balance, lowered IL-6/TNFα/CRP, reduced mortality and length of stay in surgical/critically-ill/burn patients. **Relevance to a cosmetic:** essentially none — this is **clinical nutrition in sick patients, parenteral/enteral, free/dipeptide glutamine**, not topical acetyl glutamine, and the endpoints are systemic, not skin appearance. Cited only to (a) show glutamine *is* biologically active in healing contexts and (b) to be explicit that **none of it substantiates a topical acetyl-glutamine cosmetic claim.**

**Verdict on Tier 3:** Glutamine has real systemic biology; **acetyl glutamine has no oral skin evidence**, and the systemic data is the wrong molecule, wrong route, wrong population for a cosmetic.

### Tier 4 — Human, TOPICAL (the tier that matters — and it is empty)

**No human topical study of acetyl glutamine of any design was found — no RCT, no open-label, no ex-vivo explant, nothing.** Every consumer-facing benefit ("strengthens the barrier," "increases hydration," "extends skin's healthspan," "improves the look of dryness/tightness") is therefore **extrapolated from**: (a) the single in-vitro paper [1], (b) general amino-acid/humectant reasoning, and/or (c) free-glutamine biology that may not transfer to the acetyl form.

**What is actually proven for human TOPICAL acetyl glutamine use:** **Nothing, directly.** There is no in-vivo human data at all.

**What is reasonable to *infer* (low confidence):** as a small, safe, hydrophilic amino-acid conditioner it likely contributes mild **surface conditioning / humectancy** in a finished formula — the kind of thing many amino acids do — but this is generic and not a differentiated, substantiated claim.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **Size is favorable** (~188 Da — well under the 500-Da rule of thumb) [12], so in principle it *can* cross the stratum corneum better than larger actives.
- **But it is small, polar, and ionizable** (carboxyl + amide), which limits lipid-phase partitioning and passive diffusion; hydrophilic amino-acid derivatives generally partition poorly into the lipid-rich barrier and tend to **act at/near the surface** (consistent with a humectant/conditioner role) rather than reaching the dermis.
- **The "released glutamine in-skin" assumption is the weak link.** The active narrative depends on cutaneous acylases cleaving the acetyl to free glutamine *in viable epidermis/dermis* — but (a) penetration of intact acetyl glutamine to viable layers is **unmeasured for skin**, and (b) the deacetylation is **inefficient/poor** in the systems where it has been tested [4][11]. So even the small molecule that does penetrate may not convert.
- **No published skin-penetration or skin-PK study for acetyl glutamine** was found. Any "delivers glutamine to skin" claim is **unsubstantiated**.

**Implication:** treat acetyl glutamine as a **surface-acting conditioner** unless a supplier provides real penetration + conversion data. Do not build a "feeds the dermis with glutamine" story on current evidence.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:** Suppliers/marketers recommend **~1–3%** in finished products [3]; the only efficacy data (in-vitro) used **20 mM (~0.38% w/v in medium)** [1] — note the 20 mM bench condition and a 1–3% leave-on are **not** equivalent exposures and shouldn't be conflated. CIR-surveyed use was ≤1% in the products it reviewed [7].
- **pH:** **Broadly stable across the typical cosmetic pH range**; compatible with common systems. No narrow pH window required [3].
- **Stability:** **Good** — this is the molecule's main selling point. The acetyl cap makes it far more stable than free glutamine in water/heat; it does **not** interfere with common preservatives or antioxidants [3].
- **Pairings (sensible):**
  - **Humectants / NMF actives** — glycerin, betaine, amino-acid blends, panthenol, HA: reinforce the hydration/barrier-feel story.
  - **Barrier lipids** — ceramides, cholesterol, fatty acids: pairs logically with the differentiation/barrier signal from [1] (mechanistically coherent, not jointly proven).
  - **Niacinamide** — well-evidenced barrier/tone partner; lets niacinamide carry the substantiated barrier claim while acetyl glutamine plays support.
  - **Other amino-acid-derivative conditioners / mild actives** — formulation-neutral, easy to combine.
- **Conflicts:** None notable documented. Broad pH/preservative compatibility makes it formulation-friendly.
- **Sensory:** Water-soluble, no color/odor issues at use levels, no tack; effectively a **neutral, easy-to-include** conditioner.

## 7. Safety

- **Overall: very well tolerated; low-risk.** Endogenous-adjacent (acetylated form of a body amino acid).
- **CIR review:** Covered in the CIR **Safety Assessment of Amino Acid Alkyl Amides** family (Burnett et al., *Int J Toxicol* 2017;36(Suppl 1):17S–56S) — the panel concluded such ingredients are **"safe in the present practices of use and concentration … when formulated to be non-irritating."** In-vitro genotoxicity was **negative** for the acetyl-amino-acid members tested; the surveyed products used acetyl glutamine at **≤1%** [7]. (Note: the Paula's Choice "2017 CIR, up to 1%, 8 products" summary [3] refers to this body of work; the precise per-ingredient survey numbers should be cited from the CIR document itself if used in copy.)
- **Irritation/sensitization:** Rare, mild, transient reports only; low sensitization potential expected for a small endogenous-amino-acid derivative [3][7]. Standard patch-test advice for sensitive skin.
- **Sun sensitivity:** No photosensitization signal.
- **Pregnancy/breastfeeding:** No specific red flags; an acetylated common amino acid. As always, defer to "consult your physician" in pregnancy copy rather than asserting safety.
- **Sourcing:** Produced **synthetically / by fermentation-derived glutamine then acetylation** — no animal extraction needed for cosmetic grade; **vegan-compatible** when synthetically/fermentation-sourced (confirm with supplier). No notable sustainability or provenance concerns.

## 8. Regulatory & claims

- **Cosmetic, not drug** — Acetyl Glutamine is a listed cosmetic ingredient (INCI: Acetyl Glutamine; CosIng function *skin conditioning*) [9]. Topical use is squarely cosmetic **provided claims stay appearance/feel-based.**
- ✅ **Safer cosmetic language (defensible):** "amino-acid skin conditioner," "helps skin **feel** smooth, supple and hydrated," "helps **support the look** of a healthy moisture barrier," "helps reduce the **appearance** of dryness/tightness," "a stable form of the skin-loving amino acid glutamine."
- ⚠️ **Risky / unsubstantiated — flag for legal/substantiation:**
  - **Any "anti-glycation," "fights AGEs," "anti-sugar-aging"** claim — **no mechanism, no data; do not use** (the molecule's reactive amine is acetyl-blocked). This is the single biggest claims trap given the assigned class.
  - "**Boosts collagen**," "reduces MMP," "reverses senescence," "extends skin's healthspan/longevity," "rejuvenates cells" — these ride on a **single in-vitro paper** [1] and/or **free-glutamine** biology; not substantiated for topical acetyl glutamine in humans.
  - "**Delivers/feeds glutamine to skin**," "stable glutamine that converts in skin" — **unproven**; contradicted by negative-control/deacetylation data [4][11].
  - "**Clinically proven**" anything — there is **no clinical study** of this ingredient.
  - "%-improvement" figures from [1] (e.g., senescence/MMP numbers) — **in-vitro, single study**; not consumer claims.
- See claims guardrail in [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md) and [`../../CLAUDE.md`](../../CLAUDE.md).

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Cost tier:** **Low.** A commodity amino-acid derivative available from many cosmetic/biochem suppliers (Sigma-Aldrich, Santa Cruz, Glentham, Biosynth, FabriChem, etc.) [10]; far cheaper than peptides, growth factors, or proprietary actives. No single dominant branded version surfaced — it is **generic**, which means **no marketing dossier or trademarked clinical package to lean on** (cuts both ways: cheap and unencumbered, but no supplier-supplied substantiation).
- **Supply:** Stable and broadly available (synthetic/fermentation). No scarcity or provenance concerns. Confirm cosmetic-grade purity and vegan status with supplier.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

| Marketing claim seen in the wild | Reality |
|---|---|
| "Anti-glycation / fights sugar-aging" (implied by the assigned class) | **No mechanism, no data.** The reactive amino group is acetyl-capped; this is **not** an anti-glycation active. The label is wrong — do not use. |
| "Stable form of glutamine that delivers glutamine's benefits to skin" | Acetylation gives **stability** (true) but the acetyl form was a **negative control** for glutamine delivery in fibroblasts and is **poorly deacetylated** [4][11]. The "prodrug" benefit is **unproven and partly contradicted**. |
| "Strengthens the barrier / boosts hydration" | **Plausible** (humectant + in-vitro differentiation signal) but rests on **one in-vitro paper + no human data**. "Helps the look/feel" is defensible; "strengthens the barrier" as fact is not. |
| "Extends skin's healthspan / inhibits senescence" | From a **single in-vitro study** at 20 mM in cell culture [1]. Interesting; not a substantiated topical human benefit; "healthspan/longevity" is drug-adjacent over-reach. |
| "Boosts collagen / reduces MMP" | In-vitro mRNA only [1]; the famous collagen biology is for **free glutamine** [2], a different molecule. |
| "Clinically proven anti-aging amino acid" | **No clinical study of this ingredient exists.** |
| Borrowing N-Acetyl **Glucosamine** data (HA, pigment, anti-wrinkle) | Different molecule that shares the "NAG" abbreviation. **Do not conflate.** |

**Bottom line:** acetyl glutamine is **over-positioned relative to its evidence**, especially under an anti-glycation banner it doesn't earn — but it is **not snake oil**: it is a safe, stable, cheap conditioner with a single intriguing in-vitro anti-senescence/barrier signal. The honest pitch is "**stable amino-acid skin-conditioner that helps skin feel hydrated and supple**," nothing more.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ **SUPPORTING / CONDITIONAL — and explicitly NOT an anti-glycation hero.** Acetyl glutamine is worth keeping as a **low-cost, ultra-safe, formulation-friendly amino-acid skin-conditioner** that can quietly support a **barrier/hydration** formula and add an "amino-acid-rich, glutamine-derived" narrative texture — **but only** (a) with **feel/appearance-based copy** ("helps skin feel smooth, supple, hydrated; supports the look of a healthy barrier"), (b) letting **better-evidenced actives carry the substantiated claims** (niacinamide, ceramides, glycerin/HA), and (c) **never** under an anti-glycation, "delivers glutamine to skin," collagen-boost, senescence-reversal, or "healthspan" banner.

- **One-line reasoning:** Excellent safety + stability + low cost + a single supportive in-vitro paper, undercut by **zero human data, no penetration/conversion proof, a poor-precursor/negative-control history, and an unsupported anti-glycation class label** — so it earns a quiet supporting role, not a headline and not an anti-glycation story.
- **Longevity-relevance note:** The *conceptual* hooks (cellular senescence, differentiation, the glutamine→proline/collagen axis, glutamine depletion under stress) are genuinely longevity-adjacent and make acetyl glutamine usable as **soft narrative scaffolding** within an amino-acid / barrier sub-theme — *provided* the in-vitro/free-glutamine science is kept clearly separated from what's proven for the topical acetyl form (currently nothing in humans). If the brand wants a real **anti-glycation** active, look to carbonyl scavengers with actual data (carnosine/carcinine, the B-vitamin anti-glycation set, branded anti-glycation actives) — **not this molecule.**

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Verified:** 2026-06-19. Independent adversarial fact-check of every headline claim and citation (cross-checked against PubMed / publisher pages / supplier data / CosIng).

### Overall evidence grade: **D+** (for the brand-relevant question), with the anti-glycation framing graded **F**

Grading is **for the brand-relevant question: does *topical* acetyl glutamine do something useful on *living human* skin?** On that question the evidence is **D+** — there is **no human data of any kind**, one supportive in-vitro paper, and a documented "poor glutamine precursor / negative control" problem. If the question were narrowed to "**is it an anti-glycation active?**" the grade is **F** — no mechanism, no data, and the structure argues against it. If the question were "**is it safe and stable to formulate?**" the grade is **A** (genuinely safe, stable, cheap).

- **A (strong human-topical RCT):** none. No RCT of any design exists for this ingredient.
- **B (moderate human-topical):** none. No open-label or ex-vivo human-skin study found.
- **C (weak/indirect human-topical):** none.
- **D (in-vitro / borrowed / unproven):** **this is where acetyl glutamine sits** — one in-vitro cell study [1] + free-glutamine biology that may not transfer + systemic nutrition data.
- **F (claim with mechanism *against* it):** the **anti-glycation** class label — unsupported and structurally implausible.

No hallucinated or claim-inflated citations were found; one citation metadata error corrected (see [4]).

### Main caveats (the real risk surface)

1. **No human evidence at all.** Not a single in-vivo human study (oral or topical) exists for acetyl glutamine. Every consumer benefit is extrapolation.
2. **The "stable glutamine prodrug" story is partly self-defeating.** Acetylation buys stability but the same cap makes the molecule a **poor glutamine donor** in cells ([4]) and a poor deacetylation substrate ([11]) — so it may stabilize glutamine *into uselessness* as a glutamine source. Its bioactivity, if any, is more likely **as the intact molecule** (per [1]) than as a glutamine prodrug.
3. **Single-source efficacy.** The entire positive skin signal is **one in-vitro paper from one lab** in a niche journal, with n=3, artificial H₂O₂ senescence, and no penetration/in-vivo follow-up.
4. **The assigned "Anti-glycation" class is wrong.** No mechanism or data; structurally the reactive amine is blocked. Marketing it as anti-glycation would be an unsubstantiated (and easily debunked) claim.
5. **Name-collision risk.** "NAG" and "N-acetyl…" make it easy to accidentally import **N-Acetyl Glucosamine's** (stronger) evidence. Keep them strictly separate in copy and research.
6. **Penetration/conversion unmeasured.** Small (188 Da) but hydrophilic; likely surface-acting; no skin-PK data; "delivers glutamine to the dermis" is unsupported.

### Net

Grade **D+** for topical usefulness, **F** for the anti-glycation framing, **A** for safety/formulability. Acetyl glutamine is a **safe, cheap, stable supporting conditioner with a thin single-study barrier/anti-senescence signal** — usable as quiet narrative scaffolding with feel/appearance claims, but **not** a hero, **not** an anti-glycation active, and **not** a substantiated "glutamine delivery" system. The dossier already states this; this verification confirms it.
