# Ingredient: Edelweiss extract (+ stem cell) (INCI: Leontopodium Alpinum Extract / Leontopodium Alpinum Flower-Leaf Extract / Leontopodium Alpinum Callus Culture Extract)

**Slug:** edelweiss-extract · **Class:** Botanicals & plant bioactives
**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ⬜ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product
**Verdict (short):** ⚠️ **Conditional / supporting** — a well-tolerated, story-rich antioxidant botanical with a genuinely interesting active (leontopodic acid) and one real (small) topical RCT, but the headline anti-aging claims lean heavily on supplier in-vitro data. The single human topical trial is small and **manufacturer-funded** (see §4 / §Evidence verification). Not a hero on evidence; strong as a supporting/marketing-led actor.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common name:** Edelweiss ("noble white"); the alpine flower, *Leontopodium alpinum* Cass. Taxonomically now often *Leontopodium nivale* subsp. *alpinum*. Asteraceae (daisy/sunflower family).
- **Key INCI forms seen on labels:**
  - **Leontopodium Alpinum Extract** — generic solvent/glycerin extract of plant material.
  - **Leontopodium Alpinum Flower/Leaf Extract** — from cultivated aerial parts (e.g., dsm-firmenich ALPAFLOR® EDELWEISS CB, an organically cultivated 'Helvetia' variety in glycerin).
  - **Leontopodium Alpinum Callus Culture Extract (LACCE)** — the "(+ stem cell)" / "plant stem cell" form. Made from de-differentiated plant cells (callus) grown in bioreactors rather than harvested flowers. Most of the recent skin literature uses **this** form. "Plant stem cell" is marketing shorthand for plant cell culture; it does not behave like a human stem cell and does not "transfer" stemness to skin.
  - **Leontopodium Alpinum Meristem Cell Culture** — another cell-culture variant.
- **Signature actives / markers:**
  - **Leontopodic acid A and B** — highly substituted glucaric (hexaric) acid derivatives bearing caffeic/dihydroxyphenyl-propenoate groups; the dominant, characteristic antioxidants (Schwaiger et al. 2005). Often the marker compound used to standardize extracts.
  - **Chlorogenic acid, 3,5-dicaffeoylquinic acid, di-caffeoyl glucaric acids** — other phenylpropanoids.
  - **Flavonoids** (luteolin, apigenin-7-glucoside derivatives), **bisabolane sesquiterpenes**, **sitosterol**, tannins.
  - **Leoligin / 5-methoxy-leoligin** — furanoid lignans concentrated in the **roots/hairy roots**. Pharmacologically interesting but studied for **cardiovascular** ends, not topical skin (see §4). Typically NOT present at meaningful levels in flower/leaf or callus cosmetic extracts.

## 2. Class & mechanism

Edelweiss is a **polyphenol-rich antioxidant botanical**. Proposed skin mechanisms (mostly cell-level):

1. **Direct free-radical scavenging.** Leontopodic acid is a potent radical scavenger in chemical assays — ~4× more active than resorcinol (Briggs–Rauscher) and ~2× more potent than Trolox (TEAC) (Schwaiger et al. 2005). Whole callus extracts show strong DPPH scavenging (122.19 ± 7.28 mg ascorbic-acid-equivalent/g; total polyphenols ~231 mg GAE/g) (Pralea et al. 2021).
2. **Indirect antioxidant / cytoprotection.** Induces protective nitric oxide and phase-II / antioxidant enzymes (GST, GPx) against oxidative and genotoxic insults in cell models (Costa et al. 2009; Lulli et al. 2012).
3. **MMP modulation under stress.** Down-regulates MMP-2 (UVB) and MMP-1 (blue light) in cultured skin cells, the enzymes that degrade collagen — i.e., it may blunt stress-driven collagen breakdown rather than directly "building" collagen (Cho et al. 2020; Meng et al. 2023).
4. **Anti-inflammatory signaling.** Suppresses chemokines (IL-8, IP-10, MCP-1, GM-CSF) in keratinocytes and adhesion molecules (VCAM-1) in endothelial cells (Lulli et al. 2012).
5. **Barrier / keratinization & hydration genes.** Up-regulates keratin genes (KRT5/15/19), cornification/barrier genes, and the water channel **aquaporin-3 (AQP3)** in keratinocytes (Cho et al. 2020).
6. **Anti-glycation (supplier data).** Activates glyoxalase-1 and reduces AGE formation / collagen-IV glycation in vitro (dsm-firmenich; Paul & Imfeld 2021).

## 4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

> **Bottom line up front:** Edelweiss has a **broad in-vitro base**, **no relevant human ORAL skin data**, and exactly **one small split-face human TOPICAL trial** with positive but modest results. Most marketing-grade claims (collagen boost, anti-glycation, "3× better than placebo") trace to **cell studies and supplier white papers**, not independent human RCTs.

### Tier 1 — In-vitro (the bulk of the evidence)

1. **Schwaiger et al. 2005, *Tetrahedron* 61:4621–4630.** Isolated and structurally elucidated **leontopodic acid** from edelweiss aerial parts. Antioxidant potency: ~4× resorcinol (Briggs–Rauscher), ~2× Trolox (TEAC); protected DNA from oxidative damage (3D assay). *Foundational chemistry; no skin model.*
2. **Costa et al. 2009, *J. Appl. Toxicol.* 29:7–14 (PMID 18636399; DOI 10.1002/jat.1372).** Leontopodic acid was chemoprotective against deoxynivalenol (U937) cytotoxicity, partly via phase-II/antioxidant (GST/GPx) enzyme induction; notably it did **not** protect HepG2 cells against aflatoxin-B1 (mixed/negative result — the original dossier overstated this as protective against both). *Liver/immune cell lines — not skin; relevant only as a partial mechanism.*
3. **Lulli (Daniela) et al. 2012, *Mediators of Inflammation* 2012:498373 (PMC3474292).** Concentrated ethanol edelweiss **callus** extract "ECC55" (≈55% leontopodic acids), 10–50 µg/mL, on primary human keratinocytes + HUVECs. Dose-dependently inhibited IL-8 (IC50 ~9 µg/mL), GM-CSF (IC50 ~23 µg/mL), IP-10, MCP-1 under TNF-α/IFN-γ; reduced VCAM-1 (LPS, oxLDL) and IL-6; reversed trichostatin-A senescence via sirtuins; boosted protective NO vs UV. *Strong, mechanism-rich; cell-only. Authors note VCAM-1 mechanism "deserves further investigation."*
4. **Cho et al. 2020, *Genes (Basel)* 11(3):230 (PMC7074254).** LACCE 0.1–1% on HaCaT keratinocytes + Detroit 551 fibroblasts. DPPH scavenging rose 2.85%→20.47% (0.1→1%); under H₂O₂, viability 45.5%→60.4%; 1% ≈ N-acetylcysteine. Down-regulated MMP-2 under UVB; up-regulated KRT5/15/19, DDIT4, BNIP3, IGFBP3, and AQP3 (3.19→4.5). *In-vitro arm of the combined study below.*
5. **Pralea et al. 2021, *Plants (Basel)* 11(1):100 (PMC8747157; PMID 35009103).** UPLC/IM-HRMS profiling: 27 constituents; leontopodic acid A & B dominant (A ≈178.6 µg/mg, B ≈219.9 µg/mg dry extract; top 4 = 57.1% of extract). DPPH 122.19 ± 7.28 mg AAE/g; polyphenols 230.93 ± 6.6 mg GAE/g. *Focused on antioxidant + anticancer cell screening; NO collagenase/elastase/tyrosinase assays reported.*
6. **Meng et al. 2023, *Molecules* 28(5):2172 (PMC10004670; companion 28(21):7319, PMID 37959738).** LACCE 5–15 mg/mL on human foreskin fibroblasts + blue light (18 J/cm²). Reversed collagen loss and MMP-1 rise; reduced ROS and Ca²⁺ influx (p<0.01); proposed OPN3–Ca²⁺ pathway suppression. *Cell-only; authors flag skin is "much more complex," need human trials.*
7. **dsm-firmenich anti-glycation studies (Paul & Imfeld, *SOFW Journal* 09-2021).** ALPAFLOR® EDELWEISS CB in vitro: AGE accumulation ↓ up to 55%, glyoxalase-1 activity ↑65%, collagen-IV glycation ↓67%, plus collagen I & V ↑. **Supplier-sponsored; in-vitro only; no participant data.** Treat as marketing-grade, not independent.

### Tier 2 — Animal

- No published topical-skin animal efficacy studies located for edelweiss/LACCE.
- **Leoligin (root lignan), oral, rodent CV models** — adjacent but **not skin**: inhibits intimal hyperplasia in mouse vein-graft disease (Reisinger et al. 2009, *Cardiovasc. Res.* 82:542); inhibits HMG-CoA reductase and lowers cholesterol in ApoE⁻/⁻ mice (oral); promotes cholesterol efflux from THP-1 macrophages (Wang/Ladurner et al. 2016, *J. Nat. Prod.*, PMC4924082). **Listed only to show the leoligin "longevity/cardio" buzz is a different molecule, route, and organ — do NOT borrow it for skin claims.**

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL

- **None for skin.** No oral edelweiss supplement trials with dermatologic endpoints were found. Folk-medicine oral use (digestive/fever) is traditional, not evidentiary.

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL

- **RCT (small):** **Cho et al. 2020** clinical arm — double-blind, **split-face, placebo-controlled**, **n=21 women** (mean 48 y), 4 weeks, LACCE cream vs placebo. Results vs placebo at 4 wk: periorbital wrinkle Ra ↓6.12% (placebo 2.49%), Rq ↓6.19% (2.68%); elasticity R2 ↑3.30% (1.62%); dermal density ↑5.47% (3.12%); skin thickness ↑10.19% (4.83%). **Limitations: tiny n, 4 weeks, women only, single study, no independent replication, modest absolute effects, finished-formula (other actives in base may contribute).**
  - **⚠️ Conflict of interest (verified 2026-06-19):** This trial is **not independent**. It was funded by **BIO-FD&C Co., Ltd.** (the company that manufactures the LACCE tested) plus a Korean government commercialization grant (PJ014029), and **5 of 11 authors are employed by BIO-FD&C's "Anti-Aging Research Institute."** Despite this, the paper states *"The authors declare no conflict of interest."* — an undisclosed/under-disclosed COI. The only piece of human topical evidence for edelweiss therefore comes from the ingredient's own maker. Treat the magnitude of effects with extra skepticism; do not present as independent proof.
- **Supplier "3× better than placebo" wrinkle claim** (ALPAFLOR EDELWEISS CB) — dsm-firmenich marketing; underlying study details/sample sizes not independently published/verified. **[unverified as independent RCT]**

**Explicitly: which benefits are proven for TOPICAL HUMAN use?**
- *Plausibly supported (one small, manufacturer-funded RCT):* visible periorbital fine-line appearance, elasticity, dermal density/thickness — modest, single-study, not independently replicated, funded by the ingredient maker (see COI note above).
- *In-vitro only (NOT proven on human skin topically):* antioxidant capacity, anti-inflammatory cytokine suppression, blue-light protection, anti-glycation/AGE reduction, collagen I/V "boost," AQP3/hydration, barrier-gene upregulation.
- *Borrowed from a different format/organ (do not claim for skin):* leoligin's cardiovascular/cholesterol effects.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- No published human penetration/skin-pharmacokinetic data for leontopodic acid or edelweiss extracts were found. **[unverified]**
- Leontopodic acid is a large, highly hydroxylated, polar polyglucarate ester — chemically predisposed to **poor passive penetration** of the stratum corneum; likely acts mainly at the surface/upper epidermis unless delivery-enhanced.
- In-vitro studies dose cultured cells directly, **bypassing the barrier**, so their concentrations don't translate to in-use skin levels. This is a major caveat for all the cell data above.

## 6. Formulation

- **Typical use level:** Commercial extracts (e.g., glycerite ALPAFLOR EDELWEISS CB; LACCE) are generally used around **1–5%** of the finished product; the Cho RCT cream and many cell studies bracket **0.1–1%** of standardized extract. There is no established "clinical efficacy %" — extracts vary wildly in actives.
- **pH:** Polyphenol/caffeoyl esters are most stable in mildly acidic conditions (~pH 4–6); avoid high-pH systems that hydrolyze esters and oxidize phenolics.
- **Stability:** Polyphenols oxidize/discolor with light, heat, air, and metal ions. Favor **antioxidant-protected, opaque/airless packaging**; add chelators (e.g., low-level EDTA/phytate). Glycerite forms are water-compatible.
- **Pairings (complementary):** humectants (glycerin, HA), niacinamide, panthenol, ceramides/barrier lipids, other antioxidants (vitamin C/E — synergy plausible). Fits a **barrier-repair / soothing antioxidant** layer — relevant to the brand's barrier-cream thesis.
- **Conflicts/cautions:** strong oxidants, very high/low pH, high heat in manufacture; possible interaction/discoloration with high iron/copper. Botanical may add color/odor at higher loads.
- **Sensory:** glycerite versions are pleasant, water-light; generally easy in creams/serums.

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation/sensitization:** Widely regarded as **well-tolerated, non-irritating, non-sensitizing** at cosmetic levels (~1–5%); soothing/anti-inflammatory in models. Transcriptome work showed LACCE did **not** trigger stress-response genes, supporting a low-stress profile (Cho 2020).
- **Allergy note:** *Leontopodium* is in **Asteraceae/Compositae** (ragweed, chamomile, arnica family). Compositae-sensitive individuals could in principle react; not commonly reported, but worth a patch-test note.
- **Sun sensitivity:** No evidence of photosensitization; it is **not** an SPF and does not replace sunscreen.
- **Pregnancy/breastfeeding:** No specific topical safety data. As with most botanicals, no strong red flags topically, but **no affirmative safety data** either → soft caution / "consult provider" stance. **[unverified]**
- **Regulatory safety bodies:** No dedicated **CIR** monograph or EU **SCCS** opinion located specifically for *Leontopodium alpinum* extracts as of this review; widespread cosmetic use without notable adverse signal. **[unverified — confirm before relying on a formal safety assessment]**
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** Wild edelweiss is **protected** across much of the Alps; ethical supply uses **cultivation or cell/callus culture**, not wildcrafting. Callus culture and cultivated organic supply (COSMOS/NATRUE/Fair-for-Life options) are strong sustainability + storytelling assets.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Cosmetic vs. drug line:** Edelweiss extract is a **cosmetic** ingredient. Mechanism words like **"boosts collagen," "reverses glycation," "repairs," "anti-aging"** drift toward drug/structure-function territory (esp. US FDA) and the supplier-grade efficacy numbers (55% AGE↓, 3× wrinkle) are **in-vitro / unverified-RCT** — risky to repeat verbatim.
- ✅ **Safer copy:** "antioxidant-rich alpine botanical," "helps defend against environmental/oxidative stress," "helps soothe," "supports the look of a resilient skin barrier," "visibly smooths the appearance of fine lines" (only if our finished formula is tested), "hydrating."
- ⚠️ **Flag for substantiation/legal:** "boosts collagen X%," "reduces AGEs by 55%," "3× better than placebo," "anti-glycation," "reverses aging," "anti-pollution/blue-light protection" as a hard claim. If used, anchor to **our own finished-product testing**, not borrowed supplier data.
- Penetration/efficacy claims must not imply systemic or biological alteration. Keep to **appearance/sensory** language.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

- **"Plant stem cell" framing** — biggest overreach. Callus/meristem cultures are plant cells; they do **not** confer stem-cell properties to human skin. Honest framing: a *sustainably cell-cultured antioxidant botanical*.
- **"Boosts collagen / reverses glycation / 3× better than placebo"** — these are **supplier in-vitro figures or unverified marketing trials**, not independent human RCTs. Compelling, but overstated as proof of in-use efficacy.
- **Leoligin / cardiovascular-longevity halo** — real pharmacology, but it's the **root lignan, taken orally, for blood vessels** — irrelevant to a topical cream and not present in flower/leaf-callus extracts at relevant levels. Don't let "edelweiss = longevity molecule" bleed into skin copy.
- **"Survives extreme alpine UV, so it protects your skin"** — appealing narrative; the plant's own UV adaptations don't equal an SPF or a proven human photoprotectant. In-vitro UVB/blue-light data are mechanistic, not clinical.
- **What's genuinely real:** potent antioxidant chemistry (leontopodic acid), solid in-vitro anti-inflammatory/antioxidant signal, excellent tolerability and a best-in-class sustainability/story angle, and one small positive topical RCT.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ **Conditional — supporting actor, not a hero.**
One-liner: **A beautifully on-brand, well-tolerated antioxidant botanical with a real signature active and a great Alpine/sustainability story, but its anti-aging proof is mostly in-vitro + one small RCT — use it as a supporting/soothing antioxidant and marketing asset, not as the load-bearing clinical claim.**

- **Use as:** supporting antioxidant/soothing layer in a barrier-repair cream or serum; a premium, sustainability-forward storytelling ingredient (cultivated/cell-cultured, protected alpine flower).
- **Don't use as:** the brand's evidence-backed "hero" anti-ager — the human topical evidence is too thin to carry that weight versus retinoids, vitamin C, niacinamide, peptides, ceramides.
- **Longevity relevance:** Mechanistically touches several skin-aging hallmarks (oxidative stress, inflammaging, glycation, MMP-driven collagen loss) — conceptually aligned with a skin-longevity thesis, but the **topical human evidence lags the mechanistic promise**. Treat hallmark links as plausible, not proven, in copy.
- **If adopted:** standardize to leontopodic-acid content, prefer cultivated/cell-cultured + certified-sustainable grades, formulate ~1–5% in acidic, light/air-protected systems, and **substantiate any % claims with our own finished-product testing** before publishing.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

_Independent adversarial review performed 2026-06-19. Method: each headline efficacy claim was checked against the primary sources via PubMed/PMC/publisher pages for (a) human-vs-topical vs borrowed in-vitro/animal/oral data, (b) whether the cited study is real and says what is claimed, and (c) funding/sample-size/independence._

### Overall evidence grade: **C (weak / indirect for topical human anti-aging)**

Rationale: The mechanistic and antioxidant chemistry is genuinely strong, but it is almost entirely **in-vitro**. The entire human topical case rests on **one small (n=21), 4-week, single-site, women-only split-face trial that is funded by the ingredient's own manufacturer (BIO-FD&C) with an under-disclosed conflict of interest.** There is no independent replication and no animal-skin or human-oral skin data. That is below a "B" (which would require moderate, ideally independent, human-topical evidence) but above a "D" (the antioxidant chemistry and the existence of *a* positive — if conflicted — RCT mean it is not "essentially unproven/hype"). The "stem cell," "boosts collagen," "reverses glycation 55%," and "3× better than placebo" claims, taken on their own, would individually grade **D**.

### What the verification confirmed (the dossier was accurate on these)
- **All primary citations are real and correctly attributed.** Schwaiger 2005 (*Tetrahedron* 61:4621–4630), Costa 2009 (PMID 18636399), Lulli 2012 (PMC3474292), Cho 2020 (PMC7074254), Pralea 2021 (PMC8747157), Meng 2023 (PMC10004670 + companion PMID 37959738), Reisinger 2009, Wang/Ladurner 2016 (PMC4924082) all check out.
- **Key numbers verified:** Schwaiger's ~4× resorcinol / ~2× Trolox antioxidant figures; Pralea's DPPH 122.19 ± 7.28 mg AAE/g and polyphenols 230.93 ± 6.6 mg GAE/g, leontopodic acids A+B dominant (top 4 = ~57% of extract); Cho clinical deltas (wrinkle Ra ↓6.12% vs 2.49%, thickness ↑10.19% vs 4.83%, n=21, mean ~48 y, double-blind split-face 4 wk).
- **The human/in-vitro split is honestly drawn.** The dossier correctly isolates the one topical RCT, labels everything else in-vitro, and correctly fences off the leoligin cardiovascular/oral data as a different molecule/route/organ. No oral or animal skin data is smuggled in as human topical. The dsm-firmenich anti-glycation figures (55% AGE↓, 67% collagen-IV glycation↓, 3× wrinkle) are already flagged as supplier-sponsored, in-vitro, and unverified — appropriately.

### Main caveats (new or sharpened by this review)
1. **The only human topical study is manufacturer-funded with an under-disclosed COI.** Cho 2020 was funded by **BIO-FD&C Co., Ltd.** (maker of the tested LACCE) and a Korean commercialization grant; **≥5 of 11 authors are employed at BIO-FD&C's Anti-Aging Research Institute**, yet the paper declares "no conflict of interest." The single load-bearing human result is thus an in-house manufacturer trial, not independent evidence. The original dossier flagged size/duration/single-study but **not** the funding/COI — this is now corrected in §4 and §12.
2. **Costa 2009 was overstated.** The dossier said leontopodic acid was chemoprotective against both aflatoxin-B1 (HepG2) and deoxynivalenol (U937). The paper actually reports protection of U937 from DON but **no** protection of HepG2 from AFB1 — a mixed result. Corrected in Tier-1.
3. **Even the in-vitro base is not conflict-free.** Meng 2023 has authors from ingredient/biotech suppliers (Acelbio (Chongqing) Biotechnology; Beijing Lan Divine Technology), and Lulli 2012 includes authors tied to plant-cell-culture ingredient development (IRB / Pressi & Dal Toso). These are still in-vitro and were already labeled supporting/mechanistic, but the independence caveat now applies across most of the evidence base, not just the supplier white papers.
4. **No penetration data + cell dosing bypasses the barrier.** Confirmed: no human skin-PK study exists, and leontopodic acid is a large, highly polar polyglucarate ester unlikely to penetrate well. So even the strong in-vitro antioxidant/anti-inflammatory signals may not translate to in-use skin levels.
5. **No collagenase/elastase/tyrosinase assays in the profiling paper.** Verified directly: Pralea 2021 ran antioxidant + anticancer-cell screens only — it provides no enzyme-inhibition basis for "anti-wrinkle"/"firming"/"brightening" mechanisms.

### Claims that should be softened (or only used with our own finished-product testing)
- ❌ **"Clinically proven to reduce wrinkles / increase dermal density & thickness"** → the proof is one small **manufacturer-funded** RCT. Soften to "in a small manufacturer-sponsored study, a cream containing the extract was associated with modest improvements in the *appearance* of fine lines" — or, better, substantiate on our own formula before claiming at all.
- ❌ **"Boosts collagen I & V" / "increases collagen"** → in-vitro/supplier only; structure-function/drug-adjacent. Use only appearance language.
- ❌ **"Reduces AGEs / glycation by 55%," "anti-glycation"** → dsm-firmenich in-vitro, no participants. Do not repeat the percentage.
- ❌ **"3× better than placebo"** → unverified supplier marketing trial; details not independently published. Drop.
- ❌ **"Plant stem cell" implying regenerative/stemness benefit to skin** → biologically incorrect; reframe as "sustainably cell-cultured botanical."
- ❌ **"Blue-light / pollution protection," "photoprotective," "survives alpine UV so it protects you"** → in-vitro mechanistic only; not a clinical or SPF claim. Soften to "helps defend against the look of environmental/oxidative stress."
- ✅ **Defensible as written:** "antioxidant-rich alpine botanical," "well-tolerated/soothing," "helps support a resilient-looking barrier," "hydrating," and (only if our finished formula is tested) "visibly smooths the look of fine lines." Sustainability/sourcing story (cultivated/cell-cultured, protected flower) is genuine and safe.

### Bottom line
The dossier is **fundamentally sound and unusually honest** for a botanical — its own verdict (supporting actor, not a hero; mostly in-vitro) survives adversarial scrutiny. The one material gap was that it treated the lone human RCT as merely "small/single" when it is in fact **manufacturer-funded with an under-disclosed COI**, which pushes the realistic topical-human grade to **C**. With that corrected, the dossier's strategic conclusion (use as a supporting/soothing antioxidant and sustainability/story asset, never as the load-bearing clinical claim, and substantiate any % claims on our own finished product) is the right one.
