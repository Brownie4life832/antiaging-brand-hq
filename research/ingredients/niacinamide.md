# Ingredient: Niacinamide (Vitamin B3) (INCI: Niacinamide)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 ¬∑ **Status:** ‚úÖ shortlisted (strong SUPPORTING / near-hero)
**Class:** Antioxidant (also: barrier/lipid modulator, NAD‚Āļ precursor, depigmenting agent)
**Evidence verdict at a glance:** One of the best-evidenced cosmetic actives ‚ÄĒ multiple human topical RCTs for tone/barrier/wrinkle appearance, plus a phase-3 ORAL RCT for skin-cancer chemoprevention. Brutally honest caveat: most topical trials are industry-run, often on combination formulas, and effect sizes are modest. It is a reliable workhorse, not a miracle.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common name:** Niacinamide; nicotinamide; vitamin B3 (amide form).
- **INCI:** Niacinamide.
- **Chemistry:** Pyridine-3-carboxamide (the amide of nicotinic acid). Water-soluble, MW ~122 g/mol, small and relatively polar.
- **NOT the same as:** **Niacin / nicotinic acid** ‚ÄĒ the *acid* form causes prostaglandin-D2‚Äďmediated flushing/vasodilation; the *amide* (niacinamide) does not. This distinction matters for both safety messaging and the old "don't mix with vitamin C" myth (see ¬ß10).
- **Key forms / derivatives:**
  - **Niacinamide** itself ‚ÄĒ the standard cosmetic active.
  - **Nicotinic acid (niacin)** ‚ÄĒ flushing agent, not used as a cosmetic brightener.
  - **Myristyl nicotinate / tocopheryl nicotinate** ‚ÄĒ lipophilic prodrug esters designed for better penetration; far less clinical data.
  - **NAD‚Āļ / NMN / NR (nicotinamide riboside)** ‚ÄĒ downstream/related NAD‚Āļ-boosters marketed in "longevity" supplements; topical cosmetic evidence is thin and mostly mechanistic/marketing. Do not conflate niacinamide's solid topical record with NMN/NR hype.

## 2. Class & mechanism

Niacinamide is a precursor to the redox coenzymes **NAD‚Āļ/NADH and NADP‚Āļ/NADPH** via the salvage pathway (niacinamide ‚Üí NMN via NAMPT ‚Üí NAD‚Āļ). Aged and UV-stressed skin shows depleted NAD‚Āļ; topically supplying niacinamide is thought to replenish these pools, which feed essentially every energy-, repair-, and antioxidant-dependent process in the cell. The leading mechanistic review (Boo 2021) is candid that **"there is no convincing evidence that nicotinamide has specific molecular targets"** ‚ÄĒ it is a broad metabolic substrate, not a precision drug. Proposed mechanisms:

- **NAD‚Āļ/energy & antioxidant defense.** Regenerates NADPH (pentose phosphate pathway) ‚Üí drives glutathione reductase ‚Üí replenishes reduced glutathione (GSH). Also a modest direct singlet-oxygen scavenger in vitro. This is the basis for its "Antioxidant" class assignment.
- **Barrier / lipid synthesis.** Upregulates keratinocyte synthesis of **ceramides, other sphingolipids (glucosylceramide, sphingomyelin), free fatty acids, and cholesterol**, plus **involucrin and filaggrin** ‚ÄĒ i.e., it helps the skin make its own "mortar" and corneocyte envelope. This is the mechanistic spine of the barrier-repair story.
- **Pigmentation.** Does **not** inhibit tyrosinase or melanin synthesis. Instead it **inhibits melanosome transfer** from melanocytes to keratinocytes (35‚Äď68% inhibition in coculture, reversible) ‚ÄĒ an indirect, keratinocyte-side mechanism (Hakozaki 2002).
- **ECM / photoaging.** In cell/reconstructed-skin models, upregulates collagen (I, III, V) and elastin and downregulates MMP-1/3/9 by damping ROS‚ÜíAP-1/NF-őļB signaling.
- **DNA repair / photoimmune protection.** As a PARP and sirtuin substrate, supports poly(ADP-ribose) DNA-repair cycling; enhances nucleotide-excision-repair gene expression and prevents UV-induced ATP depletion / immunosuppression. This is the mechanistic basis for the *oral* chemoprevention data (¬ß4D).
- **Sebum.** Some evidence it lowers sebum excretion rate / casual sebum levels.

## 4. Evidence review by tier

> **Reading guide.** Quality climbs left‚Üíright: in-vitro ‚Üí animal ‚Üí human ORAL ‚Üí human TOPICAL. Within topical, RCT/vehicle-controlled > open-label. Caveat across the whole field: many topical trials are **manufacturer-sponsored**, **small**, **short (8‚Äď12 wk)**, and test **combination formulas**, so isolating niacinamide's solo contribution is often impossible.

### 4A. In-vitro (cell culture / reconstructed skin) ‚ÄĒ mechanism only
- **Hakozaki et al. 2002** (BJD): no effect on mushroom tyrosinase or melanocyte melanogenesis; **35‚Äď68% inhibition of melanosome transfer** in melanocyte‚Äďkeratinocyte coculture; reduced pigmentation in pigmented reconstructed-epidermis (PREP) model. *Establishes the depigmentation mechanism.*
- Keratinocyte studies (reviewed in Boo 2021): upregulation of ceramide/sphingolipid/FFA/cholesterol synthesis, involucrin, filaggrin; downregulation of MMP-1/3/9; upregulation of collagen I/III/V and elastin; direct singlet-oxygen scavenging (k ‚Čą 1.8 √ó 10‚Āł M‚ĀĽ¬Ļs‚ĀĽ¬Ļ).
- Fibroblast studies: extended replicative lifespan, reduced senescence-associated ő≤-gal ‚ÄĒ *hypothesis-generating only.*
- **Limitation:** intracellular niacinamide in mammalian cells may not reach the ~50 ¬ĶM threshold needed to inhibit sirtuins/PARP, so some proposed mechanisms may not operate at physiologic doses. The niacinamide transporter is unidentified.

### 4B. Animal ‚ÄĒ sparse and not the basis of claims
Most preclinical work is in cell/reconstructed-skin models rather than whole-animal dermatology. Photo-immunoprotection and DNA-repair work includes mouse UV models supporting the chemoprevention rationale, but **animal data are not load-bearing** for this ingredient; human data exist and supersede it.

### 4C. Human ‚ÄĒ ORAL (strongest single RCT for this molecule)
- **Chen J, Martin AJ, Damian DL et al. 2015 ‚ÄĒ the ONTRAC trial** (*N Engl J Med* 373:1618‚Äď1626; DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa1506197). **Phase-3, double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled.** n = 386 high-risk patients (‚Č•2 nonmelanoma skin cancers in prior 5 yr). **Oral nicotinamide 500 mg twice daily vs placebo, 12 months.** Result: **~23% lower rate of new nonmelanoma skin cancers** (95% CI 4‚Äď38, p = 0.02) and **~11‚Äď20% fewer actinic keratoses** over the trial. **Benefit disappeared after stopping** the supplement. Well tolerated, no excess adverse events.
- **Meta-analysis / systematic review** (PMC9125143, 2022) pooled chemoprevention data: confirms a real but modest effect in high-risk populations, good safety.
- **Chen AC, Martin AJ, Dalziell RA, Halliday GM, Damian DL. 2016 ‚ÄĒ oral nicotinamide & TEWL** (Br J Dermatol 175(6):1363‚Äď1365): in an RCT (n‚Čą292, nicotinamide 500 mg BID vs placebo, 12 mo) oral nicotinamide reduced forehead TEWL by ~5‚Äď6% ‚ÄĒ statistically real but a small effect, and crucially **ORAL**, not topical. (Earlier drafts mis-attributed this to "Surya R" ‚ÄĒ corrected; it is the same Sydney group behind ONTRAC.)
- **CRITICAL FOR THE BRAND:** ONTRAC is the strongest, most-cited evidence behind niacinamide ‚ÄĒ but it is **ORAL, at pharmacologic dose, in immunocompromised/high-risk patients, for cancer prevention.** It is **NOT** evidence that topical niacinamide cream prevents skin cancer or "repairs DNA" in healthy users. Do not borrow this halo into cosmetic claims. (A follow-up NEJM transplant-recipient trial, Allen et al. 2023, NEJMoa2203086, did *not* show benefit in that population ‚ÄĒ further reason to be cautious.)

### 4D. Human ‚ÄĒ TOPICAL
**RCT / vehicle- or active-controlled (the strongest topical tier):**
- **Bissett DL, Oblong JE, Berge CA. 2005** (*Dermatol Surg* 31:860‚Äď865) ‚ÄĒ 12-wk **double-blind, vehicle-controlled, split-face RCT**, n = 50 women (40‚Äď60). **5% niacinamide** significantly improved fine lines/wrinkles, hyperpigmented spots, red blotchiness, sallowness (yellowing), and elasticity vs vehicle. **Effects modest but statistically significant.** Industry (P&G) authors.
- **Bissett DL et al. 2004** (*Int J Cosmet Sci* 26:231‚Äď238; DOI 10.1111/j.1467-2494.2004.00228.x) ‚ÄĒ companion paper, same program; topical niacinamide reduced yellowing, wrinkling, red blotchiness and hyperpigmented spots in aging facial skin.
- **Hakozaki et al. 2002** (clinical arms, BJD 147:20‚Äď31): **5% niacinamide, 8 wk**, significantly reduced hyperpigmentation/increased lightness vs vehicle from week 4; a separate 2% in-sunscreen split-face arm (n = 120) also significant.
- **Fu JJJ, Hillebrand GG et al. 2010** (*Br J Dermatol* 162:647‚Äď654; DOI 10.1111/j.1365-2133.2009.09436.x) ‚ÄĒ randomized parallel-group, n = 196 women, 8 wk: a cosmetic **5% niacinamide + peptides + 0.3% retinyl propionate** regimen vs **prescription 0.02% tretinoin**. The niacinamide regimen showed **non-inferior to slightly better** wrinkle improvement at 8 wk with **far better tolerability** (less erythema/dryness, preserved TEWL); equivalent by 24 wk. **Confound:** combination formula + unblinded self-assessment + tretinoin's known irritancy ‚Üí cannot isolate niacinamide.
- **Acne (anti-inflammatory) RCTs:** Khodaeiani 2013 (*Int J Dermatol*) ‚ÄĒ 4% niacinamide gel vs 1% clindamycin, n = 80, comparable efficacy (niacinamide better on oily skin). Shahmoradi 2013 (PMC3724370) ‚ÄĒ 5% niacinamide vs 2% clindamycin gel, n = 60, no significant difference. Earlier Shalita 1995 ‚ÄĒ 4% niacinamide ‚Čą 1% clindamycin. *Supports anti-inflammatory/sebum claims.*
- **Pigment-disorder RCTs:** Navarrete-Sol√≠s 2011 (PMC3142702) ‚ÄĒ 4% niacinamide vs 4% hydroquinone for melasma, n = 27, comparable MASI reduction with less irritation; Castanedo-Cazares ‚ÄĒ 4% niacinamide vs 0.05% desonide, both > placebo. Combination work: niacinamide + N-undecylenoyl phenylalanine (Bissett 2008), + N-acetylglucosamine, + tranexamic acid all outperform niacinamide alone.

**Barrier / TEWL (topical, mostly smaller/biophysical):**
- Forearm study (n = 41, 2% niacinamide, 24 d): increased stratum-corneum ceramide & FFA, decreased TEWL in dry skin.
- Mechanistic biophysical work (e.g., *Int J Pharm* 2013, stratum-corneum molecular/biophysical properties; *Sci Rep* 2025 stratum-corneum hydration/structure **[unverified ‚ÄĒ full text not accessed]**) generally supports hydration/barrier effects.

**Honest summary of the topical tier:** Tone/pigmentation and barrier/TEWL have **multiple vehicle-controlled human RCTs ‚Üí "strong" for a cosmetic.** Wrinkle/photoaging appearance is **"moderate"** (significant vs vehicle but modest, often combo formulas, mostly one research group/sponsor). Collagen, senescence, mitochondrial, microbiome, glycation = **not proven topically.**

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- Small, water-soluble molecule that penetrates the stratum corneum reasonably well; readily taken up and converted intracellularly (the niacinamide transporter is still unidentified).
- **Penetration appears non-linear with dose** ‚ÄĒ more on the label does not mean proportionally more in the skin. Widely-repeated secondary sources cite ~68% stratum-corneum penetration at 5% dropping to ~52% at 10%, but **these specific figures could not be traced to a primary peer-reviewed study and are NOT in the cited Boo 2021 review ‚ÄĒ treat as indicative, not established.** The defensible point stands: niacinamide is hydrophilic and partitions poorly into the SC lipid matrix, so increasing the label % does not yield proportional uptake. This supports keeping concentrations modest rather than chasing "10%+" (see ¬ß10).
- Lipophilic ester prodrugs (myristyl/tocopheryl nicotinate) were designed to improve delivery but lack the clinical track record of plain niacinamide.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:** **2‚Äď5% is the well-supported sweet spot** ‚ÄĒ this is where the human RCTs actually cluster (4‚Äď5%). A commonly-cited "~3.2% saturates the conversion enzymes" figure circulates in secondary skincare sources but **could not be traced to a primary study; treat it as folklore, not fact.** What IS defensible: published vehicle-controlled efficacy data sit at 2‚Äď5%, and **above ~5% there is little controlled human evidence of added benefit while irritation/flushing risk rises** in sensitive skin. Recommend formulating at **4‚Äď5%**.
- **pH:** Effective and stable across **~5‚Äď7**; not pH-fragile like L-ascorbic acid or AHAs. Most stable around pH 6.
- **Stability:** Very stable, long shelf life, heat-tolerant in normal processing, water-soluble ‚ÄĒ easy and cheap to formulate. (Avoid prolonged low-pH + heat, which can hydrolyze a little niacinamide to nicotinic acid ‚ÄĒ see flushing note.)
- **Pairs well with:** essentially everything ‚ÄĒ **retinoids** (buffers irritation, complementary), **peptides**, **hyaluronic acid**, **ceramides** (synergistic barrier story), and **vitamin C** (the "they cancel out / cause flushing" claim is an outdated myth in modern formulas ‚ÄĒ see ¬ß10).
- **Conflicts:** none significant. The only real-world issue is **trace nicotinic-acid impurity** in low-grade raw material or formulas held hot at low pH ‚Üí transient flushing. Use cosmetic-grade purified niacinamide and a near-neutral pH.
- **Sensory:** lightweight, non-greasy, plays well in serums, essences, lotions, and creams; no color/odor issues.

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation/sensitization:** Very low. CIR/clinical testing: **no stinging up to 10%**, no irritation in 21-day cumulative tests up to 5%; **not a sensitizer or photosensitizer.** Occasional transient flushing at high % or with impure material.
- **Sun sensitivity:** None ‚ÄĒ does **not** increase photosensitivity (if anything, photoprotective via DNA-repair support).
- **Pregnancy/lactation:** Considered **low-risk topically** ‚ÄĒ minimal systemic absorption through intact skin, not an ECHA/CIR reproductive hazard; niacinamide is a normal B-vitamin and a routine prenatal nutrient. Standard "consult your doctor" boilerplate still applies; we do not give medical advice.
- **Sensitization/allergens:** Low allergen risk; no common cross-reactors.
- **Oral note (context, not a topical claim):** oral nicotinamide is well tolerated to ~1 g/day; hepatotoxicity only at extreme doses (>3 g/day); isolated thrombocytopenia in dialysis patients. Irrelevant to topical cosmetic use but useful for FAQ accuracy.
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** Manufactured synthetically at large scale (vitamin/feed industry overlap); no major sustainability or supply-chain red flags.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Cosmetic vs drug line:** Niacinamide is a **cosmetic ingredient.** Claims must stay in appearance/support territory. The ORAL skin-cancer data and DNA-repair mechanism sit firmly on the **drug** side of the line and must not migrate into cosmetic copy.
- ‚úÖ **Safe cosmetic language:** "supports the skin's moisture barrier," "helps visibly even skin tone," "for a smoother, more refined look," "improves the appearance of enlarged-looking pores," "helps comfort and visibly strengthen the look of skin," "antioxidant support."
- ‚ö†ÔłŹ **Risky / drug-like ‚ÄĒ avoid or flag for substantiation/legal:** "increases ceramide synthesis / boosts collagen" (structure/function ‚Üí keep to "supports the barrier"), "treats hyperpigmentation/melasma/acne/rosacea," "repairs DNA," "prevents skin cancer," "reduces wrinkles by X%." Anything borrowing the ONTRAC cancer-prevention halo.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

- **"10%+ is stronger."** ‚ĚĆ Mostly marketing ‚ÄĒ but argue it carefully. The solid part: **human efficacy RCTs use 2‚Äď5%, and there is little-to-no controlled evidence that 10% outperforms 5%**, while higher % buys irritation risk. The shakier part: the popular "enzymes saturate at 3.2%" and "penetration drops 68%‚Üí52% from 5% to 10%" figures are **not traceable to primary studies** (see ¬ß5, ¬ß6) ‚ÄĒ use the absence-of-benefit-evidence argument, not those precise numbers.
- **"Don't combine with vitamin C ‚ÄĒ they cancel out / cause flushing."** ‚ĚĆ Outdated myth from a 1960s study using **niacin (the acid), high concentration, high heat/low pH**. Modern niacinamide + vitamin C formulations at skin-friendly pH are fine and widely co-formulated.
- **"Boosts collagen / repairs DNA / NAD‚Āļ longevity in a jar."** ‚ö†ÔłŹ Mechanistically plausible, **not proven for topical cosmetic use.** NAD‚Āļ/NMN/NR "longevity" framing is borrowed hype ‚ÄĒ keep niacinamide's claims to its real topical wins (tone, barrier, appearance).
- **"Prevents skin cancer."** ‚ö†ÔłŹ True-ish for **ORAL** 500 mg BID in **high-risk patients** (ONTRAC) ‚ÄĒ **not** a topical cosmetic claim, and not even replicated in transplant recipients (2023). Never use in product copy.
- **"Cure for acne/melasma."** ‚ĚĆ It performs *comparably* to some actives in trials and is well-tolerated, but "cure/treat" is drug language.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

‚úÖ **SUPPORTING (hero-grade).** Niacinamide is the connective tissue of the barrier-repair/renew thesis: it is the rare active that is **gentle, cheap, stable, layer-with-anything, and genuinely evidence-backed** for the brand's two most defensible claim spaces ‚ÄĒ **barrier/TEWL** and **tone**. It is a near-default inclusion across the line and a legitimate co-hero (with ceramides) for a barrier-renewal product. **One-line:** broadly proven, well-tolerated barrier/tone multitasker with unbeatable credibility-per-dollar ‚ÄĒ include at 4‚Äď5%, but resist borrowing its oral/NAD‚Āļ longevity halo into cosmetic claims.

**Longevity relevance:** Strong *thematic* fit (NAD‚Āļ precursor, DNA-repair/sirtuin/PARP substrate, the "skin-longevity" molecule par excellence) ‚ÄĒ but be disciplined: the longevity mechanism is mostly oral/cellular, while the **topical** wins are barrier and tone. Use the longevity story for brand narrative, not for product efficacy claims.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

_Verified 2026-06-19 by an adversarial fact-check pass. Method: each headline efficacy claim was tested for (a) HUMAN + TOPICAL provenance vs. quietly-borrowed in-vitro/animal/oral data, (b) whether the cited study is real and says what is claimed, (c) industry funding / small-n / single-group concentration. Primary checks done against PubMed/NEJM/Wiley/Oxford/PMC and the Boo 2021 review full text._

### Overall evidence grade: **B (moderate human-topical)**

Niacinamide is genuinely one of the better-evidenced cosmetic actives, but it is **not grade A**. Grade A would require multiple large, independent, vehicle-controlled topical RCTs on the *isolated* active. What actually exists: a handful of vehicle-controlled topical RCTs (tone/pigmentation strongest), with effect sizes that are **modest**, run largely by **one sponsor (P&G) / overlapping author teams**, often on **combination formulas**, and **short (8‚Äď12 wk)**. The single most impressive trial behind the molecule's reputation (ONTRAC) is **oral, pharmacologic-dose, in high-risk patients, for cancer prevention** ‚ÄĒ a different product, route, dose, and population. The dossier already handles most of this honestly; this section records the verification and the residual soft spots.

**Grade by claim space:**
| Claim space | Best evidence type | Grade |
|---|---|---|
| Pigmentation / even tone | Multiple human topical vehicle-controlled RCTs (Hakozaki 2002; Navarrete-Sol√≠s 2011) | **B+** (strong for a cosmetic) |
| Barrier / TEWL / hydration | Smaller human topical biophysical studies + strong mechanism; best *RCT* TEWL data is ORAL | **B‚Äď** |
| Photoaging appearance (lines, sallowness, blotchiness) | Human topical RCTs, but modest, mostly P&G, often combo | **B‚Äď/C+** |
| Acne / anti-inflammatory | Human topical RCTs vs clindamycin (Khodaeiani 2013; Shahmoradi 2013) ‚ÄĒ but "treat acne" is drug language | **B** (as comparison data), not a cosmetic claim |
| Collagen / ECM, senescence, mitochondrial, microbiome, glycation | In-vitro / NAD‚Āļ rationale only | **D topically** |
| DNA repair / skin-cancer prevention | ORAL phase-3 RCT (ONTRAC); null in transplant recipients (Allen 2023) | **A for ORAL / D as a topical cosmetic claim** |

### What checked out (real studies, accurately represented)

### Main caveats (the honest reading)
1. **Sponsor/author concentration.** The topical photoaging evidence is dominated by **P&G-affiliated authors (Bissett, Oblong, Hakozaki, Fu, Hillebrand)** and the chemoprevention/TEWL evidence by **one Sydney group (Damian/Halliday/Martin/Chen)**.
