# Ingredient: Enoxolone (18β-Glycyrrhetinic Acid) (INCI: Glycyrrhetinic Acid)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product
**Slug:** enoxolone · **Class:** NAD+ / sirtuin / AMPK / cellular-energy actives (filed here for the CD38 angle; see honesty note below)

> **One-line honest summary.** Enoxolone is one of the best-evidenced *soothing / anti-inflammatory* botanicals in dermatology (licorice-derived; decades of topical use; a real human RCT for post-laser redness). Its placement in the "NAD+/cellular-energy" class rests on a **single 2024 in-vitro paper, authored by a cosmetics company (LG H&H)**, proposing it as a **CD38 inhibitor** that protects exogenous NAD+. That mechanism is biologically plausible but **unproven in human skin** and currently rests on one industry study. Treat enoxolone as a **proven anti-inflammatory / barrier-soothing supporting active** with an **optional, honestly-framed NAD-preservation story** — not as an evidence-backed NAD+ hero.

---

## 0. The honest punchline (read this first)

- **What enoxolone actually is, evidence-wise:** a licorice-root triterpenoid with **strong, broad, long-standing dermatological credentials as an anti-inflammatory / anti-irritant / soothing agent** — used at ~0.5–2% in countless calming creams. There is even a **human split-face RCT** (post-laser) showing 2% enoxolone reduces redness and pain (Choi 2024 [1]).
- **Why the brief filed it under NAD+/sirtuin/cellular-energy:** a 2024 paper (Kang et al., *Cells*, **LG Household & Health Care R&D Center**) proposes enoxolone (with quercetin) as a **CD38 inhibitor**. CD38 is the NADase that consumes NAD+ and rises with age/senescence; inhibiting it should *preserve* NAD+ rather than supply it. In cultured human fibroblasts, 1 ppm quercetin + 5 ppm enoxolone inhibited CD38 ~55.8% and boosted the effects of *exogenous* NAD+ on sirtuins/autophagy/mitochondria [2].
- **The catch (be brutal):** (a) this is **in-vitro only, single study, industry-authored** (conflict of interest); (b) the broader, independent "flavonoids inhibit CD38" literature is about **quercetin/apigenin/luteolin — not enoxolone** (Kellenberger 2011 [3]; Escande 2013 [4]); (c) there is **no human skin data** that topical enoxolone raises or preserves skin NAD+, activates sirtuins, or visibly reverses aging via this route; (d) enoxolone is **lipophilic and poorly water-soluble (logP ~6.6)**, so even reaching the dermis reliably needs a delivery system.
- **Also note the licorice mix-up:** the famous licorice *skin-brightening* reputation belongs mostly to **glabridin / liquiritin** (other licorice molecules), **not** enoxolone. Enoxolone's lane is **soothing/anti-inflammatory**, with secondary antioxidant/photoprotective (animal) and modest pigment (in-vitro) signals.
- **Net:** enoxolone earns its place as a **credible, well-tolerated supporting "calm/repair" active**. The NAD+/CD38 "longevity" framing is a *legitimate mechanistic story* but **must be labeled as mechanism, not proven efficacy**, and never claimed for the topical human format.

**Why it's *grouped* with NAD+ actives (the kernel of truth):** the marketing/scientific bridge is **CD38 inhibition**. CD38 is the dominant NAD-consuming enzyme; it increases with age and is induced by senescent-cell secretions, driving the age-related NAD+ decline (Camacho-Pereira 2016 [4]; Chini 2019 [11]). If enoxolone inhibits CD38, it would *spare* NAD+ rather than supply it — a "defend the tank" rather than "refill the tank" logic, complementary to niacinamide/NMN precursors (see [`nad-niacinamide-longevity.md`](nad-niacinamide-longevity.md)).

## 2. Class & mechanism

**Class (as filed):** NAD+/sirtuin/cellular-energy actives — *via the proposed CD38-inhibition mechanism.*
**Class (as actually best-evidenced):** anti-inflammatory / anti-irritant botanical with antioxidant, antimicrobial, mild photoprotective and minor depigmenting activity.

Enoxolone is genuinely multi-target. Mechanistic pillars, with honest evidence levels:

1. **11β-HSD inhibition (its signature, double-edged mechanism).** Enoxolone inhibits **11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase (both type 1 and type 2)**, the enzyme that interconverts active cortisol and inactive cortisone. By blocking 11β-HSD2 locally, it **raises local cortisol availability**, potentiating endogenous (and co-applied) glucocorticoid anti-inflammatory action — topical 11β-HSD inhibitors *potentiated the anti-inflammatory action of glucocorticoids* in an **in-vivo mouse contact-hypersensitivity model** (Hennebold & Daynes 1998 [6]). **Note:** this is an animal model, not human skin — the older "human skin vasoconstrictor" framing for this mechanism was not supported by the cited record (see verification §13). **Double-edged:** the *same* 11β-HSD2 inhibition, **systemically**, is what makes oral licorice raise blood pressure (apparent mineralocorticoid excess) — a key safety frame for §7.
2. **NF-κB / cytokine suppression.** Suppresses NF-κB activation and downstream **TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6, COX-2**; inhibits **phospholipase A₂** (↓arachidonic-acid cascade/eicosanoids). Demonstrated in keratinocyte/macrophage/synovial models and disease models (Kowalska 2019 [5]; Feng 2021 [7]; Bonne 1996 [12]). Notably this anti-inflammatory action is **not** glucocorticoid-receptor-mediated.
3. **Antioxidant / Nrf2.** Radical scavenging plus **upregulation of SOD, GSH-Px/catalase** and activation of **Nrf2/HO-1**; reduces lipid peroxidation (MDA) and oxidative DNA damage (Kong 2015 [8]; asthma/Nrf2 work [13]).
4. **CD38 inhibition → NAD+ preservation → sirtuin/autophagy/mitochondria (the "longevity" mechanism, weakest tier).** Proposed by Kang 2024 [2]: enoxolone (with quercetin) suppresses CD38 expression, so co-applied exogenous NAD+ is consumed less, raising effective NAD+ and downstream **SIRT activation, autophagy, mitochondrial function** in cultured human fibroblasts. **In-vitro, single, industry study.**
5. **MMP suppression / collagen sparing (photoaging).** ↓MMP-1, ↓MMP-3 and preserved dermal collagen under UV — animal (mouse) evidence (Kong 2015 [8]).
6. **Antimicrobial.** Activity against *S. aureus* (incl. some resistant strains), *Cutibacterium acnes*, and some fungi — relevant to acne/sensitive-skin positioning (Kowalska 2019 [5]; acne network-pharmacology work [14]).
7. **Mild tyrosinase inhibition.** In-vitro anti-tyrosinase/anti-melanogenesis, but **weaker and less central than glabridin/liquiritin**; pigment is a *secondary*, not headline, property of enoxolone specifically.

**Honest mechanistic translation:** pillars 1–3 and 6 are real, well-replicated, and the basis of enoxolone's legitimate "soothing" reputation. Pillar 5 is solid in mice. **Pillar 4 (the class rationale) is the speculative one** — plausible biology, one cell study, no human skin proof.

## 4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

> Tiers, weakest-format-first within each: **in-vitro → animal → human ORAL → human TOPICAL (RCT vs lower)**. For each claim I flag whether it is **proven for human topical use** or **borrowed** from another format/species. The single human topical RCT is the most important item for a skincare brand and is highlighted.

### TIER A — In-vitro (cell / biochemistry)

**A1. The CD38 / NAD+ "longevity" paper — the entire basis for this ingredient's class placement.**
- **Kang S, Park J, Cheng Z, Ye S, Jun S-H, Kang N-G.** "Novel Approach to Skin Anti-Aging: Boosting Pharmacological Effects of Exogenous NAD⁺ by Synergistic Inhibition of CD38 Expression." *Cells* 2024;13(21):1799. DOI 10.3390/cells13211799; PMC11544843. [2]
- **Design:** cultured **human dermal fibroblasts**; models of **extrinsic (UV-induced)** and **intrinsic** aging; tested **exogenous NAD⁺** ± the phytochemical complex (**quercetin + enoxolone**).
- **Key findings:** exogenous NAD⁺ protected fibroblasts; **CD38 over-expression (induced by exogenous NAD⁺) was suppressed by quercetin + enoxolone, max 55.82% inhibition at 1 ppm quercetin + 5 ppm enoxolone**; the complex amplified NAD⁺'s effects via **↑sirtuin activation, ↑autophagy, ↑mitochondrial function**.
- **Limitations (state them plainly):** **in-vitro only** (no animal, no human skin, no 3D-skin efficacy endpoint reported for the topical product); **single study**; **authored by LG Household & Health Care R&D Center (a cosmetics manufacturer)** → **commercial conflict of interest**; the active model *requires co-applied exogenous NAD⁺* (enoxolone is the *protector*, not the NAD source); independent replication absent. Mechanistic support, **not** efficacy proof.

**A2. Independent CD38-inhibitor literature — and why it under-supports *enoxolone*.**
- **Kellenberger E, et al.** "Flavonoids as inhibitors of human CD38." *Bioorg Med Chem Lett* 2011;21(13):3939–42; PMID 21641214. Human CD38 inhibited at low-µM by **luteolinidin, kuromanin, luteolin (IC50 <10 µM)** — **flavonoids, not triterpenoids.** [3]
- **Escande C, et al.** "Flavonoid apigenin is an inhibitor of the NAD⁺ase CD38…" *Diabetes* 2013;62(4):1084–93; PMC3609577. **Apigenin** IC50 ~10.3 µM (NADase); **quercetin** ~16.4 µM in cells; raises NAD⁺ in vivo (mice). [4]
- **Camacho-Pereira J, et al.** "CD38 dictates age-related NAD decline and mitochondrial dysfunction through an SIRT3-dependent mechanism." *Cell Metab* 2016;23(6):1127–39 — establishes CD38 as the central age-related NAD-consuming enzyme. [4b]
- **Honest read:** the *robust, independent* CD38-inhibition evidence belongs to **quercetin/apigenin/luteolin**. For **enoxolone**, the CD38 claim hangs on **one** paper [2]. The class rationale is real biology, but enoxolone is **not** a well-validated CD38 inhibitor the way quercetin is.

**A3. Anti-inflammatory mechanism (foundational, well-replicated).** Bonne 1996 [12] and Kowalska 2019 [5]: ↓NF-κB, ↓TNF-α/IL-1, ↓PLA₂; 11β-HSD inhibition raising local cortisol; *not* glucocorticoid-receptor-mediated. Feng 2021 [7]: in MH7A human synovial fibroblasts + RAW264.7 macrophages, 18β-GA ↓IL-1β/IL-6/COX-2 via **MAPK/NF-κB** inhibition + **FOXO3** induction (in-vitro IC50 ~243–393 µM; cytokine effects at 100–200 µM).

**A4. Antioxidant / tyrosinase / antimicrobial (in-vitro).** Radical scavenging, ↑SOD/GSH-Px, Nrf2/HO-1 [8][13]; mushroom-tyrosinase inhibition (modest vs glabridin) [5]; antibacterial vs *S. aureus*/*C. acnes* [5][14].

### TIER B — Animal (in vivo)

**B1. Topical enoxolone protects against UV photoaging in mice — the best non-human efficacy data.**
- **Kong S-Z, et al.** "The protective effect of 18β-Glycyrrhetinic acid against UV irradiation induced photoaging in mice." *Exp Gerontol* 2015;61:147–155. DOI 10.1016/j.exger.2014.12.008. [8]
- **Design:** mouse dorsal skin pretreated **topically** with GA for 2 h before UV, **10 weeks**.
- **Findings:** ↓macroscopic + histopathological photodamage; **↑SOD, ↑GSH-Px, ↓MDA**; **↑skin collagen**; **↓MMP-1, ↓MMP-3**; ↓IL-6/TNF-α/IL-10.
- **Limitation:** **mouse; topical pretreatment vs UV** (prevention model), not human aged-skin efficacy. Strong mechanistic/animal support for an anti-photoaging story; **borrowed species.**

**B2. Anti-inflammatory in disease models (systemic/injected — borrowed format).** Feng 2021 [7]: collagen-induced arthritis in DBA/1 mice, 18β-GA 45 mg/kg/day (systemic) ↓joint swelling, ↓serum TNF-α/IL-1β/IL-6. Asthma model: ↓airway inflammation via NF-κB and Nrf2/HO-1 [13]. Radiation-induced skin injury: 18β-GA alleviated injury via Nrf2/HO-1 [16]. **All confirm mechanism; none are topical human skin.**

### TIER C — Human ORAL (mostly a *safety/risk* tier here, not an efficacy tier)

> Unlike the NAD precursors, enoxolone's oral human literature is dominated by **risk** (licorice's mineralocorticoid effect), not skin benefit.

**C1. Oral licorice / glycyrrhetinic acid → apparent mineralocorticoid excess.** Systemic 18β-GA/glycyrrhizin inhibits **11β-HSD2**, causing cortisol-driven mineralocorticoid effects → **hypertension, hypokalemia, fluid retention**; documented in case reports and reviews (acquired AME [17]; 11β-HSD/hypertension reviews [18]). **Directly relevant as a safety boundary** (see §7); not a skin-efficacy finding.
- A few oral *combination* skin-brightening studies exist for licorice constituents (e.g., glycyrrhizin + tranexamic acid for melanosis), but the active credited is generally the brightening licorice fraction, not enoxolone specifically [19].

### TIER D — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters for a skincare brand)

**D1. ⭐ The one genuine human topical RCT — post-laser redness/pain.**
- **Choi SY, et al.** "The efficacy of enoxolone in reducing erythema and pain after laser treatment: A randomized split-face pilot study." *J Cosmet Dermatol* 2024;23(?):e16329. DOI 10.1111/jocd.16329; PMID 38622995. [1]
- **Design:** **randomized split-face**, **n = 10** healthy subjects, non-ablative 1550 nm Er:Glass fractional laser; one side **2% enoxolone** in a moisturizer, other side moisturizer alone; outcomes at 30 min/60 min/24 h.
- **Findings:** enoxolone side had **significantly lower erythema index (EI) and clinician erythema assessment (CEA) at 24 h**, and **lower pain at 30 min**.
- **Limitations:** **very small (n=10), pilot, single-center, short (24 h), procedure-recovery context** (not chronic aging), no long-term or wrinkle endpoints. Still — this is a **real, randomized, vehicle-controlled human topical** result for the *soothing/anti-redness* claim. It validates **calm/recovery**, **not** anti-aging or NAD+.

**D2. Topical anti-inflammatory / eczema-adjacent use (lower-tier human).** Kowalska 2019 [5] summarizes human/clinical dermatological use of GA-containing products for inflammatory conditions (atopic-dermatitis-adjacent, irritation), with reports of efficacy comparable to mild hydrocortisone and good tolerability at ~0.5–2%. **Caveat:** many are small, older, or in multi-ingredient/glycyrrhizin formulations — pull primaries before quoting figures. The 11β-HSD2 picture in atopic dermatitis is also more complex than "block it and AD improves" (an oxazolone mouse model found local 11β-HSD1 inhibition *worsened* AD [20]) — so don't over-promise on inflammatory disease.

**D3. The NAD+/CD38/anti-aging claim, human topical: ABSENT.** **No published human topical study** shows enoxolone raises/preserves skin NAD+, activates sirtuins in vivo, or visibly reduces wrinkles via the CD38 route. That claim is **borrowed entirely from one in-vitro paper** [2].

**Bottom line by tier:**
- **Proven (human topical):** reduces **post-procedure redness/pain** (n=10 RCT); broadly **soothing/anti-inflammatory** in use.
- **Strong (animal/in-vitro):** anti-inflammatory mechanism, antioxidant, anti-photoaging (mouse), antimicrobial.
- **Speculative (in-vitro, single industry study, no human):** the **NAD+/CD38/sirtuin "longevity"** mechanism that justifies its class placement.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **The core obstacle:** enoxolone is **small enough by mass (MW 470.7 < 500 Da)** but **very lipophilic (logP ≈ 6.57)** and **poorly water-soluble**, so plain dispersions penetrate the stratum corneum **inefficiently** and unevenly (Zhang 2022 [9]; Kowalska 2019 [5]).
- **Delivery systems materially help.** In comparative ex-vivo permeation, total skin permeation of GA was **20.67% via "glycethosomes"** vs **10.56% ethosomes, 9.38% glycerosomes, 7.78% liposomes, 5.02% plain dispersion** (Zhang 2022 [9]). **Nanocrystals** likewise improved permeation and anti-inflammatory activity (Wang 2021 [10]).
- **Ester form:** **stearyl glycyrrhetinate** is an oil-phase-friendly derivative used to improve formulation handling/incorporation.
- **Practical read:** for a *surface-soothing* effect (anti-redness, anti-irritant), even modest penetration to the upper viable epidermis is plausibly enough — consistent with the positive post-laser RCT [1]. For any *dermal "longevity"/CD38/collagen* ambition, you should assume penetration is the limiting step and **either use a delivery system or treat the deep claim as unproven** (which it is anyway).
- **NAD+ context:** the CD38 mechanism [2] *also requires co-delivered exogenous NAD⁺* to reach fibroblasts — itself a large, charged, penetration-challenged molecule. So the "CD38-protected NAD+" story has **two** stacked delivery problems in real skin, neither solved in the literature.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:** **0.5–2%** is the established cosmetic/anti-inflammatory range; the human post-laser RCT used **2%** [1]. The CD38 in-vitro work used **5 ppm (0.0005%)** enoxolone *in cell medium* with 1 ppm quercetin [2] — a *culture* dose, **not** formulation guidance; do not back-derive a use level from it.
- **pH:** as a carboxylic-acid triterpenoid it's better solubilized/handled near **mildly acidic-to-neutral**; the **glycyrrhizinate salt** forms are water-dispersible and easier in aqueous systems. Confirm against the specific raw material.
- **Solubility/stability:** oil-soluble, water-poor; pre-dissolve in the oil phase or use solubilizers/delivery carriers. Generally stable; protect from prolonged heat. Delivery-enhanced forms (glycethosomes/nanocrystals) improve both stability and penetration [9][10].
- **Pairings (rational):**
  - **Niacinamide** — the cleanest pairing: niacinamide is the proven NAD-supporting/barrier active (see [`nad-niacinamide-longevity.md`](nad-niacinamide-longevity.md)); enoxolone adds soothing + a *complementary* "preserve NAD (CD38)" angle. Together they make an honest, defensible **NAD-support + calm** duo.
  - **Quercetin** — the literal co-active in the CD38 paper [2]; also an independently-validated CD38 inhibitor [3][4]. If you want to tell the CD38 story with more (still in-vitro) support, pair the two.
  - **Ceramides / barrier lipids** — fits the barrier-repair thesis; enoxolone's lipophilicity sits comfortably in a lipid system.
  - **Centella / panthenol / allantoin / bisabolol** — stacks well into a "soothing/recovery" hero.
  - **Post-procedure / sensitive-skin** positioning is the strongest evidence-aligned use.
- **Conflicts:** few hard incompatibilities; the practical issue is **getting it into solution and into skin**, not chemical clashing. Avoid relying on it in a purely aqueous, no-solubilizer base.
- **Sensory:** at use levels, low odor/color from purified material; lipophilic, so it reads as part of the emollient phase.

## 7. Safety

- **Topical irritation/sensitization:** Generally **very well tolerated** — it's used precisely to *reduce* irritation. CIR and long use history support low irritancy at cosmetic levels (Kowalska 2019 [5]). **But not zero:** a published **allergic contact dermatitis** case from glycyrrhetinic acid in an eyelid-eczema cream, confirmed by patch test (Sánchez-García 2023 [21]) — rare, but real; include in patch-test logic and don't market as "cannot irritate."
- **The systemic 11β-HSD2 caveat (important framing, low topical risk):** **oral/high systemic** glycyrrhetinic acid/glycyrrhizin causes **apparent mineralocorticoid excess → hypertension, hypokalemia, edema** [17][18]. For **normal topical cosmetic use**, systemic absorption is low and this is not an expected risk — **but** it is the reason to (a) avoid high-dose/large-surface/occluded use claims, and (b) be cautious in **pregnancy** (see below).
- **Pregnancy/breastfeeding:** **Systemic** licorice/glycyrrhizin in pregnancy is linked (via placental 11β-HSD2 inhibition) to adverse outcomes (low birth weight, offspring BP/neurocognitive effects) [18]. Topical cosmetic exposure is far lower and not clearly contraindicated, **but** given the mechanism, default to conservative copy ("consult your doctor") and avoid high-concentration/large-area positioning for pregnant users. **[partially unverified for topical specifically — flag for medical/regulatory review.]**
- **Sun sensitivity:** **None** — unlike retinoids/AHAs. Mechanistically *photoprotective* in animal models [8]. Daytime-compatible.
- **Sourcing/allergen:** licorice-derived; cross-reaction/allergy to licorice is uncommon but possible. Synthetic-grade or well-purified plant-derived material both used; specify on COA.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

**The cosmetic/drug line.** Enoxolone sits in a sensitive zone: its very name evokes a *drug-like* anti-inflammatory action, and "anti-inflammatory" itself can read as a **drug claim** in the US/EU. Keep copy in **appearance/sensory** territory.

- ✅ **Safer cosmetic language:**
  - "**helps soothe** and visibly **calm** the look of redness and irritation"
  - "**comforting** licorice-derived active; supports skin after stress/sun/procedures"
  - "supports a **calm, balanced, resilient-looking** complexion"
  - "formulated with **enoxolone (licorice-derived)** alongside niacinamide to **support skin's NAD-dependent renewal processes**" — *only with niacinamide carrying the NAD logic and the wording kept to "support…processes."*
- ⚠️ **Risky / drug-like / unsubstantiated — flag for legal/substantiation:**
  - "**anti-inflammatory**," "**treats** eczema/rosacea/dermatitis," "**reduces inflammation**" → drug territory.
  - "**inhibits CD38** / **preserves your skin's NAD+** / **activates sirtuins**" → mechanism unproven in human skin; from one in-vitro industry study. Do **not** state as fact.
  - "**reverses aging / boosts collagen by X%**" → structure/function + overreach.
  - "**brightens / fades dark spots**" → enoxolone's pigment data is weak/in-vitro; that licorice claim belongs to **glabridin/liquiritin**, not enoxolone — don't borrow it.
  - "**replaces a steroid / steroid-strength**" → drug comparison; avoid even though the 11β-HSD/hydrocortisone-potentiation science exists [6].
- **Key compliance nuance:** the *strongest human topical evidence* (post-laser redness, n=10 [1]) supports a **"calm/recovery"** claim with hedging ("helps reduce the appearance of redness after [procedure]"), ideally with our **own** finished-formula substantiation given the tiny pilot n.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

- **"NAD+ / longevity / CD38 anti-aging active."** — The *headline reason it's in this class* is the **weakest** part of its file: **one in-vitro, industry-authored study** [2], no human skin proof, and the independent CD38-inhibitor evidence is about **quercetin/apigenin/luteolin, not enoxolone** [3][4]. Plausible mechanism; **not** demonstrated efficacy. This is the exact "format/mechanism-as-proof" trap to avoid.
- **"Clinically proven to calm/soothe."** — *Mostly fair, with caveats.* There **is** a real human topical RCT — but it's a **10-person post-laser pilot** [1]. Honest phrasing: "in a small split-face study, 2% enoxolone reduced post-laser redness and pain."
- **"Licorice brightens dark spots."** — *Misattribution.* That's **glabridin/liquiritin**; enoxolone's pigment effect is weak/in-vitro. Don't transfer the brightening halo.
- **"Steroid-strength without the steroid."** — *Overreach / drug-adjacent.* The 11β-HSD/cortisol-potentiation biology is real [6], but it's also the **same mechanism behind licorice's blood-pressure risk** — a reason for restraint, not a marketing flex.
- **What's NOT hype:** enoxolone is a **genuinely well-established, well-tolerated anti-inflammatory/soothing botanical** with antioxidant, antimicrobial and (mouse) anti-photoaging support, and a real (if tiny) human anti-redness RCT. As a *calm/recovery/barrier-support* active, it earns its keep.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️→✅ **SUPPORTING (calm/soothe/recovery) — with an OPTIONAL, strictly-honest NAD-preservation story.**

**One-line:** Enoxolone is a credible, low-cost, well-tolerated **soothing/anti-inflammatory** supporting active with real (if modest) human topical evidence for **redness/recovery**; its "NAD+/CD38/sirtuin longevity" framing is a *legitimate mechanistic story* but **rests on a single in-vitro industry study** and must never be claimed as proven topical efficacy.

**How to use it for this brand:**
- **Best fit:** a **supporting "calm/barrier-recovery"** ingredient in a sensitive-skin, post-procedure, or barrier-repair hero — directly aligned with the brand's **barrier-repair-cream** thesis and "credibility over hype" stance.
- **The honest longevity bridge:** pair with **niacinamide** (the proven NAD-supporting workhorse) and optionally **quercetin** (the better-validated CD38 inhibitor) to tell a *defensible* "support + protect your skin's NAD-dependent renewal" story — **with niacinamide carrying the proof and enoxolone framed as a complementary CD38-mechanism, not as demonstrated NAD elevation.**
- **Do not** make it the NAD+/cellular-energy *hero* on its own evidence, and **do not** import the brightening or anti-inflammatory-drug claims.

**Longevity-relevance note.** Enoxolone is conceptually elegant for a skin-*longevity* brand: most NAD actives try to **refill** the tank (precursors — niacinamide/NMN), whereas the CD38 angle is about **stopping the leak** (sparing NAD+ by inhibiting the age-/senescence-induced NADase). That "two-pronged NAD strategy" is a *great education narrative* and a real area of longevity science (CD38 is a validated NAD-decline driver [4][11]). The gap is purely **translation to human skin for enoxolone specifically** — the molecule's *proven* topical value today is calm/anti-redness, and the NAD story should be told as **mechanism and inspiration, substantiated by niacinamide's data, not enoxolone's.**
