# Ingredient: Pyruvic Acid (INCI: Pyruvic Acid)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product
**Class:** Hydroxy acids & chemical exfoliants (specifically an **alpha-keto acid** — chemically distinct from AHAs/BHAs)

> **One-line honest summary:** Pyruvic acid is a real, evidence-backed *professional chemical peel agent* (acne, melasma, mild photoaging) at 40–50% in-clinic — but that evidence is built on **aggressive, low-pH, dermatologist-applied peels with stinging, erythema and desquamation**, not on a leave-on cosmetic serum. For a brand selling a daily anti-aging *routine*, it is a **conditional / niche** ingredient at best: the human data are decent for the peel format, the molecule has a credible mechanism, but it is irritating, pungent (vapor inhalation is a genuine handling caution), and there is essentially **no controlled evidence for a low-strength leave-on pyruvic product** doing anything an AHA wouldn't do more pleasantly.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common name:** Pyruvic acid (a.k.a. 2-oxopropanoic acid, alpha-ketopropionic acid, acetylformic acid, pyroracemic acid)
- **INCI:** **Pyruvic Acid** (the salt **Sodium Pyruvate** and ester **Ethyl Pyruvate** are separate INCI entries / derivatives)
- **CAS:** 127-17-3 · **EINECS/EC:** 204-824-3 [10]
- **Molecular formula / weight:** C₃H₄O₃ · **88.06 g/mol** — a *very* small molecule, central to its claimed penetration depth [9]
- **Chemical class:** The **simplest alpha-keto acid** — it carries both a carboxylic acid group and a ketone group on adjacent carbons (CH₃–CO–COOH). This is what distinguishes it from the alpha-*hydroxy* acids (glycolic, lactic) and beta-hydroxy acids (salicylic). [9]
- **pKa:** ~**2.4–2.5** — a relatively *strong* organic acid (stronger/lower pKa than glycolic ~3.8 or lactic ~3.86, and stronger than salicylic ~2.97), but weaker than trichloroacetic acid (TCA). The low pKa + small size are why it penetrates fast and deep. [6][9]
- **Physical/chemistry notes:** A clear, yellow-tinged liquid with a **sharp, pungent, acetic/vinegar-like odor**; miscible with water. Endogenous to human metabolism — it is the **end product of glycolysis** and the entry substrate for the Krebs/TCA cycle, and under physiological conditions it is reversibly interconverted with **lactic acid** (via lactate dehydrogenase). On skin, pyruvic acid is largely **converted to lactic acid**, its corresponding AHA — part of why its downstream behaviour overlaps with AHAs. [6][9]
- **Key forms/derivatives relevant to cosmetics:**
  - **Pyruvic acid** itself — the peel agent (typically 40–70% in-clinic; lower in cosmetic leave-ons).
  - **Sodium pyruvate** — the neutralized salt; better tolerated, used as a humectant/antioxidant adjunct and in some cell-culture-adjacent claims. [7]
  - **Ethyl pyruvate** — a lipophilic ester with documented systemic anti-inflammatory activity (HMGB1/NF-κB inhibition); studied mostly in *internal* injury/inflammation models, **not** as a validated topical cosmetic. [7][8]

## 2. Class & mechanism (how it works at the skin/cell level)

Pyruvic acid is best understood as a **medium-potency chemical peel / exfoliant** that sits between AHAs and TCA in strength, with several secondary properties.

Mechanistic pillars (as described in the dermatology peel literature):

1. **Keratolysis / corneocyte detachment.** Like AHAs, it reduces corneocyte cohesion in the stratum corneum, causing keratinocyte detachment and thinning of the upper epidermis → smoother surface, desquamation. Its small size + low pKa let it act fast (epidermolysis reportedly achievable in under a minute at peel strength). [6][9]
2. **Penetration to the upper papillary dermis → dermal remodeling.** At peel concentrations it reaches the upper papillary dermis and is described as stimulating **new collagen, elastic fibre and dermal glycoprotein (GAG) synthesis** — a controlled-wounding / repair response. Review sources state this dermal stimulation is *greater than glycolic acid* and *comparable to TCA*, but with less discomfort and only mild erythema. (Caveat: this is the standard narrative repeated across reviews; rigorous side-by-side *human histology* quantifying it is thin — see §4.) [3][6][9]
3. **Conversion to lactic acid.** On/in skin it is converted to lactic acid (an AHA + humectant), which contributes mild dermal edema/hydration and ongoing AHA-type activity. [9]
4. **Sebostatic effect.** It reduces sebum/oiliness — repeatedly observed in acne peel studies (see §4). Mechanism is not fully defined but is consistent and clinically useful. [1][5][6]
5. **Antimicrobial / anti-acne.** Reported antibacterial activity (relevant to *C. acnes*), supporting its acne indication. [6]
6. **Antioxidant / ROS scavenging (chemistry-level).** As an alpha-keto acid, pyruvate reacts **non-enzymatically and rapidly with hydrogen peroxide** (decarboxylation to acetate + water + CO₂) and shows measurable scavenging of other ROS in vitro. It is also a "NADH trap" / redox modulator in cell-protection literature. This is *real chemistry*, but demonstrated in test tubes and non-skin cell/injury models, **not** in human skin from a topical dose. [4][13]

**Skin-relevant translation (honest):** Items 1, 3, 4, 5 are well-supported for the **peel format in humans**. Item 2 (dermal collagen/elastin neogenesis) is the load-bearing "anti-aging" claim and is **plausible and supported by clinical surface improvements**, but rests largely on review-level assertions and surface/instrumental endpoints rather than robust controlled dermal histology. Item 6 (antioxidant) is genuine chemistry but is **borrowed from in-vitro / non-skin systems** and should not be sold as a proven topical antioxidant benefit.

## 4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

> **The single most important honesty point:** Pyruvic acid's human evidence is **real but format-specific.** It is for **40–50% (and 35%) in-clinic CHEMICAL PEELS**, mostly in **acne / melasma**, with **photoaging** evidence that is genuine but **uncontrolled and surface-level**. There is **no controlled clinical evidence** that a low-concentration *leave-on cosmetic* pyruvic product delivers the dermal-remodeling, anti-aging benefits attributed to the peel. The antioxidant/anti-inflammatory mechanisms are largely **in-vitro or from derivatives** (ethyl/sodium pyruvate), not from topical pyruvic acid on human skin.

### Tier 1 — In vitro (chemistry & cell models)

- **Kładna A, Marchlewicz M, Piechowska T, Kruk I, Aboul-Enein HY (2015), *Luminescence* 30(7):1153–1158. DOI 10.1002/bio.2879.** Pyruvic acid, sodium pyruvate, and ethyl pyruvate scavenged ROS in chemiluminescence/spectrophotometric assays. Pyruvic acid was the **most effective vs H₂O₂ (IC₅₀ ≈ 1.71 mM)** but **weak vs superoxide (IC₅₀ ≈ 69.2 mM)**; ethyl pyruvate was far more potent against superoxide. *Limitation: pure chemistry/cell-free + isolated systems; nothing about human skin or topical delivery; pyruvate is a much weaker general antioxidant than its H₂O₂ specialism implies.* [4]
- **Ethyl pyruvate anti-inflammatory mechanism (multiple, e.g. Fink 2007 *J Intern Med*; reviews in *J Inflamm* 2016, PMC8742706 2022).** Ethyl pyruvate suppresses NF-κB signaling and HMGB1 release. *Limitation: this is the **ester derivative**, studied in sepsis/organ-injury/systemic models — NOT topical pyruvic acid, NOT skin aging; do not transfer these claims to a pyruvic-acid serum.* [7][8]

### Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

- **No directly relevant published animal study of *topical pyruvic acid on skin aging* was identified.** Pyruvate/ethyl pyruvate animal work is for internal injury/inflammation (reperfusion, sepsis, rhinosinusitis), not cutaneous aging. *This is a genuine evidence gap: pyruvic acid largely skipped the animal-skin tier and is known almost entirely from human peel practice.* [7]

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL

- **Not applicable / not relevant.** Pyruvate is consumed as a sports/metabolic oral supplement, but there is **no oral-pyruvate skin-aging evidence**, and oral data do not inform topical use. (Noted only to close the tier honestly.)

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters — and here it's the *peel*, not a serum)

**Photoaging (the anti-aging headline):**
- **Ghersetich I, Brazzini B, Peris K, Cotellessa C, Manunta T, Lotti T (2004). "Pyruvic Acid Peels for the Treatment of Photoaging." *Dermatol Surg* 30(1):32–36. DOI 10.1111/j.1524-4725.2004.30002.x.** **n = 20** moderately photodamaged faces; **50% pyruvic acid**, **4 sessions at 4-week intervals.** Results: smoother texture, **fewer/less-evident fine wrinkles**, **lightening of lentigines/freckles**; erythema & pigment measured objectively by tri-stimulus colorimeter. Concluded safe/effective for moderate facial aging. *Limitations: small (n=20), **no control/placebo arm, not randomized or blinded**, single center, short follow-up, outcomes largely clinical/surface.* This is the foundational "anti-aging" citation and it is **low-tier (uncontrolled case series).* [3]
- **Berardesca E, Cameli N, Primavera G, Carrera M (2006). "Clinical and Instrumental Evaluation of Skin Improvement after Treatment with a New 50% Pyruvic Acid Peel." *Dermatol Surg* 32(4):526–531. DOI 10.1111/j.1524-4725.2006.32106.x.** **n = 20** (photodamage, superficial scarring, melasma); **50% pyruvic acid** in a buffered/solvent vehicle, **4 sessions every 2 weeks.** Instrumental gains: **significant ↑ skin elasticity**, **↓ wrinkling**, **significant ↓ pigmentation in melasma**; reported **no persistent erythema, no PIH, minimal discomfort** with the new formulation. *Limitations: small, **uncontrolled**, mixed indications, single center; the "new formulation"/vehicle is a confounder (tolerability gains may be vehicle-driven).* [2]

**Melasma / pigmentation (best-controlled anti-aging-adjacent data):**
- **Goyal S, Brar BK, Kumar S, et al. (2025). "Comparative Evaluation… 90% Lactic Acid Peel Versus 40% Pyruvic Acid Peel in Epidermal Melasma." *Indian Dermatol Online J* 16(4):564–570. DOI 10.4103/idoj.idoj_452_24.** **Randomized, n = 60** (30/arm). MASI reduction at 24 wks: pyruvic 40% **52.07%** (9.65→4.53) vs lactic 90% **55.44%** (10.42→4.59) — **no significant difference**; "good improvement" 53.3% vs 63.3%. Burning in 10% (pyruvic) vs 13.3% (lactic); 1 PIH case (lactic only). *Strength: randomized, comparative. Limitation: small per-arm, no untreated control, single center (India), epidermal melasma only.* Read: pyruvic ≈ a strong lactic peel — **not superior**. [11]

**Acne (the largest, best human evidence base — but acne, not aging):**
- **Chilicka K, Rogowska AM, Szyguła R, et al. (2020). "A comparison of the effectiveness of azelaic and pyruvic acid peels in the treatment of female adult acne: a randomized controlled trial." *Sci Rep* 10:12612. DOI 10.1038/s41598-020-69530-w.** **RCT, n = 120** women (60/arm), **50% pyruvic acid (pH 0.8)**, 6 sessions/2-wk intervals. Both arms ↓ acne ~40% (SHVSS pyruvic 2.60→1.47); **pyruvic reduced oiliness more (~19% vs ~13%)** though small effect size. Notes pyruvic "causes intense stinging/burning" and "pungent, irritating vapors." *Strength: RCT, decent n. Limitation: women-only, no placebo, acne outcome.* [1]
- **Jaffary F, Faghihi G, Saraeian S, Hosseini SM (2016). "Comparison the effectiveness of pyruvic acid 50% and salicylic acid 30% in the treatment of acne." *J Res Med Sci* 21:31. DOI 10.4103/1735-1995.181991.** Single-blind RCT; **86 enrolled, 47 completed.** Both significantly ↓ comedones/papules (P<0.001); **no difference between agents.** **Burning in >85% of both groups**; pyruvic had more scaling at session 5 (P=0.015). *Strength: randomized, comparative. Limitation: high dropout from frequent painful sessions (the authors' headline limitation).* [14]
- **Keisham C, Yumnam LS (2022). "Efficacy and tolerability of 40% pyruvic acid peel on acne vulgaris, post acne scar and hyperpigmentation in Indians." *J Cosmet Dermatol* 21(10):4405–4409. DOI 10.1111/jocd.14974.** **n = 30**, **40% pyruvic acid**, peels every 2 wks to 12 wks. Significant ↓ comedones/papules and **↓ hyperpigmentation**; **acne scars did NOT improve significantly.** *Limitation: small, uncontrolled. Useful honesty point: scarring did not respond.* [15]
- **Rusztowicz M, Chilicka K, Szyguła R, et al. (2022). "A Split-Face Comparative Study… 40% Pyruvic Acid vs. Microdermabrasion with 40% Pyruvic Acid on Biomechanical Skin Parameters in Acne Vulgaris." *J Clin Med* 11(20):6079. DOI 10.3390/jcm11206079.** **Split-face, n = 14** women; measured **hydration (Corneometer), sebum (Sebumeter), thermography**. Pyruvic-only side: significant **sebum ↓** on cheek (P<0.01) but **hydration changes not significant**; combo side did better. *Limitation: tiny (n=14), women-only, acne population; relevant because it shows pyruvic-only barely moved hydration.* [16]
- **Jankowska B, Zujko ME (2023). "The Effectiveness of Pyruvic Acid Peeling in Improving the Quality of Life of Patients with Acne Vulgaris." *J Clin Med* 12(10):3592. DOI 10.3390/jcm12103592.** **n = 200 enrolled / 158 completed**, **35% pyruvic acid (pH 1.3)**, 4 sessions/weekly. Significant QoL gains (DLQI 9.16→5.12, P<0.001; CADI, SWLS improved). *Strength: largest cohort. Limitation: **no control group**, 21% dropout, predominantly female, QoL not skin-structure endpoint.* [5]

**Bottom line by tier:** Solid **human RCT/clinical evidence exists — for 35–50% in-clinic PEELS in acne and melasma**, plus **uncontrolled** clinical photoaging data. The "stimulates collagen/elastin" anti-aging claim is **mechanistically plausible and clinically suggestive but not proven by controlled dermal histology.** Antioxidant/anti-inflammatory claims are **in-vitro / derivative-based.** There is **no controlled evidence for a leave-on cosmetic pyruvic serum** as an anti-aging active.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **In favor:** Tiny (88 g/mol) and water-miscible with a **low pKa (~2.4)** → at peel strength it penetrates rapidly and reportedly reaches the **upper papillary dermis**, deeper/faster than glycolic acid at comparable concentration. This depth is the mechanistic basis for its dermal-remodeling claims. [6][9]
- **The catch — penetration is concentration- and pH-driven:** The published *depth* and *dermal stimulation* are tied to **40–70% concentrations at very low pH (≈0.8–1.3)** under timed professional application. At the much lower concentrations and higher (skin-friendlier) pH needed for a safe leave-on cosmetic, **far less free acid penetrates**, and there are **no penetration or efficacy data** establishing that a leave-on pyruvic product reaches or remodels the dermis.
- **Fast metabolism caveat:** Once in skin, pyruvate is rapidly converted to lactate / enters metabolism, so the "pyruvate-specific" residence time is short — much of the downstream effect may simply be **AHA (lactic-acid) activity**.
- **Practical read:** Penetration is **proven for the peel format, unproven and likely modest for a cosmetic leave-on.** Do not market deep-dermal penetration for a low-strength product.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:** Established **clinical peel** range **40–70%** (commonly 40–50%; 35% used for milder protocols). pH at these strengths is **~0.8–1.5**. **No validated leave-on cosmetic concentration for anti-aging exists** — at-home/OTC products use undisclosed low levels with no efficacy data. If used in a cosmetic, treat it as a **rinse-off/short-contact exfoliant** at modest strength, not a leave-on hero. [1][2][3][5]
- **pH:** Peels work at very low pH (high free-acid fraction). A cosmetic must respect regional AHA-type pH/labeling norms; partial neutralization (toward pH ~3.5–4) improves tolerability but reduces activity. Because it converts to lactic acid, much of its cosmetic behaviour mirrors a **lactic-acid product**.
- **Stability:** Reactive ketone/acid; can darken and carries a **strong pungent vinegar-like odor** that is hard to mask — a real sensory/QC problem in consumer products. The H₂O₂-scavenging reactivity also means it can interfere with peroxide-based systems. Requires stability + odor testing.
- **Pairings (conceptual):** Conceptually overlaps with AHAs (it *becomes* lactic acid) — pairing with another strong acid stacks irritation; avoid. Could be buffered with humectants (glycerin, the lactic-acid it generates) to soften. Often co-marketed with **lactic acid** (e.g., "pyruvic 40% + lactic 10%" pro peels). [6]
- **Conflicts:** Stacking with retinoids, benzoyl peroxide, other AHAs/BHAs, or vitamin C ↑ irritation; not for same-routine layering. Vapor reactivity means caution near peroxide actives.
- **Sensory:** **Pungent, eye/airway-irritating vapor** at peel strength (studies explicitly note "pungent and irritating vapors" and "intense stinging/burning") — a meaningful negative for both clinic UX and consumer leave-on. [1] Diluted cosmetic levels are milder but the odor signature persists.

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation:** **High at peel strength** — stinging/burning is near-universal (>85% of patients in one RCT), with erythema, desquamation, occasional crusting on thin/inflamed skin. Newer buffered vehicles reduce this (Berardesca reported minimal discomfort), but irritation is the defining tolerability issue. [1][2][14]
- **Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH):** Possible, especially in darker skin types (Fitzpatrick IV–VI) and with aggressive protocols — though the melasma RCT reported low PIH rates with 40%. Counsel patch-testing and sun avoidance. [11][15]
- **Sun sensitivity:** As an exfoliant/AHA-equivalent, it increases UV sensitivity of freshly exfoliated skin → **daily SPF is mandatory** post-use; this is the standard AHA photo-caution. [9]
- **Vapor / respiratory caution (distinctive to pyruvic acid):** The pungent vapors can irritate eyes and airways — clinics are advised to ensure **ventilation / avoid inhalation**; a non-trivial handling caution that AHAs/BHAs don't share to the same degree. [1]
- **Pregnancy/breastfeeding:** **No safety data**; chemical peels are generally a **relative contraindication** in pregnancy. Default to "avoid / consult your doctor." [conservative position; general peel guidance]
- **Sensitization:** No notable allergic-sensitization signal in the literature reviewed; primary concern is **irritant** (not allergic) reaction. Endogenous metabolite, so true allergy is unlikely. [unverified — no dedicated HRIPT located]
- **Sourcing/manufacture:** Produced industrially (e.g., from tartaric acid or via fermentation/biotech routes); widely available as a commodity chemical.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (cosmetic vs drug — per CLAUDE.md)

- **Status:** In the EU/US, pyruvic acid is an **accepted cosmetic ingredient** (INCI "Pyruvic Acid," EC 204-824-3). At **professional-peel strengths (40–70%)** the procedure is typically performed by clinicians; in several markets high-strength peels sit in a **professional-use / quasi-medical** category, and at-home cosmetic products use much lower levels. [10]
- **Claims discipline (critical):**
  - ⚠️ **"Stimulates/boosts collagen and elastin," "remodels the dermis," "anti-aging," "rebuilds skin"** — these are the textbook **drug-adjacent structure/function claims** flagged in CLAUDE.md and the glossary. The supporting human data are **uncontrolled peel studies**, not controlled trials of a sold cosmetic — **do not make these claims for a leave-on product.**
  - ⚠️ **"Treats acne / clears acne / antibacterial treatment"** — acne is largely a **drug claim** (e.g., US FDA OTC acne monograph governs anti-acne *drug* actives like salicylic acid/benzoyl peroxide; pyruvic acid is **not** a monograph acne drug). Avoid therapeutic acne language for a cosmetic.
  - ✅ **Safer cosmetic language:** "exfoliates," "helps refine the look of pores and texture," "helps improve the appearance of uneven tone," "for visibly smoother skin," "resurfacing." Keep to *appearance* verbs.
  - **Antioxidant claims:** only "in-vitro" / "demonstrated in laboratory studies" framing — there is **no human topical-skin antioxidant proof**.
- All efficacy/anti-aging copy would need **substantiation and legal review** before use.

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Cost tier:** **Low** — pyruvic acid is a commodity industrial/biochemical (sold by Sigma-Aldrich, Spectrum, Alfa Chemistry, etc.); raw-material cost is not a barrier. [10]
- **Supply:** Widely available; biotech/fermentation and chemical-synthesis routes both exist; no scarcity or ethical-sourcing red flags identified.
- **The real "cost" is formulation & UX, not raw material:** odor masking, stability, irritation management, and (if sold as a peel) regulatory/professional-channel considerations are where the expense and risk sit.

## 10. Hype vs evidence

| Marketing claim (seen in the wild) | Reality |
|---|---|
| "Stimulates collagen, elastin AND hyaluronic acid" | Mechanistically plausible at *peel* strength; the HA part is largely vendor copy. **Not** proven for cosmetic leave-on; anti-aging data are **uncontrolled peel case series**. [3][6] |
| "Penetrates deep to remodel the dermis" | True *only* at 40–70% / very low pH professionally applied. A low-strength serum almost certainly does not. [6][9] |
| "Powerful antioxidant for your skin" | Pyruvate scavenges H₂O₂ **in a test tube**; **no** human topical-skin antioxidant evidence. [4][13] |
| "Gentle exfoliant for sensitive skin" | At peel strength it is one of the **more irritating, pungent** acids (>85% burning). Diluted versions are gentler but then less is proven. [1][14] |
| "One ingredient for acne *and* aging" | Decent **acne** RCT evidence; **aging** evidence is weaker (uncontrolled). It does **not** improve acne *scars*. [1][14][15] |
| "Anti-inflammatory skin soother" | That's **ethyl pyruvate** in *systemic* models — not topical pyruvic acid on skin. [7][8] |

**Net:** Pyruvic acid is **legitimately useful as a professional peel** with real (if mostly uncontrolled-for-aging, RCT-for-acne) human data. The consumer/anti-aging hype outruns the evidence, and the antioxidant/anti-inflammatory glow-up borrows from derivatives and in-vitro work.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**⚠️ CONDITIONAL — niche peel / professional-channel only; SKIP as a daily leave-on anti-aging hero.**

**Reasoning:**
- The brand's thesis is a **lean, premium, barrier-repair-led daily routine** with **claims discipline**. Pyruvic acid is the opposite profile: an **irritating, pungent, low-pH peel agent** whose proven benefits require **aggressive professional application**, whose anti-aging evidence is **uncontrolled**, and whose best-evidenced use (acne) skews toward **drug-claim territory** and away from "barrier repair."
- For the durable barrier-repair / longevity positioning, **gentler, better-evidenced, better-tolerated actives win** (lactic acid for an AHA — which pyruvic literally converts into; PHAs/gluconolactone for sensitive exfoliation; retinoids/niacinamide/ceramides for the core repair story). Pyruvic offers **no proven advantage** over lactic acid for tone/texture and is harder to formulate and sell.
- It is **not a "skip-forever"**: if the brand ever adds a **professional or clinical-strength resurfacing peel** (a separate product line / spa channel), pyruvic acid is a credible, well-documented choice for **acne/oily skin, mild photoaging, and epidermal melasma**, ideally co-formulated with lactic acid and a buffered/low-odor vehicle. That is a **deliberate, gated decision**, not a casual inclusion.

**Longevity note:** Pyruvic acid's "longevity" framing (it's a Krebs-cycle metabolite, a redox/NADH modulator, an H₂O₂ scavenger) is **scientifically real but irrelevant to a topical cosmetic** — those roles are intracellular/metabolic and demonstrated systemically, not from a face product. As a skin-longevity ingredient it offers **no autophagy/senescence/mitochondrial benefit that survives the topical-translation test**; its only durable contribution is **controlled exfoliation/resurfacing**, which is a maintenance tool, not a hallmark-targeting geroprotector. Do not lean on the metabolic-longevity story in copy.

**Action:** Log under **considering → likely "not considering as a hero," candidate for a future professional-peel SKU only.** Default routine should reach for **lactic acid** instead.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Verified:** 2026-06-19. Method: independent re-check of every headline citation against PubMed/PMC/journal records and full text where accessible. Each claim was tested for (a) HUMAN + TOPICAL evidence vs. borrowed in-vitro/animal/derivative/oral data, (b) whether the cited study is real and says what the dossier claims, (c) funding/size red flags.

### Overall grade: **B− (for the in-clinic PEEL format only) → D (as a leave-on cosmetic anti-aging active)**

This split grade is the honest verdict. The dossier itself already argues exactly this, so verification *confirms* the document rather than overturning it.

- **B− — pyruvic acid as a professional 35–50% chemical peel** for acne, epidermal melasma, and (weakly) photoaging. There is genuine human + topical evidence including **two acne RCTs** (Chilicka 2020, n=120; Jaffary 2016, n=86/47) and **one melasma RCT** (Goyal 2025, n=60). It is held below B because: the RCTs are all **active-comparator, none placebo-controlled**; the photoaging evidence is **uncontrolled case series only**; per-arm sizes are modest; and several trials are single-center, women-only, and from one regional research cluster (Chilicka/Szyguła/Rusztowicz/Jankowska, Poland).
- **D — pyruvic acid as a leave-on cosmetic serum for anti-aging.** There is **zero controlled human evidence** that a low-strength, skin-friendly-pH leave-on does anything attributable to pyruvate (vs. the lactic acid it converts into). The "deep dermal remodeling / collagen neogenesis" story is **review-asserted and concentration/pH-bound to professional peels**, not demonstrated by controlled human histology even in the peel studies. This matches the dossier's own §4/§5/§11 conclusions.

### Funding / size / independence flags
- No industry-funded efficacy trial was identified as load-bearing; the studies are academic/clinical. Berardesca 2006 tests a **specific "new formulation" vehicle**, a confounder the dossier already flags.
- **Small and clustered:** every human study is ≤120 (most ≤30), single-center, and a large share come from one Polish acne-peel research group — a real generalizability limit, now noted above.
- **No placebo arm anywhere.** All RCTs are active-comparator (vs. azelaic, salicylic, lactic, microdermabrasion). "RCT" should not be read as placebo-controlled proof of an absolute effect.

### Caveats / claims to soften (all already handled or now edited)
1. The collagen/elastin/dermal-remodeling mechanism is **review-asserted, not proven by controlled human histology** — softened in the §3 hallmark table this pass.
2. Antioxidant and anti-inflammatory benefits are **in-vitro/derivative** and must never be presented as proven topical skin benefits (dossier already restricts these to "in-vitro/laboratory" framing — correct).
3. "Anti-aging" rests on **uncontrolled case series**; the only RCT-grade human topical data are for **acne and melasma**, not aging — keep this distinction in any copy.
4. There is **no controlled evidence for a leave-on cosmetic** at all — the entire anti-aging-serum framing is unproven (dossier's central thesis; verified correct).

**Net:** The dossier is accurate, well-cited, and already conservative — no citation was found to be fabricated, misquoted, or mis-tiered. The only adjustment needed was tightening the §3 collagen-loss row so the surface/instrumental endpoints aren't read as direct proof of collagen synthesis. Verdict stands: **legitimate professional peel (B−), unproven leave-on anti-aging active (D); for this brand, reach for lactic acid instead.**
