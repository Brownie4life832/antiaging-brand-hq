# Ingredient: Luteolin (INCI: **Luteolin**)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product
**Class:** Senotherapeutics & senolytics (dietary flavone; senomorphic / antioxidant / anti-glycation) · **Slug:** `luteolin`

> Exhaustive literature review. Brutally honest on evidence quality. Luteolin is a genuinely
> well-studied dietary flavone with strong **anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and anti-glycation**
> biology, and it earns its place on this brand's senotherapeutic shelf — but its "senolytic" label
> is **borrowed from review-paper lists** (where it appears next to fisetin and curcumin), NOT from
> robust skin senolysis data. The deepest *skin-specific* evidence is **in-vitro + animal-TOPICAL
> photoaging** work (UVB/UVA mouse and rat studies), which is actually a stronger topical-skin
> dataset than spermidine or fisetin can claim. But there is **no human topical RCT on luteolin
> itself**: the only human topical data uses **tetramethoxyluteolin**, a *methylated derivative*
> (a different molecule), in a tiny uncontrolled case series. Treat luteolin as a credible
> **supporting senomorphic/antioxidant** active, not a proven senolytic hero. Frame copy around
> "soothing / antioxidant / helps defend against environmental stress," never "clears senescent
> cells in your skin."

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common name:** Luteolin. Chemically **3′,4′,5,7-tetrahydroxyflavone** — a **flavone** (the flavone sub-class, same backbone as apigenin and chrysin; *not* a flavonol like fisetin/quercetin — the difference is the missing 3-OH). [1][14]
- **INCI:** **LUTEOLIN** (recognized cosmetic INCI; listed cosmetic functions include *antioxidant* and *skin conditioning*). Frequently delivered via standardized botanical extracts rather than as the purified molecule. [12]
- **CAS:** 491-70-3. **EINECS:** 207-741-1. **Molecular formula / weight:** C₁₅H₁₀O₆ · **286.24 g/mol**. **Appearance:** yellow crystalline solid, mp ~330 °C. [13][14]
- **LogP ≈ 2.4–2.5** (moderately lipophilic); **very poorly water-soluble** (insoluble in cold water, slightly soluble in hot water; soluble in alcohol/DMSO). Classified as a **BCS Class II** compound (low solubility, decent permeability) — this is the central formulation/bioavailability problem (see §5–§6). [13][11]
- **Natural sources:** widespread in plants — celery, parsley, thyme, oregano, peppermint, broccoli, carrot, onion leaves, sweet pepper, chamomile, perilla, artichoke. Commercial luteolin is most often extracted/purified from **peanut hulls/shells**, ***Reseda luteola*** (dyer's weld — the classic historical yellow dye "weld," from which the name derives), ***Sophora japonica***, or ***Terminalia chebula***. [14]
- **Key forms / derivatives (do not conflate with luteolin itself):**
  - **Free luteolin (aglycone)** — the form in nearly all skin studies and supplements.
  - **Luteolin-7-O-glucoside (cynaroside)** and other glycosides — the form actually present in many plants; more water-soluble but different PK; not the marketed actives.
  - **Tetramethoxyluteolin (methlut / 3′,4′,5,7-tetramethoxyflavone)** — a **synthetic methylated derivative** with better lipophilicity/stability and more potent mast-cell inhibition; this is the molecule in the only human *topical* report (GentleDerm®). **It is a different molecule — its data do not automatically transfer to luteolin.** [9]
  - **7-O-methylluteolin** — another methylated derivative studied topically in mice for atopic dermatitis. [10]
  - **Nano-formats** (microemulsion gels, nanostructured lipid carriers, elastic liposomes, nanosuspensions, lipid nanocapsules) developed specifically because raw luteolin penetrates skin poorly. [11]

## 2. Class & mechanism

Luteolin is a **multi-target dietary flavone**. The longevity field files it under **senotherapeutics**, but it is important to be precise about *which kind*:

- **Senolytic vs senomorphic — the honest framing.** Luteolin appears on the canonical "first-generation senolytics" list (alongside fisetin, curcumin, navitoclax, procyanidin C1) in major review papers from the Mayo Clinic senescence group. [2][15] But that listing traces back to **transcriptome-guided screens** (Zhu et al. 2015, *Aging Cell*) where luteolin was a *candidate* — it was **weakly or inconsistently senolytic** depending on cell type, far less potent than navitoclax or the D+Q/fisetin leads. In *skin* cells, the demonstrated effect is **senomorphic** (it quiets the senescent phenotype — ↓SA-β-gal, ↓p16/p21, ↓SASP — and *prevents* stress-induced senescence) rather than cleanly **senolytic** (selectively killing existing senescent cells). The brand should describe luteolin's senescence action as **senomorphic/senescence-preventive**, and reserve the "senolytic" word for fisetin/D+Q where the killing mechanism is better characterized. [2][3][6]

Mechanistic pillars (multi-target — luteolin is "dirty" pharmacology, hitting many nodes):

1. **Antioxidant — direct + Nrf2/ARE induction.** Luteolin directly scavenges ROS and, more importantly, activates the **Nrf2/HO-1/NQO1** antioxidant program, upregulating SOD and catalase and lowering MDA (lipid-peroxidation marker). This is its dominant antioxidant route — boosting the cell's own defenses. [3][8]
2. **Anti-inflammatory — NF-κB, MAPK, JAK/STAT, TLR suppression.** Luteolin inhibits NF-κB and the MAPK (p38/JNK/ERK)–AP-1 axis and JAK/STAT, lowering COX-2, IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α, NO. In aged tissue this also means **reduced SASP**. [2][3][7]
3. **Potent mast-cell stabilizer / anti-allergic.** Luteolin (and especially tetramethoxyluteolin) blocks IgE-mediated mast-cell activation and histamine/cytokine release — the basis of its atopic-dermatitis/itch and "soothing" story. [9][10]
4. **Anti-photoaging kinase targeting.** Luteolin **directly binds and inhibits JNK1 (ATP-competitive) and p90RSK2 (ATP-independent)**, suppressing UVB→AP-1→**MMP-1/MMP-13** induction; it also restores **SIRT3** under UVB, dampening the ROS/MAPK cascade. Net: less collagenase, more collagen. [4][5]
5. **SIRT1 activation / anti-senescence.** Luteolin upregulates **SIRT1** and lowers **p53/p21**, protecting cells from oxidative-stress–induced senescence. [3][6]
6. **Anti-glycation.** Luteolin inhibits AGE formation across all three glycation stages (early HbA1c, middle methylglyoxal-adduct, late cross-linking), reportedly out-performing aminoguanidine in the early stage in vitro. [16][17]

**Net cell-level story (as claimed):** a standing Nrf2 antioxidant shield + broad anti-inflammatory/SASP damping + UV-kinase blockade that spares collagen + anti-glycation protection of the dermal matrix. Most of this chain is demonstrated in **cells, mouse, and rat**, not in human topical skin (§4).

## 4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

> **The single most important honesty point:** Luteolin actually has a **better animal-topical skin dataset** than most senotherapeutics in this collection — multiple independent UVB/UVA studies applied luteolin *to the skin* and measured wrinkles, MMPs and collagen. **But:** (a) there is **no human topical RCT on luteolin itself**; (b) the only human *topical* clinical report uses **tetramethoxyluteolin**, a *different (methylated) molecule*, in a tiny uncontrolled case series; and (c) the "senolytic" reputation is **class-list membership**, not demonstrated skin senolysis. So topical claims rest on in-vitro + animal + a derivative — strong mechanism, no finished-product human proof.

### Tier 1 — In vitro (cell & biochemical models)

- **Yan et al., 2025, *Int. J. Mol. Sci.* 26(5):1809 (DOI 10.3390/ijms26051809) — in-vitro arm.** NIH-3T3 fibroblasts, UVA 10 J/cm²; luteolin 1/2/4 µM pretreatment **dose-dependently ↓SA-β-gal, ↓p16/p21, ↓IL-1β/IL-6 (SASP), restored collagen I & IV, ↓ROS/MDA, ↑SOD/CAT, ↑Nrf2/HO-1/NQO1.** Network pharmacology flagged 143 shared targets; oxidative-stress pathway central; luteolin out-performed NAC *in this single in-vitro comparison*. *Limitation: mouse fibroblast line (NIH-3T3), single UVA dose, low-µM culture doses; "outperformed NAC" is one cell assay, not a benchmarked claim; senomorphic not senolytic.* [3]
- **Lim et al., 2013, *J. Cell. Mol. Med.* 17(5):672–680 (DOI 10.1111/jcmm.12050) — in-vitro arm.** HaCaT keratinocytes; luteolin 5–10 µM **↓UVB-induced MMP-1 and AP-1 transactivation**; in-vitro kinase + pull-down assays showed luteolin **directly binds JNK1 (ATP-competitive) and p90RSK2 (ATP-independent)** and inhibits their activity — a clean target-engagement mechanism. *Limitation: cell line; mechanism study.* [4]
- **Mu et al., 2021, *Front. Pharmacol.* 12:728261 (DOI 10.3389/fphar.2021.728261) — in-vitro arm.** Human dermal fibroblasts, UVB 20 mJ/cm²; luteolin 10/20/40 µM ↓ROS, ↓p-p38/p-JNK, ↓MMP-1, ↑collagen I, **restored SIRT3** (SIRT3 inhibitor 3-TYP abolished the protection — proving SIRT3-dependence). *Limitation: cell model.* [5]
- **Zhu RZ et al., 2021, *Korean J. Physiol. Pharmacol.* 25(4):297–305 (DOI 10.4196/kjpp.2021.25.4.297).** H₂O₂-induced senescence model (HEI-OC1 cells — an *auditory* cell line, **not skin**): luteolin ↓SA-β-gal, ↓DNA damage, ↓p53/p21 and **↑SIRT1** dose/time-dependently. *Limitation: non-skin cell line; included for the SIRT1/anti-senescence mechanism only — must not be cited as skin data.* [6]
- **Anti-glycation biochemistry — multiple, e.g. flavonoid AGE-inhibition screens.** Luteolin inhibits BSA glycation and methylglyoxal-mediated protein modification (reported IC₅₀ ~66 µM mid-stage) and late-stage AGE cross-linking; inhibits early HbA1c formation more effectively than aminoguanidine in vitro; binds BSA more strongly than apigenin (extra A-ring OH). *Limitation: protein-in-tube models, not skin.* [16][17]
- **Anti-melanogenesis — α-MSH-stimulated B16 melanoma.** Luteolin ↓melanin via cAMP-pathway suppression; ~37% tyrosinase-activity reduction at 25 µM with mixed evidence on *direct* vs indirect tyrosinase inhibition. *Limitation: murine melanoma line; no human pigmentation data.* [18]
- **Senolytic screening origin — Zhu et al., 2015, *Aging Cell* 14(4):644–658 (DOI 10.1111/acel.12344).** The transcriptome-to-senolytic framework that seeded luteolin's inclusion on senolytic lists; luteolin was a candidate, not a lead, with cell-type-dependent, modest activity. *Limitation: not skin; basis for the "first-generation senolytic" *label* but not strong skin senolysis evidence.* [15]

### Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo, **including genuine topical application**)

- **Lim et al., 2013 (mouse arm) — *J. Cell. Mol. Med.***. **SKH-1 hairless mice**, **topical** luteolin (10 or 40 nmol in 200 µL acetone on dorsal skin) before UVB (0.18 J/cm², 3×/wk) for **15 weeks**: significantly **↓wrinkle formation and ↓MMP-13** vs UVB control. *Limitation: only **n = 3 per group**, chronic-UVB photoaging (not intrinsic aging), murine; small.* [4]
- **Mu et al., 2021 (in-vivo arm) — *Front. Pharmacol.***. **Sprague-Dawley rats**, **topical** luteolin 60 or 120 mg/kg/day (in ethanol:propylene glycol 3:7) before UVB (300 mJ/cm², every other day) for **1 month**: ↓erythema/wrinkles, restored epidermal thickness, ↓MMP-1, ↑collagen I, ↓ROS, **restored SIRT3**, ↓p-p38/p-JNK. *Limitation: n = 8/group, rat, acute-ish chronic UVB; high topical doses; vehicle is irritating/penetration-enhancing.* [5]
- **Yan et al., 2025 (in-vivo arm) — *Int. J. Mol. Sci.***. **BALB/c mice (n = 6/group)**, UVA 10 J/cm²/day ×10 days; **topical** luteolin 50 or 100 nmol pretreatment ↓photoaging, ↓senescence markers, preserved dermal collagen (Masson). *Limitation: small n, short course, acute UVA.* [3]
- **Atopic-dermatitis models — luteolin & 7-O-methylluteolin (topical).** Luteolin alleviated AD-like lesions (↓SCORAD-equivalent, ↓mast-cell infiltration, restored barrier) and ↓dry-skin scratching via IgE/mast-cell inhibition in mice; topical **7-O-methylluteolin** ↑antioxidant proteins, ↑epidermal thickness, ↓mast cells in DNCB-induced AD mice. *Limitation: disease (AD) models, not aging; one uses a derivative.* [7][10]

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL (real but thin, and not about skin)

- **Schizophrenia adjunct RCT.** A double-blind, placebo-controlled RCT tested **luteolin 300 mg/day for 12 weeks** (in a flavonoid combination) — included here only as evidence luteolin is **orally tolerated** at supplement doses. *Not skin; combination product.* [19]
- **Pediatric inflammation (luteolin-containing formulation).** Children supplemented with a luteolin-containing formulation showed ↓IL-6 and ↓TNF-α serum levels — supportive of systemic anti-inflammatory action. *Combination/observational; not skin.* [19]
- **General safety/tolerability.** Reviews report oral luteolin **LD₅₀ > 5,000 mg/kg** in rodents and good tolerability in available human trials, while explicitly noting **human clinical data remain scarce** and call for more toxicology. *Oral; supportive safety context only.* [19]

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters — and it is a **derivative**, not luteolin)

- **Theoharides, Stewart & Tsilioni, 2017, *Int. J. Immunopathol. Pharmacol.* 30(2):146–151 (DOI 10.1177/0394632017707610; PMID 28480804).** A **tetramethoxyluteolin**-containing lotion (with olive-fruit extract; trademarked **GentleDerm®**) was (1) tested for **tolerability in 25 patients** with mastocytosis / mast-cell activation syndrome and very sensitive skin (questionnaire), then (2) used by **8 patients (4 atopic dermatitis, 4 psoriasis)** twice daily for **2 weeks**, with reported "significant improvement" of lesions.
  - **Critical caveats:** (a) the active is **tetramethoxyluteolin — a methylated derivative, NOT luteolin** (different solubility, potency, PK); (b) **uncontrolled, open-label, n = 8 efficacy, questionnaire/photographic** assessment, 2 weeks; (c) disease (AD/psoriasis), **not aging**; (d) the lead author is associated with the product. This is the **only** human topical clinical report in luteolin's orbit and it **does not validate a luteolin anti-aging serum**. [9]
- **No human topical RCT on luteolin itself for any indication, and none for skin aging, as of June 2026.** [verified by absence in searches]

**Bottom line by tier:** Proven for luteolin specifically → broad anti-inflammatory/antioxidant biology (cells), anti-glycation (in tube), and **photoprotection / anti-photoaging when applied topically to rodent skin** (a real strength). **NOT proven for human topical skin** — the only human topical data is a tiny uncontrolled series using a *different molecule* (tetramethoxyluteolin). "Senolytic for your skin" is unsupported; the honest skin mechanism is **senomorphic + antioxidant + anti-photoaging**.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **The core problem:** Luteolin is **BCS Class II** — **very poorly water-soluble** (essentially insoluble in cold water) with moderate logP (~2.4–2.5). Pure luteolin therefore shows **low penetration/permeation** into and through skin and tends to stay where it's deposited rather than distribute. [11][13]
- **Why the animal studies still "worked":** they used **penetration-enhancing/irritating vehicles** (acetone; ethanol:propylene glycol 3:7) and applied high doses to rodent skin — not representative of a finished cosmetic on human skin. This is a real translation gap. [4][5]
- **Delivery systems are the active research front (in-vitro/animal):**
  - **Nanostructured lipid carriers (NLCs):** ~3.4× higher luteolin deposition in stratum corneum/deeper layers vs suspension. [11]
  - **Microemulsion gels:** ~2.09× ex-vivo permeation, ~2.72× skin retention. [11]
  - **Elastic liposomes / nanovesicles / nanosuspensions / lipid nanocapsules:** consistently improve permeation flux and retention across rat/ex-vivo skin. [11]
  - **Methylated derivatives** (tetramethoxyluteolin, 7-O-methylluteolin): more lipophilic/stable — designed partly to improve skin delivery; this is why the *derivative* reached a human topical product first. [9][10]
- **Practical read:** A plain luteolin dispersion is unlikely to deliver meaningful actives to viable epidermis/dermis. **If we use luteolin topically, it should be solubilized/encapsulated** (lipid carrier, microemulsion, or a pre-solubilized cosmetic raw material). Penetration in a real human formula is *plausible with the right delivery system, unproven without one.* No human transdermal/penetration quantification for luteolin in a cosmetic exists.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:** **No established human topical efficacy concentration.** In-vitro actives were 1–40 µM; animal-topical doses were 10–100 nmol (mouse) and 60–120 mg/kg/day (rat) — *not* formulation guidance. Cosmetic use is typically **low (often fractional-percent), via solubilized/encapsulated raws or standardized botanical extracts**. Formulate conservatively; do **not** imply a validated dose-response.
- **pH:** Luteolin's phenolic -OH groups mean antioxidant performance and color are pH-sensitive; flavones are generally more stable in **mildly acidic** systems. Keep to a skin-friendly **pH ~4.5–6**. No special pH-driven incompatibility, but it is not freely water-soluble so pH alone won't dissolve it.
- **Stability:** As a polyphenol it is **oxidation- and light-sensitive** and can **discolor** (it is literally a historical **yellow dye** — "weld"). Expect/manage a **yellow tint** in the finished product; protect with antioxidants (e.g., tocopherol), chelators, opaque/airless packaging, and run real-time + accelerated stability. Encapsulation also improves storage stability. [11][14]
- **Solubilization:** The make-or-break step. Needs a real solubilizer — lipid carrier, microemulsion, glycols/surfactant system, or a vendor pre-solubilized active. Dumping powder into water will not work.
- **Pairings (conceptual):** niacinamide (barrier/anti-inflammatory + B3 NAD story); other antioxidants/flavonoids (vitamin C/E + ferulic for a network-antioxidant story); ceramides/barrier lipids (the brand's repair thesis); SPF by day (its evidence base is *photoprotection*, so it lives naturally in a daytime antioxidant-defense narrative). Conceptually complements **senolytic** fisetin (luteolin = senomorphic/soothing, fisetin = senolytic) for a layered senescence story — but that's a *narrative* pairing, not a tested combination.
- **Conflicts:** Strong oxidizers and high-pH systems risk accelerating discoloration/degradation. Watch chelation/interaction with metal ions (flavones bind metals — can be stabilizing *or* destabilizing depending on system).
- **Sensory:** Yellow color is the main sensory liability (can tint product and potentially skin/fabrics at higher loads); negligible odor at cosmetic levels.

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation/sensitization:** No notable topical irritation/sensitization signal at cosmetic levels; in mast-cell/AD models luteolin is **anti-irritant/soothing**, and the tetramethoxyluteolin lotion was specifically tolerated by **"very sensitive skin"** patients. **Caveat:** that human tolerability is for a *derivative*; the rodent efficacy used irritating vehicles (acetone/PG), so tolerability is vehicle-dependent. No published human HRIPT/patch data on a luteolin cosmetic — generate our own. [9]
- **Sun sensitivity:** No photosensitivity flag; mechanistically luteolin is **photoprotective** (anti-UVB/UVA in models). Still, antioxidants are adjuncts to — not replacements for — SPF. [3][4][5]
- **Pregnancy/breastfeeding:** No topical pregnancy data. As with all senotherapeutic/flavonoid actives, default to conservative copy ("consult your doctor"). Some flavones have phytoestrogenic/estrogen-modulating activity in vitro — not established as relevant to topical cosmetic dosing, but a reason not to over-promise.
- **Staining:** As a yellow dye, high loads could transfer color to skin/fabric — a formulation/finished-product caution more than a health one. [14]
- **General tolerability (oral, supportive context):** rodent LD₅₀ > 5,000 mg/kg; orally tolerated in available human trials at 300 mg/day; reviews still call human safety data **scarce** and ask for more toxicology. [19]
- **Sourcing/allergen:** If sourced from **peanut hulls**, flag for **peanut-allergy-aware** customers (residual-protein risk is low after purification but relevant to labeling/trust); *Reseda*/*Sophora*/synthetic-grade avoid that messaging. Verify allergen-free COA.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Cosmetic ingredient status:** Recognized INCI **Luteolin** (CAS 491-70-3); cited cosmetic functions antioxidant / skin-conditioning; used in cosmetics and supplements. Confirm legality and any concentration limits per target market before formulating. [12][13]
- **The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame):** "Senolytic," "clears/removes senescent (zombie) cells," "anti-aging," "boosts collagen," "treats eczema/psoriasis/dermatitis," "anti-inflammatory" (as a therapeutic claim) all flirt with or cross into **structure/function or drug claims**. The biggest traps here are (a) importing the **senolytic** longevity narrative, and (b) importing the **atopic-dermatitis/psoriasis** (disease) results from the tetramethoxyluteolin lotion.
- ✅ **Safer cosmetic language:** "antioxidant plant flavone," "helps defend the look of skin against environmental/oxidative stress," "helps calm the look of stressed, reactive-feeling skin," "supports a more even-looking tone," "helps protect the appearance of skin firmness from sun-related stress" (still hedge — animal data). "Inspired by senescence / skin-longevity science" (narrative, not a senolytic claim).
- ⚠️ **Risky / substantiation-or-legal-review:** "senolytic skincare," "clears senescent cells," "reverses photoaging," "boosts collagen by X%," "treats eczema/psoriasis/atopic dermatitis," "anti-inflammatory" (therapeutic), "reduces wrinkles" (performance claim). None are supported for **luteolin in human topical skin**.
- **Substantiation reality:** To make even modest topical performance claims we'd want our **own** finished-formula human testing; the literature won't substantiate a luteolin anti-aging serum, and the one human topical study used a different molecule. See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Forms/suppliers:** Purified luteolin (typ. 90–98%+) widely available from fine-chemical and nutraceutical suppliers (Sigma-Aldrich; numerous Chinese manufacturers — e.g., extract houses producing from peanut hull, *Sophora japonica*, *Reseda*, *Terminalia chebula*). Cosmetic use is more often via **standardized botanical extracts** or **pre-solubilized/encapsulated cosmetic raws**.
- **Cost tier:** **Low–mid** as a raw nutraceutical powder (it's a commodity flavone). **Mid–higher** once you pay for cosmetic-grade purity, allergen-controlled sourcing, and especially the **delivery system** (NLC/microemulsion/liposome) it really needs to perform topically. Methylated derivatives (tetramethoxyluteolin) are specialty/higher cost.
- **Supply/standardization risk:** With botanical extracts, **luteolin content and impurity profile vary** — require a COA quantifying luteolin and (if peanut-derived) allergen testing. Color/oxidation control on incoming material matters.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

- **"Senolytic skincare that clears your skin's zombie cells."** Luteolin's senolytic reputation is **review-paper list membership** seeded by a 2015 transcriptome screen where it was a *modest, cell-type-dependent candidate* [15] — not demonstrated skin senolysis. In skin cells its action is **senomorphic** (prevents/quiets senescence), not selective killing. [2][3][6]
- **The derivative switcheroo.** The one human *topical* result everyone points to used **tetramethoxyluteolin** (a methylated, more potent, more lipophilic *different molecule*) in **8 uncontrolled patients** for a *disease* (AD/psoriasis) [9] — it does not validate a luteolin anti-aging cosmetic.
- **"Clinically proven anti-photoaging."** The anti-photoaging data are genuinely good *for animal-topical and in-vitro* (real wrinkle/MMP/collagen endpoints) [4][5][3] — but **n = 3–8 rodents** with **irritating penetration-enhancing vehicles**, no human topical RCT. "Clinically proven" overstates it.
- **The solubility blind spot.** Marketing rarely mentions that **plain luteolin barely penetrates skin** (BCS Class II) [11][13] — a "1% luteolin" claim without a delivery system may be largely cosmetic theater.
- **"Anti-inflammatory / treats eczema."** True biology, but a **drug claim** for a cosmetic; the human disease evidence is the derivative case series, not luteolin. [9]
- **Borrowed oral/disease halo.** Luteolin's broad therapeutic literature (neuro, pulmonary, metabolic) is **oral/systemic and mostly preclinical** [19] — it builds a credible *story*, not topical proof.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ **SUPPORTING — credible senomorphic/antioxidant/photoprotective flavone; strong mechanism, no finished-product human proof.**
**One-line:** Luteolin is a well-pedigreed dietary flavone with a *better-than-average animal-topical skin dataset* (anti-photoaging, ↓MMP, ↑collagen) and excellent anti-inflammatory/antioxidant/anti-glycation biology — but its "senolytic" billing is overstated for skin, plain luteolin penetrates poorly, and the only human topical data is a tiny uncontrolled series on a *different molecule*. Use it as a **science-forward supporting antioxidant/soothing active in an encapsulated or solubilized form**, with strictly mechanism-framed copy, not as a proof-backed senolytic hero.

**Longevity-relevance note:** Luteolin fits the skin-*longevity* thesis cleanly on **three hallmarks at once — inflammaging, oxidative stress, and glycation** — plus a credible senescence-*prevention* (senomorphic) angle and real anti-photoaging mechanism. That multi-hallmark profile makes it a strong **education/differentiation** ingredient and a natural **complement to a true senolytic (fisetin)** in a layered "senescence" narrative. The gaps are purely **delivery** (needs encapsulation/solubilization) and **format/route translation** (animal-topical + a derivative, not human luteolin). If we ever want real topical claims, the honest path is a **delivery-optimized luteolin** plus our **own finished-formula human testing** — not the existing literature.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Overall grade: C** (weak/indirect for the *topical, human, skin-aging* use that matters — but an unusually honest and well-sourced C, on the strong end of the band because the *animal-topical* skin dataset is real). _Verified 2026-06-19._

This grade reflects luteolin **as a topical anti-aging skincare active**, not its (much stronger) general pharmacology. There is **no human topical RCT on luteolin itself** for any skin indication; the human-topical evidence floor is a single uncontrolled n=8 case series on a *different molecule*. That caps the route/format-relevant grade at C. The dossier already states this plainly — it does not overclaim to "A/B."

### Evidence-tier reality (a/b: HUMAN + TOPICAL?)
- **Topical, human, on luteolin itself: NONE.** The only human-topical study is a *derivative* (tetramethoxyluteolin) — borrowed, not transferable.
- **Topical, animal: YES and genuine** (mouse + rat, UVB/UVA, real wrinkle/MMP/collagen endpoints) — but small n (3–8/grp), rodent, acute-ish UV, and penetration-enhancing vehicles (acetone, ethanol:PG).
- **Human oral:** real but thin, combination products, **not skin** (anti-inflammatory/safety context only).
- **In-vitro:** broad and consistent (antioxidant/Nrf2, anti-inflammatory, anti-glycation, target engagement) — mechanism, not proof.

### Industry-funded / tiny?
- **[9]** is the clear conflict: author-associated commercial product, n=8, uncontrolled, questionnaire/photo endpoints — already disclosed.
- The animal-topical studies are academic but **tiny** (n=3 in [4], n=6–8 elsewhere) — disclosed.
- No other industry-funding red flags found; anti-glycation and senotherapeutic-review citations are independent academic work.

### Main caveats (carry into any copy/formulation)
1. **No human topical proof for luteolin** — strongest human-topical data is a *different molecule* in 8 uncontrolled patients with *disease* (AD/psoriasis), not aging.
2. **"Senolytic" is borrowed** — list-membership from a 2015 screen, not demonstrated skin senolysis; the honest skin mechanism is **senomorphic + antioxidant + anti-photoaging**.
3. **Penetration is the make-or-break gap** — BCS Class II, plain luteolin barely permeates; needs encapsulation/solubilization, and even then human penetration is unquantified.
4. **Animal endpoints ≠ human claims** — small-n rodent UV studies with irritating vehicles do not equal "clinically proven."
5. **Glycation/melanogenesis data are in-tube/cell-line** — no human skin proof.
