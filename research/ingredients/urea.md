# Ingredient: Urea (INCI: Urea; + Hydroxyethyl Urea)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Class:** Humectants & hydration actives · **Status:** ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product

> **Brutally honest one-liner:** Urea is one of the most genuinely well-evidenced, cheap, gentle moisturizing/barrier actives in dermatology — it is a *native component of the skin's own Natural Moisturizing Factor (NMF)*, and topical urea is proven in real human RCTs to raise stratum-corneum hydration and lower TEWL, and — in a single small (n=21), industry-affiliated human study — appears to **upregulate the skin's own barrier-differentiation genes (filaggrin, loricrin, transglutaminase-1) and antimicrobial peptides**, a promising-but-not-yet-replicated "humectant that may also do barrier biology" finding. BUT: essentially all of that evidence is for **hydration, barrier, keratolysis, and dermatosis management (xerosis, atopic dermatitis, ichthyosis, psoriasis)** — there is **no credible direct evidence that urea reduces wrinkles, builds collagen, or reverses photoaging.** It is a superb *barrier/hydration workhorse with a true "replenish what skin loses with age" story*, not an anti-wrinkle hero. Hydroxyethyl urea is a milder, less-tacky humectant cousin with thinner (mostly supplier) data.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common name:** Urea (carbamide; "carbonyldiamide"). The same molecule found in mammalian urine, but cosmetic/pharma urea is **synthetic** (made industrially from ammonia + CO₂ — the Bosch–Meiser process), not animal-derived.
- **Chemistry:** Carbonyl group bearing two amine groups, CO(NH₂)₂. MW ≈ **60.06 Da** — very small, highly water-soluble, hygroscopic. CAS **57-13-6**. It is a chaotrope (hydrogen-bond breaker), which is the basis of both its keratolytic action and its lab use as a protein denaturant.
- **INCI:** **Urea** (functions: humectant, skin-conditioning, keratolytic, buffering).
- **Key forms / derivatives (cosmetic):**
  - **Urea** — the workhorse active discussed throughout.
  - **Hydroxyethyl Urea** (INCI **Hydroxyethyl Urea**; CAS **2078-71-9**; trade name **Hydrovance**, Akzo Nobel/Nouryon) — a hydroxyethyl-substituted urea sold as a **non-tacky humectant** with "moisturization comparable to glycerin" and a **lighter sensory profile**; enables "glycerin-free" claims. It is **not keratolytic** and lacks urea's deep clinical dataset (see §4, §5).
  - **Distinct, do-not-confuse derivatives:** **Imidazolidinyl urea** and **Diazolidinyl urea** are **formaldehyde-releasing preservatives** — chemically and functionally *unrelated* to urea-the-humectant. They carry their own (preservative) sensitization profile and **must not be conflated with urea** in safety or marketing (see §7). **Polyquaternium-degradants, allantoin** (which is a urea *oxidation product / ureide* and a separate soothing active) are also distinct.
- **Where it occurs naturally:** Urea is **endogenous to human skin** — a core component of NMF (alongside amino acids, lactate, PCA, sugars, ions), formed partly from filaggrin breakdown and sweat. This is the single most defensible part of urea's brand story: *you are replenishing a molecule the skin already makes and loses.*

## 2. Class & mechanism

Urea is a **humectant + barrier active** that behaves very differently by concentration. Its actions are well characterized at the stratum-corneum and keratinocyte level.

### Hat 1 — Humectant / water-binding (low concentration, ≤10%)
- Urea is **strongly hygroscopic**: it binds and holds water in the stratum corneum (SC), raising SC water content and **reducing transepidermal water loss (TEWL)**. As a native NMF component it also helps SC corneocytes **resist osmotic stress** and stay pliable.
- Because it is tiny (60 Da) and freely water-soluble, it distributes readily into the SC. It is actively handled by skin: keratinocytes express **urea transporters UT-A1 and UT-A2** and **aquaporins AQP3, AQP7, AQP9**, which move urea (and water/glycerol) into cells [1].

### Hat 2 — Keratolytic / protein-modifying (high concentration, >10%, esp. 20–50%)
- At higher concentrations urea acts as a **chaotrope**: it **breaks hydrogen bonds and disrupts the tertiary structure of keratin**, loosening corneocyte cohesion and softening/desquamating thickened, scaly, or hyperkeratotic skin (the basis of 20–40% "callus/heel/keratosis" products and 40% nail-softening/avulsion products) [2][3].
- This is a *physical/chemical loosening of dead-cell scaffolding*, not an acid-mediated exfoliation like AHAs — so it can be combined with, and is gentler than, strong acids for many uses.

### Hat 3 — Gene-regulatory / barrier-biology (promising, but single-study and industry-linked)
- Topical urea may be **more than a passive humectant**: one controlled human study [1] reports it acts as a signaling molecule that **upregulates the skin's own barrier machinery** — urea uptake **increased mRNA and protein for the differentiation proteins filaggrin, loricrin, transglutaminase-1, and involucrin, plus epidermal lipid-synthesis enzymes, and the antimicrobial peptides LL-37/cathelicidin and β-defensin-2.** If it holds up, this means urea helps the skin **rebuild its own barrier and antimicrobial defenses**, not merely sit on top holding water. **Caveat:** this rests on a **single n=21 forearm study whose authors disclose ISDIN ties** (advisory board / former employee) and has not been independently replicated — treat as a strong hypothesis, not settled mechanism (see Evidence verification).

### Hat 4 — Penetration enhancer
- Urea increases **percutaneous absorption of co-applied actives** (corticosteroids, antifungals, etc.) [2][8]. Mechanistically this appears driven less by direct lipid-bilayer disruption and more by **massive hydration of corneocytes** (urea's water-binding raises intracorneocyte water and osmotic pressure), swelling/loosening the tissue and easing diffusion [8]. **Formulation consequence:** urea can amplify both the benefit *and* the irritation of whatever it's paired with.

**Net framing:** Urea is a **bona fide barrier-repair humectant** — replenishing a native NMF molecule, raising hydration, and lowering TEWL (all well-evidenced), *and possibly* switching on the skin's own differentiation/AMP genes (single industry-linked human study) — with a dose-dependent keratolytic gear and a penetration-enhancer side effect.

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

| Hallmark | Mechanistic rationale | Strength of *skin-relevant* (topical, human) evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Hydration / water-loss (TEWL)** | Native NMF humectant; binds water, lowers TEWL, restores SC water content | **Strong** — multiple human RCTs/controlled studies (xerosis, AD) [1][4][5][6] |
| **Barrier decline** | Upregulates filaggrin/loricrin/TGase-1/involucrin + lipid enzymes; restores cohesion | **Strong–moderate** — human gene-expression + functional barrier data [1]; AD relapse-prevention RCT [4] |
| **Inflammaging / antimicrobial resilience** | Raises LL-37 + β-defensin-2 (innate antimicrobial defense); calms dry-skin dermatoses | **Moderate** — human gene/protein data + dermatosis RCTs [1] |
| **Microbiome (defense side)** | Enhanced AMP expression supports antimicrobial balance | Weak–moderate, indirect (AMP upregulation, not microbiome composition) [1] |
| **Collagen / ECM loss** | No meaningful direct mechanism for dermal collagen synthesis | **None** for topical anti-aging endpoints |
| **Photoaging (UV)** | No UV-protective or photo-repair mechanism demonstrated | **None** |
| **Pigmentation / tone** | No tyrosinase/melanogenesis mechanism | **None** (it may improve *appearance* of dull/flaky skin via smoothing, not pigment) |
| **Oxidative stress** | Not an antioxidant | **None** |
| **Glycation / AGEs** | No anti-glycation mechanism | **None** |
| **Cellular senescence** | No senolytic/senomorphic mechanism | **None** |
| **Mitochondrial decline** | No mechanism | **None** |

**Urea's honest lane = hydration + barrier restoration + (mild) keratolytic smoothing + innate-defense support.** It addresses the *barrier/hydration* and *inflammaging* hallmarks credibly and is **silent** on collagen, photoaging, pigment, oxidative stress, glycation, senescence, and mitochondria. The age relevance is real but specific: **NMF (including urea) measurably declines with age** as profilaggrin synthesis and barrier function drop [7], so topical urea is a literal **"replace what aging skin loses"** play — just not an anti-wrinkle one.

## 4. Evidence review by tier (the core)

> **Read this first.** Urea is the inverse of most "longevity" actives: its evidence is **strongest exactly where it matters for a cosmetic claim (human topical hydration/barrier) and weakest where marketing wants to go (wrinkles/collagen/photoaging).** There are **real human RCTs** for urea — but they are about **xerosis, atopic dermatitis, ichthyosis, psoriasis, and hand-foot reactions**, i.e. hydration/barrier/keratolysis, not facial rejuvenation. **No credible RCT shows topical urea reduces wrinkles or builds collagen.** Treat any "anti-aging urea serum" wrinkle claim as unsubstantiated.

### Tier 1 — In-vitro / mechanism (consistent, real)
- **SC lipid-model / penetration-enhancer work** — Wang et al. (2016), *Biochim Biophys Acta Biomembranes* 1858(8):2008–2016 [8] (PMID 27195429): in SC lipid models, **10% urea showed no direct lipid-headgroup interaction or chain-packing change**; the authors conclude urea's penetration-enhancing effect is driven by **massive water influx into corneocytes from urea's water-binding capacity** (osmotic swelling), not bilayer disruption. *Takeaway:* urea works *with* the barrier (hydration) rather than by tearing it apart.
- **Keratinocyte transporter/gene biology** — the cell-level arm of Grether-Beck 2012 [1]: human keratinocytes **express and upregulate UT-A1/UT-A2 and AQP3/7/9** to take up urea, and respond by raising differentiation and AMP genes. Aquaporin-3 biology (water/glycerol/urea transport, role in keratinocyte differentiation) is independently well established [9].
- **Limitation:** mechanism is solid but in-vitro keratolysis/denaturation requires high urea; the gene-regulatory effects are the headline and were confirmed in vivo (below).

### Tier 2 — Animal
- **Atopic-dermatitis mouse model** within Grether-Beck 2012 [1]: topical urea **normalized barrier function and antimicrobial peptide expression** in a murine AD-like model, corroborating the human gene-expression findings.
- **Limitation:** disease (AD) model, supportive rather than aging-specific; the human data make the animal tier largely confirmatory here.

### Tier 3 — Human, ORAL / systemic
- **Not applicable as a benefit route.** Oral/systemic urea is a clinical/diagnostic agent (e.g., hyponatremia, urea breath tests), **not a skincare delivery format**, and there is no oral-urea skin-benefit literature to borrow. (This is a point *in urea's favor* for honesty: unlike many "longevity" actives, urea's case rests on **topical human data**, not borrowed oral/IV studies.)

### Tier 4 — Human, TOPICAL (the tier that matters — and it is genuinely strong, for the right endpoints)

**Barrier / gene-regulation (the standout):**
- **Grether-Beck S, Felsner I, Brenden H, et al. (2012), *J Invest Dermatol* 132(6):1561–1572.** doi:10.1038/jid.2012.42; PMID 22418868 [1] — **21 human volunteers**, 4-week forearm application of **placebo vs 10% vs 20% urea**, with biopsies. Topical urea **improved barrier function (↓TEWL)** and **upregulated filaggrin, loricrin, transglutaminase-1, involucrin, epidermal lipid-synthesis enzymes, and antimicrobial peptides LL-37 and β-defensin-2.** *This is the keystone paper* for the barrier-biology story. *Limitations (significant):* small n (21), forearm (not face), 4 weeks, dermatosis/barrier endpoints (no wrinkle/collagen endpoints), **not independently replicated**, and **authors disclose ISDIN ties** (Krutmann & Elias on the Scientific Advisory Board; Trullas a former employee). Treat as a single promising finding, not an established mechanism.

**Atopic dermatitis (maintenance / relapse — high-quality):**
- **Åkerström U, Reitamo S, Langeland T, et al. (2015), *Acta Derm Venereol* 95(5):587–592.** doi:10.2340/00015555-2051; PMID 25594845 [4] — **Randomized, double-blind, multicentre RCT**; a **5% urea** barrier cream vs reference cream for AD relapse prevention. Urea cream **reduced relapse risk by 37% (95% CI 10–55%)**, extended median time to relapse (22 vs 15 days), and at 6 months **26% vs 10% remained eczema-free**; well tolerated. *Methodologically strong (randomized, double-blind, multicentre) — but note the conflict:* the test cream is the sponsor's own product (**Canoderm, ACO Hud Nordic AB**) and the lead author is an ACO Hud employee. Robust design, manufacturer-run.
- Systematic-review/guideline context: urea-based emollients are among the **best-documented** moisturizers for AD; one cited AD RCT (**n=172**) found prolonged 5% urea use significantly increased eczema-free time, and a comparison found **4% urea reduced TEWL more than 20% glycerin** in AD skin [3].

**Xerosis / dry skin (consistent across many small controlled studies):**
- Reviewed in Piquero-Casals et al. (2021) [3] and Celleno (2018) [2]: e.g., **15% urea twice daily significantly reduced TEWL** in severe dry skin (n=12); a **placebo-controlled double-blind study (n=21)** found **20% urea once daily significantly reduced TEWL**; **5% urea increased hydration vs control in elderly (n=42, >60 y)** and **in type-2 diabetics (n=40)**; **10% urea + dexpanthenol** improved xerosis and pruritus in hemodialysis patients (n=15). *Consistent hydration/TEWL benefit; mostly small samples.*

**Ichthyosis vulgaris (keratolytic + hydration):**
- "Over a dozen" trials with **2–10% urea** show significant improvement; an **RCT (n=30)** found **10% urea** beat a glycerol cream on flaking/roughness/redness/cracking; a **double-blind trial (n=84)** found urea cream **outperformed 2% salicylic acid ointment and paraffin** [3].

**Psoriasis (adjunct / scalp / hyperkeratosis):**
- Urea shampoo (**RCT n=70**) reduced scalp-psoriasis itch and severity; high-concentration urea (40–50%) aids localized hyperkeratotic plaques; medium urea (17%) + dithranol and 40% urea + bifonazole show good responses (adjunctive) [3].

**Drug-induced hand-foot skin reaction & radiation dermatitis (prophylaxis — large RCTs):**
- **Prophylactic 10% urea cream RCT (n=871)** in HCC patients on sorafenib **significantly reduced hand-foot skin reaction** and delayed onset; a further **RCT (n=288)** concurred; a prospective study (n=98) found a **3% urea** lotion roughly doubled the chance of avoiding radiodermatitis in breast-RT patients [3]. *These are barrier-protection, not aging, but show real preventive efficacy at scale.*

**Nail (onychomycosis adjunct / softening):**
- **40% urea + topical antifungal** improved 29–85% of onychomycosis cases (systematic-review range); 40% urea is standard for non-surgical nail softening/avulsion [3].

**ANTI-AGING / WRINKLE / COLLAGEN — the honest gap:**
- **No dedicated, controlled topical RCT shows urea reduces wrinkles, increases dermal collagen, or reverses photoaging.** Targeted searches return retinoids, AHAs, collagen, urolithin A, etc. — **not urea** — for facial photoaging/wrinkle endpoints. The one "anti-aging" mention in the major review is a **multi-ingredient facial serum (urea + apple stem cell extract + others)** with a "biorevitalizing" claim — **confounded combo, not urea isolated** [3]. Any wrinkle benefit of urea is **indirect/cosmetic** (smoother, better-hydrated, less-flaky skin *looks* better) — not a structural anti-aging effect.

**What is actually proven for human TOPICAL urea:**
- **Strong:** ↑SC hydration, ↓TEWL, **barrier repair via upregulated differentiation genes + lipids + AMPs** [1]; AD relapse prevention [4]; xerosis/ichthyosis improvement [2][3]; keratolysis at high % [2][3]; prophylaxis of hand-foot/radiation dermatitis [3].
- **NOT established topically:** wrinkle reduction, collagen synthesis, photoaging reversal, pigmentation/tone correction, antioxidant or anti-glycation or anti-senescence "longevity" effects.

### Hydroxyethyl urea — separate, thinner evidence
- **Supplier (Akzo/Nouryon, Hydrovance) data:** at **~5%**, hydroxyethyl urea delivers **moisturization comparable to glycerin** with a **less tacky, lighter feel**; positioned for "glycerin-free" claims [10]. **No comparable independent RCT base** to urea's; treat its hydration claim as **moderate (supplier-grade)** and assume **no keratolytic or gene-regulatory action**.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **For hydration/barrier (its main job), urea does not need to "penetrate" deep** — it works in and on the **stratum corneum**, where it is also natively present. At 60 Da and freely water-soluble it loads the SC easily and is taken up by keratinocytes via **UT-A1/UT-A2 and AQP3/7/9** [1], reaching the living epidermis where the gene-regulatory effects occur [1]. This is why the barrier/hydration evidence is so robust: the active reaches exactly the compartment it acts on.
- **Penetration *enhancer* for others:** urea increases percutaneous absorption of co-applied drugs/actives, driven mainly by **corneocyte hyper-hydration/osmotic swelling** rather than lipid disruption [8]. Practical implication: in a multi-active formula, urea can **raise both efficacy and irritation** of partners (e.g., it will push more retinoid/acid/steroid through) — a feature to use deliberately, not by accident.
- **Hydroxyethyl urea:** larger and less studied for penetration; behaves as a surface/SC humectant. No evidence it drives the deeper gene-regulatory effects urea does.

## 6. Formulation

- **Use levels (by goal):**
  - **2–10%** — humectant / barrier / daily moisturizer (face-appropriate; 5% is a sweet spot with RCT support [4]).
  - **10–30%** — hydration + keratolytic + penetration-enhancing (body, rough/thick skin, feet).
  - **30–50%** — strong keratolytic/debridement, nail softening (not for facial use).
  - **Hydroxyethyl urea:** ~**5%** (CIR/supplier reported up to ~20% leave-on); non-tacky humectant.
- **Solubility / phase:** freely water-soluble; water-phase ingredient.
- **pH / stability — the key caution:** urea is **hydrolytically unstable** and can **decompose to ammonia + (eventually) release small amounts of ammonia/CO₂**, raising pH and causing odor/discoloration, especially at **high temperature or extreme pH**. Best stability is around **mild acidic pH (~6, roughly 5.5–7)**; formulas are commonly **buffered (e.g., with lactic acid / lactate, citrate, or amino acids)** to stabilize urea and hold pH. Avoid hot processing where possible; add to the cool-down phase. Urea-containing products often need **buffering + adequate preservation** and benefit from **packaging that limits air/heat**.
- **Sensory:** light, fast, non-greasy at low %; at high % can feel slightly "active"/sometimes mildly stingy on compromised skin. **Hydroxyethyl urea is specifically valued for a non-tacky, glycerin-like feel without stickiness** [10].
- **Pairings (synergy / common combos):**
  - **Lactic acid / lactate, amino acids, PCA, glycerin** — co-NMF humectant blends; lactate/lactic acid also **buffers and stabilizes** urea. Classic "NMF-replenishing" stack.
  - **Ceramides + cholesterol + fatty acids** — natural partner for the **barrier-repair thesis**: urea drives differentiation/lipid genes [1] while lipids supply the bricks-and-mortar.
  - **Niacinamide, panthenol, allantoin** — complementary barrier/soothing actives.
  - **AHAs (lactic/glycolic), salicylic acid** — combined keratolytic smoothing (watch cumulative irritation; urea *enhances* their penetration [8]).
  - **Antifungals / corticosteroids (Rx)** — urea is a deliberate **penetration enhancer** in medicated formulas [2][3].
- **Conflicts / cautions:** urea's **penetration-enhancing** action means it can intensify irritation from strong actives (retinoids, high-strength acids) — pair thoughtfully. Avoid high-heat processing and unbuffered high-pH systems (decomposition).

## 7. Safety

- **Excellent, long-established safety.** **CIR Expert Panel (2005), *Int J Toxicol* 24(Suppl 3):1–56** [11]: **"Urea is safe as used in cosmetic products."** Reported cosmetic use **0.01–10%** at the time of assessment; no in-vivo genotoxicity (its DNA-uncoiling is an in-vitro lab property only); **few sensitization reports** across many clinical studies.
- **Irritation (dose-dependent):** the most common adverse effect is a transient **stinging/burning**, more likely at **higher concentrations and on compromised/eczematous skin**. CIR notes 5% may cause mild irritation, 20% can be notably irritating in some, while one 60% study reported no significant irritation — i.e., **vehicle and individual susceptibility matter** [11]. For a daily facial product, **≤10%** is the comfortable, well-tolerated range [3][4].
- **Sensitization:** genuine allergic contact dermatitis to urea itself is **rare** (essentially isolated case reports, often attributable to other formula components) [3][11].
- **CRITICAL safety/marketing clarification — urea ≠ formaldehyde releaser:** **Urea (the humectant) does NOT release formaldehyde.** The confusion comes from the *unrelated preservatives* **imidazolidinyl urea** and **diazolidinyl urea**, which are formaldehyde-releasing biocides. These share only the word "urea." Do not let copy or critics conflate them — and don't use those preservatives if "formaldehyde-free" is a brand promise.
- **Sun sensitivity:** none. Urea is not a photosensitizer (though as a penetration enhancer it could increase delivery of a co-applied photosensitizing active — a partner-ingredient issue, not urea's).
- **Pregnancy / breastfeeding:** topical urea is **non-hormonal with minimal systemic absorption** at cosmetic strengths and is widely regarded as compatible with pregnancy/breastfeeding; however, dedicated pregnancy data are sparse and some clinical sources default to "consult your clinician." **Brand stance:** treat as low-concern at cosmetic levels, keep the standard "if pregnant/nursing, consult your provider" disclaimer [12].
- **Sourcing / sustainability:** cosmetic urea is **synthetic** (ammonia + CO₂; Bosch–Meiser), **not animal-derived** — vegan-compatible and cruelty-free-compatible. Massive, cheap industrial supply (urea is one of the highest-volume chemicals made globally, mostly for fertilizer); cosmetic/pharma grade is a small, well-controlled slice. No ethical-sourcing flags.
- **Hydroxyethyl urea:** assessed by CIR as **safe as used** [13]; mild, low-irritation humectant; not a formaldehyde releaser.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (cosmetic vs. drug)

- **Status:** Urea as a **moisturizer / skin-conditioning humectant** is squarely **cosmetic**. *However,* at **keratolytic strengths and for disease indications** (ichthyosis, nail avulsion, psoriasis plaques, onychomycosis adjunct, hand-foot reaction prophylaxis) urea is used as a **drug/medical-device-adjacent agent** — and many high-% urea products are sold as **OTC drugs or medical devices**, not cosmetics. **The line is concentration + claim + indication.** For a cosmetic skin-longevity brand, **stay in the ≤10–20% cosmetic-moisturizer lane** and avoid disease claims.
- **✅ Defensible cosmetic language:**
  - "Replenishes urea, a **moisture factor your skin naturally makes and loses over time** (part of its Natural Moisturizing Factor)."
  - "Delivers **lasting hydration** and helps **reduce moisture loss (TEWL)**"; "helps **support / strengthen the skin barrier**"; "helps skin **feel smoother, softer, and more supple**"; "helps **soften rough, flaky, dry skin**."
  - "Supports the skin's **own barrier renewal**" — *defensible at a careful, appearance-level register* given the human filaggrin/lipid gene data [1], but **avoid quantified or drug-like phrasing** (see below).
- **⚠️ Risky / drug-like or unsubstantiated — flag for legal/substantiation:**
  - **"Boosts collagen," "reduces wrinkles," "anti-aging," "reverses photoaging," "firms," "lifts."** **Not supported** for topical urea — these are the #1 overreach. Do not attach urea to wrinkle/collagen claims.
  - **"Treats / cures eczema, psoriasis, ichthyosis, fungal nails," "keratolytic treatment," "removes calluses/nails."** These are **drug claims** tied to high-% medicinal use; out of bounds for a cosmetic positioning.
  - **Quantified gene/biology claims** ("increases filaggrin by X%," "boosts antimicrobial peptides"): the science [1] is real but **structure/function/drug-flavored**; keep any barrier-renewal language **appearance-oriented and unquantified**, and route through substantiation review.
  - **"Antimicrobial / antibacterial skin"**: avoid — implies a drug/biocide claim.
- **Bottom line:** Sell urea as **hydration + barrier support + a true "replenish skin's own moisture factor" story**, keep it **off wrinkles/collagen/photoaging**, avoid disease/keratolytic-treatment language, and don't confuse it with formaldehyde-releasing "urea" preservatives. ## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Cost tier:** **VERY LOW.** Urea is a global commodity chemical (cosmetic/pharma grade is a refined fraction of fertilizer-scale production). Excellent value: strong evidence + great safety + near-trivial cost.
- **Suppliers:** ubiquitous — cosmetic/pharma-grade urea from countless fine-chemical and personal-care distributors worldwide; reliable, redundant supply. **Hydroxyethyl urea (Hydrovance)** from **Nouryon/Akzo Nobel** and distributors.
- **Provenance story:** **synthetic, vegan-compatible, not animal-derived.** Clean supply with no ethical flags (the "urine" association is a *marketing-perception* issue to manage, not a real sourcing one — see §10).

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

- **Where urea is *under*-credited:** it is often dismissed as a "basic" or "boring" drugstore moisturizer, yet it has **human RCT evidence for hydration/TEWL and AD/xerosis management** [3][4], plus a single (industry-affiliated, unreplicated) human study suggesting it influences barrier *biology* — filaggrin/loricrin/lipid genes, AMPs [1]. For a hydration/barrier active, urea is **honestly excellent and over-delivers on price** — just don't overstate the gene-regulatory story as if it were settled or independently confirmed.
- **Where urea is *over*-claimed (watch for this):**
  1. **"Anti-aging / wrinkle / collagen" urea serums** — **no credible topical evidence.** The wrinkle benefit is *indirect* (better-hydrated, smoother, less-flaky skin looks younger), not structural. This is the main overreach.
  2. **"Skin barrier renewal at the cellular level / antimicrobial defense"** — the biology is real [1] but easy to overstate into drug territory; keep it appearance-level.
  3. **Concentration confusion** — high-% keratolytic urea (callus/nail) marketed alongside low-% facial humectant urea as if interchangeable; they are **different jobs at different strengths.**
- **The "urine" perception problem:** urea is famously "found in urine," which spooks some consumers. Reality: **cosmetic urea is synthetic**, and urea is **made by your own skin** as part of NMF. This is a *narrative* to get ahead of (honest framing: "a moisture molecule your skin naturally produces"), not a real safety/sourcing issue.
- **Honest summary:** urea is **under-hyped for what it truly does (hydration + barrier biology) and would be over-hyped if positioned as an anti-wrinkle active.** Keep it in its lane and it's one of the most defensible ingredients in the cabinet.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️→✅ **SUPPORTING (strong) / CONDITIONAL on positioning.** **Include urea as a low-cost, exceptionally well-evidenced hydration + barrier-repair workhorse with a genuine "replenish what aging skin loses" NMF story — NOT as an anti-wrinkle or collagen hero.**

- **One-line reasoning:** Urea is a native NMF component with **real human RCT evidence** for raising hydration, lowering TEWL, and **upregulating the skin's own barrier-differentiation genes, lipids, and antimicrobial peptides** — directly serving the brand's **barrier-repair thesis** at near-zero cost and risk — but it has **no credible topical evidence for wrinkles/collagen/photoaging**, so its claims must stay on hydration/barrier.
- **Longevity-relevance note:** The age angle is **real but specific**: NMF (urea included) **declines with age** as filaggrin/profilaggrin synthesis and barrier function fall [7], so topical urea is a literal **"replace a moisture factor the skin loses with aging"** play and a credibility-building barrier partner for **ceramides, lactate/PCA/amino acids, niacinamide, and glycerin**. It is *not* a "hallmarks of aging" longevity active (no senescence/mitochondria/collagen/antioxidant mechanism) — position it as a **foundational barrier/hydration supporting active**, ideally at **5–10%** for a facial product (or **hydroxyethyl urea ~5%** where a non-tacky, glycerin-free humectant feel is wanted). ## Evidence verification (adversarial)

_Verified 2026-06-19 by independent check of the primary citations against PubMed/PMC abstracts and disclosure statements._

### Overall evidence grade: **B+ (split)**
The honest grade depends entirely on the claim:
- **For hydration / TEWL reduction / xerosis & atopic-dermatitis management:** solidly **B, bordering A** — multiple real human topical RCTs, including a randomized double-blind multicentre trial. This is genuinely well-evidenced for a humectant.
- **For the "upregulates the skin's own barrier-differentiation genes + AMPs" barrier-biology story:** **C+** — it rests on essentially **one** small (n=21) human study plus its in-vitro/mouse arms, and that study is **industry-affiliated** (see caveats). Real and impressive, but not yet replicated or independent.
- **For wrinkles / collagen / photoaging:** **D (unproven topically)** — the dossier already says this clearly and correctly. No softening needed; this is the dossier's strongest section.

The composite **B+** reflects that the dossier's *headline* claims (hydration + barrier) are well-supported and that it is admirably honest about the anti-aging gap. The deductions below are about provenance disclosure and one mechanistic over-lean, not about fabricated or misread science.

### What checks out (verified true)
- **[1] Grether-Beck 2012, *J Invest Dermatol* 132(6):1561–1572, PMID 22418868** — REAL. Confirmed: 21 human volunteers, 10%/20% urea, 4-week design, with reported upregulation of filaggrin, loricrin, transglutaminase-1, involucrin, lipid-synthesis enzymes, and AMPs LL-37 + β-defensin-2. Human + topical, as claimed. ✓ (caveat below)
- **[4] Åkerström 2015, *Acta Derm Venereol* 95(5):587–592, PMID 25594845** — REAL. Confirmed: randomized double-blind multicentre RCT, 5% urea, 37% relapse-risk reduction (95% CI 10–55%), 22 vs 15 days median time to relapse, 26% vs 10% eczema-free at 6 months. Human + topical. ✓ (funding caveat below)
- **[2] Celleno 2018, *Dermatol Ther* 31(6):e12690, PMID 30378232** — REAL single-author narrative review. ✓
- **[3] Piquero-Casals 2021, *Dermatol Ther (Heidelb)* 11(6):1905–1915, PMID 34596890** — REAL review; the secondary trials it summarizes (n=12 xerosis, n=172 AD, n=30 ichthyosis, n=70 psoriasis shampoo, n=871 sorafenib hand-foot) are reported accurately. ✓ (funding caveat below)
- **[8] PMID 27195429** — REAL paper, and its conclusion (urea's penetration enhancement is via corneocyte hyper-hydration / osmotic swelling, NOT lipid-bilayer disruption; in-vitro lipid-model study) is correctly characterized. ✓ **BUT the author/citation metadata in the dossier was WRONG** (see corrections — now fixed).
- **[11] CIR 2005, PMID 16422263** — REAL. "Urea is safe as used," 0.01–10% reported use, dose-dependent irritation, rare sensitization, no in-vivo genotoxicity — all confirmed. ✓

No fabricated citations were found. The evidence is consistently **human and topical** where the dossier claims it is, and the dossier does NOT borrow oral/IV/animal data to support topical benefit (it correctly marks the oral tier "not applicable" and the mouse tier "confirmatory"). That is a real strength.

### Main caveats (these were under-disclosed in the original)
1. **Single-sponsor (ISDIN) cluster behind the "barrier-biology" story.** The keystone mechanism paper [1] discloses that authors Krutmann and Elias sit on ISDIN's Scientific Advisory Board and Trullas is a former ISDIN employee. The major review [3] is even more entangled: its Rapid Service Fee was **paid by ISDIN**, three authors took ISDIN consultant fees, and two are **ISDIN employees** — and it shares authors (Krutmann, Trullas) with [1]. So the two pillars of the "urea switches on barrier genes" narrative trace back to one commercially interested group. The science may well be sound, but it is **not independent**, and the dossier presented it as settled fact without this disclosure.
2. **The gene-regulatory effect rests on n=21, once, on forearm.** It has not, on this check, been independently replicated in a non-industry human study. Treat "upregulates filaggrin/loricrin/AMPs in human skin" as a **single promising finding**, not an established mechanism.
3. **The flagship AD RCT [4] tested the sponsor's own product.** The 5% urea cream is **Canoderm (ACO Hud Nordic AB)**; lead author Åkerström is listed under ACO Hud's Department of Innovation. The result is real and the trial design is strong (randomized, double-blind, multicentre), but it is a **manufacturer evaluating its own cream** — a conflict the dossier called "strong, controlled evidence" without noting.
4. **Most xerosis/ichthyosis evidence is small and old.** Many supporting trials are n=12–42, single-centre, decades-old. Directionally consistent, individually weak.
5. **Hydroxyethyl urea is supplier-grade only.** The dossier already flags this correctly; no independent RCT base exists. Keep its hydration claim explicitly "supplier-reported."

### Claims softened / corrected in the dossier (done)
- **Citation [8] author/page metadata corrected** from the incorrect "Wang TF, Kasting GB, Nitsche JM / Pham QD, Björklund S … 1858(8):2008–2016" to the actual authors **Mueller J, Oliveira JSL, et al., 1858(9):2006–2018**. (The mechanistic claim it supports was accurate; only the attribution was wrong.)
- **"a rare humectant that actually does barrier biology" / "switching on the skin's own differentiation/AMP genes"** — softened to flag that this rests on a single small industry-affiliated study.
- **Industry-funding disclosure added** to citations [1], [3], and [4] inline and in §4.
- **§4 AD-relapse line** reworded so the strong-evidence framing carries its sponsor caveat.

### Claims that are fine as-is (do NOT soften)
- The entire **anti-aging / wrinkle / collagen "honest gap"** treatment (§3, §4, §8, §10) is accurate and appropriately skeptical — this is model dossier behavior.
- Hydration / TEWL / NMF-replenishment framing is well-supported.
- Safety section is accurate and the formaldehyde-releaser clarification is correct and valuable.

**Bottom line:** The dossier's *conclusions* survive adversarial review intact — urea is a well-evidenced hydration/barrier active with no wrinkle/collagen evidence. The needed fixes are about **provenance honesty** (a single commercially interested research cluster sits behind the most impressive "barrier-biology" claims) and **one citation-metadata error**, not about misread or invented science.
