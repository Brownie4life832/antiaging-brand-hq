# Ingredient: Lipochroman / Dimethylmethoxy Chromanol (INCI: Dimethylmethoxy Chromanol)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering (conditional/supporting) · **Class:** Antioxidant (synthetic vitamin-E analogue)

> Brutal-honesty summary: A genuinely potent, well-characterized lab antioxidant with a real and somewhat differentiated mechanism (it neutralizes nitrogen radicals / peroxynitrite, not just oxygen radicals). But the human evidence is thin and almost always confounded — it has essentially **never been tested topically by itself in a controlled human trial**. Every human result we have is for a multi-antioxidant blend (usually inside a sunscreen). It is a credible *supporting* antioxidant, not a hero. Treat the "20x vitamin E" marketing line as supplier copy, not proven clinical superiority.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common / trade name:** Lipochroman (also written "Lipochroman-6" or "LC-6"; supplier brand names include Lipochroman Molecule and Lipochroman AQO Molecule MB).
- **INCI:** Dimethylmethoxy Chromanol.
- **Chemical name:** 3,4-Dihydro-7-methoxy-2,2-dimethyl-2H-1-benzopyran-6-ol (equivalently 3,4-dihydro-6-hydroxy-2,2-dimethyl-7-methoxy-1(2H)-benzopyran).
- **CAS:** 83923-51-7.
- **Structure / forms:** A synthetic chroman (benzopyran) — i.e. it shares the "chromanol head" of the tocopherols (vitamin E) but has no phytyl tail and adds a 7-methoxy group. It is described as a **synthetic analogue of gamma-tocopherol**. Off-white to beige (sometimes "light yellow to brown") crystalline lipophilic powder.
- **Originator / suppliers:** Developed by **Lipotec S.A.** (Barcelona; founded 1987, acquired by **Lubrizol / Berkshire Hathaway** in 2012). Other manufacturers of the raw INCI include Soho Aneco/Anecochem, Beri Pharma (BeriCos Chromanol), and Chenlang Bio. (Sources 8, 10, 12.)
- **Note on derivatives:** "Lipochroman AQO" is a water-dispersible delivery version of the same molecule for aqueous-friendly formulating; the active is the same INCI.

## 2. Class & mechanism

A small-molecule, **lipophilic synthetic antioxidant** that mimics and extends vitamin E chemistry. Reported mechanisms:

1. **Free-radical / chain-breaking antioxidant (ROS).** The chromanol -OH donates a hydrogen to quench lipid-peroxidation radicals, like tocopherol.
2. **Reactive nitrogen species (RNS) / peroxynitrite scavenging — its differentiator.** Like gamma-tocopherol (and unlike alpha-tocopherol), the molecule is positioned to trap electrophilic nitrogen oxides and **inhibit peroxynitrite-driven nitration**. Peroxynitrite (ONOO−) nitrates proteins, peroxidizes lipids and damages DNA, so a scavenger here addresses a damage pathway most cosmetic antioxidants ignore. (Sources 1, 4, 9.)
3. **Singlet-oxygen quenching.** Demonstrated physically (see §4) — relevant to UVA/visible-light photo-oxidation.
4. **SOD-mimetic activity (claimed).** The originating patent reports it "detoxifies superoxide anions in a manner that simulates the effect of SOD," quantified via the ABEL assay (ABEL-RAC 653 per mg; 2067 SOD-equivalent units) with notable effect "at 0.1% or greater." (Source 9.) This is patent/assay data, not independent clinical proof.
5. **Reactive carbonyl species (RCS) / anti-glycation (claimed).** Supplier literature describes "triple protection" against ROS, RNS and RCS, implying an anti-AGE angle. **[unverified]** — I could not locate a peer-reviewed anti-glycation study specific to this molecule.

Marketing shorthand: a "bio-inspired" antioxidant that targets oxygen *and* nitrogen radicals.

## 4. Evidence review by tier (core of this dossier)

**Bottom line up front:** the molecule's *chemistry* is well proven; its *topical clinical benefit as a standalone* is essentially unproven in humans. Every human study below combines it with other antioxidants and/or UV filters, so its individual contribution cannot be isolated.

### Tier 1 — In-vitro (cell / chemical assays)
- **Wang, Zhong, Li, Du, Ye, Xie & Hu (2023),** *Skin Research and Technology* 29(12):e13539. DOI: 10.1111/srt.13539. Combination of DMC + turmeric (Curcuma longa) root extract (1:1) in **HaCaT keratinocytes**, DMC at 10 and 20 µg/mL, UVB challenge (50 J/cm²), n=3 biochemical replicates. The blend raised SOD activity ~1.5–1.6×, upregulated SOD1/SOD2/SOD3 mRNA and restored NQO1 vs UVB controls; described as **synergistic**. *Limitation:* single cell line; the synergy means DMC's solo effect is not isolated; the paper's accompanying human arm (see Tier 4) had no placebo. Source 5.
- **Patent assay data (Isaacs / Pangaea, now assigned to Medik8), EP2113242A1 / US8221746B2** — ABEL-assay SOD-mimetic and peroxynitrite-inhibition data (figures in §2). *Limitation:* patent assay, inventor-generated, not peer-reviewed. Sources 7, 9.
- **Supplier comparative assays** (e.g. "TBA-assay more potent than BHT, trolox or tocopherol"; "increased cell viability up to 173% vs resveratrol, vitamin E and ferulic acid"; "~20× the antioxidant activity of gamma-tocopherol"). These trace to Lipotec/Lubrizol technical literature and resellers, **not** to a confirmable independent journal. **[unverified as peer-reviewed]** — cite as manufacturer data only. Sources 2, 3, 6, 13.

### Tier 2 — Ex-vivo (skin tissue, no living subject)
- **Nonell, García-Díaz, Viladot & Delgado (2013),** *International Journal of Cosmetic Science* 35(3):272–280. DOI: 10.1111/ics.12039. Measured **singlet-oxygen quenching** by near-IR phosphorescence in solution and in **ex-vivo porcine skin**. Quenching rate constant **(1.3 ± 0.1) × 10⁸ M⁻¹ s⁻¹** in solution; in porcine skin it clearly reduced singlet-oxygen lifetime and emission intensity. *Strength:* rigorous physical chemistry confirming a real photoprotective mechanism in skin tissue. *Limitation:* not a clinical endpoint (no wrinkle/firmness/redness outcome); ex-vivo only. Sources 11, 14.

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL
- **None identified.** This is a cosmetic raw material; there is no meaningful oral/supplement human literature for it. (Do not borrow oral antioxidant claims.)

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL
*No standalone-DMC RCT exists.* All human data are for blends:

- **Souza & Maia Campos (2017),** *European Journal of Pharmaceutical Sciences* 104:52–64. DOI: 10.1016/j.ejps.2017.03.026. **Single-blind clinical trial, n=44, ages 30–50, 12 weeks**, twice-daily facial application of SPF-30 sunscreens with vs without antioxidants (DMC among them). Antioxidant-supplemented sunscreen "significantly improved skin pigmentation, the collagen degradation on the dermis and thereby the skin net elasticity after 84 days." *Limitations:* **single-blind; no untreated/SPF-only-vs-blank control isolating the antioxidants; DMC not tested alone** (effect could be the UV filters or co-antioxidants like Spirulina); SPF itself drives much of the benefit. The lowest tier of "proof" for DMC specifically. Source 16.
- **Souza, Maia Campos et al. (2017),** *Skin Pharmacology and Physiology* 30(2):81–89. DOI: 10.1159/000458158. Sunscreen "enriched by antioxidants providing protection in the whole solar spectral range," radical-scavenging measured by **EPR spectroscopy across UV/VIS/NIR**. Adding antioxidants (DMC included) raised radical-scavenging beyond the UV range into visible/near-IR. *Limitations:* measures radical scavenging of the formula, **not clinical anti-aging outcomes**; DMC's individual share not isolated. Source 15.
- **Wang et al. (2023) human arm** (same paper as Tier 1): ~23–24 subjects, DMC+turmeric blend, ~4 weeks; reported 18.29% reduction in redness, 35.68% decrease in TEWL, 32.04% increase in firmness. *Limitations:* **no placebo control**, small n, blend (not DMC alone), short duration. Source 5.

**Verdict on evidence:** Proven for *topical human use as a standalone anti-aging active* = **nothing**. Proven *mechanistically in skin tissue* = singlet-oxygen quenching (good). Proven *as part of an antioxidant-fortified sunscreen* = modest improvements in pigmentation/elasticity/TEWL/radical scavenging, all confounded. The popular "20× vitamin E" potency line is **supplier marketing**, not a confirmed clinical comparison.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- Lipophilic and very low molecular weight (≈208 g/mol) — physicochemically favorable for stratum-corneum partitioning, consistent with vitamin-E-class permeation. The ex-vivo porcine-skin singlet-oxygen result (Nonell 2013) confirms it is active *within* skin tissue, which is reassuring for relevance.
- No published human cutaneous pharmacokinetics (depth of penetration, retention) were located. **[unverified]** for exact penetration depth.
- Works at extremely low use levels (0.01–0.05%), so even modest delivery can be functionally sufficient as an antioxidant.

## 6. Formulation

- **Use level:** **0.01–0.05%** (supplier-recommended); this low ceiling itself signals high intrinsic potency. Patent notes stronger SOD-mimetic effects "at 0.1% or greater," but routine cosmetic use stays ≤0.05%.
- **Phase / solubility:** Oil-soluble (lipophilic). Add to the **oil phase at the final stage**; dissolves well in aromatic esters (C12-15 alkyl benzoate) and ester emollients. A water-dispersible grade (Lipochroman AQO) exists for aqueous systems.
- **Temperature:** Process ≤70°C; keep lower if combined with oxidant-prone materials.
- **pH:** No strict pH dependence reported for the molecule; formulate to the rest of the system. (No published optimal-pH study located — **[unverified]**.)
- **Stability:** Marketed partly *as a stabilizer* of UV filters and oxidation-prone actives; it is itself oxidation-resistant relative to vitamin C. Good fit to protect labile ingredients.
- **Pairings:** Synergy shown with turmeric/curcuminoids (in-vitro) and demonstrated benefit alongside other antioxidants and UV filters; logical companion to vitamin E, vitamin C, ferulic acid, niacinamide. Conflicts: none well documented; avoid co-processing with strong oxidizers at high heat.
- **Sensory:** Used at trace levels, so essentially **no color, odor or texture impact** in finished product.

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation / sensitization:** Reassuring profile; very low reported irritation or allergen rates at cosmetic use levels (consistent with sub-0.05% dosing). SkinSAFE lists it without major allergen flags. Sources 17, 18.
- **EWG Skin Deep:** Listed; reported as **not** on EWG restricted/unacceptable lists. (Exact numeric hazard score not retrievable on this pass — **[unverified]**; EWG data depth for niche actives is often low.) Source 18.
- **Sun sensitivity:** None reported; it is photo-*protective*, not photosensitizing.
- **Pregnancy / breastfeeding:** No reproductive/developmental concerns reported; generally regarded as low-risk topically. **Not medical advice** — defer to an OB/medical professional in copy.
- **Formal safety review:** No dedicated published CIR (Cosmetic Ingredient Review) monograph located for Dimethylmethoxy Chromanol specifically. **[unverified]** — absence of a CIR monograph is a documentation gap, not evidence of harm.
- **Sourcing / sustainability:** Fully synthetic (chemically manufactured chroman), so no agricultural land/biodiversity footprint and consistent purity batch-to-batch; flip side is it is a petrochemistry-derived synthetic, which conflicts with a "natural/clean" narrative if the brand chooses one.

## 8. Regulatory & claims

Cosmetic ingredient (no drug status in US/EU). Per CLAUDE.md claims discipline:

- ✅ **Safe cosmetic language:** "antioxidant," "helps defend against environmental/oxidative stress," "helps protect the look of skin from pollution and UV-induced free-radical damage," "supports a more even-looking tone," "visibly," "helps maintain skin's youthful appearance." Anti-pollution / environmental-defense angles are well supported by the molecule's profile.
- ⚠️ **Risky / needs substantiation or legal review:** any "X% more potent than vitamin E / 20× vitamin E" claim (manufacturer data, not independent — substantiation file required), "boosts SOD/collagen," "repairs DNA," "treats hyperpigmentation," "prevents aging." These edge toward drug/structure-function territory or unsubstantiated comparative claims.
- Note: in the **EU**, comparative and "free-from"/superiority claims must meet the Common Criteria (Reg. 655/2013) — the "20×" line would not pass without robust support.
- Pigmentation/tyrosinase-inhibition claims rest on supplier statements + confounded blend data — **do not lead with brightening.**

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Cost tier:** **Premium per kg, cheap per finished unit.** Retail benchmark (Lotioncrafter, single-gram pricing): ~$15/g, $63.75/5 g, $273.75/25 g (~$11k/kg at retail; bulk wholesale is far lower). Because use level is 0.01–0.05%, **per-formula cost is negligible** — at 0.03% a kilo of cream uses ~0.3 g of active. Source 14.
- **Suppliers:** Lubrizol/Lipotec (originator, Lipochroman brand), plus Anecochem/Soho Aneco, Beri Pharma, Chenlang Bio for the generic INCI. Multiple sources = low supply risk. Sources 8, 10, 12.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

- **Hype:** "20× more powerful than vitamin E," "triple protection (ROS/RNS/RCS)," "anti-pollution shield," potent brightener. Driven by Lipotec/Lubrizol marketing and reseller copy.
- **Reality:**
  - The **mechanism is real and partly differentiated** (singlet-oxygen quenching is physically proven in skin tissue; nitrogen-radical/peroxynitrite targeting is a legitimate, under-served pathway).
  - The **potency multipliers ("20×," "173% cell viability," "beats BHT/trolox") are manufacturer assay claims**, not independent peer-reviewed head-to-heads. Treat as directional, not proven.
  - There is **no standalone human topical trial.** Human benefits (pigmentation, elasticity, TEWL) come from antioxidant *blends in sunscreens*, where UV filters and co-antioxidants plausibly do much of the work. Crediting DMC specifically is unsupported.
  - The **anti-glycation (RCS) and DNA-protection claims are essentially uncorroborated** for this molecule in skin. [unverified]

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**Conditional / supporting** — not a hero.

One-liner: A scientifically interesting, low-dose, "set-and-forget" antioxidant insurance ingredient with a genuinely differentiated nitrogen-radical/peroxynitrite mechanism and a clean safety/cost profile — but with no standalone human proof, so it belongs as a **supporting antioxidant** (especially in any environmental-defense or daytime/SPF-adjacent product), never as the marquee active or the reason-to-believe.

**Longevity relevance:** Directly addresses the **oxidative-stress** and **photoaging** hallmarks (and uniquely touches **nitrosative stress**, a real driver of "inflammaging" and ECM nitration that most antioxidant lineups ignore). That nitrogen-radical angle is the strongest reason to keep it on the table for a skin-*longevity* positioning — but the brand should frame it honestly as oxidative-defense support, not collagen/anti-aging proof.

**Fit with the barrier-repair thesis:** Tangential. It does not repair the barrier; it protects the barrier and other actives from oxidative degradation. Best used as a formulation-protecting + environmental-defense co-star, or as a credibility-building "science-forward antioxidant" in a daytime product.

→ Log under **considering** (supporting role). Revisit to hero status only if an independent, standalone, vehicle-controlled human study appears.
