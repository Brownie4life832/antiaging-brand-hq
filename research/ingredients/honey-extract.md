# Ingredient: Honey / Honey Extract (INCI: Mel / Mel Extract)

**Slug:** honey-extract · **Class:** Humectants & hydration actives
**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product

> **One-line verdict:** A genuinely multifunctional, well-tolerated humectant with the strongest human evidence in *wound/dermatitis* contexts and only *thin* direct topical evidence for cosmetic anti-aging. Best used as a **supporting humectant/soothing actor** — not as a hero "anti-aging" claim. See [§11](#11-verdict-for-the-brand).

---

## 1. Identity

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Common name** | Honey; honey extract |
| **INCI** | **Mel** (honey); **Mel Extract** (honey extract); historically "Honey" / "Honey Extract". Manuka honey is still labelled **Mel** with origin called out in marketing copy, not INCI. |
| **CAS** | 8028-66-8 (Honey) |
| **Source** | Concentrated nectar processed by honeybees (*Apis mellifera*) or stingless bees (e.g. *Heterotrigona/Tetragonula* — "kelulut" honey). Floral source (monofloral vs polyfloral) strongly shapes chemistry. |
| **Key forms in cosmetics** | (1) **Raw/whole honey** (humectant + actives, but sticky, microbiologically tricky); (2) **Honey extract** (solvent or glycerin extraction concentrating phenolics, lower sugar load); (3) **"Honey-derived" / bio-fermented honey**; (4) **Manuka honey / Medical-grade (e.g. Medihoney™)** — high methylglyoxal (MGO); (5) **Synthetic "honey extract" actives** (e.g. honey-mimetic oligosaccharide blends). |
| **Notable chemotypes** | *Manuka* (Leptospermum spp., high MGO, non-peroxide activity); *Tualang*, *Sidr*, *Acacia*, *Heather*, *Buckwheat* (high phenolic), *Kelulut/stingless-bee* (higher water, distinct organic acids). |

**Composition (typical floral honey).** ~80% sugars (≈38–40% fructose, ≈30–31% glucose, minor sucrose/maltose), ~17–20% water, plus minor but cosmetically relevant fractions: organic acids (gluconic acid ~0.5%), enzymes (glucose oxidase, catalase, diastase), amino acids, vitamins, minerals, and **phenolics/flavonoids** (chrysin, pinocembrin, pinobanksin, quercetin, kaempferol, luteolin, galangin, apigenin, hesperetin, myricetin) and phenolic acids (gallic, caffeic, p-coumaric, ferulic, dihydroxybenzoic acids). Naturally **acidic, pH ≈ 3.4–6.1 (most commonly ~3.9)**. [Refs 11, 12, 14]

## 2. Class & mechanism

Honey sits in the **humectant / hydration-active** class but is genuinely **polypharmacological**. Mechanistically relevant pathways:

1. **Humectancy / osmotic hydration** — high concentration of hygroscopic sugars (fructose/glucose) binds and holds water in the stratum corneum, acting much like glycerin or other small-molecule humectants. [Ref 9]
2. **Antioxidant / ROS scavenging** — phenolics and flavonoids scavenge reactive oxygen species, donate hydrogen, chelate metal ions, and can raise intracellular glutathione; this is the basis of most "anti-aging" mechanistic claims. [Refs 2, 12]
3. **Anti-inflammatory** — phenolic constituents inhibit iNOS and COX-2; manuka honey down-regulates IL-4–induced CCL26 (eotaxin-3) and stabilises mast cells in keratinocyte/mast-cell models. [Refs 2, 5]
4. **Antibacterial** — multiple mechanisms: low pH, osmotic stress, enzymatic **hydrogen peroxide** generation (glucose oxidase), and for manuka, **non-peroxide activity driven by methylglyoxal (MGO)**, derived non-enzymatically from dihydroxyacetone in *Leptospermum* nectar. [Refs 6, 7]
5. **ECM modulation (in vitro)** — down-regulates MMP-1/-3/-7/-9 and (in senescent fibroblasts) up-regulates type I collagen mRNA. [Refs 1, 4]
6. **Photoprotection (in vitro/ex vivo)** — flavonoids (notably chrysin) reduce UVB-induced ROS, DNA strand breaks, and MMP induction in keratinocytes and reconstructed epidermis. [Refs 1, 8]

> ⚠️ **Anti-aging honesty flag:** every item in 2–6 is mechanistically plausible, but the *human topical cosmetic* evidence for skin-aging endpoints is limited (see §4). The mechanism stack is largely **in-vitro / ex-vivo / wound-model**, not cosmetic-RCT.

## 4. Evidence review by tier

Ordered weakest-context → strongest-context for *our* use-case (topical cosmetic anti-aging). The brutal summary: **honey's best human evidence is in wound care and inflammatory dermatoses; the anti-aging/cosmetic case rests mainly on humectancy plus pre-clinical antioxidant/photoprotection data.**

### 4a. In-vitro (cell & biochemical)

- **Karapetsas et al., 2020, *Antioxidants* (Basel) 9(7):566 — DOI 10.3390/antiox9070566.** Ethyl-acetate extracts of 5 Greek honeys tested in **HaCaT keratinocytes** and **EpiDerm EPI-200 reconstructed human epidermis**. At 20 µg/mL, two extracts attenuated UVB-induced cell death (viability restored to ~62% and ~73%), **reduced protein carbonyls and DNA strand breaks (comet assay)**, and **suppressed UVB-induced MMP-3, -7 and -9** at mRNA and protein level. *Limitations:* extracts (not whole honey), single dose, no in-vivo confirmation, active compound(s) not isolated. [Ref 1]
- **Abdul Malik et al., 2020, *Journal of Food Biochemistry* 44(2):e13098 — DOI 10.1111/jfbc.13098.** Stingless-bee (kelulut) honey at 0.02% increased **human dermal fibroblast** viability (72 h), **down-regulated MMP-1** in pre-senescent and senescent cells, and **up-regulated type I collagen** in senescent cells. *Limitations:* single cell line, very low concentration, gene-expression endpoints only (no protein/skin data). [Ref 4]
- **Tyrosinase-inhibition screens** (e.g. Sardinian honeys, Tuberoso et al., 2018, *PMC6049736*): several monofloral honeys inhibit mushroom tyrosinase in vitro (thistle highest), supporting a *potential* brightening angle. *Limitations:* mushroom-tyrosinase assays correlate poorly with human melanocytes; massive floral variability. [Ref 13]
- **Antibacterial mechanism work** (Windsor/PLOS ONE 2016, Carter 2016 review): MGO content correlates with non-peroxide antibacterial activity of *Leptospermum* honeys; UMF/MGO grading systems quantify this. [Refs 6, 7]

### 4b. Animal

- Numerous rodent burn/excisional-wound models report faster epithelialization, angiogenesis, and reduced inflammation with topical honey (summarised in Refs 3, 16). *Relevance to cosmetic anti-aging is indirect* — these are healing-of-injury models, not chronological/photo-aging models.

### 4c. Human — ORAL

- Oral honey is studied for cough, glycemic, and antioxidant-status endpoints, **not for skin aging.** Dietary honey is still a sugar and contributes to **dietary AGE/glycemic load** debates relevant to skin glycation (§10). **No oral-honey skin-anti-aging RCT exists to borrow from.** Treat oral evidence as **irrelevant** to our topical use.

### 4d. Human — TOPICAL

**RCTs / controlled (but mostly wound & dermatosis, not cosmetic aging):**

- **Jull et al., 2015, Cochrane Systematic Review — *Honey as a topical treatment for wounds*, CD005083.pub4 — DOI 10.1002/14651858.CD005083.pub4.** Pooled RCT evidence: **moderate-quality evidence that honey heals partial-thickness burns faster** than some conventional dressings, and helps **infected post-operative wounds** vs antiseptic + gauze. For most other wound types evidence was **low quality / inconclusive**; authors call for larger, well-designed, blinded trials. *This is the single most authoritative honey-topical document — and its scope is wounds, not anti-aging.* [Ref 16]
- **Alangari et al., 2017, *Immunity, Inflammation and Disease* 5(2):190–199 — DOI 10.1002/iid3.153.** Proof-of-concept, **intra-individual paired** pilot (n=14): Medihoney™ (irradiated manuka) applied overnight ×7 days significantly reduced Three-Item-Severity score vs untreated contralateral atopic-dermatitis lesions (p<0.001); mechanistically ↓IL-4–induced CCL26 and ↓mast-cell degranulation. *Limitations:* small, open-label, non-randomised, non-validated score. [Ref 5]
- **Al-Waili, 2001, *European Journal of Medical Research* — chronic seborrheic dermatitis & dandruff (n≈30).** Topical 90%-diluted crude honey, every other day, improved itching/scaling and reduced relapse on weekly maintenance. *Limitations:* single-investigator, modest blinding/controls. [Ref 10]
- **Al-Waili honey/olive-oil/beeswax (1:1:1) series (2003–2005):** partially-controlled/single-blinded studies in **atopic dermatitis, psoriasis, diaper dermatitis, pityriasis versicolor, tinea** with reported symptom and corticosteroid-sparing improvements. *Limitations:* small samples, same research group, mixed formulation (confounded by olive oil + beeswax), weak blinding — **low evidentiary weight.** [Ref 10]

**Cosmetic / instrumental (closest to our anti-aging use — but not RCTs):**

- **Honey-containing hand-cream instrumental study** (multifloral honey 0/5/10/15% w/w, **n=24** split into two ~12-person groups, Corneometer/Tewameter/Cutometer/Visioscan/Visioline; 4 weeks). Reported ↑ skin moisturization up to ~29.7%, ↑ smoothness up to ~21.3%, ↓ wrinkle area up to ~21.4%, ↓ mean wrinkle depth up to ~11.7%. **Only the moisturization increase and wrinkle-depth reduction reached statistical significance vs placebo;** the smoothness and wrinkle-area figures are descriptive, not significant. *Limitations:* **very small (n=24)**, vehicle/multi-ingredient confounding, hand (not face) skin, short duration, instrumental-only endpoints. [Ref 9]
- **Hydration/barrier study of cosmetic matrices with bee products** (glycerin honey extract matrices; forest > flower honey for moisturizing). Supports humectancy but is a formulation-screening study, not a clinical efficacy trial. [Ref 9 cluster]

**Bottom line by tier:** Proven *for topical human use* → **soothing/anti-inflammatory in inflammatory dermatoses (small studies)**, **wound/burn healing (Cochrane, moderate)**, and **measurable short-term hydration/smoothing in cosmetic creams (instrumental, non-RCT)**. **Not proven topically:** collagen building, wrinkle reversal as a stand-alone active, depigmentation, senolysis, or "anti-glycation."

## 5. Topical bioavailability

- **Sugars/humectancy** act at the stratum-corneum surface — no penetration required for the hydration benefit. This is the most reliably "bioavailable" function.
- **Phenolic actives can penetrate, but delivery format matters enormously.** **Nowak et al., 2025, *BMC Complementary Medicine and Therapies* — DOI 10.1186/s12906-025-04786-1.** Franz-cell study on porcine skin: gallic acid and dihydroxybenzoic acids from honey **did permeate and accumulate** (e.g. GA up to ~78.85 µg/g skin from a heather-honey emulsion; ~7.8–15.9% of applied GA permeated over 24 h), with **hydrogel >> raw honey** as a carrier ("several times higher" penetration than pure honey). [Ref 14]
- **Implication:** to get the antioxidant/anti-inflammatory phenolic benefit, **honey works far better as a formulated extract in an appropriate base than as raw honey smeared on.** Raw-honey "DIY" delivery is humectant-dominant; the bioactive phenolic fraction is small and variable.

## 6. Formulation

| Parameter | Guidance |
|---|---|
| **Typical use level** | Whole honey commonly **1–10%** in leave-on; humectant/sensory effect scales with level. Cosmetic "honey extract" actives often **0.5–5%**. Manuka/medical contexts use far higher (functional dressings ~100%), not relevant to a cosmetic cream. |
| **pH** | Honey is acidic (~3.9). It will *lower* a formula's pH; buffer to skin-compatible **~4.5–5.5**. Compatible with acidic-loving partners (vitamin C, AHAs) but watch total acid load/irritation. |
| **Stability** | **Glucose-oxidase / H₂O₂ activity is destroyed by heat, dilution, light, and time** — so "raw honey's antibacterial enzyme system" is largely lost in a typical heated, water-diluted emulsion. Phenolics are more robust but still oxidation-sensitive. MGO (manuka) is heat/storage-stable. Sugars are hygroscopic → can affect texture and water activity. |
| **Preservation** | **Critical.** Diluted honey loses its self-preserving osmotic effect and becomes a *sugar-rich growth medium*. Requires a robust broad-spectrum preservative system and water-activity control. Raw honey can carry osmophilic yeasts/*Clostridium* spores — irrelevant to intact-skin cosmetics but relevant to sourcing/QC. |
| **Sensory** | Adds humectant slip and a soft, slightly tacky after-feel at higher levels; pleasant "natural" story. Can feel sticky if overdosed; pair with emollients/film-formers to offset. Color/odor vary by floral source (formulation/batch consistency challenge). |
| **Pairings (synergistic)** | Glycerin & other humectants; ceramides/occlusives (offset tack, support barrier — aligns with brand barrier thesis); niacinamide (soothing/barrier); panthenol; oat; antioxidants. |
| **Cautions / conflicts** | Strong oxidizers; very high active-acid stacks (cumulative irritation/pH); formulas where added reducing sugars are undesirable. Standardize on a **defined extract** (or graded manuka) for batch-to-batch consistency rather than raw commodity honey. |

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation:** Generally **very well tolerated** on intact skin; low irritation potential. Acidic pH is mild.
- **Sensitization / allergy:** **The main real concern.** Bee products carry contact-allergy risk, chiefly via **propolis contamination** (caffeates — 3-methyl-2-butenyl caffeate, phenylethyl caffeate — are potent sensitizers; sensitization ~10–15 days; diagnosed by patch testing propolis 10% pet. + the product). **Type I immediate reactions** (urticaria, angioedema, rarely anaphylaxis) are reported with honey/royal jelly/propolis/bee pollen, especially in pollen-/bee-allergic individuals. Use **propolis-depleted, defined honey/honey extract** and flag bee-allergy. [Refs 17, 18]
- **Sun:** No photosensitization; honey phenolics are mildly *photo-protective* in vitro. Not a sunscreen — make no SPF/"sun protection" claim.
- **Pregnancy/breastfeeding:** Topical cosmetic use poses no known specific concern (infant-botulism risk is an *oral-honey-in-infants* issue, not topical adult use). Standard "consult your doctor" prudence only.
- **Sourcing safety:** Adulteration (syrup cutting), pesticide/antibiotic residues, MGO-grade misrepresentation, and microbial spores are genuine commodity-honey risks → demand certified, tested supply (see §9).

## 8. Regulatory & claims (cosmetic vs drug — per CLAUDE.md)

- **Honey/Mel is an established cosmetic ingredient** (humectant/skin-conditioning). As a *cosmetic*, we can describe **appearance and feel**, not disease treatment or structure/function changes.
- ✅ **Safer cosmetic language:** "humectant that helps the skin hold onto moisture," "helps skin feel soft, smooth and comforted," "supports a hydrated, healthy-looking complexion," "rich in antioxidant plant compounds," "soothing." Use "helps improve the appearance of," "visibly," "supports," "nourishes," "hydrates."
- ⚠️ **Risky / likely-drug or substantiation-heavy — flag for legal:**
  - "Heals wounds / heals burns," "treats eczema / dermatitis / psoriasis," "antibacterial / antimicrobial" (the wound/manuka evidence is real but pushes a *cosmetic* product toward **drug** territory — e.g. medical-grade manuka dressings are regulated devices/drugs, not cosmetics).
  - "Boosts collagen," "reverses wrinkles," "anti-glycation," "brightens / fades dark spots" — **not supported by topical human cosmetic data** for honey; would need product-specific clinical substantiation.
  - "Anti-aging" as a blanket claim — carries regulatory weight; keep it to *appearance* framing.
- **Manuka-specific:** "Medical-grade," "MGO/UMF" therapeutic claims belong to regulated wound-care products; **do not import them into cosmetic copy.**

See claims cheat-sheet in [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md) and guardrails in [`../../CLAUDE.md`](../../CLAUDE.md).

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Cost tier:** **Low–moderate** as a raw material. Commodity floral honey is cheap; **defined honey extracts** and **graded manuka (high MGO/UMF)** are markedly more expensive and carry a premium "story." Manuka pricing scales steeply with MGO/UMF grade.
- **Suppliers/forms:** Widely available as raw honey, glycerin/water honey extracts, spray-dried honey powders, and branded manuka actives. Choose **cosmetic-grade, propolis-reduced, traceable** material with CoA (sugar profile, MGO if manuka, pesticide/antibiotic screen, microbial limits).
- **Supply considerations:** Honey is one of the **most adulterated foods globally** (syrup dilution, false floral/origin claims, MGO inflation). For a credibility-driven brand, **provenance + third-party testing is non-negotiable**; consider authentication markers (e.g. **leptosperin** for genuine manuka). [Ref 7]
- **Sustainability/ethics story:** Pollinator/biodiversity narrative is a marketing asset *if* sourcing is ethical and verifiable; vegan-positioning brands exclude honey entirely (note this trade-off for positioning, which is OPEN per CLAUDE.md).

## 10. Hype vs evidence

| Marketing claim seen in the wild | Reality |
|---|---|
| "Ancient miracle anti-aging healer" | Long traditional use + real wound/derm data, but **anti-aging-specific human topical evidence is thin** (mostly humectancy + pre-clinical antioxidant). |
| "Boosts collagen, reverses wrinkles" | Collagen-I↑/MMP↓ is **in-vitro fibroblast** only; wrinkle-area reduction comes from **multi-ingredient cosmetic creams**, not honey isolated. Overclaim. |
| "Antibacterial — fights acne/balances microbiome" | Antibacterial data is **wound/manuka**; the self-preserving effect is **lost on dilution**, and healthy-skin microbiome benefit is unproven. Don't claim. |
| "Natural anti-glycation/AGE-fighter" | **Backwards-risk:** honey is ~80% reducing sugars and manuka's signature MGO is a **glycating dicarbonyl** implicated in AGE formation. Phenolic antioxidants may partly offset, but net effect on skin glycation is **unestablished** — avoid the claim entirely. [Refs 6, 15] |
| "Brightens, fades dark spots" | Some honeys inhibit tyrosinase **in vitro (mushroom enzyme)** with huge floral variability; **no topical human pigmentation trial.** |
| "Deeply nourishing humectant" | **True and defensible** — this is honey's most honest, evidence-supported cosmetic role. [Ref 9] |

**Net:** honey is *under-hyped* as a humectant and *over-hyped* as a stand-alone anti-aging active.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**Rating: ⚠️ SUPPORTING (conditional) — not a hero.**

- **Why supporting, not hero:** The brand's credibility rests on real science, and a premium **barrier-repair** thesis. Honey's *honest* strengths — humectancy, soothing, antioxidant phenolics, a warm natural-luxe story — make it an excellent **supporting humectant/comfort agent** in a barrier cream, alongside ceramides/glycerin/niacinamide. But the **direct human topical anti-aging evidence is too thin to anchor a hero claim**, and the strongest human data (wounds, dermatoses) is **drug-adjacent** and off-limits for cosmetic copy.
- **Conditions for inclusion:**
  1. Use a **defined, propolis-reduced, traceable honey extract** (or graded manuka with a story) — *not* raw commodity honey — for consistency, safety, and bioavailability (§5, §9).
  2. **Formulate for phenolic delivery** (suitable base/hydrogel), buffer pH ~4.5–5.5, robust preservation, offset tack with emollients.
  3. **Claim discipline:** humectant/soothing/antioxidant-rich/appearance language only; **no** heal/treat/antibacterial/collagen/anti-glycation claims (§8).
  4. **Allergen labelling/flagging** for bee-product sensitivity; reconsider entirely if a vegan positioning is chosen (positioning is OPEN).
- **Longevity / skin-longevity note:** Honey's contribution to a *longevity* narrative is **indirect** — protecting the barrier and reducing oxidative/inflammatory burden (inflammaging) are legitimate longevity-adjacent mechanisms, but the supporting data is pre-clinical/borrowed. The **glycation paradox** (sugar-rich, MGO-bearing) is a reason to keep honey in a *supporting humectant* lane rather than building a "fights aging at the cellular level" story around it. If the brand wants a hero antioxidant/anti-glycation actor, prefer better-evidenced ingredients and let honey play comfort-humectant.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

*Verified 2026-06-19 against primary sources (PubMed/PMC/MDPI/Cochrane/Wiley/Springer). Every citation that anchors a headline claim was located in a real indexed database and read at abstract/summary level; load-bearing numbers were checked against the source.*

**Overall grade: C+ (weak-to-moderate, context-dependent).**
- The strongest *human topical* evidence (Cochrane, manuka derm pilots) is for **wounds and inflammatory dermatoses — not cosmetic aging — and is drug-adjacent**, so it cannot anchor cosmetic copy. Grade for that body alone: **B** (Cochrane = moderate quality; derm studies = small/open-label).
- The *cosmetic anti-aging* case rests on **one small (n=24) non-RCT instrumental cream study** plus **in-vitro/ex-vivo** photoprotection and fibroblast data. Grade for the cosmetic-aging claim specifically: **C** (humectancy is defensible; everything beyond it is pre-clinical or single-small-study).
- The dossier is unusually honest for its category; it already self-flags most weaknesses. This verification tightens two spots where it slightly outran its evidence.
