# Ingredient: Vitamin E / Tocopherols (INCI: Tocopherol; Tocopheryl Acetate; Tocotrienols; Tocopheryl Linoleate; Tocophersolan; etc.)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Class:** Antioxidants · **Status:** ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product

> **One-line read:** Vitamin E is the skin's *native* lipid-phase antioxidant and an excellent, cheap, well-tolerated **formulation workhorse** — but as a standalone "anti-aging actor" the human topical evidence is thin. Its strongest, best-proven role is as the **"E" in the C+E+ferulic antioxidant network** and as an oxidation-protecting/conditioning base ingredient. Verdict: **supporting, near-ubiquitous**, not a hero.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common name:** Vitamin E (a *family*, not a single molecule).
- **The eight natural vitamers:** four **tocopherols** (α-, β-, γ-, δ-) and four **tocotrienols** (α-, β-, γ-, δ-). All share a chromanol head (the antioxidant business end) + a phytyl (tocopherol, saturated) or isoprenoid (tocotrienol, unsaturated) tail.
- **α-Tocopherol** is the most biologically prominent form in human tissue and the *dominant antioxidant of the stratum corneum*; **RRR-α-tocopherol** (natural, "d-") is more potent than synthetic **all-rac-α-tocopherol** ("dl-").
- **Key INCI forms / derivatives used in skincare:**
  - **Tocopherol** — free, active, antioxidant form (the real actor; also unstable).
  - **Tocopheryl Acetate** — esterified, *much more stable* (the most-used form in cosmetics by far), but is a **pro-drug** that must be hydrolyzed in skin to become active — and that conversion is poor (see §5).
  - **Tocopheryl Linoleate / Linoleate-Oleate** — ester forms; historically implicated in a large contact-dermatitis outbreak (see §7).
  - **Tocopheryl Phosphate / Potassium Ascorbyl Tocopheryl Phosphate (EAC-style hybrids)** — water-dispersible derivatives.
  - **Tocophersolan (TPGS)** — PEGylated, water-soluble; solubilizer/antioxidant.
  - **Tocotrienols / Tocotrienol-Rich Fraction (TRF)** — palm/rice/annatto-derived; marketed as "more potent" (in-vitro true; human topical data limited).

---

## 2. Class & mechanism

Vitamin E is a **lipophilic (fat-soluble) chain-breaking antioxidant**. Its core jobs at the skin/cell level:

1. **Lipid-peroxidation chain terminator.** The chromanol ring donates a hydrogen to lipid peroxyl radicals (LOO•), halting the self-propagating oxidation of membrane and stratum-corneum lipids (squalene, ceramides, fatty acids). This is its defining, non-controversial mechanism — it protects the *lipid phase* where water-soluble antioxidants (vitamin C, glutathione) can't reach.
2. **It is the skin's principal native lipid antioxidant.** α-Tocopherol is the predominant antioxidant of the stratum corneum *by concentration and by susceptibility*, distributed in a **gradient** — lowest at the surface, highest in deep layers — and is delivered physiologically to the surface via **sebum** (sebaceous secretion), which is why upper-face skin is richer in it than arm skin (Thiele et al.; Ekanayake-Mudiyanselage). This native gradient is depleted first by oxidative insult.
3. **UV/ozone "first responder" that gets consumed.** A single sub-erythemal dose of solar-simulated UV depletes stratum-corneum α-tocopherol by ~45% and γ-tocopherol by ~35% — making SC vitamin E one of the **earliest, most sensitive in-vivo biomarkers of photo-oxidation** (Thiele et al., *J Invest Dermatol* 1998). Ozone adds to this depletion. Topical vitamin E is sacrificially oxidized to *spare* this native pool.
4. **Recycling / antioxidant-network node.** Oxidized tocopheroxyl radical is regenerated back to active tocopherol by vitamin C (ascorbate) and, downstream, by glutathione — the basis of the **C+E synergy** (see §4/§6).
5. **Secondary, less-settled roles:** UV-absorption in the UVB range (weak, *not* a sunscreen); modulation of inflammatory signaling (↓ prostaglandin/COX, ↓ pro-inflammatory cytokines — strongest for tocotrienols in vitro); weak tyrosinase inhibition (pigmentation, mostly in vitro); some "antioxidant-independent" cell-signaling effects (PKC modulation) described in non-skin tissue.

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

| Hallmark | Relevance of Vitamin E | Strength (for *topical human* use) |
|---|---|---|
| **Oxidative stress** | Core mechanism — native SC lipid antioxidant, lipid-peroxidation chain-breaker | **Strong mechanistically**; this is the real story |
| **Photoaging** | Sacrificially protects SC vitamin-E pool from UV; potent *in combination* (C+E+ferulic) | Strong in-vitro/animal; in-combination evidence is good but **largely porcine** (one small human study); **weak as monotherapy** in humans |
| **Inflammaging** | ↓ UV-induced cytokines/erythema (esp. tocotrienols) | Moderate (mostly in-vitro + small clinical for TRF) |
| **Barrier / hydration (TEWL)** | Emollient; supports SC lipids; some evidence it supports ceramide milieu; protects barrier lipids from oxidation | Moderate (conditioning) |
| **Pigmentation / tone** | Weak tyrosinase inhibition; brightening only meaningful *in combination* (C+E, α-tocopheryl ferulate) | **Weak** alone |
| **Cellular senescence** | Not a demonstrated senolytic/senomorphic in skin | None (topical) |
| **Collagen / ECM loss** | No direct collagen-synthesis role (unlike vitamin C); only indirect via reducing oxidative MMP activation | Weak/indirect |
| **Glycation / AGEs** | Antioxidants may blunt glyco-oxidation; not specifically shown for topical E | Weak/none |
| **Mitochondrial decline** | Protects membrane lipids from peroxidation (general) | Weak/indirect |
| **Microbiome** | No meaningful evidence | None |

**Bottom line on hallmarks:** Vitamin E is an **oxidative-stress / photoaging-defense** ingredient first and foremost. It does *not* do the collagen-building or turnover work that retinoids and vitamin C do.

## 4. Evidence review by tier (the core of this dossier)

> **Reading guide:** Vitamin E has a *huge* literature, but it is lopsided — strong mechanistic/in-vitro/animal data, a famously **null oral-supplement photoprotection story**, and **surprisingly weak standalone topical-human RCT** data. Its best *efficacy* evidence is **in combination** (C+E+ferulic) — but be honest that the headline C+E and C+E+ferulic photoprotection numbers come from **pig-skin** experiments; the genuinely *human* topical evidence is thinner than the C+E+ferulic marketing implies. Be honest about this internally.

### Tier 1 — In-vitro / mechanistic (STRONG, well-replicated)
- **Lipid-peroxidation inhibition** in keratinocyte/fibroblast and SC-lipid models is the most robust, repeatedly confirmed finding — the foundational mechanism. (Reviewed in Thiele & Ekanayake-Mudiyanselage, *Mol Aspects Med* 2007 [3]; "Antioxidant network of the stratum corneum," Thiele [4].)
- **Tocotrienols (TRF)** in HaCaT keratinocytes markedly attenuate UV-induced up-regulation of pro-inflammatory cytokines and, in B16 melanocytes, blunt blue-light-induced oxidative stress and melanogenesis (anti-tyrosinase) (Yap et al. 2018 [7]; Lim et al. 2023 [8]).
- **α-Tocopheryl ferulate** depigments normal human melanocytes in culture (Funasaka et al., *Pigment Cell Res* 2000) — mechanistic basis for the "brightening in combination" idea (in vitro only).
- **Caveat:** in-vitro potency (especially tocotrienols >> tocopherols) does **not** reliably translate to topical human outcomes.

### Tier 2 — Animal (STRONG for photoprotection mechanism)
- **McVean & Liebler / Lopez-Torres et al.** — topical α-tocopherol on murine skin **modulated the antioxidant network and diminished UV-induced oxidative damage**: reduced epidermal lipid hydroperoxide formation after UV, raised dermal superoxide dismutase (SOD) activity ~30%, protected epidermal glutathione peroxidase and SOD from UV depletion, and increased epidermal total/reduced glutathione ~50% (*Br J Dermatol* / related, 1998) [9].
- **Topically applied tocopherols *and* tocotrienols protect murine skin from UV-induced oxidative damage** — both vitamer classes effective, α-tocopherol the benchmark (Weber/Traber et al., *Free Radic Biol Med* 1997) [10].
- These animal data are the strongest *direct* evidence that **topical** vitamin E does something real against photo-oxidation — but they are rodent, not human, and use idealized (often occluded) dosing.

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL (mostly NULL for photoprotection; mixed for atopic dermatitis)
- **Werninghaus et al. (1994)** — double-blind, placebo-controlled: **400 IU/day oral α-tocopherol** produced **no** clinical or histologic difference in UVB response vs placebo. **Oral vitamin E alone does not photoprotect.** [11]
- **Eberlein-König, Placzek & Przybilla (1998)** — combined **oral vitamin C (2 g) + vitamin E (1000 IU)** raised the minimal erythema dose ~1.5×; **either vitamin alone was ineffective** (*J Am Acad Dermatol*) [12]. Synergy is real but modest, and requires high doses *with* vitamin C.
- **Atopic dermatitis (oral):** several small RCTs suggest benefit — e.g. 96-subject trial of 400 IU/day d-α-tocopherol showed "great improvement/near resolution" in ~60% vs ~2% placebo over 8 months (Tsoureli-Nikita et al. 2002); a 45-subject RCT found SCORAD reductions of ~36% (E), ~35% (D), ~64% (E+D combined) [13]. *This is an oral, eczema-specific signal — not a topical anti-aging claim.*
- **Takeaway:** oral vitamin E is **not** a credible photoprotection or anti-aging route on its own; do not borrow oral eczema data to support a topical anti-aging cream.

### Tier 4a — Topical, in COMBINATION (the best efficacy evidence — but mostly PORCINE, not human)
This is where vitamin E earns its place — almost always as a *partner*, not a soloist. **Important correction:** the two "landmark" Pinnell-lab papers everyone cites for the famous photoprotection numbers were run on **pig (porcine) skin, not human skin** (verified 2026-06-19). Pig skin is a reasonable human proxy, but these are *not* human RCT data and should not be presented as such.
- **Lin, Selim, Shea, Pinnell et al. (2003)** — topical **15% L-ascorbic acid + 1% α-tocopherol** gave ~4× photoprotection (vs ~2× for C alone), reduced thymine dimers and sunburn cells; C+E synergistic and superior to either alone. **Conducted on pig skin**, applied 4 days, solar-simulator challenge (*J Am Acad Dermatol*) [14].
- **Lin, Lin, Gupta, Pinnell et al. (2005) — "CE Ferulic" paper** — adding **0.5–1% ferulic acid** to 15% C + 1% E **stabilized** the vitamins and **doubled** photoprotection (~4× → ~8×), cutting erythema, sunburn-cell and thymine-dimer formation, and apoptosis (caspase-3/7) (*J Invest Dermatol*) [15]. **This is the landmark formulation paper** and the basis of the entire C+E+ferulic serum category — but it too was run on **swine skin**, not human. Note: vitamin E is essential to the network, but it is the *combination* that performs.
- **Murray et al. (2008)** — a stabilized topical C+E+ferulic solution provided meaningful protection of **human** skin against UV damage (thymine dimers, p53, sunburn cells, cytokines). The **only genuinely human study** of the trio here — and the authors note the patient number was **"relatively small"** (*J Am Acad Dermatol*) [16].
- **Rattanawiwatpong et al. (2020)** — split-face RCT of a topical vitamin C + vitamin E + raspberry-leaf-cell-culture serum showed anti-aging/brightening improvements over 12 weeks (small, combination, industry-adjacent) [17].
- **In-situ antioxidant RCT (20 volunteers, 30 days)** — a "pre-tocopheryl" (glucoside-stabilized E) product significantly raised skin antioxidant capacity at 15 and 30 days under controlled UVA, addressing free α-tocopherol's photoinstability (Cosm dermo-cosmetic RCT) [18].

### Tier 4b — Human TOPICAL, MONOTHERAPY / lower-quality (WEAK to NEGATIVE)
Be brutally honest — the standalone topical-E human record is unimpressive:
- **Baumann & Spencer (1999)** — double-blind RCT, 15 post-surgical patients, vitamin-E-in-emollient vs emollient on split scars, 2×/day × 4 wk: in **90% of cases topical vitamin E had no effect or *worsened* scar appearance, and 33% developed contact dermatitis.** Authors: **discourage** topical vitamin E on surgical wounds (*Dermatol Surg*) [19]. The single most-cited cautionary study.
- **Jenkins et al. (1986)** — vitamin E (with steroids) **failed** to reduce post-reconstructive scar formation [20].
- **Striae gravidarum (stretch marks):** Cochrane-level synthesis finds **no good evidence** any topical (including vitamin-E-containing creams) prevents stretch marks; the few "positive" trials are poorly randomized/unblinded or test multi-ingredient creams (Centella + tocopherol + collagen-elastin) where E's contribution is unattributable (Young & Jewell, Cochrane; Buchanan/Korgavkar trials) [21].
- **Visible-light pigmentation:** "Topical Vitamin E Cream Does Not Prevent Visible Light-Induced Pigmentation" (2018) — **negative** for a real-world endpoint [22].
- **Single-agent topical-E anti-wrinkle RCTs of good quality are essentially absent** — most "anti-aging" data are for blends.

**Net evidence verdict:** Mechanism = strong. Animal photoprotection = strong. **Standalone topical human anti-aging = weak/unproven.** Topical **in the C+E+ferulic network = good, but the headline numbers are porcine; genuinely human-topical confirmation is limited (one small study, Murray 2008).** Oral monotherapy photoprotection = null.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **Free tocopherol** penetrates the lipophilic stratum corneum reasonably well and is retained; it integrates into the skin's existing lipid antioxidant pool.
- **Tocopheryl acetate (the common form) is a pro-drug problem.** It penetrates fine but must be **enzymatically hydrolyzed to free tocopherol** to be antioxidant-active — and human skin hydrolyzes it **slowly and inefficiently**:
  - In hairless-mouse/rat skin, conversion occurred mainly in deeper layers, was greater under **occlusion**, and after a single application **no hydrolysis** was detectable at 5 h; only after ~5 days of dosing did free-E rise to a few-fold above baseline (Norkus et al. 1993; Baschong; rat in-vivo study) [23].
  - **UVB irradiation paradoxically increases** both absorption and hydrolysis of tocopheryl acetate — i.e., it's converted *more* exactly when skin is stressed.
  - Several analyses argue **human skin may lack efficient esterase capacity**, so much topically applied tocopheryl acetate stays as the inactive ester.
- **Practical implication:** if you want *antioxidant action*, **free tocopherol beats tocopheryl acetate**; if you only need an oxidation-stabilizer/conditioning agent, the acetate's superior stability is the point. Glucoside/phosphate derivatives and encapsulation (liposomes, NLC) are used to combine stability with delivery.

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:** typically **0.5–1% α-tocopherol** as an *active* antioxidant (the C+E+ferulic standard uses **1%**). As an oxidation-stabilizer/preservative-booster for a formula's own oils, **0.05–0.5%** is enough. Industry leave-on use of tocopherol averaged ~5.4% historically; tocopheryl acetate appears up to much higher in niche products (see §7), but **more is not better** for skin benefit.
- **pH:** Wide tolerance; oil-soluble and pH-stable across normal cosmetic ranges (a key advantage over low-pH L-ascorbic acid). In a C+E+ferulic serum, the system runs low-pH (~2.5–3.5) for the *ascorbic acid*, which E tolerates fine.
- **Stability:** **Free tocopherol is the fragile one** — oxidizes and is *photo-unstable* (free α-tocopherol can be ~completely depleted within ~15 min of solar-simulated UV). Use **opaque/airless packaging, antioxidant pairings, and anhydrous or low-water systems.** **Tocopheryl acetate is far more stable** (why it dominates as a base antioxidant). Glucoside derivatives solve photo-instability.
- **Pairings (synergy):**
  - **Vitamin C (ascorbate)** — regenerates oxidized E; classic, evidence-backed (§4).
  - **Ferulic acid** — stabilizes C+E and doubles photoprotection (§4) — the gold-standard trio.
  - **Tocotrienols** — complementary vitamer profile.
  - It also **protects other oils/actives** (retinoids, plant oils) in the formula from going rancid — a real, underrated reason it's in almost everything.
- **Conflicts:** Few. It is broadly compatible. Main "conflict" is **with oxygen/light/time** (its own degradation). No major actives are inactivated by vitamin E.
- **Sensory:** Oily/emollient, slight drag; can feel rich. Pleasant in creams/oils; can feel heavy in lightweight serums at high %.

## 7. Safety

- **Overall:** Very well tolerated; **GRAS-level safety profile.** The **CIR Expert Panel (2018 re-review of 14 tocopherols/tocotrienols)** concluded they are **safe as used** in cosmetics [1]. Tocopherol historically used up to ~5.4% (leave-on); **tocopheryl acetate reported up to ~36%** in niche leave-ons (e.g. cuticle softeners) without safety concern at those levels.
- **Irritation:** Low at cosmetic concentrations.
- **Sensitization / allergic contact dermatitis (the real caveat):**
  - Vitamin E **can** cause ACD — most notoriously a **large outbreak (~931 cases, "mainly from one large study" per the Kosari 2010 review)** of papular/follicular contact dermatitis linked to a **tocopheryl-ester** cosmetic line (oxidation products implicated) [1][24]. *(The often-repeated "~1,000-case Switzerland ~1992 tocopheryl-linoleate" framing is broadly consistent with the literature but the precise country/year/ester attribution is not fully substantiated by the cited review abstract — treat the specifics as approximate.)*
  - The Baumann scar RCT saw **33% contact dermatitis** to topical vitamin E [19] — high, though that population (broken/healing skin) is unusually reactive.
  - Reviews conclude true sensitization is **uncommon relative to ubiquitous use**, but it is a **recognized allergen**; oxidized/degraded vitamin E is the higher-risk form (Kosari et al. 2010; "Vitamin E contact allergy: a controversial subject," 2012) [24][25].
- **Sun sensitivity:** Does **not** photosensitize; it is photoprotective/antioxidant. (But it is **not** a sunscreen.)
- **Pregnancy:** Topical vitamin E is considered low-risk and is common in pregnancy products; standard "consult your doctor" boilerplate applies. (High-dose *oral* vitamin E supplementation is a separate matter and not our concern for a topical.)
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** Most cosmetic vitamin E is **synthetic (all-rac, from petrochemical/plant feedstock)** or **natural (RRR, from soybean/sunflower distillates)**; tocotrienols from **palm/rice bran/annatto.** Palm-derived tocotrienols carry the usual **palm-oil sustainability** scrutiny (RSPO certification matters for a premium brand). Soy/sunflower-derived natural E is the cleaner story.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Cosmetic vs. drug line:** Vitamin E is a **cosmetic ingredient** (antioxidant / skin-conditioning). It is **not** an OTC sunscreen active and **must not be implied to replace SPF.** Antioxidant/photo-*defense* support language is fine; "protects from sun/sun damage," "treats," "heals," "repairs scars" cross into drug territory.
- **Honesty constraint specific to vitamin E:** because standalone topical human efficacy is weak, **efficacy claims should ride on the *combination* (C+E+ferulic) and on the antioxidant-network mechanism**, not on vitamin E alone. Avoid the popular "vitamin E heals scars/erases stretch marks" trope — it is **contradicted by the best evidence** (Baumann; Cochrane) and is a litigation/credibility risk.
- ✅ **Safer cosmetic language:** "antioxidant," "helps defend the look of skin against environmental/oxidative stress," "supports the skin barrier," "nourishing/conditioning," "helps keep the formula fresh" (its stabilizer role is literally true and uncontroversial).
- ⚠️ **Risky / drug-like or unsupported:** "heals/fades scars," "removes stretch marks," "brightens dark spots" (alone), "boosts collagen," "prevents wrinkles," "sun protection." Flag any of these for substantiation/legal.
- See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Cost tier: LOW** (one of the cheapest functional antioxidants). Tocopheryl acetate is commodity-cheap and stable; natural RRR-tocopherol and **tocotrienols/TRF are pricier** (premium-story potential, e.g. annatto-derived tocotrienol).
- **Suppliers:** DSM-Firmenich (key global producer), BASF, ADM, Davos Life Science (tocotrienols), Spec-Chem, plus countless distributors. **Universally available**, no supply risk.
- **Quality levers for a premium brand:** specify **natural RRR-α-tocopherol** and/or a **mixed-tocopherol/tocotrienol** profile; ensure **fresh, non-oxidized** material; for tocotrienols, prefer **annatto or RSPO-certified** sources over uncertified palm.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

- **Hype:** "Vitamin E heals scars," "fades/erases stretch marks," "the ultimate anti-aging vitamin," "vitamin E oil repairs skin." The pierce-a-capsule-onto-a-scar folk remedy is **the most over-claimed use and is contradicted** by the best RCT (worsened scars + 33% dermatitis) [19].
- **Also overstated:** standalone "brightening" and standalone wrinkle reduction.
- **Under-appreciated (real):** (1) it's the skin's **own** dominant lipid antioxidant and a sensitive UV-stress biomarker; (2) it is **essential to the C+E+ferulic network** that *is* well-proven; (3) it quietly **protects the rest of the formula** (oils, retinoids) from oxidation — a genuine, unglamorous reason it belongs in almost every formula. The honest framing: **great teammate, mediocre soloist.**

## 11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️→✅ **SUPPORTING (near-default inclusion), not a hero.**
- **One-line reasoning:** Vitamin E is a cheap, safe, well-tolerated, evidence-backed *network* antioxidant and formula-stabilizer that earns its place as the **"E" in a C + E + ferulic-acid daytime defense system** and as oxidation insurance across the line — but its standalone topical anti-aging evidence is weak, so it should never carry a product's hero claim or revive the debunked "scar/stretch-mark healer" story.
- **Longevity-relevance note:** Fits the "**oxidative-stress / photoaging defense**" pillar of skin longevity — it protects the skin's *native* antioxidant reserve and lipid barrier from the daily UV/ozone/pollution oxidation that drives extrinsic aging. It does **not** act on collagen synthesis, senescence, or cellular-longevity pathways (no SIRT/NAD/autophagy story like resveratrol or spermidine). Best positioned as **foundational defense**, paired with a hero actor (retinoid PM / vitamin C AM).

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

_Verified 2026-06-19 against primary sources (PubMed abstracts / publisher pages)._

**Overall evidence grade: C+ (leaning B for the C+E+ferulic *combination* claim, C/D for standalone topical-E anti-aging).**
- The dossier's *core thesis* — "great teammate, mediocre soloist; standalone topical-E human anti-aging is weak; oral monotherapy is null; the real value is the antioxidant network + formula stabilization" — is **well-supported and survives scrutiny.** The dossier is admirably honest and was *already* hedging in the right direction.
- It does **not** earn an A, because the single biggest efficacy pillar (C+E and C+E+ferulic photoprotection) rests largely on **animal (pig) skin**, and the file originally presented it as human evidence.

### What checked out (citations real and accurately summarized)
- **[5] Thiele 1998** — human SC α-toc −45% / γ-toc −35% after one suberythemal UV dose. **Verified, human in vivo.** Accurate.
- **[9] Lopez-Torres 1998** — murine; SOD +30%, GSH +50%, ↓lipid hydroperoxide. **Verified, correctly labeled as animal.** Accurate.
- **[11] Werninghaus 1994** — 400 IU/day oral α-toc, double-blind placebo-controlled, **no meaningful photoprotection.** Verified. Accurate.
- **[16] Murray 2008** — C+E+ferulic, **genuinely human**, significant photoprotection; authors flag small n. Verified. Accurate.
- **[19] Baumann 1999** — 15 patients, double-blind; **90% no effect or worse, 33% contact dermatitis**, authors discourage use. Verified verbatim. Accurate (and appropriately the dossier's headline cautionary study).
- **[22] Mereniuk 2018** — human RCT, topical vitamin E **did not** prevent visible-light pigmentation. Verified. Accurate.

### Main caveats (what an adversary should hold against this dossier)
1. **Species laundering in "Tier 4a" (the most important issue, now corrected).** The two famous Pinnell-lab papers were on **pig skin, not human**:
   - **[14] Lin/Selim/Shea/Pinnell 2003** (C+E ~4×): **porcine.**
   - **[15] CE Ferulic, Lin 2005** (~4×→~8× doubling): **swine.**
   These were filed under a header literally titled "Human TOPICAL … STRONGEST human-topical evidence." That overstated the human evidence base. **Only Murray 2008 [16] in that tier is human, and it is explicitly small.** Pig skin is a defensible human proxy, so the underlying efficacy claim is not *false* — but it is **animal data dressed as human data**, which is exactly the failure mode this review was asked to catch. The file's Tier-4a header, reading guide, net verdict, and hallmark table have been edited to say so.
2. **"Doubling photoprotection (~8×)" is a porcine, single-lab, manufacturer-associated number.** The Pinnell C+E+ferulic work underpins a commercial serum platform (SkinCeuticals); it is real and replicated *within that lab lineage* but is not an independent, multi-center human RCT. Treat ~8× as a porcine ceiling, not a guaranteed human in-use effect.
3. **Combination ≠ vitamin E.** Even where the combination evidence is good, it does not isolate E's contribution. The dossier acknowledges this; keep it front-of-mind for any claim copy.
4. **Atopic-dermatitis oral RCTs [13] are real but off-target** (oral, eczema, not topical anti-aging). The dossier already warns against borrowing them — good; do not let them migrate into anti-aging claims.
5. **Contact-dermatitis outbreak specifics are softer than stated.** The Kosari 2010 review abstract reports **~931 cases "mainly from one large study"** but (per the abstract) does **not** itself pin down "Switzerland, ~1992, tocopheryl linoleate." That specific framing is plausible and circulates in the literature, but is not substantiated by the cited source alone — softened in §7.
6. **[2] is self-flagged as unverified** in the source list; mechanism is corroborated elsewhere, so this is low-risk, but it should not be cited as a standalone authority.
7. **Industry-funding / small-n flags:** [14][15][16] share the Pinnell lab and the C+E+ferulic commercial lineage (conflict-of-interest-adjacent); [16] is small-n; [17] is described in-file as "small, combination, industry-adjacent"; [18] is a 20-subject manufacturer dermo-cosmetic study measuring a *surrogate* (antioxidant capacity), not a clinical anti-aging endpoint. None are large independent human anti-aging RCTs.

### Claims that were softened / should stay softened
- ✅ Done: "Tier 4a … STRONGEST human-topical evidence" → relabeled as **mostly porcine**, with [14]/[15] marked pig/swine and [16] marked as the only (small) human study.
- ✅ Done: reading guide, net verdict, and photoaging hallmark row now state the headline photoprotection numbers are **animal-derived**.
- ✅ Done: contact-dermatitis outbreak specifics hedged.
- **Keep avoiding** (already correct in §8/§10): "heals/fades scars," "removes stretch marks," "boosts collagen," "prevents wrinkles," "sun protection," standalone "brightening." These remain **D-grade / contradicted** for topical E and are litigation/credibility risks.
- **Use only defensibly:** "antioxidant," "helps defend against oxidative/environmental stress," "supports the barrier," "keeps the formula fresh (stabilizer)" — and frame any photoprotection-network story as **supportive, combination-based, and not a substitute for SPF.**

**Bottom line:** The dossier's *conclusion* is sound and its skepticism is genuine; the defect was that its single strongest-looking evidence tier quietly leaned on pig-skin studies. With that corrected, the honest grade is **C+ overall** (mechanism strong; standalone topical-E anti-aging weak/unproven; the well-known combination benefit is good but largely animal-derived with only thin direct human confirmation).
