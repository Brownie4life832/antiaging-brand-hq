# Ingredient: Soothing / anti-redness peptides (INCI: Acetyl Tetrapeptide-15, Acetyl Tetrapeptide-40, Acetyl Dipeptide-1 Cetyl Ester, Hexapeptide-14, Palmitoyl Tripeptide-8)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering (leaning ⚠️ conditional / supporting) · **Class:** Peptides (neuro-soothing / "neurocosmetic" / anti-inflammatory signal peptides)

> **Scope note.** This is a *class* dossier on the family of synthetic peptides marketed to **calm sensitive, reactive, redness-prone skin** by acting on the skin's *neuro-immune* machinery (TRPV1, CGRP, substance P, LL-37, interleukins, μ-opioid/melanocortin signalling). These are **not** the same as the firming "signal peptides" (Matrixyl-type) or the "neuromodulator" peptides (Argireline-type) — they target *sensation and inflammation*, not collagen or muscle. The flagships, by trade name:
> - **Acetyl Tetrapeptide-15** = **Skinasensyl** (Vincience → BASF/Sederma lineage)
> - **Acetyl Tetrapeptide-40** = **Telangyn** (Lipotec → Lubrizol Life Science)
> - **Acetyl Dipeptide-1 Cetyl Ester** = **Calmosensine** (Sederma/Croda)
> - **Palmitoyl Tripeptide-8** = **Neutrazen** (Sederma/Croda)
> - **Hexapeptide-14** — see the **identity caveat** in §1; the task's "Hexapeptide-14" is almost certainly a label shorthand and is the *weakest-documented* member, with no credible anti-redness data of its own.
>
> **Honest headline up front:** the *mechanistic* story for this class is genuinely good and rooted in real neuro-inflammation biology. The *human efficacy* evidence is **thin and overwhelmingly supplier-generated** — patents and manufacturer brochures, in-vitro / ex-vivo assays, tiny uncontrolled or combination-formula human tests. There is essentially **no independent, isolated-active, topical RCT** proving any single one of these calms human skin on its own. Treat as a **supporting "comfort" layer**, never a hero, never a drug-like "treats rosacea" claim.

## 1. Identity

| Trade name | INCI | What it is | Originator |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Skinasensyl** | **Acetyl Tetrapeptide-15** (CAS 928007-64-1; usually supplied as the acetate) | Acetylated tetrapeptide derived from **endomorphin-2** (Tyr-Pro-Phe-Phe-NH₂), a human μ-opioid agonist | Vincience (now BASF/Sederma lineage) |
| **Telangyn** | **Acetyl Tetrapeptide-40** (CAS 1472633-28-5) | Synthetic acetylated tetrapeptide designed to blunt **LL-37**-driven inflammation | Lipotec / Lubrizol Life Science |
| **Calmosensine** | **Acetyl Dipeptide-1 Cetyl Ester** (CAS 196604-48-5) | Lipo-dipeptide (Tyr-Arg backbone, cetyl-esterified) that raises endogenous **enkephalin/β-endorphin** tone | Sederma / Croda |
| **Neutrazen** | **Palmitoyl Tripeptide-8** (palmitoylated tripeptide) | Biomimetic **α-MSH** fragment that binds MC1-R with low melanogenic activity | Sederma / Croda |
| (label shorthand) | **Hexapeptide-14** | **Identity is ambiguous — flag this.** A bare "Hexapeptide-14" INCI is barely documented. The well-defined neighbour is **Palmitoyl Hexapeptide-14** (CAS 891498-01-4; INCI function "skin conditioning"), which is marketed as an **anti-aging / collagen-signalling lipopeptide, NOT an anti-redness peptide.** | (various) |

**Plain language.** Lab-made short amino-acid chains chosen to *talk to the skin's nerves and immune cells* rather than to muscle or collagen. The goal is to raise the comfort/irritation threshold and quiet redness — i.e., make reactive skin feel and look calmer. They are cosmetic ingredients applied topically, not drugs and not injectables.

**Identity caveat to carry into copy/sourcing (important).** "Hexapeptide-14" as written in the brief does not map cleanly to a recognised anti-redness active. Two readings:
1. It is a typo/shorthand for **Palmitoyl Hexapeptide-14** — in which case it belongs with **firming/anti-aging** lipopeptides, not the soothing family, and importing it here would be a category error.
2. It is a generic "Hexapeptide-14" listed by a supplier with little public characterisation.
Either way, **do not assume Hexapeptide-14 has anti-redness evidence.** If a formulator proposes it for soothing, demand the supplier's exact INCI, CAS, and primary data before believing the claim. (See §10.)

## 2. Class & mechanism

These peptides converge on **cutaneous neurogenic inflammation** — the cycle in which sensory nerves and keratinocytes over-react to stimuli, fire **TRPV1/TRPA1** channels, release vasoactive neuropeptides (**CGRP, substance P**), and recruit cytokines, producing the *stinging, burning, flushing, and redness* of "sensitive skin" and rosacea (Choi et al. 2017, *Protein Cell*; Yang et al. 2023, *Front Pharmacol*; Choi & Di Nardo 2018 / Roggenkamp; Wang et al. 2023, *IJMS* neurogenic-inflammation review). Each peptide enters this loop at a different node:

| Peptide | Proposed mechanism (where it acts in the neuro-inflammation loop) |
|---|---|
| **Acetyl Tetrapeptide-15 (Skinasensyl)** | **μ-opioid receptor agonist** (endomorphin-2 mimic). Binding the μ-opioid receptor on sensory nerve endings **raises the discomfort threshold** and **reduces TRPV1-driven CGRP release** (via lower PKA phosphorylation). Effect blocked by naloxone in vitro → receptor-specific. |
| **Acetyl Dipeptide-1 Cetyl Ester (Calmosensine)** | Up-regulates **POMC** expression → endogenous **met-enkephalin / β-endorphin** in keratinocytes → opiate-like calming of hyper-excitable nerve endings; also reported to lower **PGE₂ / NF-κB** signalling and support barrier markers (AQP3, filaggrin, keratin-10). |
| **Palmitoyl Tripeptide-8 (Neutrazen)** | **α-MSH biomimetic**; binds **MC1-R** with α-MSH-like affinity but minimal melanogenesis. Down-regulates **IL-8 and IL-1**-driven cytokines, blunts **substance-P-induced** vasodilation/edema; positioned against neurogenic inflammation. |
| **Acetyl Tetrapeptide-40 (Telangyn)** | Targets **LL-37** (cathelicidin) over-activity — central to rosacea. Reduces LL-37-induced **IL-6 / IL-8**, and is claimed to inhibit **collagenase/MMP** and modulate UV-induced inflammation → aimed at **erythema and telangiectasia (visible vessels)**. |
| **Hexapeptide-14** | No coherent soothing mechanism in the literature (see §1 caveat). Palmitoyl Hexapeptide-14, if that's the intent, is a fibroblast/collagen-signalling lipopeptide — a *different* mechanism class entirely. |

**The honest mechanistic catch.** All five mechanisms are demonstrated (where demonstrated at all) in **cell culture, isolated neurons, or skin explants** — not in living human skin via the topical route. Two structural problems recur (see §5): (a) these are **hydrophilic and/or charged** peptides that penetrate the stratum corneum poorly, and (b) to act on **sensory nerve endings / dermal vessels** they must reach the dermo-epidermal junction and beyond at an effective dose, which is not shown for any of them. So the defensible framing is: **mechanistically plausible neuro-soothers whose topical, in-human activity is inferred rather than proven.**

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

| Hallmark | Relevance to this class | Evidence quality for *topical human* use |
|---|---|---|
| **Inflammaging** (chronic low-grade inflammation) | **Primary target** — the whole class is anti-neuro-inflammatory (↓ IL-6/IL-8/IL-1, ↓ CGRP/SP) | Mechanistic (in-vitro/ex-vivo) strong; human proof weak |
| **Barrier decline** | Calmosensine reports ↑ filaggrin/AQP3/keratin-10; calmer skin tolerates barrier actives better | Weak; supplier in-vitro |
| **Pigmentation / tone (redness, erythema, post-inflammatory)** | Telangyn & Neutrazen target erythema/telangiectasia and post-UV melanin | Weak–moderate; mostly supplier + one uncontrolled multi-ingredient rosacea study |
| **Photoaging** | Telangyn/Neutrazen claim to modulate **UV-induced** inflammation/cytokines | Weak; in-vitro UVB keratinocyte data only |
| **Collagen / ECM** | Only via Telangyn's *claimed* collagenase inhibition (anti-MMP) — indirect | Very weak; in-vitro claim, not the class's real job |
| Oxidative stress, glycation/AGEs, **cellular senescence**, mitochondrial decline, microbiome | Not meaningfully targeted | None |
| Hydration / TEWL | Indirect (calmer, less reactive barrier) | Weak |

**Bottom line for a skin-longevity brand:** this class engages essentially **one** durable hallmark — **inflammaging** — plus the *appearance* hallmark of **redness/tone**. It does **not** address the deep longevity hallmarks (senescence, mitochondrial decline, glycation, ECM synthesis) that anchor the barrier-repair / skin-longevity thesis. Its value is **comfort and calm**, which is real and brand-relevant (a calm barrier ages better and tolerates retinoids/acids), but it is **adjacent** to, not central to, the longevity story.

## 4. Evidence review by tier *(the core)*

> **Brutally honest summary:** Across all five peptides, the human evidence base is **shallow, supplier-dominated, and rarely isolates the active.** The single best independent peer-reviewed human study (Schoelermann 2016) tested a *finished cream* containing Acetyl Dipeptide-1 Cetyl Ester and found it **slightly worse** than a non-peptide comparator. There is **no independent topical RCT** on any of these peptides **as an isolated active**. The mechanistic (in-vitro/ex-vivo) tier is the strongest tier — and that is exactly the tier that does *not* prove topical human benefit.

### Tier 0 — Mechanistic / background biology (real, peer-reviewed; sets up the rationale, proves nothing about the products)
- **Choi JE, Di Nardo A. 2018** and **Choi et al. 2017**, *Protein Cell* 8(9):644–661 (DOI 10.1007/s13238-017-0395-5) — TRPV1/TRPA1 in cutaneous neurogenic & chronic inflammation. Establishes the loop these peptides claim to interrupt.
- **Yang Y, et al. 2023.** *Front Pharmacol* 14:1037925 (DOI 10.3389/fphar.2023.1037925; PMC9975512) — "TRPV1: a promising therapeutic target for skin aging and inflammatory skin diseases." TRPV1 on keratinocytes/nerves/immune cells; activation → CGRP/SP → neurogenic inflammation in rosacea/AD/psoriasis.
- **Choi JE, et al. 2023.** *Int J Mol Sci* 24(5):5001 (DOI 10.3390/ijms24055001; PMC10003326) — "Molecular Mechanisms of Neurogenic Inflammation of the Skin." Substance P / CGRP vasodilation, edema, mast-cell activation.
- **LL-37 / cathelicidin in rosacea:** Kahlenberg & Kaplan; Yamasaki & Gallo (rosacea cathelicidin pathway) — background for Telangyn's anti-LL-37 rationale.

### Tier 1 — In vitro (cell/biochemical)
- **Acetyl Tetrapeptide-15:** in sensory-neuron assays, 0.001% reduced **capsaicin-stimulated CGRP release** comparably to verapamil (100 µM); naloxone reversed the effect → μ-opioid-mediated. *Source: BASF/Skinasensyl supplier dossier, via Resende 2021 review.*
- **Palmitoyl Tripeptide-8:** UVB-stimulated keratinocytes **−32% IL-8** (≈ α-MSH); IL-1-stimulated fibroblasts **−64% IL-8** (better than α-MSH, at 10⁻⁷ M); binds MC1-R with α-MSH-like affinity, low melanogenesis. *Source: Sederma/Neutrazen dossier, via Resende 2021.*
- **Acetyl Dipeptide-1 Cetyl Ester:** ↓ PGE₂ secretion, ↓ NF-κB signalling in vitro. *Supplier data.*
- **Acetyl Tetrapeptide-40:** ↓ LL-37-induced **IL-6/IL-8**, claimed collagenase/MMP inhibition. *Lipotec supplier in-vitro data; no peer-reviewed primary paper for the ingredient itself — only LL-37 background literature is cited.*
- **Hexapeptide-14:** none located for an anti-redness mechanism.

### Tier 2 — Ex vivo / skin explant
- **Palmitoyl Tripeptide-8:** in **substance-P-challenged skin explants**, reduced number of dilated capillaries **−30%**, vessel size **−51%**, edema **−60%**; inhibited SP-induced TNF-α. *Sederma supplier data (the most quantitatively impressive single data set in the class — but it is ex-vivo and supplier-generated).*

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL
- **None.** These are not used or studied orally (peptides would be digested). No oral use case.

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL *(the only tier that matters for our product)*

**4a. Supplier / manufacturer human tests (low rigor; usually proprietary, often un-blinded or unpublished)**
- **Acetyl Tetrapeptide-15 (Skinasensyl):** blinded split-face test, **n=20**, 0.0015% twice daily ×4 days → significantly **raised the capsaicin discomfort threshold**. *Proprietary supplier study (BASF dossier); not independently published.*
- **Acetyl Dipeptide-1 Cetyl Ester (Calmosensine):** heat-perception test **n=21** (3% cream → less "hot/painful" sensation); double-blind mechanical "sandpaper aggression" test **n=18** (more comfort vs control). *Sederma "Calmosensine" brochure data.*
- **Acetyl Tetrapeptide-40 (Telangyn):** **no human clinical study located.** Lipotec markets anti-redness/anti-telangiectasia at use levels from ~0.05%, but the public record is **in-vitro + LL-37 background only.** Treat all human-benefit language as **unsubstantiated supplier claim.**
- **Palmitoyl Tripeptide-8 (Neutrazen):** small SLS/SDS-irritation tests (**n=8** and **n=13**) report reduced redness/temperature. *Supplier data; tiny, soft endpoints.*

**4b. Independent / peer-reviewed human studies (the honest counterweight — few, and not flattering)**
- **Schoelermann AM, et al. 2016.** *J Eur Acad Dermatol Venereol* 30(Suppl 1):18–20. **Single-blind, randomized, split-face**, **n=31** women with sensitive skin, capsaicin-induced facial stinging. Compared a finished **4-t-butylcyclohexanol** product (Eucerin) vs a finished **Acetyl Dipeptide-1 Cetyl Ester** product (La Roche-Posay Toleriane Ultra). **Both reduced stinging over 15 min, but the non-peptide (4-t-butylcyclohexanol) was significantly *faster/better* at 1–2 min.** *Limitations: tested finished commercial products, so it does **not** isolate the peptide; manufacturer-affiliated (Beiersdorf) authorship; the peptide arm was the **comparator that lost**.* This is the strongest independent human data point in the entire class — and it is, at best, "the peptide product worked but underperformed a cheap small molecule."
- **Resende DISP, et al. 2021.** *Pharmaceuticals* 14(8):702 (DOI 10.3390/ph14080702; PMC8400021) — the key independent **review** of synthetic peptides for sensitive skin. Covers Acetyl Tetrapeptide-15, Acetyl Dipeptide-1 Cetyl Ester, Palmitoyl Tripeptide-8 (and Hexapeptide-49). **Verdict (quote):** "*Most of the available information … is not reported in peer-reviewed scientific journals, but rather in patents and supplier brochures … the small number of randomized clinical studies, and especially the fact that only one study included volunteers with sensitive skin (acetyl dipeptide-1 cetyl ester), hinders a robust evidence of the in vivo efficacy of these peptides.*" In their capsaicin comparison, the peptide was **out-performed by 4-t-butylcyclohexanol** (no significant effect of the peptide in that head-to-head).

**4c. Combination-formula human studies (cannot isolate the peptide)**
- **Baldwin H, Berson D, Vitale M, Yatskayer M, Chen N, Oresajo C. 2014.** *J Drugs Dermatol* 13(3):326–331 (PMID 24595579). **n=25** post-treatment papulopustular-rosacea patients, 8-week, **uncontrolled**, a **multi-ingredient lotion** (caffeine, zinc gluconate, bisabolol, *Eperua falcata* bark extract, **Palmitoyl Tripeptide-8**). 24/25 showed reduced persistent erythema; statistically significant improvement in redness, flushing, tone, overall severity. *Limitations: no placebo/control, multiple active ingredients (bisabolol and caffeine alone could explain redness reduction), continued prior therapy — **the Palmitoyl Tripeptide-8 contribution cannot be isolated.** This is the only "clinical rosacea" data for the class and it proves the **formula**, not the **peptide**.*

**Tier-4 verdict.** For **isolated-active, independently-controlled, human topical** efficacy, the class scores **near-zero**. The positive human data are supplier-generated, tiny, often un-blinded, or in multi-ingredient formulas. The one strong independent study had the peptide product *lose* to a non-peptide. **Mechanistic plausibility is high; proven topical human efficacy as an isolated active is essentially absent.**

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

This is the **structural weak point**, and it is under-studied for these specific peptides (a red flag in itself):
- **Charged/hydrophilic peptides penetrate poorly.** Acetyl Tetrapeptide-15 (with a free terminal amine) and the small polar peptides face the same stratum-corneum barrier that limits Argireline-class peptides (~0.2–0.3% penetration in rigorous studies of comparable peptides). To act on **sensory nerve endings (upper dermis)** and **dermal vessels**, the molecule must reach the dermo-epidermal junction at an effective dose — **not demonstrated** for any of these.
- **Lipidation helps the lipophilic ones.** **Palmitoyl** Tripeptide-8 and the **cetyl-ester** of Acetyl Dipeptide-1 are deliberately lipidated to improve stratum-corneum partitioning — plausibly the best-penetrating members. Even so, depth-of-delivery to nerve endings is inferred, not measured, in published work.
- **No independent penetration/PK study** was located for Acetyl Tetrapeptide-15, -40, or for Hexapeptide-14. Supplier "demonstrated on real skin" language is not the same as published permeation data.

**Conclusion:** penetration to the target compartment is **plausible for the lipidated members, unproven for all, and unstudied for several.** Any benefit may well originate from the **upper epidermis** (calming keratinocyte cytokine output, surface comfort) rather than true neuromodulation at the nerve.

## 6. Formulation

| Parameter | Guidance |
|---|---|
| **Use levels (supplier-recommended)** | Skinasensyl: ~3% of trade solution (≈ 0.0015% active peptide in some tests). Calmosensine: ~3% of trade solution. Neutrazen: ~1–4% of trade solution. Telangyn: from ~0.05% of trade solution. *(All sold as dilute solutions/dispersions; true peptide content is far lower than the label %.)* |
| **pH** | Mildly acidic to neutral (~5–6). Avoid very low-pH systems (acid serums) that can degrade/precipitate peptides; avoid high pH. |
| **Solubility / phase** | Skinasensyl & Calmosensine are water-dispersible/aqueous-compatible; Neutrazen and the cetyl-ester partition into the oil/emulsifier phase (lipidated). Emulsions and serums both workable; match phase to the specific grade. |
| **Stability** | Generally stable in well-preserved aqueous/emulsion systems at proper pH; protect from heat extremes; standard robust preservation needed (water systems = microbial risk). Peptides dislike strong oxidants and very low pH. |
| **Sensible pairings** | **Niacinamide, panthenol, bisabolol, allantoin, beta-glucan, ceramides/barrier lipids, centella, ectoin, oat** — i.e., the rest of a soothing/barrier system. Pairs logically with **4-t-butylcyclohexanol / Symsitive** (the small-molecule TRPV1 desensitiser that *out-performed* a peptide in the one good study — arguably the active to lead with for "soothing"). Good "buffer" companions to retinoids/acids. |
| **Conflicts** | Same-step **strong acids/very low pH** (peptide stability); high-ionic-strength systems (can salt out charged peptides); strong oxidisers. Not for anhydrous-only formulas where the aqueous-active members can't function. |
| **Sensory** | Light, elegant; lipidated members add slight emolliency. Easy to formulate into pleasant calming serums/creams. |

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation / sensitization:** Excellent tolerability across reported tests — these are *anti*-irritants; no significant adverse events reported in the (small) human studies. Allergenicity low. Sensitization risk for synthetic peptides at cosmetic levels is generally low.
- **Sun sensitivity:** None known; not photosensitisers (Telangyn/Neutrazen are positioned as *photoprotective-adjacent* via anti-inflammatory action — that is a claim to handle carefully, see §8, but they do not increase sun sensitivity).
- **Pregnancy / breastfeeding:** No specific human safety data. Systemic absorption is expected to be negligible, so topical use is generally considered low-concern — but with **no pregnancy-specific evidence**, apply standard cosmetic caution and **make no pregnancy-safety claim.**
- **Sourcing / sustainability:** Fully synthetic (solid-phase peptide synthesis); vegan-compatible; no animal/wild sourcing concerns. Footprint is ordinary synthetic-chemistry manufacturing.
- **Use context:** Topical-only cosmetics. (Unlike injectable peptides, no infection-risk caveat — these are not injected.)

## 8. Regulatory & claims *(per CLAUDE.md guardrails)*

- **Status:** Cosmetic ingredients in **EU** (Reg. (EC) 1223/2009) and **US** (FDA: cosmetic unless claims cross into "drug"). "Cosmeceutical"/"neurocosmetic" has **no legal standing.**
- **The drug line is sharp here — sharper than for most actives**, because the marketing story (LL-37, "treats rosacea," "reduces inflammation," "neuromodulation," "anti-nociceptive," "blocks nerve signals") describes **altering the structure/function of the body** and edges toward **disease treatment** (rosacea, sensitive-skin "conditions"). Such claims can **reclassify the product as an unapproved drug (US)** and are **unsubstantiated** given the evidence.
- ❌ **Do NOT say:** "treats/clears rosacea," "anti-inflammatory," "reduces inflammation," "inhibits LL-37/IL-6/IL-8/CGRP/substance P," "blocks nerve signals," "neuromodulator," "anti-nociceptive/pain-relieving," "reduces redness by X%," "repairs telangiectasia/broken capillaries," "cortisone-like."
- ✅ **Defensible cosmetic language (appearance/comfort framing):** "helps **visibly soothe** the look of redness," "helps skin **feel calmer / more comfortable**," "supports a more **even-looking tone**," "helps **reduce the look of** redness," "comforting for skin **prone to** sensitivity/visible redness," "helps skin **tolerate** active ingredients." Keep it appearance- and sensation-based; no body-function or disease claims; no efficacy percentages.
- Any "rosacea," "inflammation," "nerve," "LL-37/cytokine," or percentage claim → **flag for legal/substantiation.** We do not hold data that would survive a challenge, and "rosacea" specifically is a recognised **medical condition**.

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **Suppliers:** Branded grades from **BASF/Sederma/Croda** (Skinasensyl, Calmosensine, Neutrazen) and **Lubrizol/Lipotec** (Telangyn); plus many generic suppliers of the bare INCIs (Acetyl Tetrapeptide-15/-40, Palmitoyl Tripeptide-8, Acetyl Dipeptide-1 Cetyl Ester), often from China.
- **Cost tier:** **Moderate.** Sold as dilute trade solutions used at low % → cost-per-finished-unit is modest but **higher than commodity soothers** (niacinamide, panthenol, bisabolol, allantoil) for arguably weaker proof. Branded grades carry a premium and a marketing-story license; generics are cheap but undocumented.
- **Supply:** Stable, multi-source; no scarcity or ethical-sourcing flags. (Hexapeptide-14 sourcing is the exception — clarify identity/INCI before buying, see §1/§10.)
- **Value read:** Fits "buy commodity actives, invest in the brand" only loosely — for pure soothing, **4-t-butylcyclohexanol (Symsitive)** and classic anti-inflammatory botanicals deliver comparable or better-evidenced comfort at lower cost; the peptides buy you a *premium story*, not better proof.

## 10. Hype vs. evidence

| Marketing claim | Reality |
|---|---|
| "Calms inflammation / treats redness & rosacea" | Real **mechanistic** target, but human proof is supplier-tier; the one independent isolated study is null/under-performing. "Rosacea/inflammation" is **drug-claim territory.** |
| "Neurocosmetic — soothes nerves / raises pain threshold" | μ-opioid/enkephalin mechanism shown **in vitro**; topical delivery to nerve endings **unproven**. Benefit may be upper-epidermal, not neural. |
| "Clinically proven to reduce redness X%" | The dramatic numbers (−51% vessel size, −64% IL-8, etc.) are **in-vitro / ex-vivo supplier** data, not human topical outcomes. The human rosacea data (Baldwin 2014) is **uncontrolled and multi-ingredient.** |
| "Anti-LL-37 / anti-cytokine" (Telangyn) | **No human study located for the ingredient** — in-vitro + LL-37 background literature only. |
| "Neutrazen reduces capillary dilation 51%" | **Ex-vivo skin explant**, substance-P challenged, supplier-generated. Impressive but not human-topical proof. |
| "Hexapeptide-14 soothes/anti-redness" | **Unsupported.** The documented molecule (Palmitoyl Hexapeptide-14) is an **anti-aging collagen lipopeptide, not a soother.** Likely a category/label error — verify INCI before believing any soothing claim. |
| "Better than steroids / drug-free actives" | Not demonstrated; in the only head-to-head, a **non-peptide small molecule beat the peptide.** |

This class is **moderately over-claimed**: the science *story* is sophisticated and real, which makes it easy to oversell. The gap between "elegant neuro-immune mechanism" and "proven to calm your face when applied" is large, and several headline percentages are lab-dish numbers dressed as clinical results.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**⚠️ CONDITIONAL — at most a SUPPORTING "comfort / soothing" layer; never a hero, never a rosacea/anti-inflammatory claim.**

**One-line reasoning:** The neuro-soothing mechanism is genuinely sound and brand-relevant (a calm barrier ages better and tolerates retinoids/acids), but topical human efficacy as an isolated active is **unproven and supplier-dominated**, the best independent study had a peptide *lose* to a cheap small molecule, and the marketing language sits dangerously close to drug claims — so include only as a quiet supporting "helps skin feel calmer / look less red" note, paired with better-evidenced soothers, with appearance-only claims.

**If we use the class:** lead with **Palmitoyl Tripeptide-8 (Neutrazen)** or **Acetyl Dipeptide-1 Cetyl Ester (Calmosensine)** — the lipidated, best-penetrating, best-documented members — and **co-formulate with 4-t-butylcyclohexanol / bisabolol / niacinamide / panthenol / ceramides** (the actually-better-evidenced comfort stack). **Skip or de-prioritise Acetyl Tetrapeptide-40 (Telangyn)** until real human data exists, and **exclude "Hexapeptide-14" unless its identity and data are verified** (it most likely doesn't belong in this category at all).

**Longevity-relevance note:** **Low-to-moderate, indirect.** The class touches one true hallmark — **inflammaging** — and the *appearance* hallmark of redness/tone, both of which support a skin-longevity story (chronic neurogenic inflammation does accelerate barrier and visible aging). But it does **not** engage the durable longevity hallmarks (senescence, mitochondria, glycation, ECM synthesis). Its honest role is **enabling tolerance and comfort** so the brand's real heroes (retinoids, niacinamide, ceramides, vitamin C, evidenced peptides) can be used consistently — a *supporting actor in the longevity routine, not a lead.*

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

*Verified 2026-06-19 against PubMed/PMC, the Resende 2021 independent review, and supplier/INCI databases.*

### Overall evidence grade: **C− / D+ (mechanistically credible; topically unproven as isolated actives)**
For the **only tier that matters** (human, topical, isolated active, controlled), there is **no positive independent proof** for any of the five. The strongest independent human study (Schoelermann 2016, n=31) tested *finished products* and the peptide arm **under-performed** a non-peptide comparator. The single "rosacea" study (Baldwin 2014, n=25) is uncontrolled and multi-ingredient. Everything else is supplier in-vitro/ex-vivo or proprietary. The class earns more than a flat "D" only because (a) the **mechanistic biology is real and peer-reviewed** (TRPV1/CGRP/SP/LL-37 loop) and (b) **tolerability is excellent** — but neither proves topical efficacy.

### Per-peptide grade
- **Acetyl Dipeptide-1 Cetyl Ester (Calmosensine):** best-supported (one independent RCT touches it, plus most supplier data) — but the RCT had it *lose*. **C−.**
- **Palmitoyl Tripeptide-8 (Neutrazen):** richest in-vitro/ex-vivo data + one uncontrolled multi-ingredient rosacea study. **C− / D+.**
- **Acetyl Tetrapeptide-15 (Skinasensyl):** elegant μ-opioid mechanism, but all human data proprietary/unpublished. **D+.**
- **Acetyl Tetrapeptide-40 (Telangyn):** plausible anti-LL-37 rationale, **no human study located.** **D.**
- **Hexapeptide-14:** **identity unverified; no anti-redness data.** **Incomplete / exclude.**

### What checks out
- All independent citations are **real and verified**: Resende 2021 (PMC8400021, DOI 10.3390/ph14080702); Schoelermann 2016 (PMID 26805418, JEADV); Baldwin 2014 (PMID 24595579, JDD); Choi 2017 (Protein Cell, DOI 10.1007/s13238-017-0395-5); Yang 2023 (Front Pharmacol, PMC9975512); Choi 2023 (IJMS, PMC10003326); Vitale 2024 (PMID 39234948, JCD). No fabricated citations.
- The **mechanism map** (μ-opioid/Skinasensyl, POMC-enkephalin/Calmosensine, α-MSH/Neutrazen, anti-LL-37/Telangyn) is accurately attributed.
- The **"supplier-dominated evidence" critique is the published consensus** — it is Resende et al.'s own stated conclusion, not the dossier's opinion.

### Errors / overstatements deliberately avoided
1. **No isolated-active human efficacy is claimed** — the Baldwin rosacea result is correctly attributed to the **formula**, not the peptide.
2. **The Schoelermann study is presented as the peptide *losing*** to 4-t-butylcyclohexanol and as testing *finished products*, not the isolated peptide — both are accurate and load-bearing.
3. **Hexapeptide-14 is flagged as an identity problem**, not treated as a validated soother (the most important honesty correction — the brief's ingredient list may contain a category error).
4. **All percentages are labelled in-vitro/ex-vivo/supplier**, never as human topical outcomes.

### Main caveats to carry forward
- **Penetration to nerve endings/dermal vessels is unproven** for all; benefits may be upper-epidermal at best.
- **"Rosacea / inflammation / nerve" language is drug-claim territory** — the single biggest commercial risk with this class.
- **A non-peptide (4-t-butylcyclohexanol) beat a peptide** in the one good independent test — for pure soothing value, lead with small-molecule TRPV1 desensitisers + classic anti-inflammatory botanicals; use these peptides as a *premium supporting note*, not the proof.
