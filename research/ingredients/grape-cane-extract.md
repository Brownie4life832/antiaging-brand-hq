# Ingredient: Grape cane / vine-shoot extract (resveratrol + viniferins) (INCI: Vitis Vinifera (Grape) Cane Extract)

**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ considering / ⬜ shortlisted / ⬜ rejected / ⬜ in a product
**Class:** NAD+ / sirtuin / AMPK / cellular-energy actives (stilbene-rich botanical: trans-resveratrol + ε-viniferin + oligomeric stilbenoids)

> **One-line honest summary:** Grape cane (vine-shoot) extract is an upcycled, sustainability-forward botanical that delivers a *concentrated, multi-stilbene* cargo — most notably **trans-resveratrol plus ε-viniferin (a resveratrol dimer)** — with a genuinely attractive longevity *story* (SIRT1 activation, tyrosinase inhibition, anti-MMP). But the human evidence is almost entirely **in vitro and computational**: there is **no published human topical RCT on grape-cane extract itself**. What clinical support exists is *borrowed* from topical **resveratrol** (always in combination formulas) and from a single patented, esterified vine-shoot derivative (Caudalie's "Viniferine" = INCI *Palmitoyl Grapevine Shoot Extract*) — neither of which is the same thing as a generic grape-cane extract. Treat it as a **supporting / narrative** active with a real green-chemistry hook, not a proof-backed hero.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Common names:** Grape cane extract; vine-shoot extract; vine-stem/stalk extract; grapevine cane extract. "Canes" / "shoots" / "sarments" (Fr.) = the **woody pruning waste** (lignified one-year-old shoots) removed annually during vineyard pruning — distinct from grape *seed*, *skin*, *pomace/marc*, *leaf*, or *sap*.
- **INCI (likely):** **Vitis Vinifera (Grape) Cane Extract** (also seen as *Vitis Vinifera Stem Extract* / *Vitis Vinifera Shoot Extract*). Verify exact INCI per supplier — naming is inconsistent across vendors. [1][2]
- **Key constituents (the actual actives):** A **stilbenoid-dominant** profile, not a single molecule:
  - **trans-Resveratrol** (monomeric stilbene) — typically ~1.3–7.2% of dry extract depending on cultivar/process. [3]
  - **trans-ε-Viniferin** (a **resveratrol dehydrodimer**) — ~1.7–3.6% of dry extract. [3]
  - **E-vitisin B**, **E-piceatannol**, **ampelopsin A**, **hopeaphenol / isohopeaphenol**, **E-miyabenol C**, **R2-viniferin** (higher oligomers/tetramers). [3][4]
  - **Flavan-3-ols**: catechin, epicatechin, procyanidins; minor flavonols (quercetin, kaempferol). [2]
  - Total polyphenols can reach **~17–39% of the dry extract** (Villard Noir highest at 39.4%). [3]
- **Raw stilbene levels in the cane biomass** (pre-extraction, sourcing context): trans-resveratrol up to ~5–6 g/kg dry cane, trans-ε-viniferin ~2–3 g/kg, R2-viniferin ~0.5–1.7 g/kg, varying by cultivar/year. [4][5]
- **Chemistry notes that matter:** Resveratrol is the parent monomer (MW 228); **ε-viniferin is its dimer (MW ~454, logP ~4.6)** — markedly more **lipophilic** and **larger**, with different penetration/stability behavior (see §5–6). Higher oligomers (hopeaphenol etc., MW >500) are large and lipophilic. [9][12]
- **Easily-confused look-alikes (important for the brand to get right):**
  - **Caudalie "Viniferine"** is *not* generic grape-cane ε-viniferin. Caudalie's patented active (Vercauteren; PCT/FR 2016/052294) is isolated/stabilized from grapevine and appears on labels as **INCI: Palmitoyl Grapevine Shoot Extract** — an *esterified/stabilized* derivative. Its clinical dark-spot claims belong to *that* patented ingredient, not to a commodity grape-cane extract. [6][7]
  - **Grape seed / pine bark OPCs** (proanthocyanidins) — different fraction, different file (`grape-seed-pine-bark-opcs`).
  - **Plant stem-cell extracts** ("Vitis vinifera cell culture") — a cell-culture biotech ingredient, different file (`plant-stem-cell-extracts`).
  - **ε-Viniferin from grape *cell culture*** (e.g., *Vitis labruscana* 'Campbell Early', "Vino Chocolate™") — a biotech-fermentation source, not pruning-waste cane. [8]
  - **Japanese knotweed** (`japanese-knotweed`) — the dominant *commodity* resveratrol source; grape cane is the *grape-native, multi-stilbene* alternative.

## 2. Class & mechanism

Grape cane extract sits in the **NAD+/sirtuin/AMPK "cellular-energy" longevity family** because its two headline molecules — resveratrol and ε-viniferin — are classic **sirtuin-pathway polyphenols (STACs, "sirtuin-activating compounds")**. It is best understood as a **polyphenol antioxidant + putative sirtuin/AMPK modulator + tyrosinase inhibitor**, delivered as a botanical complex rather than a pure molecule.

Mechanistic pillars (most demonstrated in cell-free, cell, or computational systems):

1. **SIRT1 (sirtuin) activation.** In a cell-free SIRT1 fluorometric assay, pure E-resveratrol gave 130–307% activation and E-ε-viniferin 95–280% (dose-dependent, 1–100 µM); whole grape-cane extracts at 50 µM gave 142–171% (Riesling best, 171%) — "at least equivalent to 5 µM E-resveratrol." Sirtuins are NAD+-dependent deacetylases tied to longevity, mitochondrial biogenesis, and stress resistance. [3] *(Caveat: SIRT1 "activation" by resveratrol in fluorometric assays is historically confounded by the fluorophore substrate; the in-vivo relevance of direct SIRT1 activation remains debated.)*
2. **Tyrosinase inhibition (pigment pathway).** ε-viniferin and resveratrol are *competitive* tyrosinase inhibitors with stronger binding affinity than the substrate; molecular docking puts ε-viniferin at −7.73 kcal/mol (vs resveratrol −5.95) at His85/His244. This underpins the brightening/even-tone story. [3]
3. **Antioxidant / radical scavenging.** Stilbenes and flavan-3-ols quench ROS; cane extracts show DPPH free-radical scavenging (EC50 ~0.14 g/g for a red-variety acetone extract). [2]
4. **Anti-MMP / pro-collagen (ECM remodeling).** ε-viniferin (cell-culture-sourced) raised pro-collagen I (COL1A1) dose-dependently and suppressed MMP-1/MMP-3 in human skin cells. [8]
5. **AMPK activation & anti-inflammatory signaling.** α-viniferin activates AMPK (energy-sensor) and inhibits inflammatory mediators (TNF-α, IL-6); ε-viniferin lowers TNF-α/IL-6 in models. [10]
6. **NAD+/sirtuin framing.** Resveratrol is one of the original molecules linked (controversially) to the NAD+/SIRT1 longevity axis, which is why this ingredient anchors a "cellular-energy" narrative.

**Skin-relevant translation (honest):** Items 1–4 are real *biochemistry/cell-model* findings; whether a topical cosmetic dose of grape-cane extract meaningfully activates sirtuins, inhibits enough tyrosinase, or remodels ECM **in living human skin** is **not demonstrated**. The mechanism deck is strong on paper and thin on human topical proof.

## 4. EVIDENCE REVIEW BY TIER (the core of this dossier)

> **The single most important honesty point:** Searches return **no published human topical clinical trial on grape-cane / vine-shoot extract itself.** The flagship cosmetic paper (Malinowska/Billet et al., 2020) is **entirely in vitro + computational** and *explicitly states* "further experiments on skin models are still required." The human topical clinical data in this space belong to (a) **topical resveratrol in combination formulas** and (b) **one patented, esterified vine-shoot derivative** (Palmitoyl Grapevine Shoot Extract) — both adjacent to, but not identical to, a commodity grape-cane extract.

### Tier 1 — In vitro (cell-free, cell, 3D, computational)

- **Malinowska M., Billet K., Drouet S. et al., 2020, *Molecules* 25(9):2203 (DOI 10.3390/molecules25092203).** The landmark "grape cane extracts as a multifunctional rejuvenating cosmetic ingredient" study. Five cultivars (Villard Noir, Sauvignon, Savagnin Blanc, Riesling, Magdeleine Noire des Charentes). **Composition:** E-resveratrol 1.3–7.2%, E-ε-viniferin 1.7–3.6%, E-vitisin B 3.8–17.2%, E-piceatannol 3.3–12.0%; total polyphenols 16.8–39.4%. **SIRT1:** GCE at 50 µM → 142–171% activation (Riesling best), ≥ 5 µM resveratrol. **Tyrosinase:** ε-viniferin 76% / resveratrol 75% inhibition at 50 µM; IC50 ε-viniferin 52.9 µM, resveratrol 60.8 µM (both weaker than kojic acid's ~16.9 µM). **Bioavailability:** assessed *computationally* (Lipinski/Ghose/Veber/Egan/Muegge rules), **not** by Franz cell/PAMPA/human skin — catechin, epicatechin, resveratrol, piceatannol predicted to penetrate well; ε-viniferin medium; vitisin B/hopeaphenol/miyabenol C poor (MW >500). **Limitations:** in vitro + in silico only; SIRT1 tested at a single 50 µM dose for extracts; no skin-model or human confirmation. [3]
- **Wojdyło/Llobera-cited antioxidant assays.** Cane extracts show DPPH radical scavenging (red-variety acetone extract EC50 ~0.14 g/g). *In vitro chemistry only.* [2]
- **Park H. et al., 2025, *Cosmetics* 12(5):181 (DOI 10.3390/cosmetics12050181) — ε-viniferin (cell-culture-sourced).** ε-Viniferin + ε-viniferin-enriched extract ("Vino Chocolate™," 547.58 ppm ε-viniferin) from *Vitis labruscana* 'Campbell Early' cell cultures, tested on HaCaT keratinocytes + HDFn fibroblasts: significant fibroblast migration; **dose-dependent ↑pro-collagen I (COL1A1)** — ε-viniferin at 1 ppm > TGF-β1; **↓MMP-1 and MMP-3** ≥ retinoic acid; **↑filaggrin** (barrier/hydration). **Limitations:** in vitro only (no human); the source is **grape *cell culture*, not pruning-waste cane** — overlapping molecule, different ingredient. [8]
- **Yun W.J. et al., 2018 — α-Viniferin (mechanistic + small human pilot).** α-Viniferin (a resveratrol *trimer*) inhibits melanogenesis by accelerating PKA inactivation → ↓CREB/CRTC1 → ↓MITF-M/tyrosinase; topical *Caragana sinica* containing α-viniferin reduced melanin index in facial melasma/freckles. **Limitations:** different molecule (α-viniferin is a resveratrol *trimer*, from a *different* plant — not grape cane), and the human portion is a **small uncontrolled patient observation, not a controlled trial**; relevant only as weak analog support. [11]
- **Mechanism reviews — Fuloria S. et al., 2022, *Molecules* 27(16):5072 (DOI 10.3390/molecules27165072).** Reviews viniferin chemistry/pharmacology: ε-/α-viniferin antioxidant, anti-inflammatory (↓TNF-α/IL-6), anti-tyrosinase, AMPK activation, SIRT1 induction, anti-cancer; notes viniferins can be *more potent* than resveratrol in some assays but are *poorly water-soluble* (improved by liposome encapsulation). [10]

### Tier 2 — Animal (in vivo)

- **Courtois A. et al., 2018, *Nutrients* 10(11):1660 (DOI 10.3390/nu10111660).** Tissue distribution/metabolism of trans-ε-viniferin (IP injection, rat/mouse): rapid hepatic glucuronidation (and lesser sulfation); the **native (unmetabolized) form shows its highest AUC and mean residence time in white adipose tissue** (half-life ~2.7–2.9 h in fat vs ~0.95 h plasma / ~0.74 h liver; logP 4.60), suggesting fat acts as a slow-release reservoir, while glucuronide metabolites concentrate in liver/plasma/kidney. (The often-quoted "IP bioavailability >85% vs oral ~0.77%" figures are **cited by this paper from an earlier mouse study, not measured here** — attribute accordingly.) **No skin distribution data.** Relevance: confirms ε-viniferin is lipophilic and heavily first-pass metabolized — a *systemic* PK study (IP injection in rodents), not a skin study, and not evidence of topical performance. [12]
- **No published animal study of *topical grape-cane extract* on skin aging was found.** [unverified — none located]

### Tier 3 — Human ORAL

- **Essentially none specific to grape cane.** Grape cane is positioned as a *topical/cosmetic* and *nutraceutical-stilbene-source* ingredient; oral human longevity/skin data in this family belong to **resveratrol** (see the `polyphenols-resveratrol` dossier) and **Japanese knotweed**, not to cane extract per se. Oral resveratrol skin RCTs are themselves limited and mixed. [13]

### Tier 4 — Human TOPICAL (the tier that matters for a skincare brand)

> **For grape-cane extract specifically: zero.** What follows is the *adjacent* human topical evidence the category leans on — flag clearly that it is **borrowed**, not cane-specific.

- **Topical RESVERATROL (combination) — Farris P. et al., 2014, *J Drugs Dermatol* 13(12):1467–1472 (PMID 25607790).** Open-label, 12 weeks, mild–moderate photodamage: a stabilized serum of **1% resveratrol + 0.5% baicalin + 1% vitamin E** significantly improved fine lines/wrinkles, firmness, elasticity, laxity, hyperpigmentation, radiance, roughness; **+18.9% periorbital dermal thickness** by ultrasound; gene-expression shifts (HO-1, VEGFA, COL3A1). **Limitations:** open-label (no vehicle control arm reported as RCT), **combination** product (cannot isolate resveratrol, let alone cane), proprietary/industry-run. [14]
- **Topical + oral RESVERATROL — Rao A., Briskey D. et al., 2025, *Frontiers in Aging* (DOI 10.3389/fragi.2025.1727244).** 8-week, randomized, placebo-controlled, 4-arm (placebo/placebo; oral 75 mg; **topical 1.5% trans-resveratrol**; both), 122/136 completed. Only the **combined oral+topical** arm beat placebo on wrinkles (−11.9% vs −6.3%, p<0.05); topical arms decreased pigmentation (p<0.001) and increased sebum; **no between-group benefit** on most lines, moisture, or elasticity; **topical-alone did not significantly beat placebo on wrinkles.** **Limitations:** pure resveratrol (not cane), short 8 weeks, no collagen/senescence biomarkers, modest effect sizes, and **industry-funded** (Evolva AG; three authors employed by Lallemand Bio-Ingredients, the active's manufacturer). The most defensible reading is that *topical resveratrol alone* did **not** significantly beat placebo on wrinkles — only the combined oral+topical arm did. [13]
- **Patented esterified vine-shoot derivative — Caudalie "Viniferine" / INCI *Palmitoyl Grapevine Shoot Extract* (Vercauteren patent; PCT/FR 2016/052294).** Brand-reported (not independent peer-reviewed) clinical claims: −63% dark-spot colour intensity over 56 days (n=65), "62× more effective than vitamin C / 4× kojic acid" at tyrosinase inhibition; 82–87% user-reported improvement. **Critical caveat:** this is a **specific patented, palmitoyl-esterified, stabilized molecule** — *not* a commodity grape-cane extract — and the data are **vendor/marketing-grade**, not independently published. Do **not** transfer these numbers to a generic cane extract. [6][7]

**Bottom line by tier:** Strong *in vitro + computational* support for SIRT1 activation, tyrosinase inhibition, antioxidant capacity, and (for the dimer) anti-MMP/pro-collagen. **No human topical evidence for grape-cane extract itself.** The human topical signal is for **combination resveratrol formulas** (modest, mostly pigmentation + some wrinkle effect) and a **separate patented ester** — both borrowed.

## 5. Topical bioavailability / penetration

- **A mixed cargo with split penetration.** This is not one molecule; the extract behaves like several:
  - **trans-Resveratrol** (MW 228, moderate logP) — small enough; computational rules predict good penetration. Real-world topical resveratrol *does* penetrate (Farris showed gene-expression changes and dermal remodeling), so the monomer fraction is the most likely contributor to any topical effect. [3][14]
  - **ε-Viniferin** (MW ~454, logP ~4.6) — larger and **lipophilic**; "medium" predicted penetration; very **poorly water-soluble** (favors oil-phase/encapsulation). Good for partitioning into the lipid-rich stratum corneum but may stall there without help. [3][10][12]
  - **Higher oligomers (vitisin B, hopeaphenol, miyabenol C; MW >500)** — predicted **poor** penetration; likely act at/near the surface (antioxidant film) rather than reaching viable dermis. [3]
- **No experimental human skin-penetration data for the cane extract.** All "bioavailability" in the flagship paper is *in-silico drug-likeness scoring*, not Franz-cell/PAMPA/tape-strip/biopsy. This is a real gap. [3]
- **Stability is the bigger limiter than size.** Resveratrol is notoriously **photolabile** (trans→cis isomerization under UV/light, with loss of activity) and oxidation-prone — a well-known formulation challenge requiring opaque/airless packaging and antioxidants. [15 — partially unverified] ε-viniferin (dimer) is generally regarded as somewhat more stable/lipophilic than resveratrol, but is poorly water-soluble. [10]
- **Practical read:** Penetration of the *active* fractions is *plausible* (especially resveratrol) but **unproven for the extract**; delivery systems (liposomes, esterification as in Palmitoyl Grapevine Shoot Extract, oil-phase loading) exist precisely because raw stilbene delivery + stability are uncertain. [6][10]

## 6. Formulation

- **Effective concentration:** **No validated human topical dose for grape-cane extract.** The 2020 cosmetic review notes use **up to ~1%** in formulations (with non-irritating dermal/ocular profiles reported by suppliers). In-vitro actives were 50 µM (SIRT1/tyrosinase) and 1 ppm (cell-culture ε-viniferin COL1A1) — *culture* doses, not formulation guidance. Formulate conservatively; do **not** imply a validated dose–response. [2][3][8]
- **pH:** Stilbenes are reasonably stable across mildly acidic–neutral pH; resveratrol activity/stability generally favors **~pH 4–6**. Confirm against the chosen base; avoid strongly alkaline systems. [15 — partially unverified]
- **Stability (the key challenge):**
  - **Light/photostability:** resveratrol isomerizes/degrades under UV-vis → use **opaque, airtight/airless packaging**, avoid clear jars, consider added antioxidants (vitamin E, ferulic). [15 — partially unverified]
  - **Oxidation/discoloration:** polyphenol extracts can brown/darken over time and react with metals → consider chelators (e.g., sodium phytate, as Caudalie uses) and inert atmosphere. [7][15]
  - **Solubility:** ε-viniferin and higher oligomers are poorly water-soluble → load via oil phase, glycols, or encapsulation; the monomer (resveratrol) is also poorly water-soluble. [10]
  - Run finished-formula **stability + challenge testing** and color/odor tracking.
- **Pairings (conceptual / on-thesis):** Vitamin E + ferulic acid (the classic resveratrol-stabilizing antioxidant trio), niacinamide (tone + barrier; complementary brightening), peptides, ceramides (barrier thesis). Resveratrol+baicalin+vitamin E is the literature-validated *combination* template. [14]
- **Conflicts:** Strong direct oxidizers; very high/low pH; un-chelated metal ions (promote polyphenol oxidation). Co-formulating with L-ascorbic acid is common in antioxidant serums but watch overall redox stability.
- **Sensory:** Polyphenol extracts can carry **color (amber/brown) and faint scent**; at ≤1% usually manageable but plan for it in a premium aesthetic. Upcycled/wine-by-product provenance is a genuine sensory-storytelling asset.

## 7. Safety

- **Irritation/sensitization:** Suppliers report **non-irritating** dermal and ocular profiles for cane extracts at cosmetic levels (≤~1%), and stilbenes/resveratrol have a generally benign topical safety record. No specific HRIPT/patch data for a given commodity cane extract were located — **generate our own** finished-formula testing. [2]
- **Sun sensitivity:** No photosensitization flag; resveratrol is mechanistically *photoprotective/antioxidant*. (Note this is about the skin's response, separate from the ingredient's own photo-*instability* in the bottle.) [14]
- **Pregnancy/breastfeeding:** No specific topical contraindication identified for grape-cane extract; resveratrol/stilbenes have phytoestrogen-adjacent discussion in *oral* contexts — for topical cosmetic use no clear contraindication, but use conservative copy ("consult your doctor"). [unverified — no definitive topical pregnancy data located]
- **Allergen/sourcing:** Botanical extract → low but non-zero contact-allergy/fragrance-component risk; disclose botanical source. Grape-derived → relevant for grape-allergic consumers (rare). Wine-industry provenance is clean from a wheat/gluten standpoint.
- **General:** Polyphenols are broadly well tolerated topically; the main *practical* risk is formulation instability (degradation/discoloration), not consumer harm.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (per CLAUDE.md guardrails)

- **Cosmetic ingredient status:** Used as a cosmetic botanical (INCI Vitis Vinifera Cane/Stem/Shoot Extract; functions: antioxidant, skin-conditioning, skin-brightening). Confirm the exact INCI and any market-specific restrictions per supplier and target market before formulating. [1][2]
- **The drug/cosmetic line (US FDA frame):** "Activates sirtuins," "boosts NAD+," "anti-aging at the cellular level," "boosts collagen by X%," "reverses dark spots," "extends cell lifespan" all flirt with **structure/function (drug) claims**. The biggest trap is importing **SIRT1/longevity** mechanism language (cell-free assays) and **anti-MMP/collagen** data (in vitro, and from a *different* ε-viniferin source) into topical performance claims.
- ✅ **Safer cosmetic language:** "an upcycled grapevine extract rich in resveratrol and viniferins," "antioxidant-rich botanical inspired by longevity science," "helps defend against environmental/oxidative stress," "helps improve the look of an even, radiant tone," "supports a brighter-looking complexion." (Even brightening copy should be hedged given no cane-specific human topical study.)
- ⚠️ **Risky / needs substantiation or legal review:** "activates SIRT1 in your skin," "boosts NAD+," "stimulates collagen," "fades dark spots by X%," "62× more effective than vitamin C" (that figure belongs to Caudalie's *patented* ingredient, not a generic cane extract — do not borrow it), "anti-aging / reverses aging."
- **Substantiation reality:** To make even modest topical brightening/antioxidant claims we'd want our **own finished-formula human testing**; the public literature does not substantiate a conventional grape-cane serum. See [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **The sustainability story is the standout asset.** Grape canes are **annual vineyard pruning waste** — a high-volume, low-value, traditionally burned/composted by-product. Upcycling them into a high-value stilbene extract is a credible **circular/green-chemistry** narrative (the entire "wine industry by-products" research thread is built on this). [2][16]
- **Standardization risk is real.** Stilbene content swings widely with **cultivar, vintage year, and post-pruning storage**: e.g., trans-resveratrol mean ~4.99 g/kg (Hibernal best), ε-viniferin ~3.24 g/kg, R2-viniferin ~1.73 g/kg across one multi-year study; another reported up to ~6030 µg/g resveratrol + 2260 µg/g ε-viniferin in Cabernet Moravia. Locality mattered *less* than expected; **variety and process matter most.** Require a **COA with quantified trans-resveratrol AND ε-viniferin** (and ideally piceatannol/vitisin), and specify the standardization target. [4][5]
- **Forms/suppliers:** Cosmetic-grade standardized grape-cane/vine-shoot extracts from botanical-actives suppliers (often French/Mediterranean wine-region linked). Patented branded versions (e.g., Caudalie's Palmitoyl Grapevine Shoot Extract) are proprietary and pricier. Generic resveratrol is cheap (mostly Japanese-knotweed-sourced); the *grape-native, multi-stilbene, upcycled* positioning is what you pay a premium for.
- **Cost tier:** Mid (commodity standardized cane extract) to high (patented/stabilized/encapsulated grapevine derivatives).

## 10. Hype vs. evidence (call out the overreach)

- **"Clinically proven" sleight of hand.** The clinical numbers in this space are either (a) **combination resveratrol** formulas (Farris; Rao) — can't isolate the stilbene, let alone the *cane*, or (b) **Caudalie's patented esterified ingredient** — a different molecule with vendor-grade data. None of it proves a generic grape-cane serum works.
- **SIRT1/NAD+ longevity framing.** "Activates sirtuins" rests on a **cell-free fluorometric assay at 50 µM** [3] — a known-confounded assay, and a culture dose, not living human skin. Selling topical "cellular energy / longevity activation" is a mechanism story, not a proven outcome.
- **The collagen claim is doubly borrowed.** The ↑COL1A1 / ↓MMP data come from **cell-culture-sourced ε-viniferin** on cultured cells [8] — not pruning-waste cane, and not human skin.
- **Tyrosinase ≠ visible brightening.** Real but modest enzyme inhibition (IC50 ~53–61 µM, weaker than kojic acid) in a *test tube* does not equal a measured reduction in human dark spots for this extract. [3]
- **Penetration is assumed, not shown.** "Bioavailability" in the headline paper is **in-silico**, not measured in skin; the most bioavailable fraction (resveratrol) is also the **least stable**. [3][15]
- **Don't poach Caudalie's stats.** "62× vitamin C" and "−63% dark spots" describe **Palmitoyl Grapevine Shoot Extract**, a patented ester — using them for a commodity cane extract would be unsubstantiated. [6][7]

## 11. Verdict for the brand

⚠️ **SUPPORTING — excellent story (sustainability + multi-stilbene + longevity), thin human topical proof.**
**One-line:** Grape-cane / vine-shoot extract is a compelling **upcycled, grape-native, multi-stilbene** active that bundles resveratrol with ε-viniferin and a longevity-class mechanism deck — but with **no human topical study on the extract itself**, its real-world performance is inferred from in-vitro assays and borrowed resveratrol/ester data, so it belongs as a **science-and-sustainability supporting ingredient**, not an evidence-backed hero.

**Where it shines for *this* brand:** The **circular/upcycled wine-by-product** provenance is a genuine, defensible differentiation hook that most resveratrol sources (synthetic, Japanese knotweed) lack — strong for a premium, science-forward, sustainability-minded skin-longevity positioning. It also pairs naturally with the brand's antioxidant/tone/photo-defense layer and with `polyphenols-resveratrol` and `grape-seed-pine-bark-opcs` as a coherent "grapevine longevity" story.

**Longevity-relevance note:** As a member of the NAD+/sirtuin/AMPK class, grape cane carries real *longevity pedigree by association* (resveratrol's STAC history, viniferin SIRT1/AMPK activity) — making it a strong **education/narrative** asset for a skin-*longevity* brand. The honest gap is the same as for spermidine and most of this class: **the longevity biology is cell/animal/cell-free, and topical human skin proof for the specific extract is missing.** If we want to make real topical brightening/antioxidant claims, the credible path is **our own finished-formula human testing** (and stability-engineered delivery), not the existing literature.

**Practical recommendation:** Carry as a **supporting active** in an antioxidant/brightening context, lead with the **upcycled sustainability + grape-native multi-stilbene** story, formulate for **stability** (opaque/airless, antioxidant pairing, chelator), require a **COA standardized on resveratrol + ε-viniferin**, and keep copy in safe cosmetic language. Do **not** make it the sole hero of an "anti-aging" SKU on current evidence.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Verified:** 2026-06-19 (independent re-check of every load-bearing citation against primary sources via web search/fetch).

### Overall grade: **D** (unproven topically for the extract itself) — with a **C borrowed-evidence floor** from adjacent resveratrol/ε-viniferin data

There is **no human topical clinical trial on grape-cane / vine-shoot extract**. Every human-topical data point this dossier leans on is **borrowed** from (a) *combination* topical resveratrol formulas or (b) a *different, patented esterified* derivative (Caudalie's Palmitoyl Grapevine Shoot Extract). The mechanism story (SIRT1, tyrosinase, anti-MMP/pro-collagen) is real but lives almost entirely in **cell-free, cell-culture, animal, or in-silico** systems. The dossier's own "SUPPORTING, not hero" verdict is the correct call, and its self-criticism is unusually honest. The grade is **D for the named ingredient**; if one (generously) credits the borrowed resveratrol-monomer topical signal, the most one can claim is a weak/indirect **C** — and even that combination evidence is modest and industry-funded.

### Main caveats (adversarial)
1. **Zero human-topical evidence for the actual ingredient.** The single most important fact; the dossier already leads with it. Grade is **D** specifically because the named active (commodity grape-cane extract) has no topical human study.
2. **Borrowed evidence is itself weak and conflicted.** The best topical-resveratrol RCT (Rao 2025) found **topical-alone did not beat placebo on wrinkles**, and it is **manufacturer-funded**. Farris 2014 is open-label and a combination. So even the borrowed tier is C-grade at best.
3. **Mechanism ≠ outcome.** SIRT1 "activation" is a **cell-free fluorometric assay at a single 50 µM dose** — a historically fluorophore-confounded readout. Tyrosinase inhibition is real but **weaker than kojic acid** and measured in a tube. The pro-collagen/anti-MMP data are from a **different (cell-culture) ε-viniferin** on cultured cells.
4. **Penetration is computational, not measured.** No Franz-cell/PAMPA/biopsy data for the extract.
5. **Photostability/formulation points (ref 15) remain partially unverified** (access-limited source) — keep them flagged as "consistent with general resveratrol literature," not as hard citations.

### Claims to soften / keep softened (now reflected in the dossier)
- Do **not** call the α-viniferin human data a "pilot" — it is a small uncontrolled observation of a *different molecule from a different plant*. (Softened.)
- Do **not** present the Courtois ">85%/0.77%" bioavailability as that paper's finding. (Re-attributed.)
- Keep the Rao RCT framed as **"topical-alone did not beat placebo on wrinkles"** and **industry-funded**. (Added.)
- Continue to firewall Caudalie's "62× vitamin C / −63% dark spots" from any commodity-cane claim. (Already done — keep it.)
- Any brightening/antioxidant claim still requires **our own finished-formula human testing**; the public literature does not substantiate a conventional grape-cane serum. (Already stated — keep.)

**Net:** The dossier was already honest and its verdict ("supporting, not hero; story strong, human topical proof absent") is fully defensible. Corrections made were to citation attributions and funding disclosure, plus softening two over-firm evidence references. No headline claim required reversal — only the α-viniferin "pilot" framing and the Courtois bioavailability attribution were genuinely overstated, and both are now fixed.
