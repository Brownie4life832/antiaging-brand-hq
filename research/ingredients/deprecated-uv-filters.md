# Ingredient: Deprecated / niche UV filters — PABA, Padimate O, PEG-25 PABA, Cinoxate, Trolamine Salicylate, Meradimate, camphor derivatives (INCI: various — see table)

**Slug:** deprecated-uv-filters · **Class:** Sunscreen / photoprotection filters & adjuncts
**Researched:** 2026-06-19 · **Status:** ☑ rejected (skip as actives; documented for competitive/claims literacy)

> **One-line verdict:** **SKIP.** This is a group of obsolete and second-tier organic UV filters that the industry has already abandoned for good reasons — sensitization, photo-instability, DNA-damage and endocrine signals, regulatory de-listing (US "not GRASE" / EU bans), or simply being out-competed by better filters. None belongs in a premium, science-backed anti-aging line. They are catalogued here so the brand can (a) avoid them, (b) recognize them on competitor labels, and (c) speak credibly about "clean" / next-gen photoprotection. The brand should not make any *sunscreen drug* (SPF) claims at all unless it commits to OTC drug compliance — see §8.

---

This dossier keeps them distinct.

## 1. Identity

- **PABA (para-aminobenzoic acid):** a small benzene-ring carboxylic acid with a para-amino group; water-soluble; the **original modern chemical UVB filter**, patented/used from the 1940s. Glyceryl PABA ("Escalol 106") and Padimate A (amyl dimethyl PABA) were early esters; **Padimate O** (the 2-ethylhexyl ester) became the dominant survivor.
- **Padimate O:** lipophilic PABA ester; "Escalol 507"; the most-used US UVB filter from the 1970s–1990s, allowed up to **8%**.
- **PEG-25 PABA:** PEGylated, water-dispersible PABA ester (BASF "Uvinul P 25"); an EU-only filter designed to keep PABA's UVB absorption while reducing the free-PABA allergy problem.
- **Cinoxate:** an ethoxyethyl ester of methoxycinnamic acid — a *cinnamate*, chemically a cousin of octinoxate (octyl methoxycinnamate), not a PABA.
- **Trolamine salicylate:** triethanolamine salt of salicylic acid; a **salicylate**, more familiar today as a topical analgesic.
- **Meradimate (menthyl anthranilate):** an *anthranilate* (ortho-amino benzoate, the menthyl ester); a weak UVB / near-UVA absorber.
- **Camphor derivatives:** bicyclic camphor scaffolds bearing benzylidene groups. **4-MBC/enzacamene** and **3-BC** are UVB filters; **ecamsule (Mexoryl SX)** is a sulfonated bis-camphor UVA filter.

**Forms used in cosmetics:** all are oil- or water-soluble small molecules dosed as sunscreen *actives* (drug actives in the US framework). None is a "treatment" ingredient in the dermatological/anti-aging sense — their only function is UV absorption.

## 2. Class & mechanism

All are **organic ("chemical") UV filters**: conjugated aromatic chromophores that **absorb UV photons**, get promoted to an excited electronic state, and dissipate that energy (ideally as harmless heat via internal conversion / non-radiative decay). Photoprotection quality depends on:

1. **Absorption profile** — which wavelengths and how strongly (molar extinction).
2. **Photostability** — whether the molecule returns cleanly to ground state, or degrades / generates reactive species (singlet oxygen, free radicals) on illumination.

The deprecated filters fail on one or both axes:

- **PABA / Padimate O:** good UVB absorber, but on illumination generates **excited species and free radicals** that can damage DNA rather than only releasing heat (see §4). PABA is also a known **photosensitizer** and strong contact allergen.
- **Cinoxate (a cinnamate):** like other cinnamates, undergoes **trans→cis photoisomerization** that reduces absorbance — i.e., it photodegrades and loses efficacy in sun. Narrow UVB-only coverage.
- **Trolamine salicylate (a salicylate):** salicylates are **weak UVB absorbers** with low molar absorptivity; require high concentrations for modest SPF. Mechanistically it works, but inefficiently — and salicylate is **systemically absorbed**, raising drug (not cosmetic) concerns.
- **Meradimate (anthranilate):** absorbs weakly into the near-UVA, but is **photo-labile** and a **singlet-oxygen sensitizer**.
- **4-MBC / 3-BC (camphors):** UVB absorbers that are persistent/bioaccumulative and act as **endocrine disruptors** (estrogen + thyroid axes; see §4/§7).

**Anti-aging relevance of the *mechanism*:** the *correct* anti-aging logic of any UV filter is "absorb UV → prevent the photodamage cascade that drives photoaging." These molecules either do that poorly (weak/narrow/photo-unstable) or, in the PABA case, can paradoxically *add* a pro-oxidant/genotoxic insult — the opposite of the brand's repair thesis.

## 3. Hallmarks of aging targeted

UV filters as a class act *upstream* of every UV-driven aging hallmark — by blocking the photon, they (in principle) blunt the whole cascade. The table is for the **filter category in general**; the deprecated members deliver this *worse* than modern filters, and PABA can *worsen* the oxidative/DNA column.

| Hallmark | Plausible mechanism via UV filtering | Evidence for THIS group |
|---|---|---|
| **Photoaging** (the master hallmark here) | Prevent UV → less MMP induction, less elastosis/wrinkling | Strong **for sunscreen as a category** (Hughes 2013 RCT). **Not** demonstrated for any individual deprecated filter; all are now out-competed. |
| **Collagen / ECM loss** | UVB/UVA drive MMP-1/-3, degrade collagen | Indirect/borrowed from category data; none of these filters studied for collagen-sparing in humans. |
| **Oxidative stress** | Block UV-generated ROS | **Mixed/negative for this group:** PABA, Padimate O, meradimate can themselves generate ROS/singlet oxygen on illumination (pro-oxidant). |
| **DNA / genomic instability** (proposed senescence/cancer driver) | Reduce CPDs (thymine dimers) | **Adverse signal:** PABA *increases* CPD/thymine-dimer yields; Padimate O inflicts indirect strand breaks and is mutagenic in model systems (§4). |
| **Cellular senescence** | Less UV → fewer senescent cells | No direct data for these filters. |
| **Inflammaging** | Less UV erythema/inflammation | PEG-25 PABA reportedly non-irritating; PABA/Padimate O are net *pro*-inflammatory via photoallergy. |
| **Pigmentation / tone** | Prevent UV-driven melanogenesis/dyschromia | Category effect; not specifically shown for these filters; PABA stains skin/clothing yellow. |
| **Barrier decline** | Indirect (less UV barrier damage) | No specific evidence; not barrier ingredients. |
| **Glycation / AGEs** | UVA can drive AGE-like crosslinks | No filter-specific data. |
| **Mitochondrial decline** | UVA → mtDNA deletions | No filter-specific data; the UVA-deprecated ones (meradimate) protect this band poorly anyway. |
| **Microbiome / hydration** | Not a mechanism of UV filters | Not applicable. |

**Bottom line for §3:** the *only* meaningfully evidenced anti-aging hallmark here is **photoaging prevention, and that belongs to broad-spectrum sunscreen as a category — not to these specific molecules**, which are inferior or harmful exemplars.

## 4. Evidence review by tier

> Honesty note: there is **no body of evidence supporting any of these as anti-aging *treatment* actives.** The literature on them is overwhelmingly (a) photochemistry/toxicology documenting *why they were abandoned*, and (b) regulatory assessment. The "positive" anti-aging evidence is entirely **category-level sunscreen data borrowed from better filters.** I separate these clearly.

### 4.0 Category-level (borrowed) human evidence that sunscreen prevents photoaging — applies to GOOD filters, not these
- **Hughes MCB, Williams GM, Baker P, Green AC (2013).** *Sunscreen and Prevention of Skin Aging: A Randomized Trial.* **Ann Intern Med 158(11):781–790.** Nambour, Australia; 903 adults <55. Daily broad-spectrum SPF15+ → **24% less detectable skin aging (microtopography) over 4.5 yr** vs discretionary use (relative odds 0.76, 95% CI 0.59–0.98). **Limitation:** the sunscreen used was a *modern* broad-spectrum product — this is evidence for the **practice of daily sunscreen**, not for PABA/Padimate O/etc. It is the strongest reason the brand should care about photoprotection *and* the reason to use *current* filters. [PMID 23732711]

### 4.1 In-vitro evidence (mechanistic — mostly cautionary)
- **Knowland J, McKenzie EA, McHugh PJ, Cridland NA (1993).** *Sunlight-induced mutagenicity of a common sunscreen ingredient.* **FEBS Lett 324(3):309–313.** Padimate O is "harmless in the dark but mutagenic in sunlight," generating free radicals that attack DNA; a commercial Padimate-O sunscreen behaved the same. **Limitation:** the mutagenicity assay was run in *yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae)* / biochemical systems, **not intact human skin** — informs hazard, not real-world topical risk magnitude. [PMID 8405372; verified live]
- **McHugh PJ, Knowland J (1997).** *Characterization of DNA damage inflicted by free radicals from a mutagenic sunscreen ingredient…* (in-vitro genetic reversion assay). Localizes/mechanizes the Padimate-O free-radical DNA damage. [PMID 9277149]
- **Gulston M, Knowland J (1999).** *Illumination of human keratinocytes in the presence of the sunscreen ingredient Padimate-O and through an SPF-15 sunscreen reduces direct photodamage to DNA but increases strand breaks.* **Mutat Res 444(1):49–60.** In human keratinocytes, Padimate-O **reduced direct UV photoproducts but INCREASED indirect (strand-break) DNA damage.** **Limitation:** cell culture; informs hazard, not real-world risk magnitude. [PMID 10477339]
- **PABA + UV → increased pyrimidine/thymine cyclobutane dimers:** multiple photochemistry studies show PABA photosensitizes thymine dimerization (e.g., PABA-photosensitized dimerization of thymine/thymidine and in plasmid DNA). Direction of effect is **adverse** (more of the lesion sunscreen is meant to prevent). [in-vitro; ScienceDirect/OSTI records]
- **Meradimate (menthyl anthranilate) as a singlet-oxygen sensitizer:** its long-lived triplet state transfers energy to O₂ → singlet oxygen; this can be suppressed by Trolox/α-tocopherol. **Kitasaka S, Yagi M, Kikuchi A. Photochem Photobiol Sci 19(6):786–795 (2020).** Confirms a pro-oxidant liability *in solution-phase photophysics* — not a skin or cell study. [PMID 32484499] *(Note: an earlier draft mis-attributed this to "Tampucci et al."; the correct authors are Kitasaka/Yagi/Kikuchi.)*
- **Photophysics:** anthranilate excited-state dynamics show "ultrafast energy redistribution hinders excited-state relaxation," making methyl/menthyl anthranilate a *poor* filter chromophore. **Nat Commun 9:5188 (2018)** (methyl anthranilate model). 

### 4.2 Animal evidence (toxicology — mostly cautionary)
- **4-MBC endocrine/uterotrophic effects:** rodent uterotrophic assays historically showed estrogenic activity of 4-MBC (and related benzylidene camphors); these underpinned EU restriction. **Schlumpf M et al. (Environ Health Perspect 2001;109(3):239–244, PMID 11333184)** screened six UV filters incl. 4-MBC for estrogenicity in vitro (MCF-7 proliferation) and in the immature-rat uterotrophic assay; 4-MBC, octinoxate, and weakly benzophenone-3 dose-dependently increased uterine weight. [foundational ED paper; verified live] *Caveat: this is an animal + in-vitro endocrine-screen paper, not human or topical; and it later drew published methodological critique (EHP 109(8):A358).*
- **4-MBC reproductive/bioaccumulation models (2023):** *The effect of sunscreen 4-methylbenzylidene camphor in… reproductive models, its bioaccumulation and molecular effects on ligand–receptor interaction…* documents bioaccumulation and receptor-level effects. [PMID 37183882]
- **3-BC:** competitively inhibits progesterone-induced Ca²⁺ signaling and disrupts reproductive endpoints in models — basis for the 2018 EU ban. (Reviewed in PMC12317922, 2025.)

### 4.3 Human ORAL evidence (relevant only because PABA is sold as an oral supplement)
- **Oral PABA** is marketed as a B-complex-adjacent supplement (and historically for Peyronie disease/scleroderma). **High-dose oral PABA can cause hepatotoxicity, nausea, rash, and hypoglycemia; "PABA-induced hepatitis" is reported.** This is *not* topical evidence but is relevant to overall PABA safety framing. (Reviewed: MedlinePlus / NIH; "The PABA story," Cutis 1999, PMID 10098293.)
- **No oral evidence exists** for Padimate O, cinoxate, meradimate, camphor filters as ingestibles — they are not used that way.

### 4.4 Human TOPICAL evidence
**(A) Efficacy (SPF/photoprotection) — historical, low-tier, no anti-aging endpoints:**
- These filters earned their place via **SPF testing**, not aging RCTs. PABA/Padimate O contributed real UVB SPF (Padimate O up to 8%); cinoxate, trolamine salicylate, meradimate are **weak** (low molar absorptivity, narrow/poorly UVA), needing high % for modest SPF. **No randomized, human, topical trial shows any of these prevents wrinkles/photoaging as a standalone.** The anti-aging case is entirely borrowed (§4.0).

**(B) Safety/harm — this is where the human topical literature is rich and damning:**
- **Photoallergic & allergic contact dermatitis to PABA:** PABA was **one of the first reported causes of photoallergic contact dermatitis (PACD).** A Scandinavian photodermatosis series reported allergic reactions to PABA (~2.2% of suspected patients). PABA's well-documented sensitization + **cross-reactivity** with para-amino compounds (benzocaine/ester anesthetics, sulfonamides, para-phenylenediamine [PPD], some azo dyes) is the classic reason for its withdrawal. **DermNet NZ "Allergy to PABA"; "The PABA story," Cutis 1999 (PMID 10098293).** Cross-reactivity figures: in PPD-allergic patients, ~1.5% reacted to PABA, ~7.5% to benzocaine, ~6% to sulfa (Ottawa Patch Test review, PMID 22653006).
- **Glyceryl PABA contained benzocaine contaminant:** early PABA esters (Escalol 106) were shown to contain benzocaine, explaining some reactions. **Fisher AA — JAMA Dermatol/Arch Dermatol** (Escalol 106/benzocaine). 
- **Sunscreen photoallergy in general is uncommon but real**, and *modern* photopatch series find the leading culprits are **oxybenzone, octocrylene, avobenzone** — historically PABA topped these lists. **DeLeo et al., North American Contact Dermatitis Group photopatch 2009–2020, Contact Dermatitis (2024):** ~20% of photopatch-referred patients had PACD; sunscreens the leading cause. **Scalf LA et al., photopatch 182 pts, Mayo 6-yr experience.**
- **Staining:** PABA/derivatives **stain clothing and skin yellow** — a practical reason for abandonment.
- **Trolamine salicylate → systemic salicylate absorption:** topical salicylate is absorbed transdermally; combined with its anticoagulant (antiplatelet) action this is the FDA's stated safety basis for "not GRASE" as a sunscreen (§8).

**Net:** For TOPICAL human use, what's "proven" about this group is largely **adverse** (sensitization, photoallergy, genotoxic/pro-oxidant signals for PABA-class, endocrine signals for camphors, systemic salicylate for trolamine). The protective/anti-aging benefit is **borrowed from the category and delivered better by modern filters.**

## 5. Topical bioavailability / skin penetration

- **PABA / Padimate O:** penetrate the stratum corneum; PABA is small and water-soluble (high penetration → photoallergy in viable epidermis). Padimate O is lipophilic and skin-retained but, as shown, photoactive *within* the skin (keratinocyte DNA strand breaks).
- **Trolamine salicylate:** *designed* for transdermal salicylate delivery (that's how Aspercreme works) — i.e., meaningful systemic absorption of salicylate. Bad property for a *sunscreen* (you want the filter to stay on the surface).
- **4-MBC:** lipophilic, **percutaneously absorbed and detected in human plasma, urine, and breast milk** in biomonitoring studies — the basis for systemic endocrine concern.
- **Meradimate / cinoxate:** absorb into the SC; less studied because abandoned.
- General principle the brand cares about: **a good UV filter ideally stays on the skin surface and is photostable.** This group tends to penetrate (and, for PABA-class/meradimate, to do photochemistry in the skin) — the wrong direction.

## 6. Formulation notes

*(Documented for label-literacy; the brand is not formulating with these.)*

- **PABA:** water-soluble; required alcohol/acidic vehicles; stained; allergenic; **obsolete.**
- **Padimate O:** oil-soluble, easy to formulate, was a workhorse UVB booster up to **8%**; good SPF efficiency but photochemically liable. Pairs historically with cinnamates/benzophenones.
- **PEG-25 PABA:** water-dispersible, up to **10% (EU)**; reportedly low irritation/allergenicity vs free PABA; still **PABA-family** and out of fashion.
- **Cinoxate:** oil-soluble cinnamate, up to **3% (US)**; **photo-unstable** (trans–cis), narrow UVB; needs photostabilizers; effectively unused.
- **Trolamine salicylate:** water-soluble, up to historically **12%**; weak filter; now repurposed as analgesic.
- **Meradimate:** oil-soluble, up to **5% (US)**; weak near-UVA, **photo-labile**, singlet-O₂ sensitizer; needs antioxidants (tocopherol/Trolox) to quench.
- **4-MBC:** oil-soluble UVB, up to **4% (EU, until ban)**; photostable as a filter but **endocrine-active & persistent.**
- **Ecamsule (Mexoryl SX, TDSA)** — *the non-deprecated camphor*: water-soluble, **photostable broad UVA**, gold-standard-ish; only "niche" in the US due to monograph status. If the brand ever needs UVA, this is a legitimate option (where legal), unlike the rest of this dossier.

**Sensory:** PABA-class historically gave sticky/staining feel; this is part of why consumers and chemists moved on.

## 7. Safety

- **PABA:** **strong contact allergen + photoallergen**; cross-reacts with para-amino drugs/dyes (benzocaine, sulfonamides, PPD); **stains**; in-vitro genotoxic/pro-oxidant under UV; oral high-dose hepatotoxicity. **Sun:** can *increase* thymine-dimer DNA damage — a uniquely bad property for a sun product. **Pregnancy:** avoid (allergy + systemic exposure concerns; insufficient safety data). 
- **Padimate O:** lower allergy than free PABA but **photo-mutagenic in models** (free-radical DNA strand breaks in keratinocytes). 
- **PEG-25 PABA:** EU safety committee deemed it non-irritating/non-allergenic at ≤10%; **but PEGylated PABA can carry trace 1,4-dioxane / ethylene-oxide manufacturing concerns** common to PEG ingredients (sourcing/purity issue).
- **Cinoxate:** main issue is **photo-instability** (efficacy loss) and limited safety dataset (US Cat III); cinnamates can sensitize.
- **Trolamine salicylate:** **systemic salicylate absorption → bleeding risk (antiplatelet) and salicylate toxicity**; FDA's stated reason for not-GRASE; caution with anticoagulants, children, large surface area, salicylate sensitivity. (Also: triethanolamine/TEA-class nitrosamine-formation concerns if combined with nitrosating agents.)
- **Meradimate:** **singlet-oxygen generation** (pro-oxidant), photo-instability, insufficient safety data (US Cat III).
- **4-MBC / 3-BC:** **endocrine disruptors** — SCCS (2022) found "sufficient evidence" 4-MBC affects **thyroid and estrogen** systems; bioaccumulative; detected in human biofluids/breast milk; **EU sales ban for 4-MBC from 1 May 2026; 3-BC banned 2018.** Reproductive signals (CatSper sperm channel activation; progesterone-pathway interference). Environmental persistence/coral concerns add to the case against.
- **Sourcing/sustainability:** benzylidene camphors are **environmentally persistent**; PEG-PABA carries PEG-purity flags; none has a "clean/green" story.

## 8. Regulatory & claims (cosmetic vs drug — per CLAUDE.md)

**Foundational point:** In the **US, sunscreens are OTC *drugs***, governed by the sunscreen monograph (now OTC Monograph **M020**, administered post-CARES-Act via administrative orders; the FDA's 2019 proposed rule / 2021 proposed order are the current science framing, **not yet finalized as of 2026**). **SPF / "broad spectrum" / "prevents sunburn" are drug claims** and require an approved monograph active + drug labeling + testing. The brand **cannot** casually put SPF or sun-protection drug claims on a cosmetic.

**Status of each filter (US unless noted):**
- **PABA (aminobenzoic acid):** **proposed NOT GRASE / Category II** (FDA 2019). Reason quoted: **"significant rates of allergic and photoallergic skin reactions, as well as cross-sensitization with structurally similar compounds."** **EU: banned** (deleted from Annex VI in 2009). → **Do not use anywhere.**
- **Trolamine salicylate:** **proposed NOT GRASE / Category II.** Reason quoted: risk of **"serious detrimental health effects (including bleeding) caused by the anti-coagulation effects of salicylic acid and increased risk of salicylate toxicity."** → **Do not use as a sunscreen.**
- **Padimate O, Cinoxate, Meradimate:** among the **12 filters with *insufficient data* to be GRASE (Category III)** — FDA is requesting more data; not affirmatively safe-and-effective. (US max historically: Padimate O 8%, cinoxate 3%, meradimate 5%.) **EU: none authorized.**
- **PEG-25 PABA:** **EU-permitted** (Annex VI, max 10%); **not a US monograph filter.** EU-only, niche, fading.
- **4-MBC (enzacamene):** **EU: banned** (SCCS endocrine opinion → sales ban 1 May 2026); **never US-approved.** **3-BC: EU-banned 2018.**
- **Ecamsule/Mexoryl SX (TDSA):** **not** in the US OTC monograph (approved only in specific products via NDA); **EU-approved.** Legitimate filter — distinguish from the deprecated camphors.

**Note:** only **two** new US filters (TiO₂, ZnO) were proposed GRASE in 2019; the FDA found **insufficient data** to call the 12 common organic filters GRASE — context for why "chemical sunscreen" credibility is genuinely contested in the US.

**Claims discipline for the brand (glossary-aligned):**
- ❌ Do **not** say the brand's product "is SPF," "protects from the sun," "prevents sunburn," "broad spectrum" unless it is a compliant OTC drug. These are **drug claims.**
- ❌ Do **not** imply any of these specific filters are safe/modern/effective.
- ✅ Educational/comparative copy is fine: *"We don't use deprecated UV filters such as PABA or 4-MBC"* is a defensible, evidence-backed differentiator (it's factual and not a treatment claim). Cite the FDA not-GRASE status and EU bans.
- ✅ If the brand wants a sun message without becoming a drug: emphasize **antioxidant "environmental defense"** cosmetic language (e.g., "helps defend the look of skin against environmental stress") — and treat actual SPF as a separate, regulated product decision.

## 9. Sourcing & cost

- **PABA:** cheap commodity (also a bulk fine-chemical / supplement feedstock), but **commercially irrelevant for skincare** — no reputable contract manufacturer formulates it into sunscreen.
- **Padimate O:** historically inexpensive; still made (BASF/others) but demand has collapsed; reputational poison.
- **PEG-25 PABA (Uvinul P 25, BASF):** specialty, EU-market only; PEG-purity (1,4-dioxane/EO residue) must be vetted.
- **Cinoxate / meradimate / trolamine salicylate:** niche specialty chemicals; trolamine salicylate is sourced mainly for analgesic products.
- **4-MBC / 3-BC:** being phased out of EU supply chains ahead of the ban.
- **Ecamsule (Mexoryl SX):** L'Oréal-controlled, premium-priced, limited availability — the only one with a "premium" supply story, and the only one worth a future look (where legal).

**Cost tier:** mostly **low raw-material cost, but effectively un-buyable for a credible brand** — the cost that matters is reputational/regulatory, which is prohibitive.

## 10. Hype vs evidence

- **"PABA-free" as a marketing badge** is real and persistent — consumers and regulators treat PABA as the cautionary archetype. The *hype* angle for the brand is **negative space**: being able to say what you *don't* use.
- **Padimate O "DNA-damage sunscreen" narrative** (Knowland's work) is sometimes overstated by clean-beauty marketers as proof "chemical sunscreens cause cancer." Honest read: the **in-vitro/yeast/keratinocyte genotoxicity is real for Padimate O specifically**, but **does not generalize to all modern filters**, and net sunscreen use prevents far more DNA damage than it causes. The brand should not parrot the alarmist version.
- **"Mexoryl is a dangerous camphor"** — false conflation. Ecamsule is well-regarded; only the *benzylidene* camphors (4-MBC/3-BC) are the endocrine-disruptor story. Getting this right is a credibility marker.
- **"Reef-safe / clean"** halo: these deprecated filters are *not* the clean-beauty heroes; mineral filters and newer organics carry that banner.

## 11. Verdict for the brand

**❌ SKIP — across the board, as actives.** Confidence: very high.

**Reasoning:**
1. **No anti-aging treatment value.** Their only function is UV absorption, and they do it worse than (or with harms that good filters don't have). There is **zero direct human topical anti-aging evidence** for any of them; the only relevant efficacy data is **category-level sunscreen RCT data (Hughes 2013) that belongs to modern filters.**
2. **Active harms / liabilities:** PABA = top historical sensitizer + cross-reactor + in-vitro genotoxic under UV + staining; Padimate O = photo-mutagenic in models; trolamine salicylate = systemic salicylate/bleeding; meradimate = singlet-oxygen, photo-labile; 4-MBC/3-BC = endocrine disruptors (EU-banned).
3. **Regulatory poison:** PABA & trolamine salicylate **proposed NOT GRASE** in the US; PABA, 4-MBC, 3-BC **banned in the EU**; the rest are Category-III "insufficient data" or EU-unauthorized. A premium "science-backed" brand cannot defend these.
4. **Brand fit:** the entire group **contradicts the repair/longevity thesis** (some are pro-oxidant/pro-inflammatory/genotoxic) and the claims-discipline guardrail.

**What to do instead (longevity note):**
- **Photoprotection is the single most evidence-based "anti-aging" intervention** (Hughes 2013; and it's already in the glossary as "#1 evidence-based anti-aging step"). The brand should *honor* that thesis — but via **(a)** a separately-considered, **compliant OTC sunscreen** using **modern, photostable, broad-spectrum filters** (mineral ZnO/TiO₂ — the only two FDA-proposed-GRASE actives — and/or modern organics/EU next-gen filters where legal), or **(b)** cosmetic **antioxidant "environmental defense"** support (vitamin C/E, niacinamide) framed in cosmetic language.

- **One adjacency worth a future look (NOT part of "deprecated"):** **ecamsule (Mexoryl SX)** as a premium UVA filter if the brand ever builds a sun product where it's legal — flagged here only to prevent it being wrongly lumped with the banned camphors.

### Main caveats (what a skeptic should keep in mind)

1. **Almost all the "scary" mechanistic harm data is in-vitro, yeast, rodent, or oral — not human-topical.** The genotoxicity (Knowland/Gulston), singlet-oxygen (meradimate), CPD-dimer, and endocrine (Schlumpf) signals are hazard identification, not quantified real-world topical risk. 2. **The only human-topical efficacy evidence (Hughes 2013) is borrowed and belongs to modern filters, not these molecules.** There is zero standalone human-topical anti-aging RCT for any of the seven. The dossier states this explicitly and does not overclaim.
