# Device / Procedure: IV Therapy / "Beauty Drips" (glutathione, vitamin C, NAD+, Myers cocktail)

**Researched:** 2026-06-21 · **Status:** ☑ context-only · ☑ avoid (for the IV procedure itself)
**Category:** systemic (intravenous infusion) — provider-administered, often in non-medical "drip bar" / med-spa settings
**Regulatory class (US):** Not an FDA-approved therapy for any cosmetic/anti-aging endpoint. The infused substances are **drugs / compounded drug products** (IV vitamins, glutathione, NAD+); mixing them into an IV bag is **compounding**. No "wellness drip" is FDA-approved. — *flag clearly: this is the single most regulatory- and safety-loaded modality in this folder.*

> Scope note: This is an exhaustive, citation-backed evidence review for the brand's research base.
> Evidence is separated strictly by tier (mechanistic/in-vitro → animal → human: RCT → controlled →
> open-label → case/anecdote). Where a benefit is borrowed from a stronger clinical setting (e.g. a
> deficiency-correction or oncology infusion used to justify a cosmetic "beauty drip"), it is flagged.
> Claims discipline per [`../../CLAUDE.md`](../../CLAUDE.md) and [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).
> **This is a hype-heavy, safety-heavy category. The dossier is deliberately skeptical.**

## 1. Identity

- **What it is:** Elective intravenous infusion of vitamins, antioxidants, or cofactors marketed for "beauty," "glow," "skin brightening," and "anti-aging / longevity." Sold à la carte at drip bars, med-spas, and mobile/home IV services, usually by nurses under loose physician oversight.
- **Modality family:** systemic (parenteral nutrient/antioxidant delivery). Not topical, not a device.
- **The four cocktails the founder called out:**
  - **Glutathione IV** — the big Asian "skin-whitening / brightening" drip (often paired with high-dose vitamin C). The headline safety problem.
  - **Vitamin C IV** — high-dose ascorbic acid; "collagen / antioxidant / glow" claims.
  - **NAD+ IV** — the longevity-hyped, expensive "cellular energy / anti-aging" drip. Almost no human skin data.
  - **Myers' cocktail / generic multivitamin + hydration "beauty drips"** — Mg, Ca, B-complex, B12, vitamin C; the original "feel-better" infusion (Dr. John Myers, popularized by Alan Gaby).
- **Representative brands:** Drip bars and chains (e.g. The IV Lounge, Restore, Liquivida, Reset IV, mobile services). None hold an FDA approval for a cosmetic endpoint.

## 2. Mechanism of action

The entire marketing rationale is **gut bypass → higher plasma levels**. This part is pharmacokinetically real:
- **Vitamin C:** oral absorption saturates above ~1 g (intestinal SVCT transporter limit), capping plasma near ~220 µmol/L; IV bypasses this. Levine et al. (Ann Intern Med 2004) modeled a 50 g IV dose reaching ~13,400 µmol/L — roughly 30–70× oral peaks. **Higher blood level is proven; a higher blood level translating into a visible skin benefit in a healthy person is not.**
- **Glutathione:** the body's master endogenous antioxidant; proposed to lighten skin by shifting melanogenesis from dark eumelanin toward lighter pheomelanin and inhibiting tyrosinase. Plausible biochemistry; IV gives a transient plasma spike with poor evidence of durable skin uptake.
- **NAD+:** a coenzyme central to mitochondrial energy and sirtuin/PARP signaling; NAD+ declines with age. The "restore cellular energy" pitch is mechanistically attractive but **mostly extrapolated from cell/rodent work**; whether infused NAD+ even reaches cells intact (vs. being degraded to precursors first) is contested.
- **Myers / multivitamin:** corrects deficiency and rehydrates. In a non-deficient, well-hydrated person there is **no mechanistic headroom** — you cannot "supercharge" a system that is already replete; the excess water-soluble vitamins are largely renally excreted.

**Separation of plausible vs proven:** the bioavailability mechanism is real; the **skin-specific, anti-aging benefit in healthy non-deficient people is unproven across all four cocktails.**

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

Almost all claimed benefits are **systemic-antioxidant** in nature with little skin-specific proof. Map honestly:

| Hallmark | Targeted? | Strength / where the evidence comes from |
|---|---|---|
| Collagen/ECM loss | Claimed (vit C) | Vit C is a required cofactor for collagen hydroxylases — but that's a *deficiency* fact; no controlled trial shows IV vit C builds dermal collagen or firms skin in replete adults. **D** |
| Oxidative stress | Claimed (all) | Mechanistically plausible (glutathione, vit C, NAD+ are antioxidants/cofactors); no controlled skin-aging endpoint. **C/D** |
| Glycation/AGEs | Not meaningfully | No relevant human data. **F** |
| Cellular senescence | Claimed (NAD+) | Rodent/in-vitro only; no human skin trial. **D/F** |
| Mitochondrial decline | Claimed (NAD+) | Core NAD+ pitch; preclinical activity clear, human anti-aging outcomes inconclusive; **zero** IV/IM skin trials. **D/F** |
| Inflammaging | Weakly claimed | No controlled skin data. **F** |
| Photoaging | Indirect (antioxidant) | No IV-specific evidence. **F** |
| Pigmentation/tone | Claimed (glutathione → "whitening/brightening") | The one place with *any* IV trial signal — modest, transient, safety-caveated (see Tier 3). **C** |
| Microbiome | Not targeted | N/A |
| Hydration/water-loss | Real but trivial | Saline rehydrates transiently; not anti-aging. **(systemic, not dermal)** |

## 4. Evidence review by tier  *(the core of this dossier)*

### Tier 1 — Mechanistic / in-vitro / ex-vivo
- Glutathione shifts melanogenesis toward pheomelanin and inhibits tyrosinase in cell models (basis for all "whitening" claims). Vitamin C is an obligate cofactor for prolyl/lysyl hydroxylase in collagen synthesis. NAD+ supports sirtuin/PARP and mitochondrial function in cells. **All real biochemistry; none demonstrates an in-skin cosmetic effect from an infusion.**
- Levine et al., *Ann Intern Med* 2004 — pharmacokinetic modeling: IV vitamin C reaches ~30–70× the plasma concentration achievable orally. **This is the strongest "proven" claim in the whole category — and it's about a blood number, not a skin outcome.**

### Tier 2 — Animal
- NAD+ precursor (NR/NMN) rodent studies show metabolic/healthspan signals; an *Pharmacological Research* 2026 PRISMA review (below) pooled ~80 rodent studies. **Borrowed from animals; dermatologic aging endpoints rarely assessed and not in humans.**

### Tier 3 — Human, lower-grade (open-label / uncontrolled / case series / split-face uncontrolled)
- **Myers' cocktail — Gaby/Ali open-label experience:** decades of anecdote and one small RCT (below); uncontrolled reports claim symptom relief, no skin-aging endpoint. Low grade.
- **IV NAD+ tolerability — Reyna et al., *Frontiers in Aging* 2026:** retrospective EMR review, n=6 IV NAD+ (500 mg ×4 daily) vs n=8 IV NR. **All 6 NAD+ recipients had adverse experiences** (moderate–severe abdominal cramping, diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, raised heart rate, chest pressure), forcing slow ~97-min infusions. **Anti-aging/skin outcomes were not measured.** A safety/tolerability dataset, not an efficacy one.
- **Glutathione oral/topical** (context for IV): Arjinpathana & Asawanonda oral RCT (500 mg/d ×4 wk) showed modest melanin-index reduction; Watanabe et al. 2% topical lotion double-blind RCT reduced melanin over 10 wk. **These are the better-supported routes — and they are NOT IV.**

### Tier 4 — Human, controlled (RCT / vehicle- or sham-controlled)  *(the tier that supports real claims)*
- **IV glutathione — Zubair et al. (cited in Alzahrani et al., *Cureus* 2025 review):** 1200 mg IV twice weekly ×6 wk. Result: **37.5% reported lighter skin vs 18.7% placebo** — a weak, subjective signal — and benefit **faded within 6 months**. Safety: **32% had adverse events including liver dysfunction and one anaphylaxis case.** This is essentially the *only* controlled IV beauty-drip trial with a positive signal, and it comes pre-loaded with a serious harm rate.
- **Myers' cocktail — Ali et al., *J Altern Complement Med* 2009 (Yale pilot RCT):** n=34 fibromyalgia, weekly IV vs placebo. **No significant difference vs placebo**; both arms improved (placebo response). Not a skin study; included to show the best Myers RCT is null.
- **IV vitamin C for skin / NAD+ IV for skin:** **No controlled trials exist for the cosmetic/anti-aging endpoint.** The 2026 PRISMA NAD+ review (Pharmacological Research) explicitly found **no eligible human trials of IV/IM/SC NAD+ for anti-aging or wellness, and dermatologic outcomes rarely assessed** — clinical efficacy "inconclusive."
- **Reviews of IV vitamin therapy in healthy adults** (Alangari, *Cureus* 2025, "To IV or Not to IV"; Merck/MSD Manual): benefits "largely anecdotal," **discouraged in healthy people**; high-quality evidence absent outside documented deficiency/malabsorption.

**What is proven vs borrowed:** *Proven* = IV raises plasma vitamin levels far above oral (a number, not a benefit), and corrects genuine deficiency. *Borrowed* = every skin/anti-aging claim, lifted from deficiency-correction, oncology high-dose vit C, cell/rodent NAD+ work, or non-IV glutathione routes. The skin-specific controlled evidence is **one weak, harm-laden glutathione trial and otherwise a vacuum.**

## 5. Treatment parameters / protocol

The "dose" analogue: a 30–60 min infusion in a chair. Glutathione "whitening" courses run ~600–1200 mg 1–2×/week for weeks-to-months with "maintenance" pushed indefinitely; NAD+ 250–1000 mg over 1–4 hours (slow, because of cramping); Myers/vit C weekly. **Operator skill: requires venous access and resuscitation capacity for anaphylaxis — frequently administered in settings without it.** Time-to-"result": subjective/immediate placebo "glow" from hydration; any glutathione lightening is transient and reverses within ~6 months off-treatment. No durable result demonstrated.

## 6. Safety

**This section is the headline.** IV is an invasive route; harms are categorically more serious than for any topical product.

- **Route risks (all drips):** infection / sepsis (FDA documented a patient hospitalized with septic shock + multi-organ failure after a home IV vitamin infusion), vein damage/phlebitis, air embolism, **electrolyte imbalance and fluid overload** (dangerous in cardiac/renal patients), and bloodborne-pathogen transmission (HIV, hep B/C) in non-sterile facilities.
- **Glutathione IV (the worst offender):** liver dysfunction (~32% in the controlled trial), anaphylaxis, and — per the **Philippine FDA advisory (FDA Advisory No. 2019-182, "Unsafe Use of Glutathione as Skin Lightening Agent")** and the Philippine Dermatological Society — **toxic effects on liver, kidneys, and nervous system; Stevens-Johnson syndrome / toxic epidermal necrolysis; thyroid dysfunction; and reported deaths** linked to skin-whitening use. Doses used for whitening run far above the approved adjuvant-chemotherapy use [unverified — commonly cited as ~5× approved dose; not confirmed against a primary source here]. When paired with high-dose IV vitamin C, added **kidney-stone / oxalate nephropathy risk** (and hemolysis risk in G6PD deficiency).
- **NAD+ IV:** consistent **moderate-to-severe abdominal cramping, nausea, vomiting, chest pressure, raised heart rate** during infusion (Reyna 2026 — every recipient affected); long-term safety unknown.
- **Vitamin C IV high-dose:** oxalate nephropathy/kidney stones, hemolysis in G6PD deficiency, hyperosmolar risk.
- **Contraindications:** renal impairment, heart failure, G6PD deficiency, pregnancy, allergy history. **Who should not use it: essentially any healthy person seeking a cosmetic effect — the risk/benefit is upside-down.**
- **Drip-bar quality issue:** compounding under insanitary conditions; inconsistent oversight; "drip anywhere" mobile/home models amplify infection risk.

## 7. Regulatory & claims

- **Device/drug/cosmetic line:** None of these is a cosmetic. The infusates are **drugs/biologic-adjacent compounded products**; the FDA states adding vitamins to an IV bag **is compounding** and issued a compounding **risk alert (Oct 2021)** about insanitary conditions at IV hydration clinics. **No wellness drip is FDA-approved** for energy, immunity, beauty, or anti-aging.
- **Grey zone & crackdown:** the FDA regulates the drugs/compounders but **does not approve "drip bars"**; oversight falls to state medical/pharmacy boards. As of 2024–2026, NABP/FSMB/NCSBN and multiple state boards (KS, TX, CA, OR) have moved to treat IV-hydration businesses like clinical facilities, with audits and crackdowns; NBC News/NPR documented safety warnings about unregulated med-spa drips.
- **What a cosmetic brand may claim:** **Nothing about selling or administering IV therapy.** A skincare brand cannot sell, bundle, refer, or imply efficacy for beauty drips without becoming a medical actor — far outside cosmetic regulation and squarely in drug/clinical-practice territory. Per [`../../CLAUDE.md`](../../CLAUDE.md), even *adjacent* claims ("brightens from within," "boosts collagen") are drug-territory structure/function claims and must be avoided. **This modality is uninvestable and unsellable for the brand.**

## 8. Cost & accessibility / market

Consumer pricing: Myers/vitamin drips ~$100–300/session; glutathione courses run into the hundreds–thousands over a multi-week series; **NAD+ is the premium tier at ~$300–1,000+ per infusion**, often sold in packages of 4–10. The global IV-therapy / "wellness drip" market is large and fast-growing (multi-billion USD, double-digit CAGR), heavily driven by aesthetics and "longevity" framing and influencer marketing — i.e., **demand is real even though efficacy is not.** No supply/manufacturing adjacency exists for a cosmetic brand (would require a clinical/pharmacy license).

## 9. Hype vs evidence

The gap here is the widest in the devices folder.
- **Overclaim:** "bypasses the gut for superior absorption, so it works better." **Reality:** higher plasma levels are real but do not produce a demonstrated skin/anti-aging benefit in healthy people; reviews actively discourage wellness IV in the non-deficient.
- **Overclaim:** glutathione "whitening/brightening." **Reality:** one weak controlled IV trial (subjective, transient, ~32% adverse events); the better evidence is for *oral/topical*, not IV; multiple FDAs warn against IV.
- **Overclaim:** NAD+ "reverses aging at the cellular level." **Reality:** no human IV/IM anti-aging or skin trials; a 2026 systematic review calls clinical efficacy inconclusive; infusions reliably cause cramping.
- **Genuinely supported:** corrects documented deficiency/dehydration; raises plasma vitamin levels; a transient placebo "glow." That's it.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Verified:** 2026-06-21. Independent re-check of the headline claims against primary sources.

### Overall evidence grade (graded by cocktail, for the SKIN / anti-aging endpoint)
- **Glutathione IV (whitening/brightening): D.** One controlled trial (Zubair, via Alzahrani 2025 *Cureus*) with a weak, subjective, transient signal and a high adverse-event rate; multiple FDAs warn against it. Better evidence exists for *oral/topical*, not IV.
- **Vitamin C IV (collagen/glow): D.** Only the plasma-PK fact (Levine 2004) is solid; **zero** controlled skin-aging trials. Collagen-cofactor logic is deficiency biology, not a healthy-adult benefit.
- **NAD+ IV (anti-aging/longevity): F (for skin).** 2026 PRISMA review found **no eligible human IV/IM/SC anti-aging or skin trials**; only tolerability data (Reyna 2026) showing reliable adverse infusion symptoms.
- **Myers' / multivitamin "beauty drip": F.** Best RCT (Ali 2009, fibromyalgia) was **null vs placebo**; no skin endpoint; reviews discourage use in healthy people.

### Citation audit (do the studies exist and say what's claimed?)
- *Cureus* 2025 glutathione review (Alzahrani et al.), *Cureus* 2025 IV-vitamin review (Alangari), *Frontiers in Aging* 2026 NAD+ tolerability (Reyna et al.), and the 2026 *Pharmacological Research* PRISMA NAD+ review were **fetched/confirmed directly** and say what is attributed. Philippine FDA Advisory 2019-182 exists (URL returned 403 to the fetcher but is corroborated by PNA news and two peer-reviewed reviews). Levine 2004 and Ali 2009 are cited via review summaries, not fetched in full — directionally standard and well-known but flagged as **secondary-sourced here**.
- **[unverified]:** the specific "~5× the approved dose" figure for whitening glutathione and a precise death count are widely repeated but **not pinned to a fetched primary source** in this pass; stated cautiously.

### Main caveats (safety first, then evidence weakness)
1. **Safety is the dominant fact.** IV is invasive: infection/sepsis (FDA-documented multi-organ-failure case), electrolyte/fluid risk, anaphylaxis, hepatotoxicity (glutathione), oxalate nephropathy (vit C), and reliable GI/cardiac symptoms (NAD+). Glutathione whitening carries FDA warnings citing SJS/TEN and reported deaths.
2. **Evidence is weak-to-absent** for every skin/anti-aging endpoint; the bioavailability "mechanism" is the only proven piece and it's a blood number, not a benefit.
3. **Regulatory:** drug/compounding territory, not cosmetic — **unsellable and unclaimable by the brand.**

### Claims that should be softened (or never made)
- Do NOT say drips "boost collagen," "brighten from within," "detox," or "reverse cellular aging" — unsupported and drug-territory.
- Do NOT imply IV is "more effective because it bypasses the gut" — higher plasma ≠ skin benefit.
- In any education content, **lead with safety + lack of evidence**, present glutathione's signal as weak/transient/harm-laden, and never refer readers to a drip provider.

**Net:** Across all four cocktails this is a **hype-led, evidence-poor, safety-heavy** category. The strongest data point in its favor (IV raises plasma levels) is irrelevant to skin benefit in healthy people; the only positive skin trial (IV glutathione) is weak, transient, and comes with a ~32% adverse-event rate plus international regulatory warnings. For the brand the verdict is unambiguous: **❌ avoid the procedure, 📚 use it as a flagship "does this actually work / is it safe?" education and trust-building topic.** Trust the safety and regulatory findings; treat every efficacy claim in this space as guilty until proven.
