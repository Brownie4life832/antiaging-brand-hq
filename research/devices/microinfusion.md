# Device / Procedure: Microinfusion / Micro-channeling (microneedling + simultaneous serum/drug delivery)

**Researched:** 2026-06-21 · **Status:** ☑ context-only / ☑ product-adjacency candidate (with heavy caveats) / ⬜ partner candidate / ☑ avoid (for dermal-infusion claims)
**Category:** at-home device **and** in-clinic device-driven procedure (graded separately throughout)
**Regulatory class (US):** Mixed and contested. The professional benchmark (AquaGold) and at-home "micro-infusion systems" have been treated by FDA as **devices marketed WITHOUT clearance/approval** (see §7 — a 2021 FDA Warning Letter). The serums are **cosmetics**; the *act of infusing a drug* (botox/filler) is an **off-label / unapproved** use of a drug + an uncleared device. — *flag clearly.*

> Scope note: Exhaustive, citation-backed evidence review for the brand's research base. Evidence is
> separated strictly by tier. Microinfusion is a **hybrid**: it borrows almost all of its evidence
> from (a) plain microneedling RCTs and (b) microneedle transdermal-delivery science — there is
> essentially **no direct controlled trial of a branded micro-infusion *device*** (especially at-home).
> Where a benefit is borrowed from a stronger setting it is flagged. Claims discipline per
> [`../../CLAUDE.md`](../../CLAUDE.md) and [`../../brand/glossary.md`](../../brand/glossary.md).

## 1. Identity

- **What it is:** Microinfusion (a.k.a. micro-channeling) creates an array of fine micro-channels in the skin **while simultaneously depositing a serum/drug cocktail through or into those channels** — distinct from plain microneedling (channels then *topical* serum applied on top) and from RF-microneedling (adds radiofrequency heat). The hollow-needle in-clinic version is a true **microinjector**; the at-home stamp/pen versions are mostly solid-needle microneedling marketed with "infusion" language.
- **Modality family:** mechanical (channel-creation) + chemical (delivery of actives). The "infusion" is the delivery rationale layered on the microneedling injury-and-repair mechanism.
- **Key forms & representative brands/devices:**
  - **In-clinic benchmark — AquaGold® Fine Touch™ (Aquavit Pharmaceuticals):** 20 **hollow 24k-gold microchannels**, each thinner than a hair, ~**0.6 mm** depth; deposits a clinician-mixed cocktail (commonly micro-dilute botulinum toxin, hyaluronic-acid filler, PRP, vitamins) as it stamps. Marketed as a *microinjector*, not a microneedling device. **Regulatory status contested — see §7.**
  - **At-home stamp — Qure™ Micro-Infusion System:** single-use sterile head with **0.5 mm** 24k-gold-plated needles + a single-use serum ampoule poured into the device; "stamp" 2–3 passes across the face. Cosmetic serum (no drug). The viral DTC product the founder named.
  - **At-home pen — "korabeauticals" → Korabeauticals® V2 [identified, see below]:** an electric microneedling **pen** (AOVN oscillating needle cartridge, up to ~3 mm, $179.99). **Important: this is plain microneedling, not true microinfusion** — its serum is applied separately to the skin surface for glide; needles do **not** carry/deposit it. It belongs to the at-home pen category, adjacent to but distinct from stamp-style microinfusion.
  - **Other at-home stamp/roller category:** StackedSkincare (0.2 mm roller — surface microneedling/"micro-exfoliation," not infusion); BeautyBio **GloPRO** is a microneedling roller (plain microneedling, *not* infusion — explicitly distinguished here); various DTC "micro-infuser" clones (EvenSkyn, Glov, Seranova, Renewa, etc.).
- **The specific named products to cover:** Qure (✓ identified), "korabeauticals" pen (✓ identified as **Korabeauticals V2** — see §1 note and §9), AquaGold Fine Touch (✓ identified, professional benchmark).

## 2. Mechanism of action

Two stacked mechanisms — keep **plausible** separate from **proven in-skin**:

1. **Microneedling injury-and-repair (well established mechanistically).** Needles create transient micro-wounds → controlled wound-healing cascade → fibroblast activation, neocollagenesis (collagen I/III/VII) and elastin remodeling. This is the *same* mechanism as plain microneedling; the infusion does not change it.
2. **Micro-channel transdermal delivery (established in lab models; magnitude wildly inflated in marketing).** Solid/hollow microneedles physically bypass the stratum corneum, creating temporary hydrophilic channels that raise skin permeability. Ex-vivo/animal models show large fold-increases for *specific* tracer molecules (e.g. ~1,000-fold permeability increase to calcein after a 10-s application; 86–167× higher permeation of insulin nanovesicles vs no pretreatment). **Caveat:** these are model-molecule, ex-vivo or animal results — they do **not** translate to "your face absorbs X% more serum," and the channels reseal within minutes-to-hours.

**The "infusion" rationale:** depositing actives *while* channels are open (rather than after they reseal) should, in principle, increase intradermal delivery vs topical-on-top. Plausible; **not quantified for any consumer micro-infusion device**. The hollow-needle AquaGold genuinely deposits into the dermis; most at-home "infusion" stamps are solid-needle and rely on the serum tracking down the puncture tracks — a weaker, less controlled version of the same idea.

**Proven in-skin effect:** collagen induction from microneedling — **yes** (histology). Quantified intradermal delivery advantage of *microinfusion specifically* over microneedling-then-serum — **not established**.

## 4. Evidence review by tier  *(the core of this dossier)*

### Tier 1 — Mechanistic / in-vitro / ex-vivo
- **Microneedle transdermal delivery (review).** Multiple reviews (e.g. *Front. Bioeng. Biotechnol.* 2022; *Discover Pharm. Sci.* 2025) document that microneedles create transient channels raising permeability by orders of magnitude for **model molecules** (calcein ~1,000×; insulin nanovesicles 86–167×). **Limitations:** ex-vivo/animal, tracer-specific, not facial cosmetic serums, channels reseal; says nothing about clinical anti-aging benefit.

### Tier 2 — Animal
- Insulin/biologic permeation and nanoparticle delivery studies in rodent/porcine skin support the *delivery* concept. **Limitations:** species/skin differences; pharmaceutical payloads, not cosmetic serums; no aging endpoint.

### Tier 3 — Human, lower-grade (open-label / uncontrolled / case series / split-face uncontrolled)
- **El-Domyati et al.** (pilot, n=10 atrophic acne scars; 6 sessions q2wk): **statistically significant ↑ collagen I/III/VII and ↓ elastin** on biopsy (p<0.05). Strong *histologic* support for the microneedling mechanism. **Limitations:** small, uncontrolled, scars not aging-wrinkles, microneedling (not microinfusion).
- **Consumer "evidence" for at-home microinfusion (Qure etc.):** brand self-report ("93% noticed results next day"), magazine reader trials (e.g. Beauticate 8-week try-on), dermatologist endorsements, YouTube before/afters. **This is marketing/anecdote — Tier 3-minus at best.**

### Tier 4 — Human, controlled (RCT / sham- or vehicle-controlled / split-face randomized)  *(the tier that supports real claims)*
- **Microneedling for facial rejuvenation — RCTs exist but are modest.** Systematic review (*J Plast Reconstr Aesthet Surg* 2017) concludes microneedling is a "safe and effective" option for scars/wrinkles **but flags methodological shortcomings**; the field is dominated by small, heterogeneous studies. RCTs of 4 sessions show significant texture/fine-line improvement; a split-face RCT (n=25) of dermaroller + growth factor showed significant pigmentation/wrinkle improvement.
- **Microinfusion-as-*delivery* — the strongest controlled data is the melasma adjuvant literature.** Meta-analyses (e.g. *J Am Acad Dermatol* 2021; *J Cosmet Dermatol* 2024) find **microneedling + topical (tranexamic acid, vit C) beats topical alone** — moderate effect at 8 wk, large at 12–16 wk — with good tolerability. A double-blind split-face RCT (PubMed 2021) showed TXA delivered via microneedling improved melasma. **This validates the *delivery* premise** (channels + active outperform active alone) — but for **melasma/pigment**, in clinic, with microneedling, **not** for at-home anti-aging stamps and **not** for any branded micro-infusion device.
- **AquaGold (the benchmark):** widely used in clinic since ~2014 at 0.6 mm; **no robust independent RCT of the AquaGold device itself** was located in indexed literature — its credibility rests on borrowed microneedling + delivery data, not device-specific trials.
- **At-home micro-infusion devices (Qure, Korabeauticals, clones):** **no peer-reviewed RCT located for any branded at-home micro-infusion/microneedling-pen device.** `[unverified — none found]`

**What is proven vs borrowed.**
*Proven:* (a) microneedling induces collagen (histology + modest RCTs); (b) microneedling *delivery* of a topical can outperform the topical alone for **melasma** (controlled). *Borrowed/unproven:* (c) that a **branded micro-infusion device** — especially **at-home** — delivers a clinically meaningful anti-aging benefit beyond plain microneedling or good topicals. There is **zero device-specific controlled evidence** for the at-home category; AquaGold leans entirely on borrowed data.

## 5. Treatment parameters / protocol

| | In-clinic (AquaGold) | At-home stamp (Qure) | At-home pen (Korabeauticals V2) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Depth | ~0.6 mm, hollow needles | ~0.5 mm, solid 24k-plated | adjustable to ~3 mm (deeper = riskier) |
| Payload | clinician cocktail (toxin/filler/PRP/vitamins) — true intradermal | single cosmetic ampoule per session | serum applied on surface for glide (not delivered) |
| Cadence | every 4–6 wk, course of 3+ | brand suggests ~weekly | 1–2×/wk typical microneedling cadence |
| Operator | physician/trained injector | consumer | consumer |
| Time-to-visible | "glow" days; collagen weeks-months | days (hydration), weeks for texture | weeks for texture |
| Durability | weeks–months; maintenance needed | transient; requires repeat | transient; requires repeat |

**Operator skill / dose analogue:** the "dose" is depth × needle count × cocktail concentration × passes. In-clinic this is controlled; **at-home it is essentially uncontrolled**, which is the crux of the safety problem (§6).

## 6. Safety  *(expanded — this is the decisive section for a topical brand)*

**Headline:** infusing or layering serums into freshly opened skin channels converts *every topical-safety question* into an *injection-safety question*. The skin's barrier — the brand's whole thesis — is deliberately breached.

- **Infection / bloodborne-pathogen risk (the cautionary precedent).** The **CDC MMWR (New Mexico, 2018–2023; *MMWR* 2024;73(16))** documented the **first known HIV transmission cluster from a cosmetic micro-injection service** — "vampire facial" PRP microneedling at an **unlicensed** spa ("spa A"): **five HIV infections** linked to the facility (four clients + one client's partner), traced to **reused single-use equipment, no autoclave on premises, unlabeled blood tubes stored in a kitchen fridge next to food, unwrapped syringes in drawers/trash.** Of 198 former clients tested, no additional HIV (and no HBV/HCV) were found, but the cluster is the definitive warning: **non-sterile micro-injection transmits bloodborne pathogens.** This is the single most important reference for why unsterile or DIY infusion is dangerous.
- **Granuloma / foreign-body reaction — directly relevant to infusing serums.** Multiple case reports describe **foreign-body / sarcoidal granulomas** after microneedling **with cosmeceuticals** (vitamin C serum, others). Mechanism: needling drives the topical product into the dermis, causing **"intradermal tattooing of the topical product"** and a delayed granulomatous reaction (some requiring intralesional steroids or methotrexate). **This is the exact failure mode a topical brand would create by telling customers to infuse its serum** — most serums are formulated for *topical* use (with preservatives, fragrance, vitamin C, etc.) and are **not sterile and not intended for intradermal deposition.**
- **Irritant/allergic reactions.** With the barrier open, actives that are fine topically (vitamin C, retinoids, acids, fragrance, preservatives) can cause burning, prolonged sensitivity, or instant severe reactions.
- **PIH / skin-tone risk.** Microneedling carries post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation risk in darker Fitzpatrick types, especially with aggressive depth or poor technique.
- **At-home misuse.** No sterility control, reused/blunt needles (rollers), wrong depth, contaminated serum, no aseptic field. At-home devices carry higher infection/uneven-result risk than clinic settings.
- **Device-quality / counterfeit.** Aquavit has pursued counterfeit AquaGold devices in litigation; counterfeit microchannel devices raise sterility and breakage risk. The **FDA also issued a 2025 safety communication on radiofrequency-microneedling risks** — regulators are actively scrutinizing the broader microneedling category.
- **Who should not use:** active acne/infection/rosacea flare, keloid-prone, immunosuppressed, pregnancy (for active cocktails), bleeding disorders/anticoagulants; anyone unable to maintain sterility.

## 7. Regulatory & claims

- **Device/drug/cosmetic line:**
  - The **serum** alone = **cosmetic** (if claims stay cosmetic).
  - The **device that injects/deposits into skin** = a **medical device**. Critically, the **FDA Warning Letter to Aquavit Pharmaceuticals (607150, 2021-06-17)** stated that **both the AquaGold® Fine Touch™ and Aquavit's at-home "A.S.A.P! Micro Infusion System" were marketed without clearance/approval** — **adulterated** under §501(f)(1)(B) (no PMA/IDE) and **misbranded** under §502(o) (no 510(k) premarket notification). **➡️ The widely repeated med-spa claim that AquaGold is "FDA-cleared/approved" is, per this letter, inaccurate/contested.** Do **not** repeat it.
  - **Infusing a drug** (botulinum toxin, HA filler) through such a device is an **off-label use of the drug** *and* use of an **uncleared device** — a clinician-liability act, not something a cosmetic brand can sponsor.
- **What a cosmetic brand may claim** (if it ever sold/bundled a stamp + serum), per [`../../CLAUDE.md`](../../CLAUDE.md):
  - **Likely OK (cosmetic):** "helps temporarily improve the appearance of skin texture/radiance," "exfoliating/resurfacing appearance" — **appearance-level, with substantiation.**
  - **Crosses into device/drug territory (needs clearance/substantiation/counsel):** "infuses/injects actives into the dermis," "delivers X% more serum into your skin," "stimulates collagen," "micro-injection," "treats scars/wrinkles," "anti-aging" tied to a needle device. Selling a **needle device** at all likely triggers device regulation (see Aquavit letter).
  - **Hard no:** any claim implying drug delivery (peptides "like botox"), structure/function claims, or instructions to infuse the brand's serum.

Large, hype-driven, **thin on regulation and evidence** — high reputational risk for a science-honest brand to enter on the device side. Suppliers are largely white-label microneedle/cartridge OEMs.

## 9. Hype vs evidence

- **"korabeauticals" — IDENTIFIED but mis-categorized by the founder's framing.** The product is **Korabeauticals® V2**, a real, sold **electric microneedling PEN** ($179.99, AOVN oscillating cartridge, up to ~3 mm). **It is plain microneedling, not microinfusion** — its serum is applied to the skin surface for glide; the needles do **not** deposit it. Its marketing claims ("**80% more topical nutrients**," "**104% faster**") are **vendor claims with no cited peer-reviewed source `[unverified]`** and should be treated as unsubstantiated.
- **The "X% more absorption" genre** (Qure, Korabeauticals, clones): these single-number claims are lifted/extrapolated from ex-vivo tracer studies (calcein, insulin) and **do not describe facial serum absorption**. Overclaim.
- **"FDA-cleared microinjector" (AquaGold):** contested by the 2021 FDA letter (§7). Overclaim/inaccurate.
- **"Microinfusion = a facial that delivers actives deep" for at-home stamps:** the deep-delivery premise is real *in principle* and supported for **clinic microneedling + topical in melasma** — but **no at-home micro-infusion device has device-specific controlled evidence.** The category sells borrowed credibility.
- **Genuinely supported:** microneedling induces collagen (histology); microneedling can enhance delivery enough to beat topical-alone for **melasma** (controlled). That's the honest core.

## 10. Brand relevance & strategic verdict

- **Interaction with our thesis (premium *topical barrier-repair*):** **Conflicts more than it complements.** Microinfusion's entire premise is **breaching the barrier** to push actives in — the opposite of a barrier-repair message. It can be framed as *complementary* only in the narrow, honest sense that good topicals support recovery *after* a clinic procedure. As a substitute it is weak (transient, evidence-thin at home). As a **brand action it is a liability**: encouraging customers to infuse our (non-sterile, topically-formulated) serums into the dermis invites **granuloma, infection, and the vampire-facial-class catastrophe** — and the regulatory exposure of selling/endorsing an uncleared needle device.
- **Product-adjacency call:**
  - **Selling/bundling a micro-infusion device or "infuse our serum" kit → ❌ avoid.** Regulatory (uncleared device, per Aquavit letter), safety (granuloma/infection), and brand-thesis conflict. Log in [`../../ideas/not-considering.md`](../../ideas/not-considering.md).
  - **Education/SEO content on microinfusion → 📚 context-only ✅.** High-search-volume, trust-building if we tell the honest, cited story (and the safety truth).
  - **A "post-procedure / barrier-recovery" topical positioned for people who *do* get clinic microneedling → 🤝 possible complement** (sell the topical, never the needle).
- **Content/SEO angle (the honest consumer question this lets us answer):** *"Does at-home microinfusion (Qure, Korabeauticals, AquaGold-at-home) actually work — and is it safe to push serum into your skin?"* Answer: clinic microneedling has real collagen/delivery evidence; **at-home 'infusion' devices have none of their own**, the absorption numbers are unsubstantiated, the "FDA-cleared" line is contested, and **infusing topical serums risks granulomas and infection — the same category of risk behind the CDC's vampire-facial HIV cluster.** A barrier-repair brand's job is to *keep your barrier intact*, not puncture it.

## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Verified:** 2026-06-21. Independent re-check of the headline claims against primary sources.

### Overall evidence grade: **C overall — graded split:**
- **In-clinic microneedling-as-delivery (microinfusion mechanism) for pigment/delivery: B–** (real controlled/meta-analytic data, but for *microneedling + topical* in melasma, not for any branded micro-infusion *device*).
- **In-clinic AquaGold device specifically: D** (no device-specific RCT located; credibility entirely borrowed; "FDA-cleared" status contested by 2021 warning letter).
- **At-home micro-infusion devices (Qure & clones): D/F** (no peer-reviewed controlled evidence located for any branded at-home device; claims are marketing + borrowed lab tracer data).
- **"Korabeauticals" pen as *microinfusion*: F** (it is plain microneedling, not infusion; absorption claims unsubstantiated).

### Citation audit (do the studies exist and say what's claimed?)
- **CDC MMWR vampire-facial HIV cluster** — ✅ real (*MMWR* 2024;73(16)), primary fetched. Note: total **five** HIV infections linked to the facility (four clients + one partner); "three clients" appears in early news coverage — the dossier uses the MMWR figure.
- **FDA Aquavit warning letter (2021)** — ✅ real, content confirmed via FDA-hosted text: AquaGold + at-home Micro Infusion System cited as adulterated/misbranded, marketed without clearance. This is the load-bearing regulatory correction.
- **El-Domyati histology, microneedling RCTs, melasma meta-analyses** — ✅ real and correctly characterized (collagen induction; delivery-beats-topical for melasma).
- **Microneedle transdermal fold-increase figures (calcein ~1,000×; insulin 86–167×)** — ✅ real but **ex-vivo/animal, model-molecule** — flagged in-text as non-translatable to facial serum %.
- **Qure/Korabeauticals device RCTs** — ❌ none found; explicitly marked `[unverified — none found]`.
- **"80% more nutrients" / "104% faster" (Korabeauticals); "93% next-day results" (Qure)** — vendor claims, **no cited source**, marked `[unverified]`.

### Main caveats
1. **No direct controlled evidence for any branded micro-infusion device, especially at-home** — the whole category borrows from microneedling + delivery science.
2. **"Microinfusion" is inconsistently defined** — true hollow-needle injection (AquaGold) vs solid-needle stamp with serum (Qure) vs a microneedling pen mislabeled as infusion (Korabeauticals). The dossier separates these.
3. **The single biggest brand risk is not efficacy but safety/liability** — granuloma from infusing topical serums + the CDC bloodborne-pathogen precedent.

### Claims that should be softened
- Drop/never use "FDA-cleared microinjector" for AquaGold (contested by 2021 FDA letter).
- Never state at-home microinfusion "delivers X% more serum" — unsubstantiated.
- Do not call Korabeauticals "microinfusion" — it is microneedling.
- Avoid implying any at-home device has clinical anti-aging proof; it does not.

**Net:** The *underlying ideas* are real — microneedling builds collagen, and opening channels can enhance delivery (proven for melasma in clinic). But **the branded micro-infusion device category, especially at-home, has essentially no evidence of its own**, repeats an inaccurate "FDA-cleared" claim, and inflates absorption numbers. For a premium *barrier-repair topical* brand, the correct posture is **education-only (📚) and avoid (❌) on building/selling a device or "infuse our serum" kit** — the regulatory (uncleared device per the Aquavit letter) and safety (granuloma + the CDC vampire-facial HIV cluster) exposure is disqualifying, and the modality contradicts the barrier-intact thesis. Trustworthy as a cited education asset; not as a product line.
