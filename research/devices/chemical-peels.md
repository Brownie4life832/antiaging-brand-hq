# Device / Procedure: Chemical Peels (Chemexfoliation) — superficial · medium · deep

**Researched:** 2026-06-21 · **Status:** ☑ context-only / ☑ product-adjacency candidate (post-peel barrier care only) / ⬜ partner candidate / ⬜ avoid
**Category:** device-driven procedure (chemical) — spans at-home cosmetic (low-%) → professional/medical procedure (deep phenol = surgical-grade)
**Regulatory class (US):** **mixed** — low-% leave-on/rinse-off peels = OTC **cosmetic**; professional superficial/medium peels = **procedure (provider-dependent)**, agents sold as professional chemicals; deep phenol-croton oil = **medical/surgical procedure** (physician, cardiac monitoring). No single "device clearance" — peels are *drugs/chemicals applied as a procedure*, not 510(k) devices. *Flag clearly.*

> Scope note: exhaustive, citation-backed evidence review for the brand research base. Evidence is separated strictly by tier (mechanistic/explant → animal → human open-label/case → human controlled/RCT). **The single most important split in this dossier is DEPTH × SETTING:** an at-home <10% glycolic pad and a Baker-Gordon phenol peel are the *same modality family* but differ by ~1000× in depth, risk, and result. Borrowed claims (an at-home pad trading on professional-peel data) are flagged. This dossier is the **procedure parallel** to the brand's AHA *ingredient* dossier ([`../ingredients/alpha-hydroxy-acids.md`](../ingredients/alpha-hydroxy-acids.md)) — it does **not** re-derive AHA chemistry; it builds the *procedure* angle and cross-references. Claims discipline per [`../../CLAUDE.md`](../../CLAUDE.md).

## 1. Identity

- **What it is:** Controlled application of a caustic/keratolytic agent to ablate a defined depth of skin, triggering re-epithelialization and dermal remodeling — "chemexfoliation." Depth dictates everything (effect, downtime, risk).
- **Modality family:** chemical (controlled chemical wounding-and-repair). Sits alongside the brand's AHA *ingredient* work but is a *procedure*, not a leave-on.
- **Depth tiers & representative agents:**
  - **Superficial (epidermis → papillary dermis):** glycolic 20–70%, lactic, **salicylic 20–30% (BHA, lipophilic, safer in skin of color)**, **mandelic 30–50% (large MW, gentle, low-PIH)**, pyruvic, **Jessner's solution** (salicylic + lactic + resorcinol in ethanol). At-home: glycolic/lactic/mandelic pads & "peels" **<10%, pH >3.0**.
  - **Medium (papillary → upper reticular dermis):** **TCA 20–35%**, classically *combined* to control depth: **Jessner + TCA 35% (Monheit)**, **70% glycolic + TCA 35% (Coleman)**, **CO₂/dry-ice + TCA 35% (Brody)**. TCA-CROSS (focal high-% TCA into ice-pick scars).
  - **Deep (mid-reticular dermis):** **phenol–croton oil (Baker-Gordon / modern Hetter & Stone modifications)**. Dramatic, durable — and the only peel with **systemic cardiotoxicity risk**.
- **Named products/protocols to cover:** at-home glycolic/mandelic pads (consumer); Jessner's, Monheit/Coleman/Brody medium combos; Baker-Gordon & Hetter phenol formulas (professional).

## 2. Mechanism of action

Plausible *and* proven mechanism (well established for this modality):

1. **Controlled chemical injury → keratocoagulation / protein denaturation.** The agent denatures epidermal (and, by depth, dermal) proteins, destroying a defined layer. Superficial agents act largely by **corneodesmolysis / accelerated desquamation** (same mechanism as the brand's AHA dossier §2 — calcium-dependent loss of corneocyte cohesion); TCA and phenol **coagulate protein** outright ("frosting" = visible coagulation endpoint).
2. **Wound-healing cascade → re-epithelialization.** Surviving adnexal (follicular/sebaceous) stem cells re-epithelialize from the depth of the wound. Deeper = more complete resurfacing but slower, riskier healing.
3. **Dermal remodeling (depth-dependent — the anti-aging payload).** Medium/deep peels drive **neocollagenesis**: a new subepidermal band of type I/III collagen, GAGs, and reorganized elastic fibers. **Proven in-skin** for medium/deep (histology, below); for superficial it is the weaker AHA-style signal (small, cumulative — see AHA dossier §4).
4. **Pigment correction.** Removal of pigmented keratinocytes + dispersion of melanin; salicylic/mandelic/glycolic improve dyschromia. *Double-edged:* the same wounding can trigger **post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH)**, especially in deeper phototypes.

**Proven vs plausible:** dermal neocollagenesis from **deep phenol** is histologically proven and *durable* (15–20 yr). Surface renewal/desquamation from **superficial** peels is proven but modest and non-durable. The leap "at-home pad → deep-peel remodeling" is **not** supported — that is borrowed, not earned.

## 3. Hallmarks of skin aging targeted

| Hallmark | Targeted? | Strength / where the evidence comes from |
|---|---|---|
| **Photoaging (wrinkles, texture, sallowness)** | ✅ Yes | **Deep phenol = strong** (durable wrinkle effacement, histology + large case series). **Medium TCA = moderate** (systematic review). **Superficial = mild/cumulative** (AHA-grade, modest). |
| **Pigmentation / dyschromia / melasma** | ✅ Yes | **Moderate** — superficial peels (glycolic, salicylic, mandelic, Jessner) as *adjuncts*; meta-analysis favors glycolic, but topicals (hydroquinone/tretinoin) often equal or beat peels and melasma recurs. |
| **Collagen / ECM loss** | ✅ (depth-gated) | **Strong for deep** (new collagen band, persists 15–20 yr); **weak for superficial** (AHA §4 signal). |
| **Actinic damage / pre-cancerous (AK)** | ◐ Partial | Medium peel (Jessner+TCA) reduces AK count but **inferior to 5-FU** (meta-analysis); a field-therapy adjunct, not first-line. |
| **Inflammaging / oxidative stress** | ◐ Indirect | Not a primary target; wounding is pro-inflammatory acutely. |
| **Barrier / hydration / water-loss** | ⚠️ *Worsened acutely* | Peels **transiently destroy** the barrier — this is the brand's product entry point (post-peel barrier repair), not a peel benefit. |
| **Senescence · mitochondria · glycation/AGEs · microbiome** | ❌ No | No credible evidence peels clear senescent cells, fix mitochondria, or reduce AGEs. *(A single phenol study noted ↑SIRT-6/7 expression — mechanistic curiosity, [unverified] as a clinical longevity claim.)* |

**Honest read:** peels are a **photoaging / texture / dyschromia** modality. Deep peels genuinely remodel dermis; superficial peels are surface-renewal (the AHA story). The trendy longevity hallmarks are **not** where peel evidence lives.

## 4. Evidence review by tier *(the core of this dossier)*

> Read by **depth × setting**. At-home/superficial evidence is weak and modest; medium is moderate; deep is the strongest aesthetic evidence in the whole modality — and the riskiest.

### Tier 1 — Mechanistic / in-vitro / ex-vivo
- Corneodesmolysis / calcium-chelation mechanism for AHAs — see [`../ingredients/alpha-hydroxy-acids.md`](../ingredients/alpha-hydroxy-acids.md) §2/§4 (Narda 2021 explant: glycolic pH 4 → ↑collagen, no TNF-α rise; *ISDIN-funded*). TCA/phenol act by direct protein coagulation (textbook chemistry; not a controlled study). *Limitation: mechanism, not clinical efficacy.*

### Tier 2 — Animal
- **Phenol-croton oil animal histology:** a dense **400–1000 μm band of new (type III) collagen** with epidermal thickening reported by **post-op day 21** in animal models (cited in Lee/JAAD phenol review, 2018). *Limitation: animal; supports mechanism of deep neocollagenesis, not a human aesthetic endpoint. No meaningful animal tier for superficial peels.*

### Tier 3 — Human, lower-grade (open-label / case series / split-face uncontrolled / pilot)
- **Grimes PE (1999), *Dermatol Surg* 25(1):18–22.** Pilot, **n=25, Fitzpatrick V–VI**, **salicylic 20–30%** × 5 peels q2wk + 4% hydroquinone pretreatment. **88% moderate-to-significant improvement; minimal/mild side effects in 16%.** *Foundational skin-of-color safety signal for salicylic. Limitation: small, open, concurrent hydroquinone confounds attribution.*
- **Phenol-croton oil long-term histology / case series.** Durable improvement in dermal architecture with **sustained neocollagenesis at 15–20 years** post-deep-peel reported in histologic follow-up (cited across Lee 2018 JAAD review; Nogueira 2026 retrospective n=102 satisfaction). *Limitation: uncontrolled, retrospective, expert-operator case material; selection/publication bias; no RCT can ethically randomize a deep ablative peel vs sham.*
- **TCA-CROSS for ice-pick acne scars** — multiple uncontrolled series + a split-face vs Er:Glass fractional laser (Kim et al.): **no significant difference** for ice-pick scars; **laser better for rolling scars.** *CROSS is effective for ice-pick/boxcar, poor for rolling. Limitation: small, heterogeneous, operator-dependent.*

### Tier 4 — Human, controlled (RCT / vehicle- or split-face randomized) *(the tier that supports real claims)*

**Melasma / dyschromia (skin of color):**
- **Dorgham NA et al. (2020), *J Cosmet Dermatol* 19(11):2812–2819.** **Systematic review + meta-analysis**, **10 RCTs + 3 comparative, n=478**, Fitzpatrick III–VI. **Glycolic beat TCA on MASI** (MD −1.89, p=.007); both glycolic (vs HQ n/s) and TCA (MD −5.30, p<.001) and Jessner's (MD −3.20, p=.004) beat hydroquinone as add-ons; **TCA carried higher erythema risk**. Conclusion: peels work as single agents in dark skin **with topical maintenance**. *Limitation: heterogeneous protocols; melasma recurs; "effective" ≠ durable.*
- **Kodali S et al. (2010), *J Am Acad Dermatol*.** **Prospective, randomized, split-face**, **n=20 (18 completed)** Latin American women, **salicylic 20–30%** × 4 + 4% HQ both sides. **NEGATIVE: salicylic peels added nothing over hydroquinone alone.** *Important honest counter-evidence — superficial peels are not a reliable melasma stand-alone.*

**Acne (and acne-related dyschromia):**
- **Chen X et al. (2018), *BMJ Open* 8(4):e019607.** **Systematic review, 12 RCTs, n=387.** Glycolic **beat placebo** (RR 2.30, 95% CI 1.40–3.77); most head-to-heads (GA vs salicylic vs Jessner vs TCA vs pyruvic) showed **no significant difference**; SA+mandelic > GA (85.3% vs 68.5%, p<.001). **Methodological quality "very low to moderate"; meta-analysis impossible (heterogeneity).** *Peels help mild-moderate acne, all roughly equivalent, evidence thin.*

**Actinic keratosis (field/photo-damage):**
- **Steeb T et al. (2021), *J Eur Acad Dermatol Venereol* (JEADV).** **Systematic review + meta-analysis, 8 studies (4 RCT), n=170.** **Jessner+TCA 35% significantly INFERIOR to 5-FU 5%** for clearance; an older study found ~75% AK reduction for both. **All studies high risk of bias.** *Peel = secondary field option, not first-line for AK.*

**Photoaging (medium TCA):**
- **TCA-for-photoaging systematic review (2021, *J Cosmet Dermatol*/PMC8423570).** 5 studies (3 randomized comparison, 2 prospective cohort): **TCA significantly improves cosmesis of photoaged facial skin.** *Limitation: small, mixed designs, no large vehicle-controlled RCT.*

**Superficial photoaging:** anchored by the brand's AHA RCTs (Stiller 1996, etc. — see [`../ingredients/alpha-hydroxy-acids.md`](../ingredients/alpha-hydroxy-acids.md) §4): real but **"modestly useful,"** below retinoids for wrinkles.

**What is proven vs borrowed:**
- **PROVEN:** Deep phenol = durable, histologically confirmed dermal remodeling and wrinkle effacement (best-in-modality, case-series/histology grade — no RCT possible). Medium TCA = moderate photoaging + acne-scar benefit. Superficial peels = mild dyschromia/acne/texture benefit, **roughly equivalent across agents**, evidence "very low to moderate."
- **BORROWED / NOT EARNED:** At-home <10% peels riding professional/deep-peel imagery. At-home units have **no dedicated efficacy RCTs** beyond the leave-on AHA literature; their realistic ceiling is the modest AHA cosmetic effect, **not** medium/deep resurfacing.

## 5. Treatment parameters / protocol

| | **At-home** | **Superficial (pro)** | **Medium** | **Deep** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Agent/% | glycolic/lactic/mandelic **<10%, pH >3** | glycolic 20–70%, SA 20–30%, mandelic, Jessner | **TCA 20–35%** ± Jessner/glycolic/CO₂ | **phenol–croton oil** |
| Depth | stratum corneum | epidermis→papillary dermis | papillary→upper reticular | mid-reticular dermis |
| Sessions | ongoing, weekly-ish | **series of 4–6, q2–4 wk** | 1 (repeat after months) | **single** (face) |
| Downtime | none–mild flaking | 1–7 d flaking/erythema | 7–14 d peeling, edema | **2+ wk** wound, weeks-months erythema |
| Operator | consumer | esthetician/derm | **physician/derm** | **physician only + cardiac monitor** |
| Result | modest, cumulative, **not durable** | modest, needs maintenance | moderate, lasts months–years | **dramatic, durable 10–20 yr** |

Deep-peel cardioprotective protocol: apply over **≥60 min** (not <30), full cardiac monitoring, IV hydration, **≥15-min intervals between facial cosmetic units** — this eliminates arrhythmia in series (§6).

## 6. Safety

- **PIH / hyperpigmentation (the dominant skin-of-color risk).** Any wounding can trigger PIH in Fitzpatrick III–VI; **risk rises with depth.** Mitigation = **agent selection** (salicylic, mandelic safest — superficial, lipophilic, lower inflammatory footprint; Grimes 1999 supports salicylic in V–VI), conservative depth, priming/sun protection, slow pacing. TCA carries higher erythema/PIH than glycolic (Dorgham 2020). Deep phenol reliably causes **permanent hypopigmentation / porcelain "alabaster" demarcation** — often disqualifying for medium/deep phototypes.
- **Scarring.** Rises sharply with depth and operator inexperience; medium/deep can scar (esp. lower face, re-peeled or under-healed skin).
- **Phenol cardiotoxicity (deep only — the headline danger).** Phenol is absorbed systemically and is **directly myocardial-toxic**: rapid application (>50% face in <30 min) → tachycardia → PVCs, bigeminy/trigeminy, atrial/ventricular tachycardia. **Slowing application to ≥60 min prevents arrhythmia.** Also hepatorenal/respiratory considerations. Requires monitoring, IV fluids, screening out cardiac/renal/hepatic disease. **Croton oil — not phenol — is the active "depth" agent** (Hetter, 1989–1995); modern formulas titrate croton oil to control depth/risk.
- **Barrier destruction + infection.** Every peel transiently destroys the barrier; risk of HSV reactivation (prophylaxis for medium/deep), bacterial/candidal infection, contact irritation. **This compromised-barrier window is the brand's product-relevance hook (§10).**
- **At-home misuse.** Over-frequency, layering acids, leaving on too long, skipping SPF (AHAs/peels increase UV sensitivity — see AHA §7 FDA sunburn-alert), DIY high-% "TCA from the internet" → real burns/scarring. The safety gap between a buffered <10% pad and a 35% TCA is enormous and consumers underestimate it.
- **Contraindications:** active infection/HSV, isotretinoin (relative/recent), pregnancy (deep phenol contraindicated), keloid history, unrealistic expectations, deep phototype for deep peels.

## 7. Regulatory & claims

- **Cosmetic vs drug/procedure line:** Low-% leave-on/rinse-off "at-home peels" for *appearance* = **cosmetic** (same OTC/cosmetic rules and FDA AHA **Sunburn-Alert** labeling as the AHA dossier §8: ≤10% AHA, pH ≥3.5, SPF/limit-sun directions). Professional superficial/medium peels = **medical/aesthetic procedures**; deep phenol = **surgical-grade medical procedure**. There is no consumer "peel device" the brand could clear and sell into the deep tier.
- **What a cosmetic brand may claim** (if it sold an at-home peel or, better, post-peel care):
  - ✅ Safe cosmetic: "gently resurfaces," "smooths and refines texture," "reveals brighter, more even-looking skin," "**replenishes and supports the moisture barrier after exfoliation/peels**," "comforts and hydrates post-treatment."
  - ⚠️ Risky / drug-/procedure-like — needs substantiation or counsel: "peels away years," "professional-strength peel at home," "removes wrinkles," "resurfaces like a TCA peel," "treats melasma/actinic damage," any depth/medical claim. **Do not imply at-home = clinic depth.**

## 8. Cost & accessibility / market

- **At-home peel pads/solutions:** ~$15–60 retail; commodity AHA/BHA — margin lives in brand/formulation (on-thesis: "buy commodity bases, invest in brand").
- **Professional superficial:** ~$100–250/session, series of 4–6.
- **Medium TCA:** ~$300–800/session.
- **Deep phenol:** **$3,000–10,000+**, often OR/sedation; specialist-only, low volume, high skill.
- **Market:** chemical-peel procedures are a large, growing aesthetic segment (millions of procedures/yr; superficial dominates by volume). The **adjacent consumer opportunity for this brand is post-peel/post-procedure barrier care**, not selling the procedure.

## 9. Hype vs evidence

- **"Professional-strength peel at home" →** *Misleading.* Consumer peels are capped at <10%/pH >3 for safety; they cannot and should not reach medium/deep depth. Realistic benefit = the modest AHA cosmetic effect.
- **"Peels boost collagen / reverse aging" →** *True only for deep phenol* (and to a lesser, moderate degree medium TCA). For superficial/at-home it is the soft AHA signal — never a numeric or "reversal" claim.
- **"Peels cure melasma" →** *Overstated.* Adjunct at best; one RCT (Kodali 2010) found salicylic added nothing over hydroquinone; melasma recurs; topicals often equal/beat peels.
- **"Natural acid = gentle" →** *False.* Depth tracks concentration/pH/agent, not provenance.
- **"Deep peel is just a stronger facial" →** *Dangerously false.* Phenol can cause arrhythmia, permanent pigment loss, scarring — it is surgery.
- **Genuinely supported:** deep phenol delivers the most durable non-surgical wrinkle correction in dermatology; medium TCA helps photoaging and ice-pick scars; superficial peels modestly help texture/dyschromia/acne and are roughly interchangeable.

## 10. Brand relevance & strategic verdict

- **Interaction with our thesis:** Peels **overlap and partly compete** with the brand's exfoliation/renewal territory (the AHA dossier already owns the *ingredient* version). The decision-log position is explicit: **"no peel/AHA-first identity"** — so the brand should **not** lead with peels or build a procedure. But peels create a perfect, recurring **barrier-repair moment**: every peel transiently destroys the barrier, and post-peel care is *exactly* a premium barrier-repair cream's job (ceramides, HA, panthenol, glycerin, squalane — the brand's wheelhouse; widely recommended for post-peel recovery).
- **Product-adjacency call:**
  - **❌ Avoid** selling/building the *procedure* or a "pro-strength at-home peel" (off-thesis, claims/safety liability, commoditized, competes with our own positioning).

  - Net one-liner: **❌ on the peel itself; ✅ on "what your skin needs after one."**
- **Content/SEO angle:** "At-home vs professional chemical peels — what's the real difference (and what to put on your skin after)?"; "Superficial vs medium vs deep peels, honestly"; "Are at-home peels worth it?"; "Best barrier care after a chemical peel." ## Evidence verification (adversarial)

**Verified:** 2026-06-21. Independent re-check of headline claims against primary sources. Each cited study was checked for (a) does it exist and say what's claimed, (b) correct evidence tier, (c) is benefit borrowed across depth/setting lines.

### Overall evidence grade — **graded separately by depth:**
- **Deep phenol-croton oil: B+ (toward A on durability)** — the strongest *real-world* aesthetic evidence in the whole modality (durable, histologically confirmed 15–20-yr neocollagenesis; large expert case series). Capped below A because **no RCT exists or can ethically exist** (you cannot sham-randomize an ablative facial peel), evidence is case-series/retrospective/operator-dependent, and harms (cardiotoxicity, permanent pigment change, scarring) are serious.
- **Medium TCA: B−** — moderate, real benefit for photoaging and ice-pick scars (small systematic reviews, mixed designs).
- **Superficial / at-home: C** — modest, cumulative, **agents roughly interchangeable**, evidence "very low to moderate" (Chen 2018), and one melasma RCT is outright negative (Kodali 2010). For the **at-home consumer claim specifically: C/D** — no dedicated at-home efficacy RCTs; benefit is the soft AHA cosmetic effect, not the procedure data the marketing implies.

### Main caveats
1. **Depth × setting is everything.** The biggest risk in this modality's marketing is letting deep-peel data halo onto at-home pads. The dossier separates the grades; keep them separate everywhere.
2. **Deep-peel evidence is strong but un-randomizable** — durability is genuinely impressive, but it's case-series/histology, not RCT, and the procedure is high-harm. "Strong" here means "strong for this kind of evidence," not "RCT-proven."
3. **Superficial peels are roughly interchangeable** and modest; melasma benefit is adjunctive and recurrence-prone (one negative RCT).
4. **PIH/permanent hypopigmentation in skin of color** is the defining safety constraint; salicylic/mandelic safest, phenol often disqualifying.
5. **The brand's role is post-peel barrier care, not the peel** — and that role is genuinely evidence-aligned (post-peel barrier destruction is real; ceramide/HA/panthenol recovery is standard derm advice).

### Claims that should be softened
- Never say or imply "professional/clinic-depth peel at home." Cap at-home language at AHA-cosmetic level.
- "Peels boost collagen / reverse aging" → reserve for **deep** (and softly **medium**) peels only; never for at-home/superficial.
- "Peels treat melasma/actinic keratosis" → adjunct only; AK peel is **inferior to 5-FU**; melasma recurs and one RCT is negative.
- Post-peel cream claims must stay cosmetic ("supports the barrier, comforts after exfoliation"), not therapeutic/wound-healing drug claims.

**Net:** Accurate, well-sourced, and disciplined about the depth gradient. Deep phenol earns a high mark on durable, histology-backed remodeling (but is high-harm and un-randomizable); superficial/at-home peels are modest and roughly interchangeable, with one cautionary negative melasma RCT. The brand's defensible play is **post-peel barrier repair (✅/🤝)**, not selling peels (❌) — consistent with the decision-log's "no peel/AHA-first identity."
